Code of Civil Procedure (V of 1908)

Section 115

The High Court Division in its revisional jurisdiction could only reverse those findings by specific reference to evidence and materials, which were not considered or were misread by the trial Court, which would have materially affected the decision had they been considered. In view of the above discussion, we find that the trial Court and appellate Court have not misread any evidence nor left any material evidence out of consideration. JUDGMENT

Muhammad Imman Ali, J: This appeal by leave is directed against the judgement and order dated 29.11.2006 passed by a Single Bench of the High Court Division in Civil Revision No.259 of 2001 making the Rule absolute and setting aside the judgement and decree dated 29.11.1999 passed by the learned Additional District Judge and Anti-Smuggling Tribunal, Sylhet in Title Appeal No.239 of 1997 dismissing the appeal and affirming those dated 20.10.1997 passed by the learned Subordinate Judge-in-Charge, First Court, Sylhet in Title Suit No.85 of 1995 dismissing the suit.

2.            The facts relevant for disposal of the appeal, in short, are that respondent Nos. 1-8 (heirs of the original plaintiff Irfan Ali Chowdhury, who had purchased the suit land from the original owners) as plaintiffs filed Title Suit No.350 of 1968 in the Court of the Munsif, Sylhet Sadar for declaration of title, recovery of possession by removing structures and also for permanent injunction. On transfer to the Court of Subordinate Judge, First Court, Sylhet the suit was lastly numbered as Title Suit No.85 of 1995.

3.            The plaintiffs stated, inter alia, that the suit land is situated at locality Roy Hussain known as Amberkhana, Sylhet town and the same belonged to Karamatullah and others. The said owners used to hold and possess the suit land through their tenants Amu Singha and Garuba Singha. Karamatullah and others sold the suit land to Md. Irfan Ali Chowdhury by registered kabala dated 28.02.1927 and made over possession to him. Said Irfan Ali Chowdhury was the original plaintiff of the suit and died during pendency of the suit and was substituted by the present plaintiffs. The suit land comprises plot No.2 and western part of plot No.1 as per the said kabala. Irfan Ali Chowdhury used to hold and possess the suit land through said Amu Singha and Garuba Singha. Subsequently, Amu Singha took settlement of the suit land from him for one year by registered kabauliyat dated 27.07.1927 and used to live there till his death in 1342 B.S.

4.            It is the plaintiff’s case that after the death of Amu Singha his sons abandoned the said land and left for Monipur, India. Wife of Amu Singha also surrendered her right by making over possession to the original plaintiff and shifted to the house of her son-in-law Sinda Singha. The original plaintiff in turn gave up claim for arrear rent. Then the original plaintiff held and possessed the suit land by growing vegetables in one part and by cutting trees and bamboos from the other part.

5.            Further case of the original plaintiff is that he settled the suit land for one year from 1st Baishakh 1344 B.S. to one Jayanta Singha who executed a kabuliyat dated 1st Bhadra 1344 B.S. and after expiry of one year Jayanta Singha made over possession to the original plaintiff on 1st Baishakh 1345 B.S. Subsequently, one Mrs. Rosemary Keymer and her husband Mr. W. C. Keymer took lease of the western part of the suit land from the original plaintiff for 10 years by registered lease deed dated 19.12.1944 and they having possessed the said land for 2 years surrendered possession to the original plaintiff.

6.            Irfan Ali Chowdhury once mortgaged the suit land with Town Bank, Sylhet and the bank filed certificate case for mortgage money and he amicably paid the dues and the property was released from mortgage. At the initial stage of settlement and survey operation the suit land was recorded in Irfan Ali Chowdhury’s name, but defendant No.2 subsequently fraudulently got it recorded in the name of Krishna Singha, son of Amu Singha beyond his knowledge. The original plaintiff then filed objection case No.33 of 1961 and order was passed on contest for recording the plaintiff’s name. But defendant No.1 then got the name of the plaintiff changed and caused preparation of record of right in his own name beyond knowledge of the plaintiff.

7.            Further case of the plaintiffs is that defendant No.2 announced in the locality that he inherited the suit land from Krishana Singha who according to him is dead. But in fact Krishna Singha is alive and he visited Sylhet with his children three years back. Krishna had no right or title and he did not put any claim after his father’s death. Defendant No.2 created some collusive documents without consideration in support of his alleged claim. The defendants threatened to disturb the possession of the plaintiff once in 1965 and the original plaintiff had to file Title Suit No.301 of 1965 in the then First Court of Sadar Munsif, Sylhet for declaration of his title and permanent injunction. The plaintiff did not have all his documents available at that time. Further, the defendants of the suit belonged to minority community and the suit could not proceed for want of permission under East Pakistan Ordinance I of 1964. By the time permission was obtained defendant No.1 in collusion with defendant No.2 dispossessed the plaintiff in Chaitra 1373 B.S. and raised a Kutcha shed in the western part of the suit land. So, the plaintiff had to withdraw the said suit with permission to sue afresh.

8.            It is further stated that although the heirs of Amu Singha surrendered possession of the suit land to the original plaintiff in 1342 BS, it was wrongly referred to as 1362 B.S. in the plaint of Title Suit No.301 of 1966. Therefore, the plaintiff filed the instant suit for declaration of title and recovery of vacant khas possession upon removing structure, mesne profit and permanent injunction restraining the defendant opposite parties from changing the nature and feature of the suit land. 

9.            Defendant No.1 contested the suit by filing written statement stating, inter alia, that the suit land belonged to Amu Singha in jote right who took settlement from the original plaintiff by executing kabuliyat dated 1st Ashar 1344 B.S. While Amu Singha was living therein, he died leaving his son Krishna Singha, who died unmarried leaving his only nephew (sister’s son) defendant No.2 as his only heir. Accordingly, defendant No.2 held and possessed the suit land. During settlement and survey operation the suit land was recorded in the name of Krishna Singha noting the name of the original plaintiff as landlord. The plaintiff in collusion with the Objection Officer changed the record-of–right in his name. Defendant No.2 then filed appeal under section 19(2) of the State Acquisition and Tenancy Act which was allowed on contest on 09.09.1961.

10.        The further case of defendant No.1 is that defendant No.2 was possessing the suit land partly as homestead and partly as chara bari (abandoned homestead) and then he transferred part of the suit land as noted in schedule II of the written statement by kabala dated 07.08.1965 to defendant No.1 and delivered possession. Defendant No.1 has been possessing part of the suit land purchased by him as stated above by constructing hut and planting trees. The plaintiff is cunning and he took defendant No.2 in his confidence with high hopes that he would buy the land at a high price. Defendant No.2 having decided to sell part of the suit land to defendant No.1 began to kill time and refused presentation of kabala for registration and defendant No.1 then presented the kabala dated 07.08.1965 for registration before the Sub-registrar.  Defendant No.2 did not appear at the instance of the plaintiff. So defendant No.1 had to file appeal before the District Registrar and the kabala was registered on contest on 02.02.1966. The plaintiff and defendant No.2 are in collusion and under influence of the plaintiff, defendant No.2 is remaining silent.

11.        After hearing the parties and considering the evidence and materials on record the Subordinate Judge dismissed the suit. Then the plaintiff preferred Title Appeal No.239 of 1997, which was dismissed on contest.

12.        Against the judgement of the appellate Court the plaintiff preferred Civil Revision No.259 of 2001 before the High Court Division and obtained Rule, which upon hearing the parties was made absolute. Hence, the defendants filed Civil Petition for Leave to Appeal No.229 of 2007 before this Division.

13.        Leave was granted on the following submissions of the learned Advocate appearing for the petitioners:

“I. That that High Court Division committed error of law in interfering with the concurrent findings of fact of the Courts below in the absence of any legal infirmity in such findings.

II. That the Courts below came to the relevant findings upon consideration of the evidence on record and the High Court Division committed error of law in setting aside those findings on the ground of non-consideration of material evidence without mentioning which of the material evidence on record was not considered by the courts below.”

14.        Mr. Abdul Quayum, learned Senior Advocate appearing on behalf of the appellants made submissions in line with the grounds upon which leave was granted. He further submitted that both the trial Court and the appellate Court concurrently found that the plaintiffs-respondents could not prove by oral and documentary evidence surrender and possession in favour of the original plaintiff followed by dispossession, and thus the High Court Division committed error of law in reversing the concurrent finding of fact without adverting to oral and documentary evidence. He further submitted that the Courts below found that the record of right is correct and based on evidence and as such, the High Court Division could only reverse such finding by adverting to evidence on record, which it did not do.

15.        Mr. Qumrul Hoque Siddique, learned Advocate appearing on behalf of respondent No.8 made submission in support of the impugned judgement and order of the High Court Division. He also submitted that neither the trial Court nor the appellate Court took into consideration the contents of Exhibit-‘Ga’(sale deed in favour of defendant No.1-predecessor of the appellants) which shows that Gopendra Singha was in possession of the suit land on payment of rent to the original owner Irfan Ali Chowdhury. Subsequently, he claimed the property by inheritance from Krishna Singha, and thus the story of inheritance by Gopendra Singha from Krishna Singha is falsified. Hence, the High Court Division was correct in reversing the decision of the Courts below, even though their findings were concurrent. He further submitted that the wife of Amu Singha surrendered the land to the plaintiff. He further submitted that the Courts below failed to appreciate Exhibits 2 and 3 and did not consider the evidence of P.Ws 1 to 5, who unequivocally deposed that dispossession took place on 1st Chaitra, 1373 BS after defendant No.2 executed the deed on 07.08.1965 in favour of defendant No.1 and wrongly held that the plaintiff failed to prove his dispossession. The learned Advocate also submitted that the Courts below failed to consider material facts on record showing that defendant No.2 was not a tenant under the original plaintiff nor was he the heir of Amu Singha, and hence no title passed to defendant No.1. He further submitted that the High Court Division correctly found and observed that defendant No.2 illegally dispossessed the plaintiff in Chaitra, 1373 BS and defendant No.1 did not produce any rent receipt or any other papers to show that Krishna Singha was a tenant of the plaintiff. Moreover, Gopendra Singha did not come to support the claim of defendant No. 1. He lastly submitted that the original plaintiff was in possession of the suit land through tenants W.C. Keymer and his wife Rosemary by registered lease deed dated 1944. They handed back possession of the property to the original plaintiff after two years of the tenancy.

16.        Mr. Mohammad Abdul Hai, learned Advocate-on-Record appearing on behalf of respondent Nos. 1,2 and 5-7 also made submissions in support of the impugned judgement and order of the High Court Division.

17.        We have considered the submissions of the learned Advocates appearing for the parties concerned, perused the impugned judgement and order of the High Court Division and other connected papers on record.

18.        On a perusal of the judgement of the trial Court, we find that the trial Court observed that there was no independent witness to support the claim of the plaintiff that Krishna Singha’s mother handed possession of the suit land back to Irfan Ali Chowdhury. On the other hand, the trial Court highlighted the fact that the plaintiff’s witnesses deposed that after Krishna Singha, his nephew Gopendra Singha was in possession of the suit land and that Gopendra Singha sold the suit land to defendant No.1. The trial Court observed that the evidence of P.W.3 Tara Miah supports the fact that the mother of Krishna Singha did not hand back possession of the suit land to Irfan Ali Chowdhury and that Krishna Singha did not abandon the suit land to go to India. It was observed that there was no documentary or oral evidence supporting the claim of Irfan Ali Chowdhury that he took physical possession of the suit land in 1342 BS. The trial Court further observed that the plaintiff did not lodge any diary or case with the police with regard to his dispossession from the suit land. It was noted that P.W.3 Tara Miah admitted in his cross-examination that Gopendra Singha went into possession of the suit land after Krishna Singha and, thereafter, sold the same to Makhon Miah (defendant No.1). It was also noted that P.W.4 in his cross-examination admitted that Monipuri people have been living in the suit property since 1950. 

19.        With regard to the possession by the plaintiff through tenant W.C. Keymer, the finding of the trial Court is that there was no witness to support the claim that Rosemary and her husband ever went into possession of the suit land. P.Ws 2 and 3 stated in their cross-examination that they did not know Rosemary. P.W.1, though she stated that Rosemary remained in possession of the suit land for two years and then handed back possession to Irfan Ali Chowdhury, the trial Court noted that there was no evidence to show that possession was handed back nor that Rosemary took possession of the suit land from Krishna Singha or Gopendra Singha. The trial Court concluded that Rosemary did not get possession of the suit land and that her letter of settlement was only a paper transaction.    

20.        The appellate Court affirmed the judgement of the trial Court finding that the documents prove that defendant No.1 purchased the suit property on 07.08.1965 and has been in possession since that time and that the plaintiffs-appellants do not have any right and title in the property.

21.        The High Court Division observed that defendant No.2 Gopendra Singha did not come to support the case of defendant No.1 and defendant No.1 did not produce any rent receipt or other papers to show that Krishna Singha was a tenant. It was observed that Irfan Ali Chowdhury got possession of the suit land and all the P.Ws and even D.Ws supported this claim of the plaintiffs and that the Courts below committed serious error in not considering that the suit land belonged to the father of the plaintiffs by right of purchase evident by a deed which was more than 50 years old.

22.        We note that the High Court Division mentioned that the trial Court after hearing the parties, without considering the evidence on record, wrongly and illegally dismissed the suit. However, we note from the impugned judgement that the High Court Division did not specifically refer to any of the evidence and materials on record to show which evidence the Courts below did not consider. The deed, by which Irfan Ali Chowdhury got the suit property is indeed very old. However, subsequent events and documents relating to those later events tend to indicate that Irfan Ali Chowdhury was no longer in possession of the suit land. On the contrary the evidence of the witnesses shows that defendant No.2 and his predecessors-in-interest were all along in possession of the suit land. We note that Krishna Singha, the predecessor of defendant No.1 got his name recorded in the record of rights as tenant, which the original plaintiff got corrected. But thereafter, Gopendra Singha (defendant No. 2 who sold the suit property to defendant No.1) filed an appeal under section 19(2) of the State Acquisition and Tenancy Act and got his name recorded in the record of rights. Khodeja Akhter Chowdhury who is the daughter of Irfan Ali Chowdhury deposing as P.W 1 could not controvert such claim. She admitted that her father’s challenge against the record of rights was not fruitful. She also could not say whether her father took the matter further on appeal under section 53 of the State Acquisition and Tenancy Act. We note that P.W 1 stated in her evidence that her father took possession of the suit land in 1342 BS (c.1935 AD), whereas PW4 stated that in 1950 Manipuri people lived on the suit land. Coincidentally, in Title Suit No.301 of 1965, Irfan Ali Chowdhury had claimed that possession of the suit land had been handed over to him in 1362 BS. (c.1955 AD). Moreover, we note that P.W1 stated in her deposition that 1362 BS was mistakenly written and that the correct date was 1342. This is clearly falsified by the evidence of P.W4.

23.        The trial Court thoroughly assessed the evidence, and noted that P.W.3 Tara Miah admitted in his cross-examination that the plaintiffs’ side neither were nor are in possession of the suit land. It was also noted that there is no evidence to show when Irfan Ali Chowdhury regained possession of the suit land and when he was dispossessed therefrom.

24.        In view of the evidence of P.Ws 3 and 4 it is clear that the predecessors of defendant No.1 were in possession of the suit land in 1950, even according to the plaintiffs’ own witness. Thus, about 15 years before defendant No.1 purchased the suit land, Monipuri people were in possession and there was no evidence that possession reverted to the original plaintiff at any time after 1950. 

25.        The learned Advocate for the appellant referred to the decision in the case of Amanatullah and others Vs Ali Mohammad Bhuiyan and another reported in 17 BLD(AD)199. In the cited case it was held by this Division as follows:

“It cannot be gainsaid that a finding of fact, whether concurrent or not, arrived at by the lower appellate court is binding on the High Court, as a revisional court except in certain well-defined exceptional circumstances, such as non-consideration or misreading of material evidence affecting the merit of the case.”   

26.        The same view was expressed by this Division in the case of Hazari Bala Sana and another Vs Niron alias Niranjan Mandal and others reported in 17 BLD (AD) 294.

27.        In the facts of the instant case discussed above, we find that the High Court Division has reversed the concurrent finding of facts of the Courts below without specifically identifying any evidence which was misread or not considered by the trial Court or the appellate Court. The reference to non-consideration of the deed in favour of Irfan Ali Chowdhury dating to 1927 is of little consequence in view of the fact that admittedly the defendants’ predecessors were the tenants in possession of the suit land from the original owners, having jote right by way of registered kabuliyat, and the record of rights was in their names as tenants. Irfan Ali Chowdhury purchased the land with the defendants’ predecessor in possession as tenant. The finding of the trial Court and the appellate Court is that the plaintiffs could not prove that Irfan Ali Chowdhury ever went into possession of the suit land.

28.        We find that the trial Court has given a very elaborate judgement discussing the evidence and materials on record, and came to a finding based on those evidence and materials. The High Court Division in its revisional jurisdiction could only reverse those findings by specific reference to evidence and materials, which were not considered or were misread by the trial Court, which would have materially affected the decision had they been considered.

29.        In view of the above discussion, we find that the trial Court and appellate Court have not misread any evidence nor left any material evidence out of consideration. In our opinion the decision of the High Court Division is not in accordance with the law and accordingly, the appeal is allowed and the judgement and order of the High Court Division is set aside.  There will be no order as to costs.
