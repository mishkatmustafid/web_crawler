Constitution of Bangladesh, 1972

Article 102

The Agreement entered into by the writ-petitioner had no legal basis. Hence, there was no question of any right accruing in favour of the writ-petitioner. We say so, firstly because the Liquidator apparently did not exist at that time. Secondly, his power with regard to the Mill was only to the extent of selling the same through tender and not by any agreement. Every part of the transaction that followed, including payment and acceptance of installments was without lawful authority. In the facts of the instant case the contract entered into by the Official Liquidator is not a statutory contract, apart from the fact that, the post of the liquidator had been abolished and in any event the liquidator was authorised to sell the property in question through tender.          . . . (24 and 25)

For the Appellants : Mr. Mahbubey Alam, Attorney General, instructed by Mr. Haridas Paul, Advocate-on-Record

For the Respondents :  Mr. Rokanuddin Mahmud, Senior Advocate with Mr. Sheik Fazle-Noor Taposh  and Mr. Mehedi Hasan, Advocates,   instructed by Mrs. Madhumalati Chowdhury Barua, Advocate-on-Record

JUDGMENT

Muhammad Imman Ali, J:- This civil appeal, by leave, is directed against the judgement and order dated 15.06.2015 passed by a Division Bench of the High Court Division in Writ Petition No.10820 of 2014 disposing of the Rule Nisi.

2.             The facts of the case, in brief, are that the writ-petitioner (respondent No.1 herein) is a proprietorship firm established under the laws of Bangladesh which is represented by its proprietor, Md. Faruk Islam Bhuiyan. The Chisty Textile Mills Limited, Doulatpur, Cumilla (the Mill) was established as a private industry in 1962 which was incorporated under the Companies Act, 1913. After the independence of Bangladesh in 1971, along with other textile and jute mills of the country, the Mill was also nationalised and was taken over pursuant to President’s Order No.27 of 1972 and was vested upon Bangladesh Textile Mills Corporation (BTMC) for its maintenance, management and improvement.

3.             Subsequently, as continuous loss was being incurred by the Mill, the Government closed down the operation of the Mill from the month of June in 1998. Thereafter, with the intention of privatising the Mill through the Privatisation Commission, the Government invited international tender. However, even after inviting international tender four times, the Mill was not sold for different reasons. Pursuant to the decision of the Bangladesh Government and by virtue of article 23(2) of the President’s Order No.27 of 1972 read with Order dated 01.11.2003 of the Ministry of Textiles and Jute, the Mill was declared to be wound up with effect from 09.11.2003. Accordingly, writ-respondent No.2 was appointed as the Liquidator for the Mill to take all actions required to be taken and all responsibilities required to be discharged in this regard vide Memo dated 13.11.2003. According to the letter dated 02.08.2003 signed by the Senior Assistant Secretary and the circular from the Cabinet Division, it was stated that all actions required to be taken and all responsibilities required to sell or discharge the Mill would also be monitored by the Ministry of Industry. The writ-petitioner vide different applications made to writ-respondent No.2 expressed its intention of purchasing the Mill. In response to the said applications, writ-respondent No.2 informed the writ-petitioner that a land dispute existed and only after disposal of the case regarding the land dispute and after review and assessing the valuation of all the movable and immovable properties, inventories and liabilities of the Mill by a CA firm, a tender should be floated to that effect. Subsequently, a CA firm was appointed in this regard which analysed and decided that the value of the assets of the Mill was taka 15.11 crore and liabilities were 44.56 crore. The writ-petitioner being aware of such valuation again expressed its intention to purchase the Mill. However, when the file was presented for approval before the Minister of the Ministry of Textiles and Jute, he declined to approve it and decided that the Mill would be sold at taka 35 crore in the meeting dated 04.07.2013, which was evident from the minutes of the meeting bearing reference dated 10.07.2013. The writ-petitioner was informed about the decision of the Minister vide letter dated 14.08.2013. Due to the subsisting recession at that time, the writ-petitioner was unable to pay the entire amount at once. Hence the writ-petitioner made a number of applications for paying the purchase price of taka 35 crore by making a down payment of taka 15 crore and paying the rest of taka 20 crore in instalments. The application was allowed and accordingly, the writ-petitioner vide pay order Nos.0000061 and 0847087 dated 13.11.2013 paid a total of taka 15 crore to writ-respondent No.2 which was duly received by him.

4.             Thereafter, writ-respondent No.2 and the writ-petitioner entered into a deed of agreement for sale dated 01.01.2014 for sale of the movable and immovable assets of the non-operative Chisty Textile Mills Limited wherein it had been specifically agreed by the parties that upon payment by the writ-petitioner of the remaining balance of taka 20 crore in 5(five) equal instalments within 10 (ten) months from the date of the agreement, writ-respondent No.2 would have the legal obligation to execute and register necessary sale deed in favour of the writ-petitioner in order to transfer the ownership of Chisty Textile Mills Limited along with all its movable and immovable proprieties.

5.             In accordance with clause 3 of the agreement, the possession of the Mill was transferred and handed over to the writ-petitioner vide letter dated 06.01.2014 and subsequently, the writ-petitioner acknowledged the delivery of the possession of the Mills on 07.01.2014 vide Memo dated 09.01.2014.

6.             All of a sudden, writ-respondent No.2 issued a Memo dated 18.02.2014 restraining the writ-petitioner from carrying out any activity in relation to Chisty Textile Mills Limited until further instruction. In the said Memo, writ-respondent No.2 asserted that pursuant to some news report on 11.02.2014 in the Daily Janakantha and the Daily Sangbad which alleged some fraud with respect to the winding up of the Mill, all activities of the Mill should be stopped.

7.             Upon receiving the said Memo dated 18.02.2014, the writ-petitioner made an application dated 24.02.2014 before respondent No.2 praying for cancellation of the same and reinstatement of the Memo dated 09.01.2014.

8.             Thereafter, writ-respondent No.2, in accordance with the order of Prime Minister’s Office’s Memo dated 27.02.2014 issued Memo dated 17.04.2014 wherein, upon summarising the entire transaction it was stated that the operation of the said Mill has duly been stayed by writ-respondent No.2 and that the matter is under process and a meeting has been called in this regard. In the meantime, the writ-petitioner in accordance with the Agreement, paid the 1st instalment of taka 4.00 crore vide pay orders No.0850777 and 0850778 dated 13.04.2014 of taka 2.00 crore each along with fine of taka 5.00 lac by pay order No.0850776 dated 13.04.2014. Subsequently, the writ-petitioner expressed its willingness to pay the 2nd instalment of taka 4.00 crore to writ-respondent No.2, who verbally instructed the writ-petitioner, who accordingly made payment of the 2nd instalment vide pay order No.0006430 dated 30.006.2014 of taka 4.00 crore.

9.             Thereafter, the writ-petitioner tried to deposit the 3rd instalment by pay order which writ-respondent No.2 was reluctant to receive. In this connection, the writ-petitioner vide letter dated 17.07.2014 prayed before respondent No.2 to accept the 3rd instalment along with the rest of the outstanding purchase price and thereafter execute a sale deed and register the same in favour of the writ-petitioner as agreed between the parties in the Agreement.

10.         Having received no response from writ-respondent No.2, the writ-petitioner sent a letter to the Minister, Ministry of Jute and Textile dated 28.08.2014 concerning the same matter as stated above, but the writ-petitioner did not receive any reply.  

11.         Writ-respondent Nos.1 and 2 contested the writ-petition by filing affidavit-in-opposition contending, inter alia, that as per the terms and condition of clause 3 of the agreement for sale in question dated 01.01.2014, possession could be handed over after executing the final deed. Since question was raised in the process of transferring the mill in question, the authority issued the letter dated 18.02.2014 and thereby restrained the writ-petitioner from carrying out any activity in relation to the mill in question only in order to protect the property of the State.

12.         The further case of respondent No.1 is that serious allegation was raised for selling the Mill in question in such a way without holding any competent tender and when the matter came to the notice of the office of the Prime Minister, who of her own accord, formed an enquiry committee to enquire into the matter and who, after conducting enquiry on 31.08..2014, submitted a detailed report wherein it was categorically found that the entire selling process of the mill in question was done without floating public tender inasmuch as the same had been sold through the Liquidation Cell, which had been abolished on 16.02.2011.

13.         Thereafter, the matter was sent to the Ministry of Law, Justice and Parliamentary Affairs for legal opinion who on 20.07.2014 opined that the entire sale process had been done illegally. After conducting the enquiry, by an office order dated 28.09.2014, the matter was intimated to the Liquidator, Liquidation Cell. Therefore, under such a compelling situation, writ-respondent No.1 was under a moral obligation to accept the defaulted instalment from the writ-petitioner.

14.         The further case of writ-respondent No.2 in short is that the writ-petitioner proved himself to be a defaulter and the writ-petitioner in making the payment of 2nd instalment caused much delay, inasmuch as according to the terms of the agreement the premium of 2nd instalment ought to have been paid by 30th April, 2014 and thereby the writ-petitioner himself violated the terms of the agreement dated 01.01.2014 and as such writ-respondent No.2 did not accept premium for 2nd instalment from the writ-petitioner.   

15.         By the impugned judgement and order, the High Court Division disposed of the Rule Nisi directing the writ-respondents to allow the writ-petitioner to resume its activities in relation to Chisty Textile Mills Limited and to receive the installments for the balance amount from the writ-petitioner and execute and register necessary sale deed in favour of the writ-petitioner in compliance with the terms of the Deed of Agreement for sale dated 01.01.2014 executed by writ-respondent No.2 in accordance with law.

16.         Hence, the writ-respondents as the petitioners filed Civil Petition for Leave to Appeal No. 3468 of 2015.

Leave was granted to consider the following grounds:

“I.  Because, the subject matter of the writ-petition being a commercial contract and not the contract of the sovereign the same cannot be amenable in writ jurisdiction; the High Court Division committed error in entertaining the writ petition without considering the aforesaid aspect of the matter and hence the judgement and order of the High Court Division is liable to be set aside.

II.  Because, the property in question namely ‘Chisty Textile Mills Ltd.’ is an important property of the Government and without holding any competent tender by floating public tender notice, selling of the property by negotiation through Liquidator is illegal, the High Court Division committed error in not considering the aforesaid aspect of the matter and thus the judgement and order of the High Court Division is liable to be set aside.”

17.         Mr. Mahbubey Alam, learned Attorney General, appearing on behalf of the appellants made submissions in line with the grounds upon which leave was granted. By reference to the decisions in the case of Sharping Matshajibi Samabaya Samity Ltd. Vs. Bangladesh & ors. reported in 39 DLR (AD)85, Bangladesh Power Development Board and others vs Md. Asaduzzaman Sikder reported in 9 BLC (AD)1 and Superintendent Engineer, RHD Sylhet and others v. Md. Eunus and Brothers (Pvt.) Ltd. reported in 31 (AD)BLD 1, the learned Attorney General submitted that the contract in question was not a sovereign contract, but purely a commercial contract and as such the High Court Division erred in entertaining the matter under the writ jurisdiction.

18.         Mr. Rokanuddin Mahmud, learned Counsel with Mr. Sheik Fazle-Noor Taposh, and Mr. Mehedi Hasan Chowdhury, learned Advocates, appearing on behalf of the respondents made submissions in support of the impugned judgement and order of the High Court Division. It was further submitted that the decision of the Bangladesh Government by virtue of Article 23 (2) of the President Order 27 of 1972 was that with order dated 01.11.2003 of the Ministry of Textiles and Jute, the Mill in question was declared to be wound up with effect from 09.11.2003. Accordingly, on 13.11.2003 liquidator was appointed who disposed of the Mill in favour of the writ-petitioner in a transparent way, and before that 2 times the matter was put in auction, but competent bidder was not found and continuous loss was being incurred from the Mill. Subsequently, the writ-petitioner purchased the Mill at the highest rate of Tk. 35 crore. It was further submitted that the contract dated 01.01.2014 was confirmed in between the parties and part consideration was paid and the writ petitioner did not breach any condition of contract for sale so, the seller has no option to go beyond the agreement for sale and the Ministry of Textile and Jute has legal obligation to perform its part of the contract. The price settled at Tk. 35 crore is proper value of the property in question and part consideration money was received without any objection so, the appellants subsequently cannot refuse to perform their part of the contract which is illegal. It was also submitted that the appellants have most arbitrarily, illegally and with mala fide intention restrained the respondents from carrying on any activity in relation to the Mill although the respondent has lawful possession in the same in accordance with the agreement. It was submitted that the appellants restrained the respondents from carrying out any activity in relation to the Mill without giving opportunity to be heard and as such, the actions of the appellants have violated the principle of natural justice. The appellant's action in restraining the writ-petitioner from carrying on any activity related to the Mill and their deliberate failure to receive installment for the balance amount from the respondents and execute and register necessary sale deed in favour of them in compliance with the terms of the Agreement have clearly infringed the writ-petitioner's fundamental right to property guaranteed and protected under Article 42 of Part III of the Constitution of the People's Republic of Bangladesh and as such, direction upon the appellants to allow the respondents to resume all activities in relation to the Mill and also to receive the installment for the balance amount from the writ-petitioner-respondent and to execute and register necessary sale deed in order to transfer the ownership of the Mill along with all its movable and immovable properties in compliance with the terms of the agreement is justified. The respondents have the fundamental right to indiscriminately enjoy the protection of law and to be treated in accordance with law, and therefore the action of the appellant in restraining the respondent from carrying on any activity related to the Mill is clearly detrimental. The respondent have throughout complied with the terms of the agreement and made every effort to perform the agreement by trying to pay the balance amount of the purchase price which was not accepted without objection. It was submitted that legitimate expectation has been developed when the possession of the Mill was transferred in accordance with the agreement. After the appellants restrained the petitioner from carrying on any activity in relation to the Mill, the respondents received the 1st and 2nd installments from the respondent under the agreement and therefore, the respondents further legitimately expressed that the restraining order of the appellants has no legal force in the eye of law. However, although the appellants accepted the 1st and 2nd installment money from the respondent under the Agreement, the appellant did not allow the respondents to resume their activity in relation to the Mill. The appellants most illegally, unfairly and with ulterior motive refused to accept the remaining installments from the respondents and take necessary steps to execute and register sale deed in accordance with the terms and conditions of the Agreement although more than 60% of the purchase price has already been paid, there has been an utter violation of the principle of promissory estoppel. It was further submitted that the appellants entered into the agreement with the respondent in exercise of its statutory power granted vide Memo No.MOT/Admn/B-13/2001/dated 13.11.2003 and as such, violation of the terms of the agreement is illegal. Lastly it was submitted that the writ is quite maintainable and the Government cannot go beyond its promise made in accordance with law and the liquidator is the proper authority to deal with the matter and the respondent is the bona fide purchaser with value of the Mill in question.

19.         We have considered the submissions of the learned Attorney General for the appellants and the learned Counsel appearing for the respondents and perused the impugned judgement of the High Court Division and other connected papers on record.

20.         It appears from the impugned judgement that the Government invited international tender on four occasions, but the Mill could not be sold. Upon decision for winding up of the Mill, the Official Liquidator writ-respondent No.2 was appointed to take all actions required to be taken and all responsibilities required to be discharged in this regard.

21.         From the record we find that the writ-petitioner, vide different applications made to respondent No.2, expressed its intention of purchasing the Mill. However, we do not find that writ-respondent No.2 was invested with any power to sell the Mill other than by tender. We find nothing on record to suggest that the Mill could be sold by mutual agreement. The powers of the Liquidator are enumerated in his terms of reference. But these do not include selling the property under liquidation by any means other than through tender. Moreover we note that the Liquidation Cell had already been abolished in the meantime and was not in existence at the time of the Deed of Agreement.

22.         The fact that the writ-petitioner was in default in paying his instalments in accordance with the terms of the Agreement is a secondary issue. The primary question that requires an answer is the validity of entering into an agreement for sale at the behest of the Minister of the concerned Ministry.

23.         The High Court Division observed that debarring the writ-petitioner from carrying on any activity relating to the Mills in spite of the instalments received from the writ-petitioner and the respondents’ deliberate failure to receive instalment for the balance amount from the writ-petitioner and execute and register necessary sale deed in favour of the writ-petitioner in compliance with the terms and of the Agreement have clearly infringed the writ-petitioner’s fundamental right to property guaranteed and protected under article 42 of part III of the Constitution of the People’s Republic of Bangladesh although the writ-petitioner has throughout complied with the terms of the Agreement and had made every effort to perform his part of the obligation in terms of the Agreement by trying to pay the balance amount of the purchase price which was not accepted by the respondents. However, we are of the view that the Agreement entered into by the writ-petitioner had no legal basis. Hence, there was no question of any right accruing in favour of the writ-petitioner. We say so, firstly because the Liquidator apparently did not exist at that time. Secondly, his power with regard to the Mill was only to the extent of selling the same through tender and not by any agreement. Every part of the transaction that followed, including payment and acceptance of instalments was without lawful authority.

24.         Before concluding, we should also deal with the matter as to whether the contract entered into by the respondents with the Government of Bangladesh is a sovereign contract or merely a commercial contract. In this regard we may refer to the decision in the case of Sharping Matshajibi Samabaya Samity Ltd Vs. Bangladesh and ors. reported in 39 DLR(AD)85. In that case Badrul Haider Chowdhury, J as his Lordship was then, observed as follows:

      “Modern Government has multifarious activities. Some of its functions are Governmental function while exercising sovereign power and again some of the functions can be described as trading function.” His Lordship went on to say that “when the Government grants lease, privilege, settlement, etc., the Government acts and discharges sovereign function ‘performing function in connection with affairs of the republic or the local authority”. His Lordship observed that the Government acts in these affairs in pursuance of some statutory power and any such contract when rooted in statute, the Government discharges its sovereign functions. In the instant case the learned Advocate for the respondents submitted that the sale of the property having taken place in furtherance of authority provided in President’s Order 27 of 1972, the contract for sale was a statutory contract. In this context we may profitably refer to the decision in the case of Bangladesh Power Development Board and others vs Md. Asaduzzaman Sikder reported in 9 BLC (AD) 1, where it was argued that the contract entered into by virtue of section 17(2)(a) of P.O.59 of 1972 rendered it as a statutory contract. However, their Lordships expressed the opinion that “The contract which the Board may enter into as per the aforesaid provision is an enacting or enabling provision in the execution of its works, like the work as was undertaken upon entering into contract with the respondent. The contract that was entered into by the appellants with the respondent was not in terms of statutory provision or in exercise of statutory power but the contract so entered into was an ordinary commercial contract or contract of general nature or, in other words, a pure and simple contract.” Their Lordships concluded that the relief sought for and granted by the High Court Division was not legally available to the respondent. With respect, we are of the view that in the facts of the instant case the contract entered into by the Official Liquidator is not a statutory contract, apart from the fact that, as we have noted above, the post of the liquidator had been abolished and in any event the liquidator was authorised to sell the property in question through tender. 

25.         In the later case of Superintendent Engineer, RHD Sylhet and others v. Md. Eunus and Brothers (Pvt.) Ltd. reported in 31 BLD (AD) 1 the decision of this Division reported in 9 BLC (AD)1, noted earlier, was considered and six conditions were formulated, which would permit entertaining an application within the writ jurisdiction in case of breach of contract. These were as follows:

(a)    the contract is entered into by the Government in the capacity as sovereign;

(b)    where contractual obligation sought to be enforced in writ jurisdiction arises out of statutory duty or sovereign obligation or public function of a public authority;

(c)    where contract is entered into in exercise of an enacting power conferred by a statute that by itself does not render the contract a statutory contract, but ‘if entering into a contract containing prescribed terms and conditions is a must under the statute then that contract becomes a statutory contract. If a contract incorporates certain terms and conditions in it which are statutory then the said contract to that extent is statutory’;

(d)    where a statute may expressly or impliedly confer power on a statutory body to enter into contracts in order to enable it to discharge its functions and the contract so entered by the statutory power then merely because one of the parties to the contract is statutory or public body such contract is not a statutory contract;

(e)    when contract is entered into by a public authority invested with the statutory power, in case of breach thereof relief in writ jurisdiction may be sought as against such on the plea that the contract was entered into by the public authority invested with a statutory power;

(f)     where the contract has been entered into in exercise of statutory power by a statutory authority in terms of the statutory provisions and then breach thereof gives right to the aggrieved party to invoke writ jurisdiction because the relief sought is against breach of statutory obligation.”

26.         In the aforesaid case tender had been issued by the Roads and Highways Department for the construction of a bridge. The writ-petitioner challenged the Department due to the fact that their bid was not successful. Before this Division it was argued that the writ petition involved a commercial contract and hence, the High Court Division was wrong in holding the writ petition maintainable. Upon referring to the six conditions noted above, it was held that the contract does not fulfill any of the requirements to make the same statutory contract or contract entered into by the Government in the capacity as sovereign.

27.         In the light of the above discussion we find that the contract in question having been entered into by the official liquidator was not a sovereign contract and hence it was not amenable to the writ jurisdiction. 

28.         Considering all aspects of the case, the appeal is allowed and the judgement and order of the High Court Division is set aside. There will be no order as to costs.
