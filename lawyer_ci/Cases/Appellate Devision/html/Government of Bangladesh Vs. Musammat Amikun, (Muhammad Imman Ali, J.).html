Evidence Act ((I of 1872)

Section 137

Re-examination of any witness may, at the discretion of the Court, be allowed for the purpose of clarifying any evidence or for producing fresh evidence which was not produced/provided earlier. In other words, a witness may be recalled to supplement his or her evidence, but not to correct what he or she stated earlier.          . . . (12)

Abandoned Building (Supplementary Provision) Ordinance (LIV of 1985)

Section 5

It has been held time and again that when a building is declared abandoned, the onus is on the claimant of the building to prove that the building is not abandoned. In the instant case, the respondent failed to prove her claim to be the heir of the original allottee and also that the building was unlawfully declared abandoned. She could not prove that she was in possession of the building. That would mean that the applicant’s mother was at that time with her son-in-law and not in the case building. The Court of Settlement, having called the relevant records for the building in question, came to a finding that the building was in the possession of the Government and declared it as having been lawfully declared as abandoned property.         . . . (15)

Mostafa Kamal (Md.) Vs. First Court of Settlement and others, 48 DLR (AD) 61 ref.

JUDGMENT

Muhammad Imman Ali, J:- This civil appeal, by leave, is directed against the judgement and order dated 27.05.2001 passed by the High Court Division in Writ Petition No.665 of 1996 making the Rule Nisi absolute.

2.        The facts of the case, in brief, are that the respondent herein as petitioner filed Writ Petition No.665 of 1996 before the High Court Division stating, inter alia, that the property in question was allotted to her father late Md. Nazir on June 26, 1962. The writ petitioner's father gifted the property to her and in support thereof he affirmed an affidavit on April 9, 1964 before the Magistrate, First Class, Dhaka. The writ petitioner's father died in 1971. Before his death, all the installments of the lease amount were cleared and to that effect a letter was written on March 4, 1982 by the Administrative Officer, Mohammadpur Housing Estate. The writ petitioner filed Succession Case No.111 of 1984 and obtained a certificate that she is the sole heir of late Md. Nazir. While residing in the property in question, she came to know that the property was listed as abandoned property, and thereupon in 1982 she made representation for release of the property from the list of abandoned property. The authority, by letter dated January 4, 1984 asked the writ petitioner to file the papers including a certificate from the local Ward Commissioner to the effect that the writ petitioner is a legal claimant of the property in question.

3.        Since the property was listed as abandoned building, the writ petitioner filed a case before the Court of Settlement seeking release of the building and the same was dismissed on the finding that the writ petitioner is not "aware to (sic) the relevant facts about the particulars of the original allottee and as such her statement to the effect that she is the daughter of Nazir cannot be reliable", that “it is evident from her own application that she was not in possession of the case house and as such applied for release of the property from the abandoned property list. Therefore, the claim of the petitioner about physical possession in the case property appears to be false", that "at present one Harunur Rashid is in possession of the house", that "neither the petitioner has been able to prove that she is the daughter and heir of the original owner Md. Nazir, nor she has possession in the case property.  Rather, it appears from the Government files and records that the property of Md. Nazir was found abandoned after commencement of P.O.16/72 and the Government took control and management of the property from the very beginning. There is no evidence before us to show that Md. Nazir died in this country and that he was available after the war of liberation". Then the writ-petitioner filed the aforementioned writ petition upon which Rule Nisi was issued.

4.             After hearing the parties and upon consideration of the evidence and materials, by the impugned judgement and order, the High Court Division made the Rule Nisi absolute.

5.             Hence, the writ respondents as petitioners filed Civil Petition for Leave to Appeal No.1995 of 2001 and leave was granted to consider the following submissions of the learned Attorney General for the petitioners:

“I. That the findings of the Court of Settlement having been based on the materials on record, the High Court Division erred in law in setting aside the findings of the Court of Settlement on artificial reasoning.

II. That the claimant of the property having failed to discharge the onus of rebutting the presumption that the property rightly vested to the Government as abandoned property, the High Court Division erred in law in making the Rule absolute.

III. That the High Court Division not being the Court of appeal sitting in writ jurisdiction erred in law in setting aside the findings of the Court of Settlement and making the Rule Nisi absolute without any cogent reason.”

6.             Mr. Goutom Kumar Roy, learned Deputy Attorney General, appearing on behalf of the appellants made submissions in line with the grounds upon which leave was granted. He further submitted that the claimant claimed that she had been in possession of the case building all along, whereas in her petition to the Ministry of Works in about 1986, she had claimed that she was living with her husband in Sowari Ghat, Dhaka. Hence, her claim of possession is false and the building was rightly included in the ‘Ka’ list of abandoned building. He submitted that the Court of Settlement found the building to be abandoned based on records produced before it. The High Court Division acted illegally in setting aside such findings.  

7.             Mr. A.M. Aminuddin, learned Senior Advocate appearing for the respondent made submissions in support of the impugned judgement and order of the High Court Division. He submitted that at the time of hearing, the respondent was examined as P.W.1 on 27.2.1994, while she was very sick and extremely nervous on that date, she made some statements by way of her deposition which were apparently inconsistent with her stand in the case and that accordingly an application was made on her behalf soon thereafter on 3.5.1994 with a prayer for her re-examination in the case by cancelling her earlier deposition on 27.2.1994, and as such the Court of Settlement being satisfied vide order dated 3.5.1994 allowed the prayer for re-examination. Although a considerable time had elapsed, her deposition was not taken on re-examination, rather the hearing of the case was concluded ultimately dismissing the case on the basis of her initial deposition, although the Government acting through the Ministry of Works failed to produce any material on the basis of which, the building in question could be regarded as an abandoned property. He submitted that the Court of Settlement dismissed the case of the respondent on the finding that she was not the rightful claimant of the building in question and she was not the legal heir of the original allottee, late Md. Nazir and also held that the respondent could not prove that she was in possession of the building in question, and relying on the Government assertion to the effect that one Harunur Rashid was in possession of the building for the Government, as such the Government treated the same as abandoned property since 1972, as a matter of course. These findings of the Court of Settlement were arrived at obviously on the strength of certain observations, annotation, addendum and commentary in the concerned files of the Government and not on the basis of any valid evidence, documentary or otherwise, rather these findings are contrary to the available documentary evidence on record. He submitted that the respondent conversely has been able to prove her title to the building in question, on the strength of Lease Agreement dated 19.4.1962, letter of possession dated 20.6.1962 (Annexures-A  and A-l) demonstrating grant of lease of the said building in favour of her father, Md. Nazir. The respondent in the memo of the instant writ petition has also annexed letter dated 4.3.1982 vide Annexure-C, issued by the Mohammadpur Housing Estate, which makes it obvious that all the Government dues up to 1982 so far as it relates to the building in question were already paid by said Md. Nazir. The respondent also annexed a Succession Certificate and a certificate from the Chairman of Ward No.12 concerned, in which the building is situated vide Annexure-E and E-1, which revealed that the respondent was the lone legal heir of said Md. Nazir as his only daughter. All these Annexures are in original and were very much available before the Court of Settlement. He submitted that Annexure-E and F are the Government letters in original addressed to the respondent showing the said building as her address and that possession of all these original papers/documents provided a valid rebuttable presumption that the respondent is the genuine claimant of the building in question as the heir of said Md. Nazir vis-a-vis the respondent’s consistent claim to be in possession of the building, although the Government claims that the same was in possession initially through Chand Mia and presently through one Harunur Rashid, but the Government could not produce any of them before the Court of Settlement, in spite of Court's order passed on 31.12.1994 in this regard. He submitted that the Court of Settlement arrived at the aforesaid finding that the petitioner/respondent is not the heir of Md. Nazir, merely on suspicion, caused by some of her oral testimony which she made, because of her illness and fright, in utter disregard to the documentary evidence, viz. Annexure-B and D, i.e. Affidavit dated 09.04.1964 sworn before the Magistrate, First Class as well as Succession Certificate obtained from Third Court of the then Subordinate Judge, Dhaka. He submitted that the Court of Settlement having passed the order on 03.05.1995 to re-examine the respondent, deprived her of a hearing by the order dated 23.11.1995 and thus the same is violative of practice and is clearly violative of the law and the principles of natural justice as well. He submitted that the Court of Settlement was satisfied that the deposition of the petitioner/respondent was not correctly recorded and as such passed the order dated 03.05.1994 allowing re-examination of the petitioner, but ultimately passed the impugned decision on the basis of the said incorrect initial deposition. He submitted that the Court in order to ascertain the factum of possession passed an order on 03.12.1994 to issue notice upon the persons claimed by the Government to be in possession of the building in question and also passed an order for personal attendance of the respondent, but ultimately passed the impugned decision without recording any satisfaction in respect of service of notice upon them, or without hearing any of them, which is neither proper nor legal. He submitted that the Court of Settlement arrived at the iniquitous and wrong decision only on consulting the Government records, without giving the respondent any opportunity and access to that. Conversely the Court of Settlement felt the necessity of ascertaining the factum of possession by examining persons alleged to be in possession of the building in question, but without examining any such person came to finding in respect of possession of the building on the basis of Government records concerned.

8.             We have considered the submissions of the learned Deputy Attorney General appearing for the appellants and the learned Advocate appearing for the respondent, perused the impugned judgement and order of the High Court Division and other connected papers on record.

 

9.             On perusal of the decision of the Court of Settlement, we note that the Government challenged the identity of the petitioner before the Court of Settlement, Musammat Amikun (respondent herein). It was claimed by the Government that the petitioner before the Court of Settlement was a fictitious person. Upon considering the oral testimony of the petitioner, the Court of Settlement noted that she was not aware to the relevant facts about the particulars of the original allottee, and as such, her statement to the effect that she was the daughter of Md. Nazir was not reliable. In this regard, we also note that before the Court of Settlement, the petitioner deposed that she was born in Mymensingh, whereas in her petition filed before the Chairman of the Court of Settlement, she had stated that she was born in Dhaka.

10.         With regard to the possession of the building in question, the Court of Settlement found that the petitioner before it was unable to prove her possession therein and relied upon the Government’s claim that the building was under its possession through one Chan Miah as caretaker of the building and later through one Harunur Rashid. The Court of Settlement noted that it was evident from the petition before it that the petitioner was not in physical possession of the building.

11.         The High Court Division reversed the decision of the Court of Settlement taking into consideration that the petitioner before the Court of Settlement (respondent herein) was in possession of all the original papers which in its estimation “raises valid equitable presumption that the petitioner is the genuine claimant of the house as the heir of Md. Nazir.” The High Court Division observed that the Court of Settlement arrived at the finding that the petitioner is not the heir of Md. Nazir only on suspicion caused by some of her oral testimony which she made because of her illness, nervousness and panic, in utter disregard of the documentary evidence. At this stage, we have to say that we do not find any evidence to suggest that the petitioner before the Court of Settlement was suffering from any illness, nervousness or panic at the time when she deposed before the Court of Settlement.

12.         Mr. A.M. Aminuddin has drawn our attention to the fact that the petitioner had filed an application for taking her evidence again. Although the case was adjourned for re-examining the petitioner, she was not re-examined. In this regard, we must make it clear that re-examination of any witness may, at the discretion of the Court, be allowed for the purpose of clarifying any evidence or for producing fresh evidence which was not produced/provided earlier. In other words, a witness may be recalled to supplement his or her evidence, but not to correct what he or she stated earlier. The fact that the petitioner, while deposing before the Court of Settlement contradicted her own statement made in her petition, could not be corrected on her re-examination. On the other hand, the contradiction speaks a volume about the veracity of her statement.

13.         With regard to the original documents, which the High Court Division relied upon in finding that the respondent herein was in possession of the case building, we are constrained to hold that in the facts of the present case, simply being in possession of the original documents relating to a building which has been declared abandoned, does not ipso facto prove the entitlement of the possessor of the original document to be entitled to a declaration that the building was not lawfully declared as abandoned. Merely proving the title of Md. Nazir does not help the petitioner before the Court of Settlement in establishing that the building was not lawfully declared abandoned when she made fundamental errors about her predecessor and her own place of birth. We also note that the respondent herein firstly claimed that she had acquired the building in question by way of gift from her father. But subsequently, she attempted to prove her ownership by way of inheritance by producing a succession certificate. We also note that the respondent filed Succession Case No.111 of 1984 when Md. Nazir is claimed to have died in 1971. All these facts tend to support the view taken by the Court of Settlement that the respondent, Most. Amikun was not a genuine heir of Md. Nazir.

14.         By the impugned judgement, the High Court Division reversed the decision of the Court of Settlement as if it was sitting in appeal. In the case of Mostafa Kamal (Md.)-Vs-First Court of Settlement and others, 48 DLR(AD)61, this Division endorsed the legal principle that the High Court Division in exercise of its writ jurisdiction cannot sit as a court of appeal over the judgement of the Court of Settlement for re-settling the questions of fact. In that case also the Court of Settlement on consideration of the materials on record arrived at a finding that the petitioner in that case was not the daughter and heir of the original lessee and that she had not been in possession of the house in question at the relevant time. The respondent herein had to prove not only her title in the building but also that she was the genuine and legal heir of the original allottee of the building.

15.         It has been held time and again that when a building is declared abandoned, the onus is on the claimant of the building to prove that the building is not abandoned. In the instant case, the respondent failed to prove her claim to be the heir of the original allottee and also that the building was unlawfully declared abandoned. She could not prove that she was in possession of the building. We note from annexure-F that the letter dated 7 September, 1982 from the Section Officer of the Ministry of Home Affairs addressed to the applicant’s mother that she was living at 6/3 Champatali Lane, Sowari Ghat, Dhaka, which is the address given by the claimant as her husband’s address. That would mean that the applicant’s mother was at that time with her son-in-law and not in the case building. The Court of Settlement, having called the relevant records for the building in question, came to a finding that the building was in the possession of the Government and declared it as having been lawfully declared as abandoned property.

16.         In the facts and circumstances discussed above, we find merit in the appeal, which is accordingly allowed, without however, any order as to costs.

17.         The impugned judgement and order of the High Court Division is set aside.
