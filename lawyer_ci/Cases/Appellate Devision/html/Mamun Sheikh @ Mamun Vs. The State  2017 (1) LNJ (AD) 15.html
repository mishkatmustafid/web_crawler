JUDGMENT

Syed Mahmud Hossain, J: This appeal by leave is directed against the judgment and order dated the 18th, 19th and 20th day of November, 2008 passed by the High Court Division  in Death Reference No.42 and Criminal Appeal Nos.1387 and 1439 of 2005 with Jail Appeal No.344 of 2005 accepting the death reference and dismissing Criminal Appeal No.1439 of 2005 and jail appeal and maintaining the sentence of death passed by the learned Sessions Judge, Narayanganj in Sessions Case No.312 of 2004. Criminal Appeal No.1387 of 2005 was, however, allowed by the High Court Division.

2.            The prosecution case, in brief, is that on 05.09.2003, Md. Al-Amin, son of A. Hakim Bepari as informant, lodged a First Information Report (FIR) with Fatullah Police Station, which was registered as Fatullah Police Station Case No.16 dated 05.09.2003 under sections 385/326/307 of the Penal Code. The informant stated, inter-alia, that on 02.09.2003 at about 10.00 a. m. the accused along with other 4/5 unknown persons came to their house and demanded subscription (chanda) amounting to Tk.20,000.00 and his father denied paying it and as a result, the accused-persons threatened to kill him and later on they left the place. On 05.09.2003 at about 2.30 p. m., the father of the informant went to the side of the house of the accused to help his servant carrying food for cows and at that time, accused Mamun, accused Zayeda along with other 3/4 persons caught hold of his father and took him in the house of the accused and the accused Mamun brought an iron ‘shabol’ from his house and inflicted ‘shabol’ blow on the belly with the intention to kill him causing grievous injury and as a result, the intestine came out of the body. Accused Zayeda inflicted chapatti blow on throat of the informant’s father causing serious injury and other accused-persons assaulted him causing injury. The servant ran to the house of the informant and told about the occurrence. Hearing the shout of his father, the inmates of the informant’s house went to the house of the accused and rescued Hakim Bepari and took him to Khanpur Hospital where the doctor referred him to the Dhaka Medical College Hospital and accordingly, the victim was admitted to the Dhaka Medical College Hospital. A delay was caused to lodge the FIR because of making arrangement for treatment of the victim. On the basis of the FIR, the above police station case was started.

3.            After lodging the FIR on 10.09.2003, the investigation officer of the case made an application before the cognizance Court to add section 302 of the Penal Code with the case describing the fact that during treatment, the victim of the case died on 08.09.2003 and accordingly section 302 was added to the case. The accused surrendered before the Court on 17.09.2003 and thereafter, on 13.03.2004 accused Mamun was enlarged on bail granted by the High Court Division.

4.            Police investigated the case and submitted charge sheet against accused-Mamun and another on 31.03.2004 under sections 302/114 of the Penal Code.

5.            The case record was transmitted to the Court of Sessions Judge, Narayangonj for trial and the same was registered as Session Case No.312 of 2004. The  trial Court framed charge against the accused under sections 302/114 of the Penal Code.

6.            During trial the prosecution examined as many as 19 witnesses out of 24 witnesses cited in the charge sheet in support of its case whereas the defence examined none.

7.            The trial Court upon hearing the parties by the judgment and order dated 09.04.2005 convicted accused Mamun alias Mamun Sheikh under section 302 of the Penal Code and sentenced him to death and co-accused Zayeda had been convicted under section 304(ka) of the Penal Code and sentenced to imprisonment for 3(three) years with a fine of Tk.10,000/-, in default, to suffer rigorous imprisonment for 6 (six) months more. 

8.            A reference under section 374 of the Code of Criminal Procedure was sent by the learned Sessions Judge, Narayangonj to the High Court Division for confirmation of death of condemned-prisoner, Mamun Sheikh alias Mamun, which was registered as Death Reference No.42 of 2005.

9.            Being aggrieved by and dissatisfied with the judgment and order of conviction and sentence

passed by the trial Court, Condemned-prisoner Mamun Sheikh alias Mamun preferred Criminal Appeal No.1439 of 2005 with Jail Appeal No.344 of 2005 and Convict Zayeda Begum preferred Criminal Appeal No.1387 of 2005 before the High Court Division. After hearing, a Division Bench of the High Court Division by the judgment and order dated 18th, 19th and 20th November,2008 accepted the death reference and dismissed criminal and jail appeals preferred by condemned-prisoner Mamun Sheikh alias Mamun and allowed criminal appeal preferred by Zayeda Begum.

10.        Feeling aggrieved by and dissatisfied with judgment and order passed by the High Court Division, appellant Mamun Sheikh alias Mamun as the leave-petitioner moved this Division by filing Criminal Petition for Leave to Appeal No.312 of 2009, in which, leave was granted on 20.04.2010, resulting in Criminal Appeal No.37 of 2010. 

11.        Mr. Abdul Baset Majumder, learned Senior Advocate, appearing on behalf of the appellant, submits that the High Court Division has committed illegality in accepting the death reference without considering the materials on record such as the FIR, charge sheet, evidence of the P.Ws. and as such, the impugned judgment should be set aside. He further submits that the High Court Division committed illegality in affirming the death sentence on consideration of the evidence of P.Ws.3, 4, 9 and 19 as eye witnesses though from the evidence of other witnesses it appears that the sole eye witness was the servant Shahinoor who was not examined at all. Even in the impugned judgment, the High Court Division observed that the evidence of P.W.9 was not consistent with the post-mortem report regarding injury on the hand of the victim and that the evidence adduced by her suffered from inadmissibility and could not be considered by treating her as an eye witness and as such, the impugned judgment and order should be set aside.

12.        Mr. Giasuddin Ahmed, learned Advocate-on-Record, appearing on behalf of the State-respondent, supports the impugned judgment. He submits that the prosecution had been able to prove the charge brought against the accused-appellant beyond all reasonable doubt and as such, no interference is called for.

13.        We have considered the submissions of the learned Senior Advocate for the appellant and the learned Advocate-on-Record for the respondent, perused the impugned judgment and the materials on record.

14.        Before entering into the merit of the appeal, it would be pertinent to go through the grounds, for which, leave was granted. The grounds are quoted below:

“The High Court Division has committed illegality in affirming the death sentence without considering the materials on record such as the FIR, charge sheet, the statement of the P.Ws. and as such, the High Court Division has committed illegality accepting the death reference in not considering that there is no eye-witness in the case and even the important, disinterested reliable neighbours of the alleged place of occurrence were not examined as cited in the charge sheet and those who were examined are highly interested and as such, the conviction and sentence imposed against the condemned-prisoner is liable to be interfered with. The High Court Division committed illegality in affirming the death sentence on consideration of the statements of evidence of P.Ws.3, 4, 9 and 19 as the eye witnesses though from the statements of other witnesses, it appeared that the sole eye witness was the servant Shahinoor who was not examined at all and even in the impugned judgment, the High Court Division observed that the statement of P.W.9 was not consistent with the post-mortem report regarding injury on the hand of the victim and that the statement made by her suffered from inadmissibility and cannot be considered by treating her as an eye witness and as such, the impugned judgment and order is liable to interfered with.”

15.        Having gone through the evidence of P.Ws, we find that P.Ws.3, 4, 9 and 18 claimed that they were witnesses to the occurrence.

16.        P.W.3., Baby Akther, stated that she was an eye witnesses to the occurrence. She deposed that on 05.09.2003 at about 2.30 p.m. she heard hue and cry. She came out of the house and saw accused Mamun causing injuries on the neck of Hakim Bepari by a shabol (n¡hm) and the intestines of the victim came out of the body. She  saw Mamun pressing Hakim Bepari on the ground keeping his leg on the back of Hakim Bepari. She wanted to know the reason from Mamun who did not say anything and rather reacted angrily. After four days she heard that Hakim Bepari died. This witness also deposed that she made a statement before the Magistrate which was marked as exhibit-3 and her signature thereon as exhibit-3(1). During cross-examination, this witness stated that when accused Mamun inflicted injury with shabol, Zayeda Begum told her to snatch away the shabol. During cross-examination, she further asserted that she herself saw the occurrence and that she denied the suggestion that Mamun did not inflict any injury on Hakim Bepari.

17.        P.W.4, Munior Hossain alias Murad, another eye-witness to the occurrence stated that at the time of occurrence he was taking his midday meal at his house. At that time his wife shouted saying that Mamun had inflicted blow by shabol on the hand of Hakim Bepari. So he rushed to the place of occurrence and found Abdul Hakim Bepari lying on the ground with his intestine out of the body. During cross-examination this witness deposed that he found the victim inside the house of accused Mamun where he also found wife of accused Mamun. He further found Zayeda Begum was washing the blood of Hakim Bepari. He had seen the victim being carried to the hospital.

18.        P.W.5, Md. Al-mamun, another son of deceased Abdul Hakim Bepari, deposed that Al-Amin is his elder brother, the informant of the case, and that the victim Hakim Bepari was his father. On 05.09.2003 while at his poultry farm at a local bazar, he was informed about the occurrence by the local people. Upon arrival at the place of occurrence, he found his father lying on the ground with his intestine outside the body. After that, his father was taken to Khanpur hospital from where he was shifted to the Dhaka Medical College Hospital. While on their way to Dhaka Dhaka Medical College Hospital by an ambulance, his father disclosed to him that accused Mamun dealt shabol blow on his belly and as a result, the intestine came out of the body and accused Zayeda Begum dealt a ‘chapati’ blow on his neck. This witness proved the wearing apparel of the deceased marked as Exhibit-4. He also identified those materials before the Court. During cross-examination, this witness asserted that he had stated to the Investigating Officer about the discloser made by his father Hakim Bepari regarding occurrence and that accused Zayeda Begum and her husband (Mamun) committed the murder and not by any other else. This witness deposed that on 02.09.2003 accused Mamun demanded subscription (Q¡yc¡) from his father. This witness denied that Baby Akther (P.W.3), Shahinoor and Al-Amin had not seen the occurrence. This witness stated that he heard about the occurrence from his father. He also denied the defence suggestion that his father purchased a cow from Mamun for a consideration of Tk.20,000/- but the price was not paid.

19.        P.W.9, Rokeya Begum, wife of the deceased Hakim Bepari, claimed that she is an eye-witness to the occurrence. She deposed that her husband took meal on the date of occurrence after praying his ‘jumma’ prayer. After that, he had gone to assist his servant in loading a bag of fodder and at that time she heard hue and cry. She rushed to the place of occurrence and saw accused Mamun pressing Hakim Bepari on the ground by his leg and put the shabol through his mouth and as a result the intestine of Hakim Bepari came out of his body. The wife of accused Mamun, namely, Zayeda Begum was standing there with a chapati in her hand. On arrival of her daughter, Baby Akther and another son at the place occurrence accused Mamun fled away leaving the shabol.

20.        P.W.18, Masuda Khatun, another eye-witness and a neighbour of the deceased, deposed that about 10 years back one Friday, Hakim Bepari, one of her neighbours, was done to death in front of the house of accused Mamun. Hearing the hue and cry, she went out of the house and rushed to the place of occurrence and saw accused Zayeda Begum bringing a shabol from her house and gave it to accused Mamun by which Mamun inflicted blow on the belly of Hakim Bepari and as a result, the intestine of the Hakim Bepari came out of the body. She identified the shabol before the Court. During cross-examination, this witness denied that she did not go to the place of occurrence when the occurrence took place and that she did not see the occurrence. She also denied that Mamun did not inflict any blow on the victim Hakim Bepari by a shabol as alleged by the prosecution.

21.        Apart from the evidence of P.Ws., Hakim Bepari also made a dying declaration recorded by two doctors of DMCH but they did not prove the dying declaration before the Court by giving evidence. Therefore, the dying declaration as contained in the paper-book cannot be treated as substantive evidence. But in the instant case the dying declaration may be a circumstance which lends the support to the evidence of P.Ws.5 and 15 who in their evidence deposed about the dying declaration made by their father to them before his death. Therefore, we do not have any reason whatsoever to maintain any doubt as to making of a statement of this nature by the victim himself who was very much in the apprehension of death.

22.        The principle ‘Leterm Mortem’ means “words said before the death” and in a legal term it is called dying declaration. The word “dying declaration” itself tells the meaning. A dying declaration is considered credible and trustworthy evidence based upon the general belief that most people who know that they are about to die do not lie. This is based on the maxim ‘Nemo mariturus presumitur mentire”, that is, a man will not meet his maker with lie on his mouth or ‘true sits upon the lips of a dying man’. It is an exception to the principle of excluding hearsay evidence rule. Hearsay evidence is not given any weightage in Courts because the person who is giving this evidence is not telling his experiences but that of another person who cannot be examined to verify the facts. The admissibility of dying declaration is explained in section 32(1) of the Evidence Act. According to this section when the statement is made by a person as to the cause of his death or any of the circumstances of the transaction which resulted in his death, in cases in which the cause of that person’s death comes into question. In English law, he must be death under expectation of death and only then the declaration is valid.      

23.        In this connection, case of Arshed Ali Mirza vs. State (2002)7 BLC 265, it has been held as under:

“A dying declaration whether written or oral, by the victim has a special sanctity in the eye of law. If the Court can unhesitatingly accept it and safely act upon it, by itself it provides a good basis for conviction. In the instant case, dying declaration by victim Innas Ali, rings intrinsically true and it is well supported by natural, probable and competent witnesses. Dying declaration in such circumstances becomes an unimpeachable piece of evidence.”

24.        Reliance may be placed on the case of Nurjahan Begum vs. The State (1990)42 DLR (AD)130  wherein it has been held as under:

“The statement of a person about the cause of death or circumstances leading to his death is substantive evidence under section 32(1) of the Evidence Act, and if by careful examination it is found to be reliable, then it may by itself be the basis for conviction even without corroboration.................Such statement is called “dying declaration in the ordinary terms though this term has not been used in this section. A dying declaration may be recorded by any person who is available and it may be written or may be verbal; it may also be indicated by signs and gestures, answer to questions, if the person making it is not in a position to speak. ...................The Legislature in its wisdom has put a dying declaration on par with evidence on oath for the simple reason that a man under the apprehension of death is not likely to speak falsehood and involve innocent person in preference to his assailant. That is why particular sanctity is attached to such a statement and it is admissible as substantive evidence. But the Court must take care to see that the statement is genuine and was actually made by the dying man, for it is not unlikely that he did not make any statement, but some interested person fabricated it from motive and tried to use it as evidence.”(emphasis supplied)

25.        In the case of SHUDHAKAR Versus STATE OF MADHYA PRADESH (2012)7 Supreme Court Cases 569, it has been held as under:

“The dying declaration, if found reliable, could form the basis of conviction. This principle has also earlier been stated by this Court in Surinder Kumar v. State of Haryana 6(2011)10 SCC 173 wherein the Court, while stating the above principle, on facts and because of the fact that the dying declaration in the said case was found to be shrouded by suspicious circumstances and no witness in support thereof had been examined, acquitted the accused. However, the Court observed that when a dying declaration is true and voluntary, there is no impediment in basing the conviction on such a declaration, without corroboration.” (emphasis supplied)

26.        In the case of BHAJJU ALIAS KARAN SINGH Versus STATE OF MADHYA PRADESH (2012)4 Supreme Court Cases 327, it has been held as under:

“Once the Court is satisfied that the declaration was true and voluntary, it undoubtedly can base its conviction on the dying declaration, without requiring any further corroboration. It cannot be laid down as an absolute rule of law that the dying declaration cannot form the sole basis of conviction unless it is corroboration by other evidence.”

27.        In the case in hand, the dying declaration available in the paper-book, recorded and certified by two doctors is not admissible because none of the doctors was examined before the Court in support of it but this written dying declaration lends support to the evidence adduced by P.Ws.5 and 15 in respect of dying declaration made by deceased Abdul Hakim Bepari to those witnesses.

28.        Having gone through the evidence of the eye witnesses and also the dying declaration of the deceased Abdul Hakim Bepari, we are of the view that the High Court Division was justified in accepting the death reference and confirming the sentence of death of condemned-prisoner Mamun Sheikh alias Mamun.

29.        Considering the evidence on record and the attending facts and circumstances, we find that the appellant killed Abdul Hakim Bepari in such a brutal and inhuman way that he does not deserve any leniency in respect of awarding sentence. Therefore, we are not inclined to commute the sentence of death imposed upon condemned-prisoner Mamun Sheikh alias Mamun. Accordingly, this criminal appeal is dismissed.
