JUDGMENT

Muhammad Imman Ali, J: This civil petition for leave to appeal is directed against the judgement and decree dated 14.07.2014 passed by a Division Bench of the High Court Division in First Appeal No.264 of 2011 along with Civil Rule No.363 (F) of 2012, allowing the appeal in part and making the Rule absolute.

2.            Facts of the case, in short, are that the petitioner herein as plaintiff on 02.10.2003 filed Money Suit No.62 of 2003 (and the same was renumbered as Money Suit No.15 of 2010) in the Court of Joint District Judge, Second Court, Dhaka impleading the respondents as defendants praying for a decree against defendant Nos.l to 5 for US$ 34,534,708.27 (equivalent to Bangladeshi Tk. 202,71,87,375.44) with interest till realization of the decretal amount including compound interest and also praying for attaching the monthly bills payable to defendant Nos.1 to 5 from the Bangladesh Power Development Board, shortly BPDB, defendant No.9.

3.            A meeting was held between the plaintiff and Mr. Kazi Tajul Islam Faruque, the Chairman of defendant No.l Company, Representative of defendant Nos.4 and 5(A) companies on 30.04.1998, and defendant No.1 proposed to give consultancy service to defendant Nos.2, 4 and 5 to promote and secure 1 (one) or 2 (two) numbers of 130 Megawatt (MW) to 140 MW Barge Mounted Power Plant. One to be set up at Baghabari under Sirajgonj District and another one at any place in Bangladesh, and accordingly, an appointment letter as consultant was signed between the plaintiff as consultant and Mr. Kazi Tajul Islam Faruque as resident representative of defendant No.4 company on 01.05.1998, which was counter signed by defendant No.2 as per the clause No.1 of the contract, exhibit No.2. The plaintiff, who has technical and financial expertise, consulted in several sittings with Government officials and convinced them that if the tender of defendant No.4 be accepted it would be beneficial for the people of Bangladesh and on the basis of consultancy of the plaintiff, the Government accepted the tender of defendant No.4 and executed an agreement with defendant No.3 on 10.06.1998 and 11.06.1998, exhibit Nos.‘Neo’ and ‘Ta’ respectively, for the first power plant and subsequently on 28.03.2004 another agreement were executed in between defendant No.3 and the Government of Bangladesh for the second power plant, exhibit Nos.28 and 29. Defendant No.3 by letter dated 20.07.1999 and 27.10.1999 admitted the plaintiff as consultant of the company defendant No.3. Defendant No.3 paid in total USD 5,15,291.73 as consultancy fees to the plaintiff in dollar and taka. The consultancy fee was fixed at US$ 1.00 (one) million for the above Baghabari Project and US$ 2.00 (two) millions for another such project proposed to be set up anywhere in Bangladesh. The mode of payment of the consultancy fees by Defendant Nos.1, 2, and 5 to the plaintiff is stipulated in clause 04 of the appointment letter and it was agreed that the said payment would be made to the plaintiff as consultant, who shall get his consultancy fees from the resident representative, Defendant Nos.1 and 5 within 30 (thirty) days from signing of the contracts by defendant No.2 with the concerned authority of the Government. The responsibility/job of the consultant shall be confined only to assist the resident representative and the managing director of the group to sign the concerned contract/contracts with the concerned authority of the Government to promote 1 or 2 (one or two) numbers of barge mounted power plant as mentioned in his appointment letter, and responsibilities for the execution of the business deal shall be vested with the managing director of the group and the resident representative. The consultant can never be held responsible for the execution of the proposed project /projects in any circumstance.

4.            The plaintiff’s consultancy fees of US$ 1.00 (one) million, which was agreed to be paid to him within 30 days of signing the contracts with the concerned authorities of the Government, has not yet been fully paid, and thus Defendant Nos.1 to 5 have violated clause No.4 of the said letter of appointment. Defendant Nos.l to 5 have paid to the plaintiff a total fees of US$ 5,15,291.73 out of the agreed US$ 1.00 (one) million. There is a balance of US$ 4,84,708.27 still to be paid to the plaintiff as on 11.10.2000 for the first power plant. Defendants have to pay US$ 2.00 (two) millions for the second power plant and no payment was made to the plaintiff as consultancy fee for the second power plant. The plaintiff had several discussions as well as correspondences with defendant Nos.1 and 2. In the mean time, defendant No.3 as plaintiff, filed a suit against the present plaintiff as defendant being Title Suit No.193 of 2002 in the Court of Joint District Judge, Second Court, Dhaka for declaration that the plaintiff of that suit (Westmont Power Bangladesh Limited) does not owe any amount to the defendant (petitioner herein)and that the said company is not liable to make any payment to the defendant (petitioner herein) as consultancy fee. The plaintiff of the instant suit, as defendant, filed written statement on 19.11.2002 denying all the material allegations made in the said plaint and reiterated his claim for US$ 4,84,709.00 and asserted that defendant No.3 (the plaintiff in that suit) not having come forward in making payment of the defendant's (plaintiff of this suit) dues amicably, the plaintiff of this suit being the defendant in that suit was entitled to a sum of US$ 17,775,268.49 as balance of his consultancy fees.

5.            The plaintiff further asserted in his plaint that the defendants fraudulently taking advantage of their introduction with BPDB and the authorities of the Government at the instance and consultancy of the plaintiff, directly approached the concerned authorities for approval of the second power plant without any reference to the plaintiff and got the same approved on 09.07.2003 (but contracts signed on 28.03.2004 and 22.05.2004) as an extension of the first project at Baghabari, Sirajgonj without going for any fresh tender. It was a clear violation of the said contract by defendant Nos.1 to 5 for which the fees and compensation have been sought. Thus defendant Nos.l to 5 having fraudulently avoided payment of consultancy fees for their second power plant at Baghabari and kept the balance for their first power plant at Baghahari unpaid for such a long period and avoided the payment thereof. Therefore, the plaintiff has been compelled to file the instant suit against the defendants for reliefs and redress.

6.            Defendant Nos.1 and 5 contested the suit by filing written statement contending, inter alia, that the suit is barred by law and limitation and bad for defect of parties as well as for non-joinder and mis-­joinder of parties. If there is any liability to the plaintiff, it is upon the two companies of Malaysia and not defendant Nos.1, 2 and 3. As per terms of the consultancy, the plaintiff is not entitled to get any remuneration from any of the aforesaid 3 (three) companies. But as a gesture of gentlemanly behaviour, the shareholders of the companies paid the full claim of the plaintiff to discharge the moral liability of the shareholders. The plaintiff by a letter dated 07.10.2000 issued a ‘No Claim’ certificate against the companies subject to clearance of entire dues and subsequently US$ 22,000.00 was paid to the plaintiff as full and final settlement and as such prayed for dismissal of the suit with costs.

7.            After hearing the parties and considering the materials on record the Joint District Judge, Second Court, Dhaka, by his judgement and decree dated 02.05.2011 dismissed the suit. Being aggrieved, the plaintiff filed First Appeal No.264 of 2011 before the High Court Division and a Rule was issued in Civil Rule No.363 (F) of 2012. After hearing the parties the appeal was allowed in part and the Rule was made absolute. Hence, the plaintiff is now before us having filed the instant civil petition for leave to appeal.

8.            Mr. Muhammad Iqbal Hussain Sharif, the petitioner appeared in person and made the following submissions before this Division:

9.       “That the High Court Division in their judgment at page 26 and 27 observed that, “So in our view, the plaintiff is entitled to get consultancy fees for the First Power Plant, but the plaintiff did nothing for the Second Power Plant as it was signed between the defendants and the Government on 28.03.2004 and 22.05.2004 and before that defendant No.3 filed Title Suit No.193 of 2002 against the plaintiff on 08.07.2002 in the Court of Joint District Judge, Second Court, Dhaka, and the plaintiff also filed the present suit on 02.10.2003 before signing of the 2nd agreement with the Government for barge mounted plant, and as such, he is not entitled to get any consultancy fee for the Second power plant. Admittedly the plaintiff got U.S dollar 5,15,291.73 out of his consultancy fee of U.S dollar 1.00 (one) million, and as such, the plaintiff is entitled to get rest U.S dollar 4,84,708.27 without interest". But the principal defendants' tender was submitted for the first power plant on January 30, 1997 as well as their proposal for 02 (two) power plants to Her Excellency, The Prime Minister of Bangladesh was submitted on April 29, 1998, were mercilessly cancelled at an electronic speed by the proforma defendant No.9/BPDB on April 30, 1998 (Exhibit Nos. 21 and 22) and the same was again approved by the same proforma defendant No.9/BPDB on May 17, 1998, just after 17 days of such electronic cancellation. As such, the impugned judgement and decree requires interference by this Division. He further submitted that the Courts below failed to take consideration that the appointment letter of the petitioner is a valid contract under the Contract Act as it was duly signed by both the parties and contained all relevant clauses of the enforceable agreement and that defendant Nos.1, 2, 3 and 5 respondents herein confirmed the appointment by making partial payments to the plaintiff by account payee cheques of HSBC and the Janata Bank Ltd., Dhaka (Exhibit Nos.10, 11, 12 and 8). Thus, the impugned judgement and  decree is erroneous being not based on evidence which requires interference by this Division. He submitted that the trial Court failed to appreciate the nature of the suit for realization of unpaid dues for rendering personal service and letter of appointment and held the letter of appointment as void contract under gross misconception of law of contract, which deserves interference by this Division.

10.    That plaintiff on 30.04.1998 had a meeting with defendant No.5 who  informed  him  that they participated in a tender on 30.01.1997 for a 130 Megawatt barge mounted power plant and became technically qualified as well as the lowest bidder. Though they became lowest bidder, they were not given  work  order  for  which  they sought for consultancy service from him and the petitioner after  scrutiny  of the  papers,  agreed  to give consultancy services to the respondents. It was agreed that the petitioner will get US$ 1.00 (one) million for the first power plant to be established at Baghabari and US$ 2.00 (two) millions for the second power plant to be established anywhere in Bangladesh. It was also agreed that within 3 (three) weeks from 03.05.1998, the plaintiff would secure the tender for the power project and if the plaintiff failed to secure the power business within 3 (three) weeks, he would not get any remuneration. The plaintiff got the appointment letter on 01.05.1998, exhibit-2, which was to be confirmed by defendant No.2 when he would come to Bangladesh. According to the term of the appointment letter, the responsibility of the plaintiff was only to assist defendant Nos.2 and 5, implementation of the projects rested on defendant Nos.2 and 5 and as such, the petitioner is not liable for the non-execution of any power project.

11.    That plaintiff on perusal of documents, prepared a draft application on 04.05.1998, which, on behalf of defendant No.4 was submitted to the Prime Minister for establishing 2 (two) Barge Mounted Plants on 04.05.1998, exhibit No.23, defendant Nos.2, 3 and 5 paid some money to the plaintiff through foreign currency account, exhibit No.3 series, and they also paid some money in Bangladeshi currency through account payee cheque and in cash. As such, the petitioner has been deprived of his fees, which require investigation by this Division.

12.    That the High Court Division at the time of delivering the judgment, did not consider the Second  Power Project, which was officially approved on September 7, 2003 (Page No.23 and 24 of the plaint in the Money Suit), much before the filing of Money Suit No.15 of 2010. Originally, Money Suit No.62 of 2003 of dated October 2, 2003 and the contracts for the Second Power Project were signed on March 28, 2004 and May 22, 2004, during the period of the Money Suit No.15 of 2010. Originally, Money Suit No.62 of 2003 and also more than 7 years before the judgment of the said Money Suit on May 2, 2011. Hence, it is not at all a pre-matured case by any means. As such interference by this Division is necessary.

13.    That the High Court Division did not consider the legal aspect of the petitioner's case that the first and second projects were secured by a single letter on May 17, 1998 and the first project was signed on June 10 and 11, 1998. But the principal defendants willfully and with mala fide did not clear the fees of that projects till date, though they were bound to clear the fees within July 10, 1998, viz. more than 18 years before as per their legally signed contract. Rather, the Principal defendants issued 3 legal notices and also filed Title Suit No.193 of 2002 against the plaintiff and sought: "A decree declaring that the plaintiff does not owe any amount to the defendant and is also not liable to make any claim to the plaintiff for any amount of the alleged consultancy fee" and also demanded "Permanent injunction against the defendant restraining the petitioner from illegally using the pad of the plaintiff and claiming  any  money  from  the  plaintiff, which compelled this plaintiff to file the Money Suit No.15 of 2010 (Originally, Money Suit No.62 of 2003 of dated October 2, 2003) at the last moment in order to realize the petitioner's consultancy fees. As such, interference by this Division is necessary.”

14.        No one has appeared for the respondents.

15.        We have considered the submissions of the petitioner, perused the impugned judgement and decree and other connected papers on record.

16.        We find from the judgement of the trial Court that it was observed that that the consultancy agreement entered into by the plaintiff was not under the plaintiff’s control, and the plaintiff was not a person empowered by the office of the Prime Minister to sign the contract. The trial Court also observed that the plaintiff’s appointment letter is against government purchase policy/public policy and this was the most illegal and impossible agreement and if it is accepted, this shall spread corruption in all the offices and throughout the country as well.

17.        The High Court Division, on the other hand, held that the defendants appointed the plaintiff as their consultant in Bangladesh with the aim to secure 1 or 2 power projects through proper discussions/presentations of the bid documents before the authority concerned. There was no illegality in appointing the plaintiff as a consultant and there was no impossible or illegal term in the appointment letter. The High Court Division did not accept that such consultancy agreement would spread corruption as envisaged by the trial Court.

18.        With regard to the merits of the plaintiff’s claim, the High Court Division concluded that the contract finalised in respect of the first power plant project, which was signed on 10.06.1998 and 11.06.1998, was reconfirmed by defendant No. 2 on 22.05.1998. It was noted that D.W.1 in his deposition admitted that three weeks’ time was allowed to the plaintiff from 3.5.1998 within which to complete his task. In fact, they were invited to sign the contract on 17.05.1998, i.e. within 15 days. It was held that the plaintiff was entitled to get consultancy fees for the first power plant.

19.        On the other hand, the High Court Division concluded that the plaintiff did nothing for the second power plant, for which the contract was signed on 28.03.2004 and 22.05.2004 and before that the defendant No. 3 had filed Title Suit No. 193 of 2002 against the plaintiff and the plaintiff had also filed the instant suit on 02.10.2003, before signing of the second agreement and as such he is not entitled to get any consultancy fee for the second contract.

20.        We find from the evidence and materials on record that the consultation agreement stipulated that the contract would be concluded within three weeks from 03.05.1998. It transpires that in fact the contract for the first power plant was signed on 10.06.1998 and 11.06.1998. However, the High Court Division accepted that the contract was signed as a consequence of the consultancy agreement and within the period stipulated since an invitation was issued to sign the contract on 17.05.1998. We, therefore, do not find any illegality in the conclusion of the High Court Division that the plaintiff was entitled to receive the full amount of the consultancy fees agreed for the first power plant contract.

21.        With regard to the consultancy fees for the second power plant, the High Court Division concluded that this contract was signed long after the consultancy agreement and was not resultant from the said consultancy. We also find that the plaintiff admitted in his cross-examination that the second contract was signed six years after 03.05.1998. We, therefore, do not find any illegality in the reasoning given by the High Court Division that the plaintiff is not entitled to receive any consultancy fees for the second contract.

22.        In the facts and circumstances discussed above, we find that the impugned judgement and order does not suffer from any illegality or infirmity and does not call for any interference. Accordingly, the civil petition for leave to appeal is dismissed.
