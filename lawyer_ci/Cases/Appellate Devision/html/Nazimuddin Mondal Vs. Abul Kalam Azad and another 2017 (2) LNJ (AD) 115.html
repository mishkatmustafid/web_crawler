Evidence Act (I of 1872)

Section 101

It is an established principle that the plaintiff is bound to prove his case. The plaintiff’s witnesses did not support his claim that he signed the document believing the same to be an agreement for a cinema hall. The plaintiff has therefore not satisfied his burden of proof under section 101 of the Evidence Act. Moreover, the disputed deed shows that value of disputed land casts doubt on presumption of transfer by way of gift rather than it was deed of sale.         . . . (19 and 20)

JUDGMENT

Muhammad Imman Ali, J: This civil appeal by leave is directed against the judgement and order dated 29.08.2005 passed by a Single Bench of the High Court Division in Civil Revision No.6407 of 2001 making the Rule absolute. 

2.            The facts, relevant for disposal of the appeal, are that respondent No.1 as plaintiff instituted Other Class Suit No.13 of 1998in the court of Assistant Judge, Durgapur, Rajshahi for declaration that the deed of gift mentioned in schedule "kha" to the plaint in connection with the land mentioned in schedule "ka" to the plaint was false, fabricated and without any legal effect.

3.            The plaintiff-respondent No.1 herein stated, inter-alia, that he was an illiterate person and only knew how to put his signature. The plaintiff along with pro-forma defendant No. 2 purchased the land mentioned in schedule “ka" to the plaint vide a registered sale deed No.5279 dated 14.11.1993 for a consideration of Tk.90,000/-.The possession of the land was duly handed over to them and they also got their names mutated on 01.11.1994 in Mutation Case No.463/IX-11/94-95. About three years before the filing of the suit, defendant No.2 along with his brother, i.e. defendant No.l (appellant herein)proposed to the plaintiff that if a Cinema Hall was established on the suit land it would bring them a huge sum of money as profit. Defendant No. 2 proposed that, in that event, they have to execute a deed for establishing a joint venture cinema hall. Placing reliance on their words the plaintiff accepted the said offer. Thereafter, the defendant took the plaintiff to the Registry Office and prepared a deed by a known deed writer, and in presence of their own witnesses took the signature of the plaintiff on a deed. After execution of the said deed, the plaintiff found that the defendants are no taking initiative to establish the said cinema hall. Thereafter, the plaintiff inquired into the matter at the Sub-Registry Office and on 10.02.1998 came to know that the defendants in collusion with each other created a false and fabricated deed of gift by using the signature of the petitioner with a statement that the plaintiff had gifted the suit land to defendant No.l. Hence he filed the suit for declaration that the said deed of gift is false, collusive and without consideration.

4.            Defendant Nos.1-2 filed written statement denying the material allegations made in the plaint contending, inter alia, that the plaintiff-respondent No.1 herein is an educated person. The plaintiff and pro-forma defendant No.2 purchased the land mentioned in schedule "ka" of the plaint vide a registered sale deed being No.5279 dated 14.11.1993 for a consideration of Tk.90,000/-. The possession of the land was duly handed over to them and the said land was mutated on 01.11.1994 in Mutation Case No.463/IX-11,94-9G and the defendant respondent No.2 was jointly enjoying the land with the plaintiff. The suit land is adjacent to the house and the other lands of the defendant No.1, and he proposed to the plaintiff and the defendant respondent No.2 to hand over the land to him. The plaintiff as the boyhood friend of the defendant No.1 and defendant respondent No.2 as his brother willingly gifted the suit land to defendant No.1 vide registered deed of gift No.828 dated 12.02.1995. Thereafter, the plaintiff at the instance of the vested quarter instituted the suit to hamper the interest of defendant No.1.

5.            After hearing the parties and upon considering the evidence and materials on record, the learned Assistant Judge, Durgapur by his judgement and decree dated 12.02.1995 decreed the suit. Then defendant No.l filed Title Appeal No.189 of 1999 before the District Judge, Rajshahi. On transfer the said appeal was heard by the learned Subordinate Judge, Second Court, Rajshahi, who by his judgement and decree dated 05.08.2001 allowed the appeal setting aside the judgement and decree passed by the trial Court.

6.            Against the aforesaid judgement and decree passed by the appellate Court, the plaintiff filed Civil Revision No. 6407 of 2001 before the High Court Division and obtained Rule, which upon hearing the parties was made absolute. 

7.            Being aggrieved by the said judgement and order of the High Court Division, Civil Petition for Leave to Appeal No.380 of 2008 was filed by defendant No.1 and leave was granted to consider the following grounds:

“I. That plaintiff respondent filed a suit for declaration that the deed of gift dated 12.02.1995 given by the plaintiff-respondent to the defendant petitioner is not binding upon the plaintiff respondent as he has not given any deed of gift rather he has executed a deed for establishing Cinema Hall; that this being the fact, the High Court Division ought to have dismissed the appeal as the simple suit for declaration is not maintainable without a prayer for consequential relief as the same is barred by the provision of section 39 of the Specific Relief Act.

II. That the suit is for simple declaration that the deed dated 12.02.1995 is not binding upon the plaintiff and the cancellation of the said deed and a payment of ad-valorem court fee is quite distinguishable with each other and as such the  appellate Court erred in law and fact in allowing the passing an order of payment of ad-valorem court fee, inasmuch as the question of limitation is involved as per the provision of article 91 of the Limitation Act, which is a question of law and fact and as such arrived at erroneous decision in decreeing the suit giving opportunity to make payment of ad-valorem court fee.

8.            Mr. Golam Arshed, learned Advocate appearing on behalf the appellant made submissions in line with the grounds upon which leave was granted. He further submitted that the plaintiff falsely stated that two persons purchased the land and that it was mutated by two persons. In fact, the land was purchased by three persons, including the appellant and the mutation was in the names of all three purchasers. The actual fact is that the land in dispute is adjacent to the land of defendant No.1 and the plaintiff sold the land to him, but because of their close friendship and in order to save on stamp duty they agreed to show the transfer as a gift. This was borne out by the evidence on record, but the High Court Division erred in not considering this aspect. He further submitted that the plaintiff was not able to prove that he signed the papers believing that it was an agreement and not a deed of gift. The learned Advocate also submitted that the suit for cancellation only ought to have been filed within three years, whereas the instant suit was filed beyond the period of limitation.

9.            Mr. Md. Mahbub Uddin Khokon, learned Advocate appearing for respondent No. 1 made submissions in support of the impugned judgement and order of the High Court Division. In addition, the learned advocate placed before us the following grounds:

“I. Because, the learned Appellate Court below failed to consider that the learned Assistant Judge, Durgapur in his aforesaid Judgement and Decree correctly summarized the case of the plaintiff -present respondent and appreciated the corroboration of the statement of witnesses produced by the plaintiff-present respondent and as such the appeal should be dismissed.

II. Because, the learned Appellate Court below in his Judgement and Decree failed to consider the ingredients of Muslim Heba and as such the appeal should be dismissed.

III.     Because, the learned court below in his Judgement and Decree committed an error both in law and in facts not considering that the aforesaid deed in question was made as a part of conspiracy of the defendant-appellant and as such the appeal should be dismissed.

IV. Because, the learned court below failed to consider that in the trial the defendant-appellant failed to prove that the possession of the suit land was transferred to the respondent which is a mandatory part of Muslim Heba and as such the appeal should be dismissed.

V. Because, the learned appellate court failed to consider that in the trial court the defendant present appellant did not contradict that the plaintiff-present respondent is a man of 30/32 years and sound health and as such the learned trial Judge correctly appreciated that the question of Heba by the plaintiff present respondent does not arise and as such the appeal should be dismissed.

VI. Because, the learned appellate Court tailed to appreciate that in the trial the defendant present appellant failed to produce any independent credible D.W. to support their case which was correctly appreciated by the learned trial Judge and as such the appeal should be dismissed.

VII. Because, the learned court below failed to consider that the learned trial Judge correctly summarized that from the evidences adduced by the P.W.'s and D.W.'s it was clearly proved that there was a proposal from the defendant present appellant to establish a cinema hall in the suit land and the plaintiff present respondent signed the aforesaid deed relying upon the proposal of the defendant present appellant and as such the appeal should be dismissed.

VIII. Because, the learned appellate judge failed to consider that the learned trial Judge correctly appreciated that the evidence adduced by the D.W's it was submitted that the plaintiff present respondent has taken consideration against the aforesaid transfer which is a gross violation of Muslim Heba and as such  the appeal should be dismissed.

X. Because, the learned appellate court below failed to consider that the aforesaid Heba Deed No. 282 dated 12.02.95 was collusive, fraudulent, concocted and no legal effect at all and as such the appeal should be dismissed.”

10.        We have considered the submissions of the learned Advocates for the parties concerned, perused the impugned judgement and other connected papers on record.

11.        Land measuring 79 decimals was purchased jointly by Nazimuddin Mondal (defendant No.1), Md. Shomsher Ali Mondal (defendant No.2) and Md. Abul Kalam Azad (the plaintiff) in equal share for Tk.90,000/-by registered deed No.5279 dated 14.11.1993. By deed No.828 dated 12.02.1995, two of the original purchasers, namely Shomsher Ali Mondal and Md. Abul Kalam Azad transferred their shares, i.e. 52¾ decimals out of the 79 decimals to Nazimuddin Mondal (the third of the joint owners). The transfer was shown as a gift, and yet the transfer was valued at Tk.3,000/-. Abul Kalam Azad as plaintiff filed the suit in respect of 26 decimals, i.e. his share of the land transferred, claiming that he did not intend to transfer the land by way of gift and that he signed the papers under the impression that he was entering a joint venture for opening a cinema hall.

12.        We find from the evidence that Shomsher Ali-defendent No.2, who deposed as D.W.2, is the brother of defendant No.1 and also a joint owner of the property. He stated that he along with the plaintiff and defendant No.1 jointly purchased 79 decimals of land and they were in joint possession in equal share and on 12.02.1995 he and the plaintiff sold their respective shares of the said land to defendant No. 1. He also stated that at the time when they purchased the land his elder brother Nazim (defendant No.1) had said that if either the plaintiff or defendant No. 2 wished to sell their portion of the land, then to sell to him. Accordingly, he and the plaintiff agreed to sell to defendant No.1.

13.        The fact that the land in question was sold and not gifted to defendant No.1 was supported by the evidence of D.W.3, who stated in his deposition that earlier the entire 79 decimals of the suit land was in joint possession of all three, but subsequently Nazim(defendant No.1) enjoys the entire land. D.W. 3 stated in his cross-examination that Nazim paid Tk. 60,000/- to Kalam (plaintiff) and Shomsher Ali (defendant No. 2). D.W. 4 and D.W. 6 also supported the fact, that the land was sold and they were both present at the time of writing of the deed as well as when it was presented for registration. D.W. 6 admitted that he advised execution of a deed of gift rather than a deed of sale in order to save stamp duty. 

14.        The trial Court decreed the suit disbelieving defendant No.1 on the ground that in his plaint he had claimed that the deed in question was a deed of gift, whereas in his deposition he claimed that the transaction was one of sale. The trial Court observed that the written statement, deposition in chief and cross-examination were contradictory and hence not acceptable. The trial Court also discarded the evidence of D.W.3 and D.W.4 on the ground that they both deposed that the land was sold, whereas deed No.828 shows that it is a deed of gift.

15.        The appellate Court noted that the plaintiff suppressed the fact that the three of them together purchased the suit land and only disclosed that he and defendant No. 2 purchased the land by deed No.5279 dated 14.11.1993. The appellate Court further observed that the plaintiff himself went to the Sub-registry Office when all three together purchased the land from Ashek Ali and, he therefore, would know the difference between a deed of agreement and a deed of gift. The appellate Court also noted that from the signatures of the plaintiff it appeared that the plaintiff was an educated person. However, upon assessment of the evidence on record the appellate Court concluded that the story of a deed of gift was not believable. On the other hand the appellate Court accepted that the plaintiff and defendant No.2 sold the suit land to defendant No.1, but in order to save on stamp duty they created a deed of gift.

16.        The appellate Court also considered that since the plaintiff is himself a party to the deed, he ought to have prayed for cancellation of the deed, and that a suit for simple declaration is not maintainable.

17.        The High Court Division did not specifically refer to the findings and observations of the appellate Court apart from noticing that the Subordinate Judge was in error in placing reliance on the contradictory statements of the D.Ws, who made statements beyond pleading. The High Court Division accepted that it was not enough to pray for declaration that the impugned deed of gift was collusive, fraudulent, illegal and ineffective and that cancellation of the impugned deed was the proper relief under section 39 of the Specific Relief Act. Accordingly, the High Court Division restored the judgement and decree of the trial Court with a direction upon the plaintiff to pay ad valorem court fee.

18.        From the evidence and materials on record we find that the plaintiff has not been altogether honest in his claim regarding the purchase of the 79 decimals of land by deed No. 5270 dated 14.10.1993. He consistently claimed that two persons purchased the land in question, whereas the deed shows that the land was purchased jointly by three persons. He suppressed the fact that defendant No.1 was a joint owner of the suit land. It is also hard to believe that the plaintiff considered that he was signing a deed of agreement for a cinema hall, which in fact does not require registration.

19.        It is an established principle that the plaintiff is bound to prove his case. The plaintiff’s witnesses did not support his claim that he signed the document believing the same to be an agreement for a cinema hall. The plaintiff has therefore not satisfied his burden of proof under section 101 of the Evidence Act. Moreover, the disputed deed shows that the land was valued at Tk.3000/- which casts doubt on the claim that the transfer was by way of gift.                 

20.        The High Court Division correctly found that the transaction was a sale and not a gift, but then erred in not concluding that consequently the plaintiff’s claim of the deed being a deed of gift was not proved. The plaintiff had to prove that he signed the papers believing that he was signing an agreement for the cinema hall and not a deed of gift. But none of the plaintiff’s witnesses supported the plaintiff’s claim. Hence, the plaintiff’s case was not proved. Thus the impugned judgement and order cannot be sustained.

21.        The evidence on record unquestionably discloses that the transaction was one of sale. It is equally evident that the requisite stamp duties were not paid at the time of registering the deed in question. 

22.        In view of the above discussion, we are of the opinion that the plaintiff did not prove his case in accordance with law. Aaccordingly, the appeal is allowed, however, without any order as to costs. The impugned judgement and order of the High Court Division is set aside.
