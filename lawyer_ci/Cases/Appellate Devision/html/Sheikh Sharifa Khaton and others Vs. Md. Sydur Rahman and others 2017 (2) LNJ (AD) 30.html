JUDGMENT

Muhammad Imman Ali, J: This civil petition for leave to appeal is directed against the judgement and order dated 30.04.2014 passed by a Division Bench of the High Court Division in First Appeal No.343 of 2009 allowing the appeal.

2.              The petitioners herein as plaintiffs instituted Title Suit No. 2996 of 2008 in the Court of Joint District Judge, 2nd Court, Dhaka for specific performance of contract. They stated, inter alia, that the suit land is situated under District-Dhaka, P.S. Mirpur, Mouza-Senpara Parbata, S.A. Plot No.586 (previous) and 690 (present), R.S. 2694 and Dhaka City Jarip Khatian No. 10525, S.A. Plot No.837, R.S. Plot No.13688 and Dhaka Jarip Dag No.47007 measuring .0745 acre, i.e. 4Katha bhiti land and semi pucca structure thereon. The land belonged to Dukhu Mia and Nurul Islam and they were enjoying and possessing the same. Thereafter, on 15.02.1969 by registered Deed Nos. 2112 and 2213 the land was transferred to Mohammad Mizanur Rahman and Abdul Ali respectively. Then the land was recorded in the S.A. Khatian in equal share in their names and they were paying all kinds of rent and taxes. Then they constructed tin-shed house on the land and were possessing the same.

3.              Thereafter, on 05.07.1984 by executing deed No. 13369 Abdul Ali transferred his share to Mohammad Mizanur Rahman, who became the sole owner of the entire land, and in the City Jarip his name was duly recorded. Mohammad Mizanur Rahman had two sons, namely Sydur Rahman and Bazlur Rahman. In his life time Mohammad Mizanur Rahman orally gifted the land to his two sons and executed an affidavit on 05.02.2004 before a Notary Public, Dhaka. In this way Sydur Rahman and Bazlur Rahman became the owners of the land in equal share.

4.              On 21.03.2007 by executing registered deed No.2290, Bazlur Rahman transferred his share to Shantikutir Cooperative Society and handed over possession of the land. Thereafter, on 20.02.2008 by executing another registered deed being No. 1626 Shantikutir transferred the same land to Md. Sydur Rahman. In this way Sydur Rahman became the owner of the entire land and he mutated his name and paid all kinds of taxes, gas, water and electricity bills etc. in favour of the authority concerned and had been living in the tin-shed house. On 26.02.2008 the defendant Md. Sydur Rahman executed registered bainapatra No.1772 in favour of the plaintiffs and consideration money was fixed at Tk.61,75,000 and as earnest money he received Tk.12,00,000/- from the plaintiffs. It was agreed that after three months upon receiving the balance consideration money he will execute and register the deed in favour of the plaintiffs. Thereafter, the plaintiffs offered Tk. 49,75,000/- to the defendant and requested him to execute a registered deed in their favour. Initially, he more or less agreed to execute and register the deed. But after some time he was minded to decline to execute the registered deed in favour of the plaintiffs. Then there was a Salish in between the parties. Subsequently, on 12.05.2008 the defendant refused to execute and register a deed in favour of the plaintiffs. Hence, the plaintiffs were constrained to file the instant suit by depositing balance consideration money of Tk.49,75,000/- by way of chalan bearing No.02/80 dated 26.05.2008 into Court.

5.              The defendant contested the suit by filing written statement denying the material averments made in the plaint. He stated, inter alia, that the defendant executed the bainapatra on the assertion that the plaintiffs would pay the consideration money of Tk. 71,75,000/-. He went to the Registry Office for registration of the bainapatra and accordingly bainaptra was registered. But earnest money of Tk.12,00,000/- was not paid at the Registry Office and it was committed to be paid in the house of the defendant. But, subsequently the money was not paid. There was a fiduciary relationship between the plaintiffs and the defendant because his daughter was a student of the plaintiffs’ school. With this honest belief he executed the bainapatra. But, the plaintiffs did not offer the earnest money and also stated that full consideration money was fixed at Tk. 71,75,000/-, but the plaintiffs asserted that the full consideration money was fixed Tk. 61,75,000/-. He also stated in his written statement that on good faith he signed the agreement for sale and after  three months of specified time the defendant No.1 would execute the title deed in favour of the plaintiffs if the plaintiffs included the full consideration money which along with value of constructions would amount to Tk.71,75,000/-. But neither the plaintiffs abided by those conditions nor served any legal notice upon defendant No.1. They outrightly filed the instant suit for specific performance of contract. It is stated that the suit has no cause of action. The plaintiffs filed the suit on false plea. 

6.              The trial Court after hearing the parties, considering the evidence and other materials on record decreed the suit in favour of the plaintiffs by judgement and decree dated 31.08.2009. 

7.              Being aggrieved, defendant No. 1 filed First Appeal No. 343 of 2009 before the High Court Division, which upon hearing the parties was allowed. Hence, the plaintiffs are now before us having filed the instant civil petition for leave to appeal.

8.              Mr. Mahbubey Alam, learned Senior Advocate appearing for the petitioners submitted that the impugned deed of contract for sale was executed and registered on 26.02.2008 and defendant No.1 refused to register sub-kabala on 12.03.2008, and 26.05.2008 the plaintiffs filed the instant suit for specific performance of contract, as such the instant suit has been filed within statutory period of 6(Six) months, and as such the suit is not time barred. He also submitted that the High Court Division failed to consider that the plaintiffs successfully proved the case by adducing sufficient oral and documentary evidence in support of their case, including one attesting witness (P.W.2) of Bainanama as well as exhibited documents as exhibit Nos.1-12, as such the impugned judgement and order of the High Court Division is not sustainable. He submitted that the High Court Division committed serious error of law in reversing the judgement and decree of the trial Court on a baseless assumption regarding payment of Baina money though the defendant admitted the same through a registered deed of agreement for sale, as such as per decision of our apex Court oral evidence of denial of the document are not admissible, but this aspect was not considered by the High Court Division. He submitted that High Court Division has utterly failed to consider that the burden of proof lies on him who claims, that he did not receive the baina money as mentioned in the said admitted and registered bainanama (exhibit-2), as such the impugned judgement and order of the High Court Division is liable to be set aside. He points out that the High Court Division failed to consider the provision of law laid down in section 58 and 92 of the Evidence Act wherein it is provided that admitted facts need not be proved and the contents of a document which is a registered deed cannot be orally denied, as such the impugned judgement and order of the High Court Division is not sustainable.

9.              The learned Advocate lastly submitted that the High Court Division has committed serious error of law in reversing the judgement and decree of the trial Court by not considering that simple oral denial contradicting the contents of a registered deed, even without any corroborating witness, is not permissible in the eye of law and in the present case the defendant admits the registration and execution of the impugned bainapatra, as such the baseless finding of the High Court Division regarding payment of baina money is against the principle of section 58 and 92 of the Evidence Act and as such the judgement and order of reversal passed by the High Court Division is not sustainable.

10.          Mr. Md. Khalilur Rahman, learned Advocate appearing for the respondent No. 1 made submissions in support of the impugned judgement and order of the High Court Division.

11.          We have considered the submissions of the learned Advocates for the parties concerned, perused the impugned judgement and order of the High Court Division and other connected papers on record.    

12.          We find that the learned Judges of the High Court Division have given concurring, though separate, judgements, which are very elaborate and comprehensive, having scrutinised the deposition of the witnesses and other evidence and materials on record.

13.          It was noted by the High Court Division that the bainapatra, exhibit-2 was not signed by the plaintiffs, who are parties to the same. It has been rightly held that it is not at all a bainapatra in the eye of law. A bainapatra being an agreement would have to be signed by all the parties to the agreement in order for it to have legal effect.

14.          It has further been noted that the bainapatra was not produced before the Court either by any party to the agreement or anyone who was present at the time of the agreement. Admittedly the plaintiffs gave evidence in Court through their Attorney, who was not present either at the time of execution of the bainapatra or at the time of its registration. Naturally he was not in position to depose about its execution or registration. P.W. 1, the power of attorney holder also did not witness the alleged payment of Tk. 12 lacs at the time of execution or registration of the bainapatra. It appears that none of the witnesses of the plaintiffs were present at the time of the transaction. P.W. 2, who deposed that he was present at the time of the execution and registration of the bainapatra, stated in his cross-examination that he could not say where the 12 lac takas was paid.

15.          On the question of whether the plaintiffs offered the balance money to the defendant, the High Court Division noted that P.W. Nos. 1-3 in their deposition nowhere deposed that the money was offered to the defendant-appellant. The High Court Division also noted that the plaintiffs could have themselves deposed in the case by commission, but chose not to do so. It was further observed that the husband of plaintiff No. 1 Zahidul Islam Faruqui was the only competent witness who could have been produced by the plaintiffs, but the plaintiffs willfully withheld him for reasons best known to them. It was also observed that although the plaintiffs stated in their plaint paragraphs 8 and 9 that defendant No. 1 declined to perform the contract, but their statement was not supported by the testimony of any witness. There was mentioned of a shalish about the performance of the contract but none was produced as witness to support such a contention. 

16.          In view of the evidence as meticulously assessed by the High Court Division, the veracity of the agreement for sale upon receipt of Tk. 12 lacs has become doubtful. In our opinion the High Court Division has properly assessed the evidence and materials on record and rightly allowed the appeal reversing the judgement and decree of the trial Court.

We do not find any illegality or infirmity in the impugned judgement and, accordingly, the civil petition for leave to appeal is dismissed.
