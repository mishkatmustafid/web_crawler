Code of Civil Procedure (V of 1908)

Order 21

Rules 43, 68, 69, 77, 78 and 79

Whether the executing Court is competent to cancel the auction after confirming the auction-sale:

Once the auction-sale is confirmed by the executing Court upon being satisfied that the auction-purchaser has complied with the Directions and Orders issued/passed by the executing Court from time to time, including the payment of the consideration money within the stipulated time, then there is no scope for the executing Court to show any sympathy to the Judgment-Debtor even if the Judgment-Debtor pays full decreetal amount together with a further amount of solatium after confirmation of auction-sale. .....(12)

JUDGMENT

M. Khurshid Alam Sarkar, J. By filing this application, the plaintiff (Judgment-Debtor) is praying for recalling the Order dated 13.08.2018 passed by this Court confirming auction-sale of the coastal tanker M.T. ASHA held on 20.03.2018 and for accepting an amount of Tk. 39,38,372/- under Payment Order No. 0946692/215 dated 22.09.2018 in satisfaction of the decreetal amount in Admiralty Suit No. 05 of 2002 with a further amount of Tk. 4,59,500/- as solatium vide Pay Order No. 1002200/215 dated 03.02.2019 made out in the name of the auction-purchaser.

2.             Succinctly, the background facts of filing the instant application is that the Plaintiff-Judgment Debtor (hereinafter, “the plaintiff”) filed Admiralty Suit No. 05 of 2002 for realization of Tk. 31,81,709.80 along with interest @ 18% as freight bills against the principal defendants of the suit. The defendant No. 1 (Jamuna Oil Company Ltd) contested the suit by filing written statement on 07.08.2002, denying the material allegations of the plaint, and making a counter-claim of Tk. 39,38,371.47 alleging short landing of its cargo by the plaintiff. The said Admiralty Suit No. 5 of 2002 was dismissed for default on 18.03.2010. By the Judgment and Order dated 10.03.2011, the counter-claim of the defendant No. 1 was decreed in favour of the defendant No. 1 and against the plaintiff for an amount of Tk. 39,38,371.47 and, against the said Judgment and Order, the plaintiff filed Civil Petition for Leave to Appeal No. 1819 of 2011, which was dismissed by the Hon’ble Appellate Division by its Judgment and Order dated 15.11.2015. Against the aforesaid Judgment dated 15.11.2015, the plaintiff filed Civil Review Petition No. 173 of 2016, which was pending before the Hon’ble Appellate Division at the time of filing the instant application and, subsequently, it has been dismissed for default (non-appearance) on 15.11.2018. Meanwhile, the defendant No. 1 as decree-holder filed the instant Execution Case No. 02 of 2013, and had the coastal tanker M.T. ASHA attached for sale through auction. The plaintiff filed an application on 23.10.2017 praying for stay of the auction-sale along with an account-payee cheque for an amount of Tk. 39,38,372/- equivalent to the decreetal amount. Upon hearing the application, this Court passed an Order dated 23.10.2017 staying the auction proceedings and, upon considering the ill health of the Managing Director of the plaintiff-company, allowed until 23.02.2018 for encashment of the said cheque, ordering that otherwise a proceeding shall be caused under Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881(shortly, NI Act). As the plaintiff was unable to arrange for encashment of the cheque, this Court by Order dated 27.02.2018 asked the plaintiff to show cause as to why a proceeding under the NI Act shall not be initiated against it, and further vacated the Order of stay on auction proceedings and directed the Marshall to take steps for auction-sale of the vessel. The auction notice was published on 10.03.2018, and the auction was held on 20.03.2018. On the day of the auction i.e. 20.03.2018, the auction-purchaser, Al-Haj Abul Kalam Azad (Babul), Proprietor M/s Padma Ship Recycling Industries, bid an amount of Tk. 1,05,70,700/-, which was the highest bid for the vessel, submitting a Pay Order of 10% of the bid-money as earnest money. Against the Order dated 20.03.2018 holding the auction, the plaintiff filed CMP No. 361 of 2018, along with a Bank Guarantee for the decreetal amount, and the Hon’ble Judge-in-Chambers was pleased to pass an Order dated 27.03.2018 staying the auction and directed for the Bank Guarantee to be kept with the Registrar General of the Supreme Court, where it remains till date unencashed. The ensuing Civil Petition for Leave to Appeal No. 2573 of 2018, however, was dismissed by Order dated 07.08.2018. Thereafter, the auction-sale was confirmed by this Court’s Order dated 13.08.2018. Now, when an application by the auction-purchaser for a direction to deliver the vessel is pending before this Court, the plaintiff has come up with this application praying for recalling this Court’s above-mentioned Order dated 13.08.2018, upon depositing two separate Pay Orders, one for satisfaction of the decreetal amount and another in the name of the auction-purchaser as solatium.

3.             The auction-purchaser is contesting this application by filing written objection contending, inter-alia, that the Judgment-Debtor (plaintiff) having exhausted all the stages in the suit and, thereafter, again all the stages in the execution case, now has come up with this device with a malafide intention to abuse the process of this Court and simply attempting to harass this bonafide auction-purchaser. The auction held on 20.03.2018 was a continuation of the auction process that was initiated by publication of auction notices 1st time on 17.10.2017. The Judgment-Debtor, including his counsel in person, has made several representations or promises to this Court for paying off the decreetal amount, but ultimately it proved that the Judgment-Debtor persistently flouted with the Court proceedings and obtained unfair advantage to delay the process of execution. It is stated that the Judgment-Debtor obtained stay of the auction 1st time on 23.10.2017 by misleading this Court with a cheque for paying the decreetal amount, but the cheque was dishonored. The Judgment-Debtor also misled the Hon'ble Appellate Division by presenting a Bank Guarantee which was encashable after 6 (six) months and also presenting blank cheque instead of Pay Order as per the direction of the Court, which the Appellate Division refused to accept. 

4.             Mr. Mustafizur Rahman Khan, the learned Advocate appearing for the Judgment-Debtor contends that there has been serious illegalities in calling and holding the auction for selling the vessel inasmuch as the auction-sale was held on 20.03.2018, while the notice for such auction-sale was given only on 10.03.2018; that is to say, there was only 10 (ten) days between the notice and the auction. By taking me through Order 21 Rule 68 of the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 (CPC), he submits that in the case of property of the kind described in the Proviso to Order 21 Rule 43 of the CPC, no sale in execution shall, without the consent in writing of the Judgment-Debtor, take place until after the expiration of at least 30 (thirty) days in the case of immovable property, and of at least 15 (fifteen) days in the case of movable property, calculated from the date on which the copy of the proclamation has been affixed on the Court-house of the Judge ordering the sale and the proclamation has to be in the form prescribed under Order 21 Rule 66 of the CPC. He submits that the Proviso of Order 21 Rule 43 of the CPC is inapplicable in the instant case in that the said provision concerns goods which are subject to speedy and natural decay or when the expense of keeping it in custody is likely to exceed its value. In an effort to elaborate his above submissions, he contends that in the instant case, the first proclamation that approximates the requirement of Order 21 Rule 66 of the CPC was not published until 10.03.2018 and, hence, the property intended to be sold being a movable property, the auction-sale ought not to have occurred until 25.03.2018. He submits that the requirement under Order 21 Rule 68 of the CPC is a substantive requirement of law, as opposed to a procedural one, in view of the provision of Order 21 Rule 69(3) of the CPC, which provides that every sale shall be stopped if, before the lot is knocked down, the debt and costs including the costs of the sale, are tendered to the officer conducting sale, or proof is given to his satisfaction that the amount of such debt and costs have been paid into Court which ordered the sale. According to Mr. Khan, Order 21 Rule 69(3) of the CPC gives an opportunity to the Judgment-Debtor to pay off the decreetal amount along with costs, even after the proclamation of sale of 15 (fifteen) days in case of a movable property, before any auction takes place, unless the Judgment-Debtor has consented to a shorter period under Order 21 Rule 68 of the CPC. He argues that since in the instant case, the plaintiff had never consented to a shorter period under Order 21 Rule 68 of the CPC, the auction suffers from an illegality for violating the said provisions of Order 21 Rule 68 of the CPC, which is to the prejudice of the rights to property of the plaintiff. Mr. Khan forcefully submits that this is a serious illegality, which cannot be regarded as a procedural irregularity within the terms of Order 21 Rule 78 of the CPC not vitiating the sale. In an effort to buttress up his argument on this issue, he refers to the case of Narayan Chandra Vs Manhar Mandal 43 DLR(AD) 152 and submits that on the ground of non-compliance of these provisions the Appellate Division set aside the auction-sale held in the afore-cited case.

5.             He, then, alleges that as per the plaintiff’s claim, the market value of the vessel is about 20 (twenty) crore, but the vessel has been sold in auction only at 1 (one) crore and odds. He contends that it was the duty of the concerned officials of the Admiralty Section of this Court to either appoint a valuer for assessment of the value of the vessel or to take information from the market by appointing a surveyor regarding the price and, then, place the same to the Hon’ble Presiding Judge of the Admiralty Court, and had the value of the vessel been assessed before inviting the auction, the Hon’ble Presiding Judge would not have agreed to accept the bid at such a low price.

6.             Mr. Khan next alleges that the plaintiff has deposited a Bank Guarantee of the decreetal amount before the Hon’ble Appellate Division on 27.03.2018 which is kept under the safe custody of the Registrar General, who is the Marshall of this Court, and till date it remains uncashed. But this information was not disclosed to the Hon’ble Presiding Judge of the Admiralty Court at the time of filing the application for confirmation of auction-sale on 13.08.2018. He contends that had this information been placed before the Hon’ble Presiding Judge on 13.08.2018, the auction-sale would not have been confirmed by the Hon’ble Presiding Judge. He argues that because of obtaining this confirmation Order, the auction-purchaser is now taking recourse to the relevant provisions of the CPC to defeat the plaintiff’s right.

7.             He makes a humble prayer before this Court to apply the principles of equity in this case, pointing out to a number incidents took place in this suit; such as (i) the plaintiff’s suit was not professionally conducted by the filing lawyer, for, the suit was, at first, dismissed for default and, thereafter, it was not restored, (ii)  the defendant’s counter-claim was decreed exparte simply on the findings that there was a short delivery of only 1,49,746 liters of diesel out of 1866.56 Metric Tons and (iii) the auction was held without obtaining any valuation of the vessel. He argues that had the plaintiff been able to produce his witnesses (PWs), there was a fair chance to establish his case that it was the concerned staffs of the defendant No. 1 who were liable for the short delivery. By referring to the case of Denim Attires Ltd Vs Iffat Obaid and others reported in 2019 ALR(AD) Online 107  (CP No. 2592 of 2017, Judgment delivered on 11.03.2018), he submits that the Hon’ble Appellate Division granted equitable relief in the cited case, even after the property was delivered to the auction-purchaser by executing the registered deed of sale following  the auction-sale.

8.             Per contra, Mr. Anik R. Haque, the learned Advocate appearing for the auction-purchaser submits that the Hon’ble Appellate Division has affirmed both the original Judgments and Decree and the auction-sale in the execution case, and the Appellate Division having not found any legal infirmity in the Orders passed by the High Court Division upheld the auction-sale on both the occasions. By placing the provisions of Order 21 Rule 77(2) of the CPC, Mr. Haque submits that upon payment of the purchase-money, the auction-sale has been finalized and the sale has become absolute and the auction-purchaser has acquired the legal title in the property which cannot be taken away or circumvented. He submits that it is now an obligation for the executing Court to deliver the moveable property to the auction-purchaser under Order 21 Rule 79 of the CPC. By referring to the case of Fariduddin Chowdhury Vs. Sylhet Gas Works Ltd 1995 BLT (AD) 168 and the case of Sonali Bank Vs. Artharin Adalat 15 MLR (AD) 20, Mr. Haque submits that once the auction is completed, no relief is available against such auction-sale, as there is no provision in any law or in Order 21 of the CPC to set aside a confirmation of sale in case of moveable property. He submits that once the sale is confirmed and made absolute, the Judgment-Debtor losses his right to receive back the property in question even on satisfying the decreetal amount. He argues that if the confirmation of sale is set aside in the present case, it will tantamount to declaring the title of the auction-purchaser nugatory, thereby, extending and giving an unwarranted benevolence to the Judgment-Debtor.

9.             He submits that the auction-sale was conducted as per the law in compliance with Order 21 Rule 68 of the CPC. He further submits that the specification of time in Order 21 Rule 68 of the CPC is merely a procedural law, which is confirmed by Order 21 Rule 78 of the CPC which provides that an auction-sale concerning a moveable property will not be vitiated due to an irregularity and, thus, it is open to the Judgment-Debtor to institute suit against the person responsible for the irregularity, if any, in conducting the auction-sale provided the Judgment-Debtor has sustained an injury. He contends that, in this case, the Judgment-Debtor will not be able to show that it has sustained an injury owing to such irregularity, because the Judgment-Debtor has been avoiding to pay the decreetal amount in the last 6 (six) years by abusing the process of the Court. 

10.         Mr. Haque lastly submits that in view of the dubious conduct of the Judgment-Debtor in an effort to frustrate the execution of the decree, it is clear that he has not come in clean hands before this Court and, as such, he is not entitled to plea for equitable relief. 

11.         One day the plaintiff (Judgment-Debtor) having appeared in person in the Court-room was telling so many stories by wailing and lamentation regarding this suit and prayed for not to dispose of the application summarily. Pursuant thereto, this Court assured him that the Court would take his statements into consideration by examining all the relevant papers, Order-sheets and the applicable laws.

12.         Upon hearing the learned Advocate for the auction-purchaser and the learned Advocate for the Judgment-Debtor (plaintiff), perusing the Judgment and Decree (exparte) dated 10.03.2011 passed by the Admiralty Court in the Admiralty Suit No. 05 of 2002, the Judgment and Order dated 15.11.2015 passed by the Appellate Division in the CP No. 1819 of 2011, which was preferred against the aforesaid Judgment and Decree dated 10.03.2011, the entire Order-sheets of the suit as well as the execution case and examining the all the provisions of Order 21 of the CPC together with relevant case-laws, it appears to this Court that there is hardly any scope for this Court to recall the Order dated 13.08.2018 by which the auction was confirmed inasmuch as this Court, upon exhausting all the formalities for execution of the decree, has eventually confirmed the auction-sale of the coastal tanker MT ASHA held on 20.03.2018. It is the settled law of this jurisdiction that once the auction-sale is confirmed by the executing Court upon being satisfied that the auction-purchaser has complied with the Directions and Orders issued/passed by the executing Court from time to time, including the payment of the consideration money within the stipulated time, then there is no scope for the executing Court to show any sympathy to the Judgment-Debtor even if the Judgment-Debtor pays full decreetal amount together with a further amount of solatium after confirmation of auction-sale. In this case, it is apparent that after confirmation of auction-sale the Judgment-Debtor (plaintiff), having managed the funds, has deposited a Pay Order of the decreetal amount together with a Pay Order for an amount of Tk. 4,59,500/- as solatium vide Pay Order No. 1002200/215 dated 03.02.2019, however, this Court is unable to exercise its discretion at this stage. While it is evident from the record that this suit was dealt with by his previous engaged lawyer in a very cavalier fashion, there is no provision in the law to help the plaintiff at the fag end of the execution of a lawful decree. This Court has taken note of the facts that (i) the original suit was dismissed for default, but the engaged lawyer of that time did not restore the suit within the prescribed time and consequently this plaintiff missed the chance to prove his case by adducing relevant witnesses before the trial Court  as to whether the Judgment-Debtor (plaintiff) was  responsible for the shortage of delivery or the Jamuna Oil Company (defendant) was liable for the said shortage and (ii) after the suit was dismissed for default, the plaintiff’s engaged lawyer again missed the chance to contest the counter-claim and eventually the original suit was decreed exparte. It is worthwhile to note here that the basis of exparte decree is allegation against the plaintiff that he has misappropriated the diesel, but the Hon’ble Appellate Division has not agreed with the said findings, albeit the plaintiff’s appeal was dismissed.

13.         Be that as it may, from a minute perusal of the Order sheets, it transpires that when the execution case was kicked off, the Judgment-Debtor (plaintiff) was struggling to manage required funds for depositing before the executing Court to meet up the decreetal amount, and for this purpose he was taking time form this Court again and again but ultimately he failed hopelessly. And, eventually, this Court did not have any option other than to confirm the auction-sale. At a belated stage, when the Judgment-Debtor (plaintiff) managed the funds to deposit before this Court against the decreetal amount and also for an amount of solatium, by now, this Court has become powerless to do anything for this Judgment-Debtor (plaintiff).

14.         This Court rejects the plaintiff’s allegation that the vessel has been sold in auction at a shockingly low price, for, the auction was invited twice through publishing advertisements in the daily newspapers. Although it was incumbent upon the concerned officials of the administrative wing of the Admiralty Court to obtain a valuation report through a surveyor or through the concerned business forum, however, because of not placing the valuation report before the Hon’ble Presiding Judge of the Admiralty Court, it cannot now be hold that the auction-sale has been illegal. But I would record here that it would have been a fairer practice on part of Advocate M. Golam Arshed, who had filed the application for confirmation of auction-sale, had he disclosed the facts of depositing the Bank Guarantee of decreetal amount by the plaintiff before the Hon’ble Appellate Division, which still remains uncashed.

The learned Advocate for the Judgment-Debtor (plaintiff) has tried his best to make out a case by referring to the case of Denim Attires Ltd Vs Iffat Obaid and others reported in 2019 ALR (AD) Online 107 (CP No. 2592 conduct of proceedings in an Artha Rin Adalat. In the case of M/S. Antibiotic Stores and others Vs. Subordinate Judge and Artha Rin Adalat reported in 8 MLR(AD) page 4 this Division again observed that under Section 5(4) of the Artha Rin Adalat Ain, 1990, the Artha Rin Adalat is a Civil Court and subject to the provisions of the Ain, the Artha Rin Adalat have all the powers and jurisdictions under the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908.

16.               Since the instant auction was held under Artha Rin Adalat Ain, 1990 which did not provide specific procedure to be followed for holding auction and as to time limit for payment of auction money, the Adalat followed the provision of the Code of Civil Procedure rightly. Consequently, we do not find any force in the submissions of Mr. Golam Arshed.

17.               Accordingly, we find substance in the appeal.

18.               Thus, the appeal is allowed. Judgment and order of the High Court Division is set aside. The impugned orders of the Adalat are hereby declared to have been passed without lawful authority and are of no legal effect. The Adalat shall proceed with the Execution Case in accordance with law.
