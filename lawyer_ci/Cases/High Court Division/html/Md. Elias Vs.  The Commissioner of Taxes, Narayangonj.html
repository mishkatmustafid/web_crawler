Income Tax Ordinance (XXXVI of 1984)

Sections 120 and 159(2)

In exercising revisional jurisdiction of the authority has to be satisfied on two Counts, (i) the order of the Assessing Officer sought to be revised is erroneous; and (ii) it is prejudicial to the interests of the revenue. If one of the conditions is absent i.e. if the order of the Income Tax Officer is erroneous but is not prejudicial to the revenue or if it is not erroneous but is prejudicial to the revenue---recourse is not under section 120 of the Act. It appears from the order passed by the Tribunal that representative of the assessee argued that the DCT as well as CT(A) was wrong in calculating manufacture of bricks without considering capacity of one section of brick field and payment of VAT on the gradation of brick manufacturing. So, findings of the Tribunal in one line that the assessee could not produce evidential documents to disprove finding of the taxing authority below is not tenable in law since under section 159(2) of the ordinance, Tribunal had the scope to call for particulars as it may require or cause further inquiry. Under the facts and circumstances of the case and for the reasons stated above, we are inclined to answer the questions referred to us in affirmative infavour of the assessee-applicant .....(8, 10 and 11)

JUDGMENT

 

Borhanuddin, J: This application under section 160(1) of the Income Tax Ordinance (hereinafter stated as ‘the ordinance’) has been preferred by the applicant referring following questions of law:

a.       Whether the Taxes Appellate Tribunal and authorities below erred in law in calculating the number of bricks basing upon the boundary of the field while under rule 5 of the Moushumi It Vata Mulya Shangjojan Kar Bidhimala 2004 the Government has granted the applicant permission to assess quantum of bricks as 15 lacs in a 1.5 section field?

 

b.       Whether the authority has committed error in passing the order under section 120 of the Income Tax Ordinance 1984 though there is no finding in the said order that the assessment order was erroneous?

 

2.             Facts relevant are that the applicant as assessee submitted his return to the concern Deputy Commissioner of Taxes (hereinafter called ‘the  DCT’) for the assessment year 2008-2009 showing total income at taka 2,20,000/- from the business of fertilizer and bricks; In this reference application, the applicant formulated questions of law relating to income from the business of bricks; The DCT after hearing the assessee and considering  submitted return estimated total income from the business of bricks at taka 2,95,000/- calculating manufacture of 5,13,901 piece bricks under section 83(2) of the Ordinance. During  orthodox inspection, the Additional Income Tax Commissioner (hereinafter called ‘the AITC’) found that the assessee did not disclose actual production of bricks in his return and thus issued show cause notice upon the assessee under section 120 of the Ordinance. But the assessee did not respond as such the respondent assigned an inspector to inquire into the matter and after holding inquiry the inspector submitted his report based on which the AITC estimated  total income of the assessee at taka 27,22,933/- holding that the  assessee manufactured 50,00,000 piece of bricks during the period from a brick field having 1.5 sections which has the capacity to manufacture 17,000 pieces of  brick in one circle i.e. within 21 days and thus manufacture  of bricks stands 50,00,000 pieces in 7/8 circles during 6 months of the  year and directed the DCT to  amend tax determining order. Accordingly, the DCT revised his order. Being aggrieved, the assessee filed appeal to the commissioners of Taxes(Appeal) [hereinafter called ‘the CT(A)’] who after hearing the assessee, estimate income from the brick business at taka 1,40,000/- on the basis of previous record calculating manufacture of bricks at 45,00,000 pieces holding that estimation of manufacturing bricks at 50,00,000 pieces is too high. Against the order, the assessee moved before the Taxes Appellate Tribunal who after hearing the parties affirmed order of the CT(A) holding that the assessee could not adduce evidential document to disprove findings  of the authority below.

 

3.             Having aggrieved by and dissatisfied with the order, the assessee as applicant preferred this application under 160(1) of the Ordinance.

 

4.             Mr. Mohammod Hossain, learned advocate appearing for the applicant submits that order of the Tribunal is subject to interference because of the fact that section 120 of the ordinance categorically stated that the revisional authority can invoke power under the section if he consider that the order passed by the DCT is erroneous one but nowhere in the order passed by the AITC it appears that the revisional authority detect error in the order of the DCT as such the Tribunal committed illegality in not holding that the order passed by the revisional authority is not tenable in the eye of law. He also submits that the National Board of Revenue vide SRO no.60 dated 09.03.2004 determined the procedure for calculating manufacture of bricks wherein it is stated that in one section of the brick field the estimated production is 10 lac pieces and in 1.5 section it comes to 15 lac pieces as such calculation of the CT(A) at 45 lac pieces in 1.5 sections of the brick field is contrary to the said SRO.

 

5.             Ms. Mahfuza Begum, learned Assistant Attorney General by filing affidavit in opposition submits that the AITC passed the order invoking revisional juricdiction under section 120 of the ordinance since order of the DCT was prejudicial to the interest of the revenue and the Tribunal affirmed order of the CT(A) based on  previous record of the assessee.

 

6.             Heard learned advocate for the applicant and learned Assistant Attorney General for the respondent.

 

7.             The dispute arose out of the order passed by the AITC under section 120 of the ordinance which runs as under:

 

120. Power of Inspecting Joint         Commissioner to revise orders of Deputy Commissioner of    Taxes:

1) The Inspecting Joint Commissioner may call for from the Deputy Commissioner of Taxes and examine the record of any proceeding under this Ordinance, and, if he considers that any order passed therein by the Deputy Commissioner of Taxes is erroneous in so far as it is prejudicial to the interest of the revenue, he may, after giving the assessee an opportunity of being heard, and after making or causing to be made, such inquiry as he thinks necessary, pass such order thereon as in his view the circumstances of the case would justify, including an order enhancing or modifying the assessment or cancelling the assessment and directing a fresh assessment to be made”.

 

8.             From the revisional order it appears that the AITC passed the order based on a inquiry report conducted by an inspector of the respondent. It reveals from the order that though notice was served upon the assessee but he did not attend. Presumably, the assessee could not contradict the report based on unilateral inquiry. A bare reading of section 120 of the Ordinance makes it clear that in exercising revisional jurisdiction the authority has to be satisfied on two Counts, i) the order of the Assessing Officer sought to be revised is erroneous; and (ii) it is prejudicial to the interests of the revenue. If one of the condition is absent i.e. if the order of the Income Tax Officer is erroneous but is not prejudicial to the revenue or if it is not erroneous but is prejudicial to the revenue ….. recourse is not under section 120 of the Act. There can be no doubt that the provision cannot be invoked to correct each and every type of mistake or error committed by the Assessing Officer. It is only when an order is erroneous that the Section will be attracted. An incorrect assumption of facts or an incorrect application of law will satisfy the requirement of the order being erroneous. In the same category fall orders passed without applying principle of natural justice or without application of mind. There must be some grievous error in the order passed by the income tax officer, which might set a bad trend or pattern for similar assessment, which on a broad reckoning, the revisional authority might think to be prejudicial to the interest of revenue administration. The scheme of the 0rdinance is to levy and collect tax in accordance with the provisions of the ordinance and this task is entrusted to the revenue. If due to an erroneous order of the income tax authority, the revenue is losing tax lawfully payable by a person, it will certainly be prejudicial to the interest of the revenue. (Ref- M/S. The Malabar Industrial Company -Vs- Commissioner of Tax, Kerala State).

 

9.             There is nothing in the order passed by the revisional authority why he considers order passed by the DCT is erroneous. The logic of the AITC in calculating manufacture of bricks in one circle consisting 21 days which continues 7/8 circles during 6 months of a year is miscalculation based on arithmetical error and manifests non application of judicial mind. Order passed by the CT (A) and affirmed by the Tribunal based on the previous record of the assessee calculating manufacture of bricks at 45,00,000 pieces and  estimating income of the assessee from the business at taka 27,22,933/- does not hold water inasmuch as from the order of the CT(A) it appears that in the previous year total income of the assessee  estimated at taka 10,50,00/- and net income estimated at taka 1,40,000/- though the CT(A) himself  held that estimation of manufacturing bricks by the DCT and AICT was too high.

 

It appears from the order passed by the Tribunal that representative of the assessee argued that the DCT as well as CT(A) was wrong in calculating manufacture of bricks withoutconsidering capacity of one section of brick field and payment of VAT on the gradation of brick manufacturing. So, findings of the Tribunal in one line that the assessee could not produce evidential documents to disprove finding of the taxing authority below is not tenable in law since under section 159(2) of the ordinance, Tribunal had the scope to call for particulars as it may require or cause further inquiry.

 

11.               Under the facts and circumstances of the case and for the reasons stated above, we are inclined to answer the questions referred to us in affirmative infavour of the assessee-applicant.

 

12.               The reference application is disposed of with the above observation.

 

13.               The Registrar of the Bangladesh Supreme Court, High Court Division, is hereby directed to take necessary step under section 161(2) of the Income Tax Ordinance, 1984.
