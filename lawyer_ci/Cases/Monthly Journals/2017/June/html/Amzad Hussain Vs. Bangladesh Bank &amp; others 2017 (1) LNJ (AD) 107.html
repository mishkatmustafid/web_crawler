Foreign Exchange Regulation Act (VII of 1947)

Section 3(2)(iii)

We find from the amended version of the Act that the requirement of “giving the authorised dealer a reasonable opportunity of explaining its possession” was added by way of amendment in 2015. This amendment though it gives a right to the aruthorised dealer to be given an opportunity to explain his position, was not in existence at the time of the impugned cancellation of the petitioner’s license. This amendment fortifies the view that in the law as it existed prior to the amendment, there was no requirement to give an opportunity to the licensee to be heard. In the facts and circumstances of this case the principle of justice has not been violated. Clearly, the petitioner did not feel sufficiently aggrieved by the lack of opportunity given him by way of show cause notice or opportunity of being heard. He did not immediately rush to the High Court Division challenging the violation of the principle of natural justice. On the contrary, he took the decision to make himself be heard by the authority concerned by making representations and by supplying other supporting evidence.         . . . (11 and 13)

JUDGMENT

Muhammad Imman Ali, J: This civil petition for leave to appeal is directed against the judgement and order dated 18.01.2012 passed by a Division Bench of the High Court Division in Writ Petition No. 2528 of 2005 discharging the Rule.

2.            The facts of the case, in brief, are that the petitioner was granted a Money Changer License under section 3 of the Foreign Exchange Regulation Act, 1947(the Act, 1947) in the name of his proprietorship firm “Universal Money Changer” issued by respondent No. 3, Deputy General Manager, Foreign Exchange Policy Division Bangladesh Bank dated 18.09.1997 to buy and sell foreign currency from the incoming and outgoing passengers. The said license was issued on yearly basis subject to renewal on yearly basis which was renewed up to 17.09.2003. Respondent No. 3 issued FE Circular No. 03/2002 dated 19.2.2002 providing guidelines relating to renewal of Money Changer License, release of foreign currency, change of address etc. The said circular provided for transaction of foreign currency fixing a yearly target of 2.50 lacs US Dollar for each Money Changer. In case of failure, extension of Money Changer License for the following year would not be considered. It has been provided that for unavoidable circumstances if any Money Changer fails to fulfill yearly target, renewal of license for the following year would be considered on written undertaking that the concerned Money Changer would fulfill the target. This opportunity will be available to the concerned Money Changer for once during its entire business period. The petitioner submitted an application on 14.10.2003 along with original Money Changer License (Annexure-A to the writ petition), requisite fees and relevant papers in the office of the respondents for renewal of his Money Changer License for the next year commencing from 18.09.2003 (Annexure-C to the writ petition). Bangladesh Bank issued Memo dated 04.4.2004 under the signature of the respondent No. 4, Assistant Director Policy Division, Bangladesh Bank stating that as the petitioner failed to fulfill the yearly target of foreign currency transaction in terms of FE Circular No. 03/2002 dated 19.02.2002 there was no scope to renew his Money Changer License as the transaction of foreign currency of the petitioner’s firm in the year 2003 was 2.02 lac US Dollar which is far below of the fixed target and in exercise of the power conferred by section 3(2)(iii) of the Act, 1947 the Money Changer License of the petitioner was cancelled with a direction to deposit his original Money Changer License and the encashment certificate for foreign currency in hand, to Foreign Exchange Policy Division, Bangladesh Bank. Challenging this decision of Bangladesh Bank, the petitioner filed Writ Petition No.2528 of 2005 before the High Court Division and obtained Rule.

3.            Respondent No. 1 filed an affidavit-in-opposition stating that there was no ambiguity in the impugned memo and the license of the writ petitioner was cancelled in compliance of the law since the petitioner did not fulfill the requirements of the Circular dated 19.02.2002. The petitioner also filed an Affidavit-in-reply.

4.            In due course after hearing both the parties, by the impugned judgement and order the said Rule was discharged.    Hence, the petitioner is now before us having filed the instant civil petition for leave to appeal.

5.            Mr. A.M. Amin Uddin, learned Senior Advocate appearing on behalf of the petitioner submitted that the respondent neither gave any prior notice to the petitioner to show cause nor gave him any chance of personal hearing before cancelling his Money Changer License, but the High Court Division overlooked Annexure-E filed by the petitioner by which the petitioner claimed that his license having been issued on 18.09.1997 his transaction should have been calculated on the basis of his business year from 18.09.2002 to 17.09.2003. He pointed out that for the period 18.09.2002 to 17.09.2003 the business transaction of the petitioner amounted to more than the required US$ 2.50 lacs. He further submitted that the High Court Division wrongly interpreted the rules of natural justice upon holding that the respondents gave post decisional hearing to the petitioner which does not fulfill the criteria of the principle of natural justice when the petitioner did not get an opportunity of being heard. The learned Advocate also submitted that the law does not provide for cancelling the petitioner’s license and at worst the respondents could have declined to renew the license which would have allowed the petitioner to take fresh steps to fulfill the conditions required for renewing his license. 

6.            Mr. Shamim Khaled Ahmed, learned Senior Advocate appearing on behalf of respondent No. 1 made submissions in support of the impugned judgement and order of the High Court Division. 

7.            We have considered the submissions of the learned Advocates appearing on behalf of the respective parties and perused the impugned judgement of the High Court Division and other connected papers on record. 

8.            On perusal of the impugned judgement we find that the High Court Division has firstly observed that the calculations given by the petitioner with a view to showing that he had achieved the target of US$ 2.50 lacs, whereas the respondents denied such conditions, are disputed factual matters which cannot be decided in a writ of certiorari.

9.            The High Court Division next considered that section 3(2)(iii) of the Act clearly provides that in a fit case Bangladesh Bank can take any decision for revoking the license of any person.

10.        With regard to the question of breach of the principle of natural justice, the High Court Division elaborately dealt with the issue by reference to decisions of the Indian Supreme Court as well as those of this Division and noted that section 3(2)(iii) of the Act does not contemplate such requirement of holding any inquiry or giving any prior notice for taking any decision. On the other hand, the High Court Division relying upon the aforementioned decisions of the superior Courts concluded that the petitioner was effectively given a post decisional hearing when he made several representations to the respondents starting 9 days after the impugned order cancelling his license. The High Court Division observed that it could not be said that for not giving prior notice by the respondents before cancelling the license of the petitioner he has been prejudiced for violation of principle of natural justice. Moreover, he did not move the High Court Division immediately after the order cancelling his license and instead he corresponded with the respondents over a period of one year explaining his position. 

11.        The law allows for the Bangladesh Bank to grant license for a specified period, or within specified amounts, retaining for itself the power to revoke the license for sufficient reason. We find from the amended version of the Act that the requirement of “giving the authorised dealer a reasonable opportunity of explaining its possession” was added by way of amendment in 2015. This amendment though it gives a right to the aruthorised dealer to be given an opportunity to explain his position, was not in existence at the time of the impugned cancellation of the petitioner’s license. This amendment fortifies the view that in the law as it existed prior to the amendment, there was no requirement to give an opportunity to the licensee to be heard.

13.        Taking into consideration the decisions referred before the High Court Division, we are of the view that the High Court Division has correctly decided that in the facts and circumstances of this case the principle of justice has not been violated. Clearly, the petitioner did not feel sufficiently aggrieved by the lack of opportunity given him by way of show cause notice or opportunity of being heard. He did not immediately rush to the High Court Division challenging the violation of the principle of natural justice. On the contrary, he took the decision to make himself be heard by the authority concerned by making representations and by supplying other supporting evidence.             

14.        In view of the above discussion, we do not find any illegality or impropriety in the impugned judgement. Accordingly, the civil petition for leave to appeal is dismissed.
