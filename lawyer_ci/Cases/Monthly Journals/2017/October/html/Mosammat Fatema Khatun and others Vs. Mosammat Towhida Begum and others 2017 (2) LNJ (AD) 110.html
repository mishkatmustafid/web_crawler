Code of Civil Procedure (V of 1908)

Order XLI Rule 31

The appellate Court as the last court of facts has meticulously discussed the evidence on record and rightly reversed the findings of the trial Court. The observation of the High Court Division that the appellate Court did not reverse the findings of the trial Court is not correct. The appellate Court has elaborately dealt with the points raised by the trial Court. Moreover, we find that the conclusion of the trial Court is not consistent with the findings and observations made by it. Accordingly, the impugned judgment and order of the High Court Division is not sustainable. Hence, the appeal is allowed, without however, any order as to costs.            . . .(17)

JUDGMENT

Muhammad Imman Ali, J: This civil appeal, by leave, is directed against the judgement and order dated 16.08.2009 passed by a Single Bench of the High Court Division in Civil Revision No.5266 of 2004 making the Rule absolute.

2.        The appellants herein as plaintiffs instituted Other Class Suit No. 322 of 2001 in the Court of Subordinate Judge, Sirajgonj, for declaration of title. The plaintiff stated, inter alia, that the suit property measuring an area of 6.30 acres of land belonged to Asutosh Sarker, Jagodish Sarker, Hrishikesh Sarker, Debendra Nath Sarkar and Gangamoyee Sarker. The plaintiffs’ predecessor took settlement of the said lands from the said landlords’ by several amalnamas and they were recognised as korfa tenants under khatian Nos.22-31 of the landlords’ khatian No.21. The lands were accordingly recorded in the C.S. khatian in the names of the plaintiffs’ predecessors and they exercised their title and possession over statutory period. Of the said tenants, Adam Pramanik died leaving plaintiff No.1 as daughter and brother plaintiff No.2, who died leaving behind two sons, namely Afer Pramanik and Wafor Pramanik, who also died leaving 3 sons plaintiffs Nos.3-5. Afer Pramanik died leaving 4 sons plaintiffs Nos.6-9. Rabi Pramanik died leaving one daughter plaintiff No.10. Gazi Pramanik died leaving 2 sons, namely Biru Pramanik and Miruddin Pramanik. Biru Pramanik died leaving 2 sons plaintiff Nos.11 and 12. Arajdi Pramanik died leaving son plaintiff No.13 and other plaintiffs have also inherited the suit lands from their predecessors. Plaintiff No.1 also sold some lands to plaintiffs Nos.21-32 on 9th April, 1979. Defendant Nos.3-9 gifted .33 acre of land in favour of plaintiff No.47. Plaintiff Nos.23-29 sold .30 acre of land to plaintiffs Monjur Rahman, Abdul Kader and Mohsin Reza. Some other plaintiffs also sold some lands to other plaintiffs and in this way they have been in possession by way of inheritance and purchase. The defendants have no right title and interest in the suit lands. During the last settlement operation the record was initially prepared in the names of their predecessors. Subsequently, it was detected that the S.A. khatian was prepared in the name of Jagodish Sarker which was incorrect. During the war of liberation, the plaintiffs’ documents were destroyed and taking advantage of the situation, defendant No.l created some ante-dated papers and instituted a suit in the Court of Munsif, Sirajgonj and obtained a fraudulent decree by practicing fraud upon the Court. The plaintiffs are not bound by the said decree. During the R.S. operation the plaintiffs went to record their names, when defendant No.l protested by producing the said fraudulent decree. Hence, the plaintiffs filed the suit.

3.        Defendant Nos.2, 5 and 6 filed two separate written statements, but defendant No.2 did not contest the suit. Defendant Nos.5 and 6 who are respondent Nos.1 and 2 herein stated, inter alia, that they purchased the suit lands measuring 6.30 acres appertaining to C.S. khatian Nos.22-31 by two registered deeds dated 30th December, 1976. Their further case is that the suit lands belonged to Jagodish Sarker, Asutosh Sarker, Hrishikesh Sarker, Debendra Nath Sarker, sons of Taroni Chandra Sarker and Gongamoyee Sarker, wife of late Taroni Sarker. While the aforesaid owners were enjoying the said lands by an amicable partition, Jagodish Chandra Sarker got the suit lands but other co-sharers-defendant Nos.2 and 3 got 100 bighas of land under Mouja Porjona. Jagodish Chandra Sarker settled the said lands to the predecessor of the defendants Hossain Ali on 11th Kartick, 1352 B.S. During the S.A. operation, Jagodish Chandra's name was wrongly recorded. Accordingly, Hossain Ali instituted Title Suit No.140 of 1975 in the Court of Subordinate Judge, Pabna for declaration of title against Jagodish Chandra Sarker and the suit was decreed ex parte on 29th January, 1974. Pursuant to the decree, Hossain Ali's name was recorded in the S.A. khatian. Subsequently, Hossain Ali instituted Other Class Suit No.282 of 1976 against Assistant Custodian for permanent injunction. During the R.S. operation Jagodish Chandra Sarker's name was recorded, but on appeal the defendant’s name was corrected. Defendant No.6 thereupon transferred some lands to Hossain Ali Sarker, Sufia Khatun, Sakera Khatun and others. The plaintiffs have no right, title and interest in the suit land.

4.            After hearing the parties and considering the materials on record, the Subordinate Judge, First Court, Sirajganj by his judgement and decree dated 19.04.2000 dismissed the suit.

5.            Against the said judgement and decree, the plaintiffs preferred Title Appeal No.29 of 2000 before the District Judge, Sirajganj. On transfer, the appeal was heard by the Additional District Judge, Sirajgonj, who after hearing the parties allowed the appeal reversing the judgement and decree of the trial Court. Then respondent Nos.1 and 2 preferred Civil Revision No.5266 of 2004 before the High Court Division and obtained Rule, which upon hearing the parties was made absolute. Against the said judgement and order of the High Court Division, the plaintiffs filed Civil Petition for Leave to Appeal No.2011 of 2009 and leave was granted to consider the following submissions of the learned Advocate for the petitioner:

“I. That the High Court Division erred in law in interfering with the judgement and decree of the appellate Court without reversing the findings arrived at by the appellate Court in failing to notice that the plaintiffs predecessors being korfa tenants under the landlords, who are in continuous possession in the suit lands, their tenancy matured into title after the acquisition of the rent receiving interest.

II. That there is no material on record to show that the plaintiffs’ predecessors have been evicted from the suit lands by the ex-landlords or that they have surrendered their possession, the High Court Division, in the premises, erred in law in dismissing the suit without reversing the finding of possession arrived at by the appellate Court.

6.            Mr. Mahbubey Alam, learned Senior Advocate appearing on behalf of the appellants made submissions in line with the grounds upon which leave was granted. He further submitted that the name of the predecessors of the plaintiffs having been recorded as korfa tenants and there being no materials on record to show that they were evicted from the suit land or they have surrendered their possession, the High Court Division committed an error in dismissing the suit without considering that after acquisition of the rent receiving interest the tenant acquired right in the suit land by operation of law. He further submitted that the trial Court as well as the Appellate Court having found the right and possession of the plaintiffs in the suit land, the High Court Division committed an error in dismissing the suit without reversing the finding of the court below and thus the same is not a proper judgement of reversal. He submitted that the plaintiffs are able to prove their title and possession over the suit land. The trial Court on a technical point dismissed the suit and the appellate Court being the Court of law and fact on elaborate discussion of evidence on record decreed the suit, but the High Court Division without reversing the finding and decision of the appellate Court as contemplated under Order XLI, rule 31 of the Code of Civil Procedure made the Rule absolute and as such the same is liable to be set aside.

7.            Mr. Probir Neogi, learned Senior Advocate appearing for respondent Nos.1 and 2 made the following submissions before us:

“I.  That the appellate Court committed error of law in reversing the  decision  of the  trial Court without adverting  to the fundamental findings of the trial Court to the effect that the defendants having obtained two decrees in respect of the suit property in two  earlier suits,  the  instant suit   was not maintainable without seeking any relief in respect of the said earlier decrees, and as such the judgement and decree of the appellate Court are not sustainable in law, and the High Court Division rightly made the Rule absolute.

II. That the appellate Court being final court of facts committed an error of law, resulting in erroneous decision, occasioning failure of justice in decreeing the suit without considering the evidence on record and without rebutting the findings arrived at by the trial Court on consideration of exhibit 'Kha' of the defendants, the pattan in favour of their predecessor in the year 1945. Korfa pattan and the C.S, S.A. and R.S. record, rent receipt and also the decrees declaring the title of the defendants to the suit land, and as such the judgement of the High Court Division in revision making the Rule absolute suffers from no legal infirmity and calls for no interference by this Division.

III. That the appellate Court committed error of law resulting in erroneous decision occasioning failure of justice in failing to hold that the suit was not maintainable being hit by proviso to section 42 of the Specific Relief Act, and the High Court Division rightly made the Rule absolute.

IV. That the appellate Court totally failed to consider the contesting defendants' case and credible evidence led by them.

V. That the judgement of the appellate Court was against the preponderance of evidence led by the parties, and as such, the High Court Division rightly made the Rule absolute.

8.            We have considered the submissions of the learned Advocates appearing for the parties concerned, perused the impugned judgement and order of the High Court Division and other connected papers on record.

9.            We find from the impugned judgement that the High Court Division reversed the judgement and decree of the appellate Court observing that the finding of fact arrived at by the lower appellate Court is not based on materials and evidence on record. It observed that the appellate Court reversed the finding of the trial Court without any basis. It appears to us that the High Court Division reversed the judgement and decree of the lower appellate Court without giving any specific reasons other than noting that the appellate Court did not reverse the finding of the trial Court.

10.        The trial Court observed that the decree in Other Class Suit No.140 of 1974 is not binding of the plaintiffs since they or their predecessor were not party to that suit and similarly in Other Class Suit No.282 of 1976 neither the plaintiffs nor their predecessor were party and hence the decree is not binding upon the plaintiffs. It was further observed that defendant No.1 having claimed that he took pattan of the suit land, could not produce the original amalnama or dakhila and did not give any satisfactory explanation. As a result the claim of defendant Nos.5/6 regarding the alleged pattan is not believable. However, the trial Court then went on to consider that the mutation khatian and rent receipts are evidence to prove their possession. It is noted that the trial Court took into account the submission of the learned Advocate for the plaintiffs that defendant No.5 who deposed as D.W.1 admitted that he was the Nayeb in the Tahsil Office and that the submission of the learned Advocate for the plaintiffs that he had created false and forged pattan and obtained mutation and rent receipts in his name with a view to grab the land, was not without basis. Upon considering the evidence of the witnesses and the materials on record, the trial Court concluded that the plaintiffs are in possession of the suit land and although defendant Nos.5/6 produced mutation khatian and rent receipts, there is good reason to suspect that those are collusive. However, the trial Court dismissed the suit of the plaintiffs on the finding that the korfa title of the plaintiffs’ predecessor subsisted, but they were unable to produce any evidence to prove such title.

11.        We find from the judgement of the appellate Court that it was observed that the decree in Other Class Suit No.140 of 1974 was not binding on the plaintiffs since neither they nor their predecessor (mourash) were party in that suit. It was further noted that in this regard the learned Advocate on behalf of the defendants was not able to give any satisfactory explanation.

15.        The appellate Court further noted that the trial Court found possession of the suit land in favour of the plaintiffs and also noted that the defendants’ claimed that defendant No.1 took pattan of the suit land, but were unable to produce the original amalnama/pattannama/ dakhila and were unable to give any satisfactory explanation. The appellate Court as the last court of fact found that the claim of pattan as made by defendant Nos.5/6 was not believable.

16.        The appellate Court concluded that since there was nothing to suggest that the korfa title of the plaintiffs’ predecessor had been lost, it is reasonable to conclude that the tenancy in the land has devolved upon the plaintiffs.

17.        We also note that sub-khatian Nos.22 to 31 of khatian No.21 record the name of the plaintiffs’ predecessor Adam Pramanik as korfa tenant since 1922.

18.           In view of the above discussion, we find that the appellate Court as the last court of facts has meticulously discussed the evidence on record and rightly reversed the findings of the trial Court. The observation of the High Court Division that the appellate Court did not reverse the findings of the trial Court is not correct. The appellate Court has elaborately dealt with the points raised by the trial Court. Moreover, we find that the conclusion of the trial Court is not consistent with the findings and observations made by it. Accordingly, the impugned judgement and order of the High Court Division is not sustainable. Hence, the appeal is allowed, without however, any order as to costs.

         The impugned judgement and order of the High Court Division is set aside.
