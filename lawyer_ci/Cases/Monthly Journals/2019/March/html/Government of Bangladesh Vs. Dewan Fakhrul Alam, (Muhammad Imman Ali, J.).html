Requisition of Property Act (XIII of 1948)

Section 8

The power to release any property is, from requisition, not from acquisition, which has been given under the Requisition of Property Act upon the Deputy Commissioner which is a discretionary power and the discretion is to be exercised only when a thing necessary for any person from whom the property has been requisition to be entitled to such restoration he may restore to such person or to his successor-in-interest. However, the provision of section 8 of the Requisition of Property Act applies only in respect of requisition not acquisition.     . . . (16)

Requisition of Property Act (XIII of 1948)

Section 5(6)

The land in question may be released. Our considered views are that the land in question cannot be released from the acquisition because as per Section 5(6) of the Requisition of Property Act compensation has already been paid to the owner. So, there is no scope to withdraw the proposal of the acquisition and return back the land to the person from whom the same has been acquired although the final gazette has not yet been published.          . . . (17)

Requisition of Property Act (XIII of 1948)

Section 5(7)

We do not find any such provisions in the law that if any land after payment of compensation is remained unused that would be the basis to asking for release of the same from acquisition, even if, publication of the gazette notification has not yet done under sub section 7 of section 5 of the Requisition of Property Act. The land in question after being acquisitioned upon payment of compensation cannot be withdrawn from acquisition and give it back to the heirs of the awardee on the plea of non-utilization as directed by the High Court Division. Hence, the impugned judgment cannot be sustained in law. . . . (22 and 26)

Requisition of Property Act (XIII of 1948)

Section 5(6)

Once a property is acquired and vested in the Government after making payment of compensation there is no scope to release the same to the original owner or to his heirs. ...(29)

JUDGMENT

Md. Nuruzzaman, j: This Civil Appeal, by leave, is directed against the judgment and order dated 20.02.2014 passed by the High Court Division in Writ Petition No.10627 of 2012 making the Rule Nisi absolute.

2.             Facts leading to filing of this civil appeal, in short, are that:

         the present respondent Nos.1-6 as the writ petitioners preferred the Writ Petition No.10627 of 2012 under article 102 of the Constitution of the People’s Republic of Bangladesh before the High Court Division for direction upon the respondents to release the 0.75 acres out of 1.50 acres of land proposed to acquire under L.A. Case No.4/77-78 vide notice of acquisition dated 12.12.1978 which were requisitioned vide notice dated 29.06.1978 issued under sections 5(1a) and 3, respectively, under the (Emergency) Requisition of Property Act, 1948 (Act XIII of 1948)(Hereinafter to be referred as the Requisition of Property Act) by the writ respondent No.3 appertaining to S.A. Khatian No.2128, S.A. Dag No.1141, J.L No.56 under Mouza-Rupatali, Police Station-Kotwali, District-Barisal to the writ petitioners upon reimbursing the Government to the extent actually paid as compensation to the writ petitioners’ predecessor at the time of acquisition. It is stated that 1.50 acres of land under Mouja-Rupatali, Police Station-Kotwali, J.L. No.56, District-Barisal being S.A. Khatian No.2128, S.A. Plot No.1141 was acquired under the Requisition of Property Act by the Deputy Commissioner, Barishal-(the writ respondent No.3) for requiring body the Roads and Highway Department under the Ministry of Communication for extension of Barishal Highways which originally belonged to Dr. Anwar Hossain, who was the full brother of the writ petitioners. Dr. Anwar Hossain died leaving behind the writ petitioners, as legal heirs, who applied to the Ministry of land on 22.04.2001 (Annexure-B to the writ petition) for releasing the unused land in favour of the writ petitioners upon which the Deputy Commissioner, Barishal was asked by the Ministry, vide letter dated 30.05.2001 (Annexure-C to the writ petition) to give a report. The Roads and Highway Department, Barishal upon proper inquiry also found that 0.75 acres land out of total acquired 1.50 acres of land is totally unused by the requiring body. The requiring body, Roads and Highway Department vide Memo dated 10.09.2001 and 08.12.2001 (Annexure-D and D(1) to the writ petition) made a recommendation to release the unused 0.75 acre land and in view of that recommendation Ministry of Communication vide letter dated 11.04.2005 (Annexure-E to the writ petition) gave no objection and consented to release the unused land to the original owners on condition to refund the compensation money as earlier paid to the title holder to the account of Roads and Highway Department. In pursuant to the said letter dated 11.04.2005 (Annexure-E to the writ petition) the Ministry of Land vide letter dated 27.07.2006 (Annexure-F to the writ petition) requested writ respondent No.3, i.e., the Deputy Commissioner, Barishal to submits a report mentioning 4 (four) terms in the said letter pursuant to which the writ respondent No.3, the Deputy Commissioner, Barishal, vide letter dated 29.09.2009 informed the Secretary, Ministry of Land that 1.50 acres of land under S.A. Plot No.1141 was acquired vide L.A. Case No.4/77-78 and in respect of the said land final Gazette has not yet been published under section 5(7) of the Requisition of Property Act and 0.75 acre of land was lying unused to the requiring body, the Roads and Highway Department. Finally the writ respondent No.3 in the said letter recommended that as the price of the land now has already been increased the writ petitioners’ application can be considered if compensation money repaid at the present value of the land. The writ petitioners although applied to the writ respondent No.3 to release and hand over the unused land as per direction of Ministry to the writ petitioners but got no reply. Therefore, the writ petitioners served legal notice upon the writ respondents demanding justice but in vain. The writ petitioners finding no other alternative efficacious remedy moved the above mentioned writ petition and obtained the Rule Nisi.          

3.             The writ-respondents contested the Rule Nisi without filing any affidavit-in-opposition.

4.             A Division Bench of the High Court Division upon hearing the parties made the Rule absolute by the impugned judgment and order dated 20.02.2014.  

5.             The Government of Bangladesh and others feeling aggrieved by the judgment and order dated 20.02.2014 of the High Court Division preferred the Civil Petition for Leave to Appeal No.1521 of 2014 before this Division and obtained leave.

6.             Mr. Goutom Kumar Roy, the learned Deputy Attorney General appearing on behalf of the appellants submits that the lands having been acquired vide L.A. Case No.4/1977-78 from the title holder Dr. Anwar Hossain for expansion of Highways of Barishal and proper compensation having been paid there is no scope to release the acquired lands and as such, the High Court Division erred in law in making the Rule absolute and directing the respondents to release the land and as such, the impugned judgment and order passed by the High Court Division is liable to be set aside. He further submits that serious disputed question of facts relating to payment of compensation at the earlier rate or present market value of the land of the immovable properties having been involved the High Court Division erred in law in entertaining the writ-petition and making the Rule absolute sitting in special statutory jurisdiction under article 102 of the Constitution of the People’s Republic of Bangladesh and as such, the impugned judgment and order passed by the High Court Division is liable to be set aside.         

7.             Per contra, Mr. Nazrul Islam Chowdhury, the learned Senior Counsel appearing on behalf of the respondent Nos.1-6 submits that the authority on the recommendation of the requiring body has jurisdiction to release unused acquired land on refund of compensation which was paid to the awardee at the time of acquiring and, as such, the High Court Division rightly passed the impugned judgment. He further submits that the requiring body on an inquiry found that 0.75 acre of land still has been laying as unused and, as such, made the recommendation to release the same in favour of the writ petitioners (herein respondents) as the legal heirs of the original owner. He finally submits that no gazette notification has yet been published finally complying the provision of sub section (7) of section 5 of the Requisition of Property Act  in respect of the land to be acquired. Therefore, acquisition has not yet been completed under the Requisition of Property Act and as such, there is no legal bar to release the land in question, hence, the High Court Division has rightly passed the impugned judgment directing to release the property. Therefore, on above backdrops the impugned judgment calls for no interference by this Hon’ble Division.

8.             We have heard the learned Advocates of both the sides. Perused the impugned judgment of the High Court Division and other connected materials on record.

9.             On perusal the averment of the appeal it reveals that 1.50 acres of land under Mouza Rupatali, Police Station: Kotwali, J.L. No.56, District: Barishal appertaining to S. A. Khatian No.2128, S.A. Plot No.1141 was acquired from its owner and possessor vide L.A. Case No.4 of 1977-78 under the Requisition of Property Act by the Deputy Commissioner, Barishal as respondent No.3 for requiring body Roads and Highway Department under the Ministry of Communication for expansion of Barishal Highways. The writ respondents (herein) as legal heirs of the owner as petitioners filed an application on 03.04.2000 (Annexure-B) to the Ministry of Land for releasing unused land in favour of them. The Ministry of Land vide letter dated 30.05.2001 (Annexure-C) asked the Deputy Commissioner, Barishal (respondent No.3) to give a report after proper inquiry. The Roads and Highway Department, Barishal was asked to submit a report to the respondent No.3, Deputy Commissioner, after proper inquiry. Thereafter, the Executive Engineer Roads and Highway Department, Barishal Division vide Memo dated 20.09.2004 and 08.12.2004 (Annexure-D and D-1) made recommendation to release unused 0.75 acre of land out of 1.50 acres of acquired land. In pursuance of the above memos the Ministry of Communication issued no objection letter vide memo dated 11.04.2005 (Annexure-E) with condition on refund the compensation money as earlier paid to the title holder.

10.         The Senior Assistant Secretary, Ministry of Land (respondent No.8) vide letter dated 27.07.2006 asked the respondent No.3 to provide the information whether compensation was paid to the awardee of 1.50 acres of land of S.A. Plot No.1141 which was acquired vide L.A. Case No.4 of 1977-78 and final gazette notification under section 5(7) of the Requisition of Property Act was published. The respondent No.3, Deputy Commissioner, Barishal in pursuance of the aforementioned letter informed the Ministry that 90% of the compensation has already paid and 0.75 acre of land has been laying unused as the requiring body Roads and Highway Department could not utilized the land for the purpose it was acquired. The respondent No.3, therefore, in a letter dated 24.09.2009 recommended that as the price of land now has been increased the unused land can be released and returned back to the writ petitioners (herein the respondents) if the writ petitioners paid the value of the unused 0.75 acre of land at the rate of present value of the land.

11.         In view of the claim of the present market value of the land by the respondent No.3 vide letter dated 24.09.2009, the writ petitioners (herein respondents) served a legal notice to the respondents demanding justice, however, in vain. Therefore, the writ petitioner filed the writ petition before the High Court Division praying for the release of the land and direction upon the writ respondents to accept the refund of the compensation at the rate of compensation as earlier paid to the original owner but not as per present value of the land.

12.         The High Court Division vide its judgment and order dated 20.02.2014 made the Rule absolute directing writ respondents to release the unused 0.75 acre of land appertaining to S.A. Plot No.1141 acquired under L.A. Case No.4 of 1977-78 Barishal in favour of the writ petitioners on refunding the actual compensation money as paid earlier to the title holder / the predecessor of the writ petitioners at the time of acquisition.

13.         The legal question raised herein is to be determined by this Division whether the land in question can be released from acquisition after payment of compensation and whether the writ petition is maintainable.

14.         Section 3 of the (Emergency) Requisition of Property Act, 1948 under which the land in question was requisition and acquisition reads as follows:

“3. Requisition of Property – When any property is required for a public purpose or in public interest, the Deputy Commissioner may requisition it by an order in writing:

Provided the projects covering more than one district or the projects of which the requiring body is the Dacca Improvement Trust, the Chittagong Development Authority or the khulna Development Authority, the proposal shall require the approval of the Government before proceedings under the Act are started:

Provided further that no property used by the public for the purpose of religious worship graveyard and cremation ground shall be requisitioned:

Provided further that when a property is required permanently for a public purpose or in public interest, the Deputy Commissioner may also requisition it with a view to its permanent acquisition for such public purpose or in such public interest.”

15.         The Government under the aforesaid provision has requisitioned and finally acquired the land in question paying 90% of the compensation of the land.

“8. Release from Requisition – (1) Where any requisitioned property is to be released from requisition the Deputy Commissioner may restore it to the persons from whom the property was requisitioned or to his successor-in-interest or to such other persons as may appear to the Deputy Commissioner to be entitled to such restoration.

(2) The delivery of possession of the requisitioned property to the person referred to in sub-section (1) shall be a full discharge of the Deputy Commissioner from all liability in respect of such delivery, but shall not prejudice any rights in respect of the property which any other person may be entitled by due process of law to enforce against the person to whom possession of the property is so delivered:

Provided that when the person, to whom the requisitioned property is to be restored on release from requisition, willfully neglects or refuses to take delivery of the requisitioned property on being directed in writing to take possession of such requisitioned property by the Deputy Commissioner, such requisitioned property shall be deemed and shall always be deemed to have been restored to such person within the meaning of this sub-section with effect from the date and time specified in the aforesaid direction.

(3) Where the person to whom possession of any requisitioned property is to be given cannot be found and has no agent or other person empowered to accept delivery on his behalf the Deputy Commissioner shall cause a notice declaring that the property is released from requisition to be affixed on some conspicuous part of the property and published in the Official Gazette.

(4) When a notice referred to in sub-section (3) is published in the Official Gazette, the property specified in such notice shall cease to be subject to requisition on and from the date of such publication and be deemed to have been delivered to the person entitled to possession thereof; and the Deputy Commissioner shall not be liable for any compensation or other claim in respect of the property for any period after the said date.

16.         On perusal of the aforesaid provisions of law it appears that the power to release any property is, from requisition, not from acquisition, which has been given under the abovementioned law upon the Deputy Commissioner which is a discretionary power and the discretion is to  be exercised only when a thing necessary for any person from whom the property has been requisition to be entitled to such restoration he may restore to such person or to his successor-in-interest. However, the provision of section 8 of the Requisition of Property Act applies only in respect of requisition not acquisition.

8B. Withdrawal from Acquisition – When the owner of any property which has been acquired under section 5 is willing to receive back the property, the Government may, at any time before the payment of compensation, withdraw from the acquisition of any such property by notification published in the Official Gazette, and on such withdrawal, the property shall be restored to the possession of the owner, and with effect from the date of such restoration, the property shall vest back in the owner with the rights which he had before such property was acquired.”

17.         Upon a bare reading and careful scrutiny of the above provision of law it appears that the Deputy Commissioner under the aforementioned provision of law has been authorised to withdraw the proposal of acquisition and return back the land to the person from whom the same has been acquired however, before the payment of compensation. So, the question raised by the learned Senior Counsel for the respondents herein that in the instant appeal the final gazette has not yet been published under sub section (7) of Section 5 of the Requisition of Property Act, therefore, the land in question may be released. Our considered views are that the land in question cannot be released from the acquisition because as per Section 5(6) of the Requisition of Property Act compensation has already been paid to the owner. So, there is no scope to withdraw the proposal of the acquisition and return back the land to the person from whom the same has been acquired although the final gazette has not yet been published. Therefore, the submission advanced by the Senior Counsel has no substances.

18.         This Division in the case of Bangladesh Vs. Commercial Trust Bangladesh Ltd., reported 46 DLR (AD) 89 observed that

“after compensation is paid for acquisition of land no order can be made for derequisition of such acquired land.”

19.         Furthermore, from the record of L.A. Case No.4 of 1977-78 as submitted before us through additional paper book dated 22.10.2018 it is clearly divulged that the awardee /title holder has already received the compensation and on 02.12.1978 possession of the land had already been handed over to requiring body.

20.         Moreso, on careful scrutiny it further reveals from the additional paper book submitted by appellants, the Deputy Commissioner (writ respondent No.3) has already sent the report to the Divisional Commissioner to publish the gazette notification under section 5(7) of the Requisition of the Property Act. However, due to inadvertency some mistakes have been occurred in the said report for which gazette notification has not yet been finally published. However, publication of the final gazette notification never rescind by the concerned authority, rather, the same is still under process after receiving the compensation of the land by the awardee.

21.         Section 5(7) of the Requisition of Property Act, 1948 read as follows:

“5(7). The Government shall then publish in the Official Gazette a notice to the effect that the Government has decided to acquire the requisitioned property. On and from the beginning of the day on which the notice is so published the requisitioned property shall vest absolutely in the Government free from all encumbrances and the period of such requisitioned of such property shall end.”

22.         We have gone through the provisions of sub section 7 of section 5 of the Requisition of Property Act which deal with the publication of the gazette and not release from the requisition of any property and withdrawal from the acquisition. However, we do not find any such provisions in the law that if any land after payment of compensation is remained unused that would be the basis to asking for release of the same from acquisition, even if, publication of the gazette notification has not yet done under sub section 7 of section 5 of the Requisition of Property Act. 

23.         The similar issue of releasing of the remaining unused land from acquisition has also been dealt with by this Division in the case of Badal Rani Misra alias Badal Rani Goswami and others Vs. Bangladesh and others reported in 27 DLR(AD)65 wherein this Division has held that

“the question relating to the release of requisitioned land, it is not disputed, falls within the administrative function of the requisitioning authority and if the said authority, having regard to the facts in the instant case that the order of requisition of the plots was passed by it upon approval of the proposal of the requiring body by the Government considered it necessary to seek approval of the Government before passing an order of release  of the same, it does not appear how that discretion called for interference by the High Court and how the High Court could pass an order of release under the Requisition of Property Act.”

24.         In the case of Al-haj Abul Bashar being dead his heirs Hosne-Ara Begum and others Vs. Bangladesh and others reported in 50 DLR (AD) 11 this Division has also observed that

“from the Inter-Ministerial Communications it appears that the Ministry of Land Administration directed the Secretaries of various departments of Bangladesh that unutilized requisitioned land may be returned to the original owners, if not required. These Inter-Ministerial Communications only lay down the policy of the Government which the various Government departments shall follow in the case of unutilized requisitioned land. These Inter-Ministerial Communications are merely policy guidelines as to how the excess and unutilized lands are to be treated but these instructions do not create any legal right in favour of the petitioners to ask for return of the land from the Government after the same has been validly requisitioned under the law. It is also on record that CDA with the approval of the Government had already completed the project for distributing plots to the low-paid employees and, as such, in reality it cannot be said that the land is not being utilized. Mere non-use of the land for years will not give any right to the petitioners. We also find that after completing all necessary formalities the land had already been handed over to the requiring body, namely, CDA. Since no legal or vested right has accrued in favour of the petitioners by mere Inter-Ministerial Communications the petitioners cannot have any legal right to ask for return of the land”.        

25.         In the case of Brahamanbaria Pourashava Vs. Secretary, Ministry of Land Reforms, Government of Bangladesh and others reported in 51 DLR (AD)84 this Division has also held that

“Furthermore, it will indeed be unconscionable if after about 22 years the appellant, which is a public body, be expropriated when it had built buildings on the acquired land upon making all necessary payments to the Government. Again, there is no principle of law under which once a property has been validly acquired for a public purpose, as admitted by the Government and has vested in the requiring body then, even if, the said property be utilized for  a different purpose, the acquisition shall not stand void. A simple reading of section 8B of the Act shows that it does not come into operation in the circumstances of the case. So the order of withdrawal from acquisition in question cannot be sustained.”

26.         It is vividly visible from the memo No. BPRH/2h-8/91-166 dated 30.03.1998 issued by the Ministry of Communication placed before this Division through additional paper book that there was an instruction from the Ministry of Communication to all concern to the effect that before passing any proposal for derequisition of the land of Highways the possibility of the expansion of the same in future 6/8 lanes must be considered. Thereafter, from the office of the Additional Chief Engineer, Barishal Zone vide memo No.20-1(4)/2398/1(2) dated 10.11.2004 has informed the Chief Engineer, Roads and Highways (pJS), Sarak Bhaban, Ramna, Dhaka that the land proposed to be derequisitioned would be required for expansion of the Barishal Jhalakhati Highways for making it 8 lanes from the present condition. However, this Communication was not produced before the High Court Division while the writ petition was heard, but added by the learned Deputy Attorney General, before this Division at the hearing of the appeal. In the present case, the appellant Government submits that the land in question would be required for the expansion of the Barishal-Jhalakhati highways to make it 8 lanes for the purpose for which land was requisitioned and eventually, the same was acquired upon paying the compensation under the law. So, under such circumstances and discussions made above, we are, therefore, of the considered opinion that the land in question after being acquisitioned upon payment of compensation cannot be withdrawn from acquisition and give it back to the heirs of the awardee on the plea of non-utilization as directed by the High Court Division. Hence, the impugned judgment cannot be sustained in law. 

27.         Therefore, the contentions advanced by Mr. Nazrul Islam Chowdhury, the learned Senior Counsel referring the provision of section 5(7) of the Requisition of the Property Act pointed that since the final gazette notification has not yet been published, therefore, the aforesaid land in question has not been finally acquired and no title has vest with the Government. Therefore, according to section 8 of the Act, the property in question can be released by the Government are completely devoid of legal propositions.

28.         Therefore, in view of the compliance of  the provision of section 5(6) of the Requisition of the Property Act  and in view of the decision of this Division as quoted above, there is no scope to release the land in question from the acquisition.

29.         In view of the above discussions and authority cited herein above it is crystal clear that once a property is acquired and vested in the Government after making payment of compensation there is no scope to release the same to the original owner or to his heirs.

30.         We, therefore, find force in the submissions advanced by the learned Deputy Attorney General. The writ petition is not maintainable and as such, the decision arrived at by the High Court Division being not based on proper appreciation of fats and legal proposition, the same calls for interference by this Division.

31.          Accordingly, the impugned judgment and order dated 20.02.2014 passed by the High Court Division in Writ Petition No.10627 of 2012 is set aside and thus, this civil appeal is therefore allowed without any order as to costs.
