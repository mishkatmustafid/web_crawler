Companies Act (XVIII of 1994)

Section 110(1)(2)(3)

In the facts and circumstances as detailed in the materials on record the High Court Division rightly found that there was adequate substance in the allegations that the petitioner's interest runs contrary to the interest of the respondent group of companies; that he had been using his position as Managing Director of these companies, his network as well as the brand name and goodwill of the "Paradise Group" in sponsoring "Star Paradise", established in the name of his son and daughter; that he has lost trust of the majority shareholders; that he was writing letters to bankers of the companies to hamper the business of the respondent companies; that his activities and conduct had led the respondent companies to financial hardship, shortage of cash flow and that created a deadlock situation in the business of "Paradise Group", since the banks were discouraged and prevented from opening L.C. or to give other credit facilities to the companies of the Paradise Group; and that he was leading these companies to financial sickness. The High Court Division also found that his unfair conduct is proved by his letters dated 08.02.2017 and 27.10.2017, addressed to Standard Bank Limited wherein the petitioner has falsely represented himself as the Managing Director of the companies, though he was not, and in which he had requested the bank to cancel 3 (three) letters of credit. Besides, the allegations of creating two forged resolutions dated 20.04.2016 and 04.11.2016 sent to the banker of these companies tantamount to extreme breach of fiduciary duties by the petitioner. He even did not pay any heed to the request made to him by other directors in the Board meeting dated 17.04.2017 to sign necessary documents and papers for issuing PG, BG and L/C and other credit facilities all of which were indispensable to run the respondent companies as going concern. Hence, the allegation that the petitioner was unwilling or unfit to continue as Managing Director is well founded. It appears from the record that in the instant case, though the petitioner is a minority shareholder, he however, had the upper hand in controlling and conducting the affairs of the companies, for more than 5(five) years. Hence, he cannot be said to have been oppressed since he was at the helm of affairs of the companies. Therefore, his prayer to buy him out is not considered as just and proper one. Â Â Â  . . . JUDGMENT

Muhammad Imman Ali, J: These petitions for leave to appeal have been filed against the judgement and order dated the 16th day of November, 2017 passed by the High Court Division in Company Matter Nos.120, 121 and 122 of 2017 dismissing the same. The connected contempt petitions were rejected. Since all the petitions relate to the same subject matter and arise out of one judgement of the High Court Division, they are dealt with by this single judgement.

2.Â Â Â Â Â Â Â Â Â Â Â Â  The facts relevant for disposal of these civil petitions for leave to appeal are as follows:

Â Â Â Â Â Â Â Â  Respondent No.1-company in each matter is a family owned private company. The petitioner and respondent Nos.2 to 4 are full brothers, while respondent Nos.5 to 8 are their spouses respectively. These are the only shareholders of the respondent-companies. As per Articles of Association of the respondent-companies, the petitioner shall be the first Managing Director of the respondent-companies and shall continue in that office for a period of five years and respondent No.2 shall be the first and permanent Chairman of the respondent-companies and they shall continue in their respective offices until they voluntarily resign or become disqualified to be a Director of the respondent-companies. No need has been felt for any change in the internal management of the respondent-companies, including the signatories of the bank accounts, or change in the office of Chairman or Managing Director. The bank accounts of the companies were to be operated upon signature of the Managing Director (the petitioner) and any one of the other Directors, as should be nominated by the Board. Accordingly, the bank accounts of the respondent-companies are being operated on the joint signatures of the petitioner and any one of respondent Nos.2 or 3. The respondent-companiesâ financial success and position as market leaders have been the main reason for allowing the management to continue as it is. There has absolutely never been any complaint or allegation against the Managing Director (the petitioner) or about the management of the respondent-companies. It is mentioned further that out of the four Directors of the respondent-companies, the petitioner and respondent Nos.2 and 3 were the most active, while respondent No.4 was more dormant. In the meantime, the children of the shareholders of the respondent-companies began attaining majority and returning to the country, having received education in the leading Universities of the West. The shareholders of the respondent-companies therefore considered it fit to appoint their sons to various executive capacities in the Paradise Group in the hope and expectation of grooming them for smooth transition of the family business to the next generation. But as ill luck would have it, due to the high handed behaviour of the son of respondent No.2, the relationship of amity that had existed among the brothers previously has broken down, and disputes and differences have arisen between them with respect to the affairs of the respondent-companies. The hitherto congenial atmosphere in the respondent-companies and among the brothers/shareholders/directors has been so much vitiated and soured by his conduct that his uncles, namely, the petitioner and respondent No.3 were greatly hurt. In the background of the above facts and circumstances, respondent No.2 made some one sided proposals to resolve the dispute and differences, which the petitioner and respondent No.3 found to be lacking in good faith and as promoting the personal interests of respondent No.2 and his son, undermining the stake of the other brothers. This prompted the petitioner and respondent No.3 to make counter proposals to resolve the disputes and differences by effectively partitioning the businesses and undertakings of the Paradise Group as well as immovable properties acquired in their individual names, keeping the business interest of the respondent-companies uppermost. Unfortunately such proposals did not yield any solution. In the course of the dispute that has arisen between the shareholders/directors of the respondent-companies, the disputants have coalesced into by and large two groups, with the petitioner and respondent No.3 being on one side and respondent Nos.2 and 4 being on the other. In the above situation, respondent No.2 at the instigation and incitement of his son, issued a notice dated 12.12.2016 convening a meeting of the Board of Directors of the respondent-companies at its office to be held on 22.12.2016, with the purported agenda for (i) discussing about the incompetency and financial irregularities committed by the Managing Director and to remove the petitioner from the post of Managing Director, (ii) appointing a new Managing Director, (iii) authorising signatories to operate bank accounts of the company and authorising a person to make all kind of correspondences with the Bank and other financial institutions, and (iv) any other matter(s) with the permission of the Chairman. At no stage of the business of the respondent-companies, it was ever considered that the Managing Director was incompetent or committed any financial irregularities, and it was considered unthinkable to change the petitioner from the post of Managing Director, given the fact that the respondent-companiesâ business and financial situation grew by leaps and bounds under his able management and stewardship. The petitioner was taken aback by the contents of the notice, and objected to it by a letter dated 19.12.2016. In the said letter, the petitioner expressed his apprehension, which he shared with respondent No.3, that respondent No.2 was acting with the ulterior motive of having a quorate meeting of the Board of Directors and then using his casting vote as Chairman to achieve his purpose of removing the petitioner from the office of Managing Director and changing the authority of operation of its bank accounts in his favour. Notwithstanding the above, respondent No.2, without even considering the proposal for amicable settlement, proceeded to convene the meeting on 22.12.2016, but it was not held due to lack of quorum as only respondent No.4 attended it. According to the Articles of Association, the attendance in person of three Directors is required for quorum. Respondent No.2 then issued a notice dated 26.12.2016 convening a meeting of the Board of Directors on 08.01.2017 with the same agenda as before, which was again not attended by the petitioner and respondent No.3. Respondent No.2, who is the named Chairman of the company under the Articles of Association, has a casting vote in case of equality of votes in a meeting of the Board of Directors. Respondent No.2 was trying to abuse his position in order to remove the petitioner as Managing Director and/or to gain control of operation of bank accounts. By convening successive meetings of the Board of Directors which were not attended by the petitioner and respondent No.3, respondent No.2 was attempting to lay ground for vacation of the office of Director by the petitioner and respondent No.3 on an application under section 108(l)(f) of the Companies Act, 1994 (the Act). In the above situation, the petitioner, finding no alternative, filed an application under section 85(3) of the Companies Act, 1994 read with rule 8 of the Companies Rules, 2009 praying for theÂ  convening of an Extraordinary General Meeting of the shareholders of respondent No.1-company to be chaired by a neutral Chairman appointed by the Court to consider and adopt resolutions for amendment of the concerned Articles of Association of the respondent-companies so that, first, the office of the Chairman rotates amongst the shareholders on annual basis, and secondly, the provision for the Chairman to have a casting vote is removed. Upon preliminary hearing, the said application filed under section 85(3) was admitted by the Court and upon consideration of the prayer made in the application for interim relief, passed an order restraining respondent No.2 from convening any meeting of the shareholders of the Board of Directors of the respondent-companies for conducting any business for removal of the petitioner (the Managing Director) and/or to change the current arrangement in the operation of the bank accounts of the companies. Respondent No.2 issued another notice dated 08.02.2017 convening a meeting of the Board of Directors on 13.02.2017, the purported agenda of the notice dated 08.02.2017 was (i) to discuss about current disputes existing amongst the Directors regarding the mismanagement of the company and to take decision to ensure smooth operation of businessÂ  of theÂ  company,Â  (ii) to distribute powers and responsibilities amongst the Directors of the Company, (iii) to discuss about the business of the company and set up new business target for the year 2017, (iv) to appoint a Chief Executive Officer (CEO) of the company and specify his powers and responsibilities as CEO, (v) to appoint an Executive Director (Sales) of the company and specify his duties and responsibilities, (vi) to change authorised signatories to operate bank accounts of the company and authorise persons to make all kind of correspondences with the bank and other financial institutions, (vii) to appoint statutory auditor for the year ended 2015-2016 and fix their remuneration subject to approval in General Meeting, (viii) to change registered and business address of the company, (ix) to approve the appointment of Md. Mustafa Kamal ACS as Company Secretary of the Group who has already been appointed on 04.02.2017 and (x) any other matter with the permission of the Chairman. Respondent Nos.2 and 4, as Directors, are conducting the affairs of the company and exercising their powers in a manner which is prejudicial to the interest of the petitioner as a member inasmuch as his right to participate in the management of the respondent-companies as aforesaid is being negated in blatant disregard of the provisions of the Companies Act, 1994 and the Articles of Association. By reason of the matters aforesaid, the respondent-companies have acted and are continuing to act in a manner which is discriminating against the petitioner's interest as a member, inasmuch as he is being ousted from the respondent-companies. The purported resolution of the meeting dated 16.02.2017, which has been referred to in the letter dated 23.02.2017 written by respondent No.2, discriminates against the interest of the petitioner as a member; hence, the petitioner filed the petitions before the Company Bench of the High Court Division.

17.Â Â Â Â Â  It appears from the record that in the instant case, though the petitioner is a minority shareholder, he however, had the upper hand in controlling and conducting the affairs of the companies, for more than 5(five) years. Hence, he cannot be said to have been oppressed since he was at the helm of affairs of the companies. Therefore, his prayer to buy him out is not considered as just and proper one.

18.Â Â Â Â Â  For any meeting to conduct business, quorum is necessary. Whether or not quorum exists can only be ascertained when the meeting is about to commence. Hence, lack of quorum does not negative the existence of the meeting, but means only that no business could be transacted due to lack of quorum. Consequently absence in such a meeting is certainly absence as understood in section 108 of the Act.

19.Â Â Â Â Â Â Â Â  We are of the view that the High Court Division correctly held that the Board meetings which could not be called to order due to lack of quorum were 'meetings' in the eye of law andÂ the petitioner's deliberate absence without leave must be treated as 'absence' in those meetings and will attract the mischief of section 108(1)(f) of the Act and his office would fall vacant by operation of law, for which no resolution need be taken, save to record the fact of vacation of the office of the absentee in the minutes and to submit a Form XII to the RJSC and, thereafter, that director will not be entitled to receive any notice of Board meeting or to attend the meeting of the Board. The High Court Division further held that although the impugned Board resolution dated 16.02.2017 has become infructuous because of the order of stay and the subsequent EGM decision dated 17.05.2017, however, the conduct of the petitioner in deliberately absenting himself from three consecutive Board meetings, so that quorum could not be formed and the meetings could not be called to order, amounts to absence without leave in the said Board meetings, within the meaning and for the purpose of section 108 (1)(f) of the Act and the impugned Board resolution dated 16.02.2017 was valid. Such conduct of the petitioner also amounts to breach of his statutory duties and it was clearly prejudicial to the interest of the company inasmuch as a director, by resorting to any tactics or measures whatsoever, cannot lead the company to a situation in which it will become impracticable for the company to call or hold its Board meeting or any other meetings permitted or required by law.
