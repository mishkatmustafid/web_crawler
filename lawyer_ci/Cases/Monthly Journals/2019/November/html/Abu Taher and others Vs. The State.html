Evidence Act (I of 1872)

Section 8

Per Muhammad Imman Ali, J. minority view:

It is clear that no link in the chain is missing and the circumstances clearly lead to the guilt of the accused and as such the same are incompatible with reasonable hypothesis of the innocence of the accused persons. The circumstances as evident appear to have been established beyond all doubt. Hence, Abu Taher and Alamgir cannot escape the guilt. On the other hand, accused Aminul did not accompany his two brothers in search of the victim and hence may get the benefit of doubt. In the case of accused Aminul, his failure to pursue the victim in order to take revenge for his mother’s beating breaks the chain of circumstances, and he therefore cannot be convicted of the offence alleged.                                                ....(15)

Penal Code, (XLV of 1860)

Section 149

Per Hasan Foez Siddique, J. delivered the main Judgment, majority view:

The essential ingredients of section 149 are that there must be an unlawful assembly for commission of an offence by any member of the said unlawful assembly and such offence must have been committed in prosecution of the common object of the assembly; or must be such as the members of the assembly knew that the offence to be likely to be committed. If those elements are satisfied, then only a conviction under section 149 of the Penal Code may be substantiated. Even in order to hold all the appellants vicariously liable for the offence committed it was the duty of the prosecution to establish that they were the members of unlawful assembly and they shared the common object of killing victim Rubi. In absence of evidence and findings as to common object of killing the victim the conviction of all the accused persons including the appellants, who are the members of a same family is, unsafe.                                -- (30)

 

Penal Code, (XLV of 1860)

Section 149

Per Hasan Foez Siddique, J. delivered the main Judgment, majority view:

To secure conviction with the aid of section 149 of the Penal Code an overt act on the part of a member of unlawful assembly is not necessarily required by participation with common object must be proved to the hilt which is absent in this case. When there as general allegation against a large number of person the court will hesitate to convict all of them on theory of constructive liability on vague evidence. Similarly, the individual liability of any of the accused person has not been proved in this case. Normally, this Division does not interfere with the concurrent findings of fact of the court below in the absence of very special circumstances or gross error of law committed by the High Court Division. But where circumstances show that the accused persons are entitled to get benefit of doubt it is duty of this court to step in and correct the erroneous decision of the High Court Division. In consideration of the evidence on record as discussed, we are of the view that all the convict appellants are entitled to get the benefit of doubt. In such view of the matter, the order of conviction and sentence of the appellants is liable to be set aside.                                                           .... (38 to 41)

 

Evidence Act (I of 1872)

Section 8

Per Hasan Foez Siddique, J. delivered the main Judgment, majority view:

As to prove the case, by circumstantial evidence, well settled principle is that the circumstances from which the conclusion of guilt is to be drawn should be fully established and not may be established. There is not only a grammatical but a legal distinction between may be proved and must be or should proved. There must be a chain of evidence so complete as not to leave any reasonable ground for the conclusion consistent with the innocence of the accused and must show that in all human provability the offence must have been done by the accused. The circumstances should be closely scrutinized and the same must be cogent, sufficient, reliable and must be of a such a character as to exclude all possibility of the accused being innocent and there should be no weak link, every weak link being a ground of reasonable suspicion, always calling from acquittal. Great care must be taken in evaluating circumstantial evidence and if the evidence relief on is reasonably capable of two inferences, the one in favour of the accused must be accepted.                            ....(33)

 

Sharder Birdichand Sarda V. State of Moharastra AIR 1984, SC 1622, Raghubir Singh Vs. State of U.P (1972) III SCC 79 ref.

JUDGMENT

Syed Mahmud Hossain, C. J.: I have gone through the judgments proposed to be delivered by my brothers, Muhammad Imman Ali, J. and Hasan Foez Siddique, J. I agree with the reasoning, findings and decision given by  Hasan Foez Siddique,J                                    C.J.

 

2.                   Muhammad Imman Ali, J:- I have had the privilege of reading the judgement proposed to be delivered by my learned Brother Mr. Justice Hasan Foez Siddique. With due respect I cannot agree with the reasoning and propose to state my views shortly.

3.                The facts of the case are fully stated in the judgement of my learned brother and I propose to narrate only those facts which are necessary for lucidity of my decision. This criminal appeal is directed against the judgement and order dated 09.08.2007 passed by a Division Bench of the High Court Division in Criminal Appeal No.351 of 2000 dismissing the appeal affirming the judgment and order dated 08.02.2000 passed by Metropolitan Additional Sessions Judge, Court No.2, Dhaka in Metro Sessions Case No.1318 of 1999 arising out of Demra Police Station Case No.39 dated 17.07.1994 corresponding to G.R. No.200/94, convicting the accused appellants under sections 302/149 of the Penal Code and sentencing them to suffer imprisonment for life and to pay a fine of Tk.3,000/- (three thousand) each, in default to suffer rigorous imprisonment for 1(one) year more.

4.                Rahima Khatun alias Rubi aged about 17 years, daughter of Md. Sayed Ali, the informant, went to bed in the same room as her mother and siblings in their family home at 10:00 p.m. in the night following 16.07.1994. In the morning Rubi was not found in the room. On inquiry, the informant’s brother Tayab Ali (PW2) informed them that he had seen the dead body of Rubi hanging from a tree on the west side of his brother Israfil’s compound. The informant at once rushed to that place and found his daughter hanging from a Menda tree branch and her legs were touching the ground. He found injuries on her face, hands and legs and also found a few pieces of cloth inside her mouth. The police came and brought down Rubi’s dead body from the Menda tree and prepared inquest report on the spot. The informant stated in the First Information Report (FIR) that on his return home in the previous evening (16.07.1994), he had heard that his daughter had a quarrel with his brother Israfil’s sons and they had threatened to kill her. His brother, accused Israfil, nephews Aminul, Abu Taher, Alamgir, their mother (Khursheda) and Abu Taher’s wife (Shamsun Nahar) had held out threats

5.                The prosecution story, in brief, is that on 16.07.1994 Rubi had an argument and altercation with Khursheda, Israfil’s wife in connection with a plate, which belonged to Rubi. When Rubi’s brother Bashar came home and said something, Israfil’s wife Khursheda, son Aminul, and Abu Taher’s wife (Shamsun Nahar) caught hold of Bashar. Tayab Ali (PW2) another brother of Israfil and of the informant intervened and stopped the quarrel. During the altercation, Rubi inflicted a lathi blow on Khursheda. About an hour later accused Abu Taher and Alamgir, both sons of Israfil returned home and found out about the lathi blow upon their mother, who was crying. After hearing about the incident, Abu Taher and Alamgir went to Rubi’s living quarters and kicked on her door. They were turned away by Tayab Ali. The next morning Rubi’s dead body was found hanging from the Menda tree near the south side of Abu Taher’s house. The post-mortem report gave details of injuries visible near the mouth, cheek and front of the left and right leg as well as a circular, continuous ligature mark in the middle of the neck of the victim. The opinion given was that the death was caused by strangulation, which was ante-mortem and homicidal.

6.                The trial court, upon consideration of all the evidence and materials on record convicted all six accused persons, namely Israfil, Abu Taher, Alamgir, Aminul, Khursheda and Shamsun Nahar under sections 302/149 of the Penal Code and sentenced them as stated above. The criminal appeal preferred by the convicted persons was dismissed by the High Court Division, thereby upholding the judgement of the trial court. The appellants then filed Criminal Petition for Leave to Appeal No. 344 of 2008 and obtained leave giving rise to this appeal. The grounds of appeal before this Division essentially are that there are no ingredients of an offence under sections 302/149 of the Penal Code by altering the charge from section 302/34 of the said Code, particularly since none of the prosecution witnesses spoke of any unlawful assembly. The other and more fundamental ground taken was that both the trial Court and the High Court Division found that there was no direct evidence, but proceeded to pass the judgement and order of conviction and sentence based solely on circumstantial evidence.

7.                There can be no gainsaying that conviction can be based on circumstantial evidence alone, otherwise criminals who commit crimes in the dead of night, where there are no eyewitnesses, would go scot-free, thus making a mockery of the criminal justice system. The effect would be anarchy in society. However, there are certain factors which are to be taken into account in adjudicating cases based on circumstantial evidence. These can be summarised as follows: (i) the circumstances must be fully established; (ii) the chain of circumstances must be complete as to be consistent with the guilt of the accused and preclude any notion that the accused may be innocent; (iii) the evidence must be such that it can safely be concluded that in all human probability the act must have been done by the accused. In other words, the facts so established should be consistent only with the hypothesis of the guilt of the accused, that is to say, they should not be explainable on any other hypothesis except that the accused is guilty.

8.                The case is one of a tragic death of a 17/18 year old girl in the prime of her life. The post-mortem report clearly indicated that the death was by strangulation, which was ante-mortem and homicidal in nature. There can be no doubt that the victim was done to death, as was found by the trial Court and the High Court Division. The vital question is whether the accused persons or any of them were involved in the acts leading to the death of the victim.

9.                Let us consider the circumstances which are relevant for the purpose of ascertaining whether the accused persons had any part to play which could be connected with the ultimate death of the victim.

 

1.       There was an altercation/quarrel relating to a plate belonging to the victim which was found in the house of the accused.

2.       Tayab Ali (PW2) was a witness to the quarrel and deposed that in the course of the altercation the victim struck Khursheda with a lathi.

3.       According to the deposition of the informant, when his brother Israfil returned home and heard about the incident, he rebuked his six sons for not avenging the lathi blow on his wife dealt by Rubi and goaded his sons to go to the informant’s house and slaughter them one by one. This was not controverted in his cross examination.

4.       One hour after that incident Abu Taher and Alamgir came home and found their mother crying and heard details about the incident and went to the living quarters of the victim and kicked the door. (We find from the deposition of PW 1 that the wooden latch (wLj) of the door was broken in the process and this fact was not challenged in his cross-examination. On the other hand, PW2 in his cross-examination stated that the khil was not broken and that the window was broken).

5.       At sunrise Tayab Ali (PW2) reported that the victim’s body was hanging from the branch of a Menda tree near Abu Taher’s house and her feet were resting on the ground. According to PW12 the branch was less than 5’6” high. The victim had been gagged with numerous pieces of cut cloth, which according to PW7, were removed by the police.

6.       The abrasion injuries on the body of the victim and the pieces of cloth found in her mouth, as deposed by PW 14 indicate that she was gagged and dragged from her house to the place where she was strangulated with a piece of rope.

7.       All the accused persons were not found on their compound from the morning of 17.07.1994.

8.       The accused put up a defence that the victim committed suicide as she was not allowed to marry the person of her choice. This was not supported by any witness, nor did the accused attempt to substantiate the said defence.

 

10.               From the above, we find a continuity in the sequence of events starting from the quarrel between the victim and accused Khursheda on 16.07.1994. Clearly the action of the victim in dealing a lathi blow on Khursheda caused her to cry and complain when her sons arrived on the scene. The natural reaction of the two sons, namely Abu Taher and Alamgir being goaded by their father Israfil was to find Rubi and take revenge for the beating and humiliation suffered by their mother. We note that the other brother, namely Aminul did not accompany Abu Taher and Alamgir. The two brothers who went to seek revenge could not reach Rubi as she was behind closed doors. On finding Rubi’s door closed they kicked the door and broke the wooden latch (wLj) of the door and left the place being turned away by their another the paternal uncle, Tayab Ali.  Between this failure to take revenge and the death of the victim being discovered at dawn, there were the few hours of darkness. Nothing intervened. There was no evidence that the victim came out of her house or that anyone else entered that house. In my view there is no break in the chain of events from the occurrence of the quarrel and discovery of victim’s dead body.

 

11.         The occurrence took place in the night that followed after the quarrel and kicking and breaking the latch of the door. Tayab Ali, brother of the informant and of the accused Israfil, is a neutral witness being paternal uncle of the victim Rubi and paternal uncle of accused Abu Taher, Alamgir and Aminul. No enmity was alleged between this witness and the accused. There is no reason to disbelieve his evidence. He deposed with regard to the circumstances of the quarrel between the victim and the mother of accused Abu Taher, Alamgir and Aminul, and the fact that Abu Taher and Alamgir pursued the victim to her living quarters and tried to kick open the locked door, and that the dead body was found at the break of dawn tied to a tree next to the house of Abu Taher. This clearly is a chain of events which cannot be disbelieved.

 

12.         It is to be noted that the case was sent for hearing to the Metropolitan Sessions Judge, who framed charge against the accuse-appellants under sections 302/34 of the Penal Code and read over and explained to them to which they pleaded not guilty and claimed to be tried. Before pronouncement of judgement charge was altered and framed under sections 302/149 of the Penal Code which was again read over and explained to them to which they pleaded not guilty and claimed to be tried. Looking at the evidence as a whole, I do not find any cogent reason as to why the charge was altered. If at all, the injuries found on the body of the victim tend to suggest that the blows to the body of the victim were neither intended to nor done with the knowledge that they would cause death of the victim. On the contrary, the injuries appear to be incidental to the victim being gagged and dragged from her house. Gagging of the victim with pieces of cloth was done with the intention of preventing her from making any sound. That certainly did not cause her death. The cause of death was the fatal injury on the middle of her neck. This was caused by tightening the rope around the neck of the victim.

 

13.               The defence case is that the accused persons are absolutely innocent and Rubi had committed suicide as she had not given marriage with someone she loved and they have been falsely implicated in the case. This clearly was negated by the fact that the victim had been gagged with a handful of cut piece cloth and her body was not in fact hanging from the tree, rather her feet were touching the ground. Moreover, in a case of suicide, the ligature mark would not be in the middle of the neck. No other hypothesis as to the innocence of the accused was put forward.

 

14.               In a case where the only evidence is circumstantial, it is important that the prosecution establish the guilt of the accused by cogent and reliable evidence and the proved circumstances must unequivocally point to the guilt of the accused and must exclude any hypothesis inconsistent with the innocence of the accused. It is also important to see what motive the accused would have in committing the offence alleged. In the instant case the motive is clear. The victim struck Khursheda with a lathi and her sons being instigated by the words of their father went to take revenge. Clearly accused Abu Taher and Alamgir were most vengeful as they immediately went in search for the victim and where prevented only by the locked door and by their uncle Tayab Ali (PW2). No other intervening event took place between that time and the break of dawn which would explain the death of the victim. The dead body having been found adjacent to the house of accused Abu Taher, the inference is clear that he is complicit in the commission of the offence. The proximity of time between the quarrel, assault on Khursheda, the mother of accused Abu Taher and Alamgir, reaction of accused Abu Taher and Alamgir in searching for the victim from her living quarters and being prevented only due to the door being locked and the intervention by their uncle Tayeb Ali and the victim being strangulated to death in the dead hours of that night and absence of all the accused persons from their respective living quarters from early morning, in my view, establish the circumstances proving the guilt of accused Abu Taher and Alamgir. Since, evidently these two accused persons were most animated by the beating of their mother, their revengeful reaction speaks of their involvement in the offence alleged.

 

15.               From the facts and circumstances discussed above it is clear that no link in the chain is missing and the circumstances clearly lead to the guilt of the accused and as such the same are incompatible with reasonable hypothesis of the innocence of the accused persons. The circumstances as evident appear to have been established beyond all doubt. Hence, Abu Taher and Alamgir cannot escape the guilt. On the other hand, accused Aminul did not accompany his two brothers in search of the victim and hence may get the benefit of doubt. In the case of accused Aminul, his failure to pursue the victim in order to take revenge for his mother’s beating breaks the chain of circumstances, and he therefore cannot be convicted of the offence alleged.

 

16.               The evidence on record does not indicate any assembly of persons and, therefore, the commission under section 149 of the Penal Code is not sustainable. However the injury found would certainly attract an offence under sections 302/35 of the Penal Code. Accordingly the appeal is allowed in part. Accused-appellant Aminul Hoque is found not guilty and is acquitted of the charge levelled against him. The impugned judgement and order in respect of appellant Aminul Hoque is set aside and he be set at liberty if not wanted in connection with any other case. The appeal in respect of appellants Abu Taher and Alamgir is dismissed.

                                                                                                                

17.            Hasan Foez Siddique, J: This appeal is directed against the judgment and order dated 09.08.2007 passed by the High Court Division in Criminal Appeal No.351 of 2000 affirming those dated 08.02.2000 passed by the Additional Sessions Judge, Court No.3, Dhaka in Metro Sessions Case No.1318 of 1999 arising out of Demra Police Station Case No.39 dated 17.07.1994 corresponding to G.R. Case No.2008 of 1994 convicting the appellants under sections 302/149 of the Penal Code and sentencing each of them to suffer imprisonment for life and to pay a fine of tk.3000/- each, in default, to suffer rigorous imprisonment for 1(one) year more.

 

18.            The facts of the case, in short, were that in the night of the following day dated 16.07.1994 P.W.1 informant Md. Sayed Ali along with his wife and daughters went to bed in their dwelling hut. In the morning of 17.07.1994, the informant’s wife did not find their daughter Rahima Khatun alias Rubi, aged about 17 years, in her bed room. Accordingly, they started searching. At that time, one Shahabuddin informed them that he had seen the dead body of victim Rubi hanging in a ‘menda’ tree situated at the western side of Israfil’s dwelling homestead. The informant rushed there and found his daughter’s deadbody hanging in a ‘menda’ tree. He found marks of injuries on her person and also found some pieces of cloths pushed in her mouth. He raised alarm. People of the area rushed there. They apprehended that the victim had been killed by someone. It was stated that day before the night of occurrence a quarrel cropped up between the sons of Israfil and the victim. P.W.1, returning home, heard about the previous day quarrel that accused appellants and their other family members had threatened the victim. The police, getting information, rushed to the P.O. and brought down the dead body of the victim from the ‘menda’ tree and held inquest of the same.

 

19.            The police took up the matter for holding investigation and submitted charge sheet on 12.05.1996 against the appellants and others under section 302/34 of the Penal Code. The trial Court framed charges against the appellants and others under section 302/34 of the Penal Code who pleaded not guilty and claimed to be tried.

 

20.            The prosecution examined 17 witnesses in support of its case and defence examined none. After closing of the evidence and examining the accused persons under section 342 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, the trial Court heard the submissions of the parties. The defence case was that the accused persons were absolutely innocent and victim Rubi had committed suicide.

 

21.            The trial Court convicted accused Israfil, Abu Taher, Alamgir, Aminul Haque, Khorsheda Begum and Shamsunahar under section 302/149 of the Penal Code and sentenced each of them to suffer imprisonment for life and pay fine of taka 3,000/- each, in default, to suffer R.I. for one year more. Against the said judgment and order of conviction, the convicts preferred Criminal Appeal No.351 of 2000 in the High Court Division and the High Court Division, by the impugned judgment and order, dismissed the said appeal.

 

22.            Then the appellants Abu Taher, Alamgir and Aminul Haque preferred this appeal in this Division getting leave.

 

23.            Mr. A.F.M. Meshbahuddin, learned Senior Counsel appearing for the appellants, submits that the High Court Division upheld the order of conviction of the appellants assuming that they had killed the victim with a common object by making unlawful assembly inasmuch as the ingredients of common object and unlawful assembly have not been proved against the accused persons beyond shadow of doubt. He submits that the occurrence took place in a place which is only 50 yards far from the dwelling homestead of P.W.1 and that the prosecution case was that the victim was brought out from inside of her dwelling hut and, thereafter, was killed at the place of occurrence but neither her parents nor her uncle P.W.2 Tayeb Ali, brother and other inmates of the house heard any kind of sound, which is absurd. He further submits that the courts below have failed to take notice that the prosecution has hopelessly failed to prove its case against the appellants beyond all shadow of doubt by examining any eye witness of the occurrence and, as such, the order of conviction and sentence of the appellants is not sustainable in law. He, lastly, submits that prosecution has failed to establish any such chain of circumstances wherefrom it can be said conclusively that the appellants, none else, had killed the victim.

 

24.            Mr. Biswajit Debnath, learned Deputy Attorney General appearing for the State, supporting the judgment and order of conviction, submits that the prosecution, by examining eye witnesses of previous day quarrel, proved the motive of the accused persons to kill the victim and also proved that next morning of the night of occurrence the whole family members including the appellants absconded from their house. Those two facts conclusively established a chain of circumstances to infer that none else the appellants had committed the offence, the courts below rightly convicted the appellants.

29                 In this case, the conviction has been awarded under sections 302/149 of the Penal Code mainly on the basis of circumstantial evidence since there is no eye witness of the occurrence.

 

30                 From the judgments of the courts below it appears that both the courts failed to draw any conclusion considering circumstances available in the evidence that all the accused persons, with an object to kill the victim, making unlawful assembly went to the place of occurrence and had killed the victim Rubi and, thereafter, hanged her deadbody in a tree inasmuch as section 149 of the Penal Code is a specific offence and there should be a clear finding as to what was the object of the unlawful assembly and whether the object of unlawful assembly was to commit murder. The essential ingredients of section 149 are that there must be an unlawful assembly for commission of an offence by any member of the said unlawful assembly and such offence must have been committed in prosecution of the common object of the assembly; or must be such as the members of the assembly knew that the offence to be likely to be committed. If those elements are satisfied, then only a conviction under section 149 of the Penal Code may be substantiated. Even in order to hold all the appellants vicariously liable for the offence committed it was the duty of the prosecution to establish that they were the members of unlawful assembly and they shared the common object of killing victim Rubi. In absence of evidence and finding as to common object of killing the victim the conviction of all the accused persons including the appellants, who are the members of a same family, is unsafe. 

 

31                 We have already held that in this case there was no eye witness of the occurrence and the appellants were convicted solely on the basis of circumstantial evidence.

 

32                 In the case of Sharder Birdichand Sarda V. State of Moharastra reported in AIR 1984 SC 1622 Supreme Court of India observed that following conditions must be fulfilled before a case against an accused can be said to be fully established by circumstantial evidence, namely,

(i)      The circumstances from which the conclusion of guilt is to be drawn should be fully established;

(ii)           The facts so established should be consistent only with the hypothesis of the guilt of the accused, that is to say, they should not explainable on any other hypothesis except that the accused is guilty;

(iii)        The circumstances should be of a conclusive nature and tendency;

(iv)        They should exclude every possible hypothesis except the one to be proved, and

(v)          There must be a chain of evidence so complete as not to leave any reasonable ground for the conclusion consistent with the innocence of the accused and must show that in all human probability the act must have been done by the accused.

 

33.            The circumstances, which, the prosecution has tried to establish in this case, are that there was previous day quarrel between the appellants and the victim and, on the next morning, dead body of the victim was recovered and the appellants were not found in their house. As to prove the case, by circumstantial evidence, well settled principle is that the circumstances from which the conclusion of guilt is to be drawn should be fully established and not ‘may be’ established. There is not only a grammatical but a legal distinction between ‘may be proved’ and ‘must be or should be proved’. There must be a chain of evidence so complete as not to leave any reasonable ground for the conclusion consistent with the innocence of the accused and must show that in all human probability the offence must have been done by the accused. The circumstances should be closely scrutinized and the same must be cogent, succinct, reliable and must be of a such a character as to exclude all possibility of the accused being innocent and there should be no weak links, every weak link being a ground of reasonable suspicion, always calling for an acquittal. Great care must be taken in evaluating circumstantial evidence and if the evidence relied on is reasonably capable of two inferences, the one in favour of the accused must be accepted.

35.            It is well known that different persons are differently constituted, and that some accused persons, though innocent, deliberately abscond rather face the ordinal of a criminal case. Even an innocent man may feel panicky and try to evade arrest when wrongly suspected of a grave crime. Absconding by itself need not necessarily lead to an inference of culpable mind against all the absconders. Absconding by itself is not conclusive either of guilt of a guilty conscience because the person may abscond on account of fear of being involved in the offence or for any other allied reason.

 

36.            In the case of Raghubir Singh V. State of U.P. reported in (1972)III SCC 79 it was observed that the act of absconding, even if proved, is normally considered a somewhat weak link in the chain of circumstances utilized for establishing the guilt of the accused. Moreover, the alleged facts of absconding was not put to the appellants is their examination under section 342 of the Code of Criminal Procedure so as to enable them to offer explanation.

 

37.            Probability of two views are possible in this case. The courts below convicted the accused (1)Israfil, (2)Abu Taher, (3)Alamgir, (4)Aminul Haque (5)Khorshida Begum and C(6)Shamsun Naher assuming that all of them, forming unlawful assembly with a common object to kill the victim, went to the place of occurrence and had killed the victim but we do not find any evidence either circumstantial or direct. There is no answer to the question as to who went to the place of occurrance and what was their actual number? All the members of the same family have been convicted relying only on presumption, surmises and conjectures. It is the cardinal principle of criminal jurisprudence that a case can be said to be proved only when there is certain and explicit evidence and no person can be convicted on pure on moral conviction. Another golden thread which runs through the web of the administration of justice in criminal cases is that if two views are possible on the evidence adduced in the case, one pointing to the guilt of the accused and other to his innocence, the view which favourable to the accused should be adopted. This principle has a special relevance in cases wherein the guilt of the accused is sought to be established by circumstantial evidence. On consideration of the evidence and the circumstances referred to above, we are satisfied that this is a case in which the circumstantial evidence did not prove the case against accused conclusively and unerringly, and at any rate two reasonable views are possible.
