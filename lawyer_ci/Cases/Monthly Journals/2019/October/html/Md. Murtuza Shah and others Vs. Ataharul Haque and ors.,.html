(20)

Article 102

The Court of Settlement believed the prosecution story thoroughly examining the oral and documentary evidence. The High Court Division without controverting the aforesaid unimpeachable evidence reversed the facts of finding of the Court of Settlement, as if, it is a Court of appeal which is not only beyond the jurisdiction under article 102 of the Constitution, rather, such exercise is colourable and fenciful which is deprecated by this Division. So, the High Court Division under article 102 acted as a Court of appeal.      . . . (23)

Constitution of Bangladesh, 1972

Article 102

The High Court Division only could interfere with the finding of the Court of Settlement, if it could be shown that the tribunal had acted without jurisdiction or  made findings upon no evidence or without considering any material evidence causing prejudice to a party or it had acted malafide or in violation of any principle of natural justice. Md. Nuruzzaman, j: Both the Civil Appeals, by leave, are directed against the judgment and order dated 04.08.2011 passed by the High Court Division in Writ Petition Nos. 5329 of 2006 and 2516 of 2011 making the Rules Nisi absolute and declared the judgment and order dated 3.5.2006 passed by the First Court of Settlement, Dhaka in Case No. 83 of 2003 to have been passed without lawful authority and is of no legal effect.

2.             Both the civil appeals are heard together as both the writs were arose out of the same property. Those were disposed of by the High Court Division by the single judgment. They are dealt with by this single judgment.

3.             Facts leading, to filing of both the civil appeals, in short, are that:

After taking over possession of the said land, the said Noor Mohammad had been possessing the same by erecting dwelling house and started living therein; during Liberation War, the said Noor Mohammad along with his family left the said house and after liberation he did not come back to the said house and, as such, Government took over possession the same as abandoned property. Thereafter, Mr. Ataharul Hoque, the writ petitioner of the Writ Petition No.5329 of 2006 entered into possession of the said house on 01.05.1972 and started to live therein with his family. He by dint of possession filed an application to the concerned authority for allotting the said House No.3, Lane-11, Block-C, Section-10, Mirpur Housing Estate, Dhaka in his favour. On the basis of the said application the writ respondent No.2 of the Writ Petition No.5329 of 2006 issued a Demand Note on 18.01.1975 upon the applicant with a direction to deposit arrear rent from 01.05.1972 to 31.12.1975 at the rate of Tk.85/- per month amounting to Tk.2,720/- within 23.01.1975. 10.         Mr. Abdul Matin Khasru, the learned Senior Counsel, appearing on behalf of the appellant in both the civil appeals submits that the High Court Division erred in law having failed to appreciate that the respondent No.1 (writ petitioner) being a third party had no locus standi to file the writ petition challenging the judgment and order of the Court of Settlement and, as such, the instant appeals should be allowed. He next submits that the High Court Division erred in law having failed to consider that the original allottee late Noor Mohammad (father of the appellants), filed an application to the appropriate authority with proper documents within time stipulated by law to get his property released and the Court of Settlement after meticulous assessment of materials on record passed its judgment excluding the property in question from the ‘Ka’ list of the Abandoned Buildings as per provision of law enacted under the Ordinance No.54 of 1985 and, as such, the instant appeals should be allowed. He finally submits that the High Court Division erred in law having failed to appreciate that the explanation of delay in filing the writ petition is not satisfactory and, as such, the impugned judgment is liable to be set aside and the instant appeals should be allowed.

12.         Mr. Fida M. Kamal, the learned Senior Counsel appearing on behalf of the respondent No.1 in Civil Appeal No.64 of 2016 and the respondent No.3 in Civil Appeal No.65 of 2016 made submissions in support of the impugned judgment and order of the High Court Division.

13.         We have heard the learned Senior counsel appearing for the appellant in both the civil appeals, the learned Deputy Attorney General appearing for the respondent No.3 in Civil Appeal No.64 of 2016, the respondent No.1 in Civil Appeal No.65 of 2016, also Mr. Fida M. Kamal, the learned Senior counsel appearing for the respondent No.1 in Civil Appeal No.64 of 2016 and the respondent No.3 in Civil Appeal No.65 of 2016. Perused the impugned judgment of the High Court Division, judgment of the Court of Settlement, other connected materials available on record.

14.         In the instant appeals leave were granted to consider as to whether the High Court Division erred in failing to appreciate that respondent No.1 (Writ Petitioner) being third party had no locus standi to file the writ petition challenging the judgment and order of the First Court of Settlement, Dhaka allowing the case and directing the Government to exclude the case property from the “Ka” list of the Abandoned Buildings.

15.         Secondly, as to whether the High Court Division erred in law in failing to appreciate that the Government could not produce any evidence either documentary or orally to show that the property in question comes within the definition of “Abandoned Property” under Article 2 of the Bangladesh Abandoned Property (Control, Management and Disposal) Order 1972.

16.         Thirdly, as to whether the High Court Division erred in law in failing to appreciate that the explanation of delay in filing the writ petition is not satisfactory and the Court of Settlement passed the judgment and order after proper and meticulous assessment of evidence on record allowing the case and directing the Government to exclude the case property from the “Ka” list of the Abandoned Buildings.

17.         As regards to the ground filing of writ petition by the third party is required to scan as to whether he has any legal right over the property in question pending settlement case between the admitted allottee owner and Government Lessor. The original allottee when questioning the vesting of property, as per section 7 of the said ordinance as to the abandoned property and listing the same in “Ka” list, before the competent Settlement Court to decide the propriety of vesting, there is no reason and scope to resolve any claim made by the third party through Government vide a contract of purchase. Because, the provision of ordinance provides no room to resolve any claim of right and title of contracting party by the Court of Settlement. The Court of settlement only can see as per application under section 7 of the ordinance, as to whether the case property is abandoned or not.    

18.         The High Court Division in the judgment has already noticed that the respondent No.1-Ataharul Haque had applied to be added as a party in the settlement case before the Court of Settlement which was refused. The respondent No.1 did not challenge the order of refusal, nevertheless, after passing the judgment by the Settlement Court excluding the property from the “Ka” list of the Abandoned Buildings filed the writ petition challenging the aforementioned excluding before the High Court Division. In the above facts and circumstances, this Division is of the firmed view that writ petition by the third party could not be maintainable, rather, it is quite superfluous. In this regard we can safely held that before issuing the Rule the High Court Division ought to have gone through the merit of the Writ Petition and if it is apparent from the statement of the Writ Petition that the Petitioner neither aggrieved person nor have any locus standi to file the Writ Petition, such type of Vexatious application should be rejected summarily .The Writ Petitioner of the Writ Petition No.5329 of 2006 has got no personal interest in the case property either as tenant or so called purchaser.  His claim must be governed according to the result of his landlord vendor’s case. It is, therefore, our considered view that the High Court Division appears to have misconstrued and misconceived their jurisdiction under article 102 in holding that the Writ Petition is maintainable.

19.         As regards to the second ground whether as per definition of “Abandoned Property” under Article 2 of the Bangladesh Abandoned Property (Control, Management and Disposal) Order 1972, the property in question comes within that purview. The concept of abandoned property, according to article No.2(1) of President Order No.16 of 1972 runs as follows:

Defining part

(1)    “abandoned property” means any property owned by any person who is not present in Bangladesh or whose whereabouts are not known or who has ceased to occupy supervise or manage in person his property, including –

Inclusion clauses

(i)     any property owned by any person who is a citizen of a State which at any time after the 25th day of March, 1971, was at war with or engaged in military  operation against the People’s Republic of Bangladesh;

(ii)   any property taken over under the Bangladesh (Taking over of Control and Management of Industrial and Commercial Concerns) Order, 1972 (Acting President’s Order No.1 of 1972), but does not include –

Exclusion clauses

(a)    any property the owner of which is residing outside Bangladesh for any purpose which, in the opinion of the Government, is not prejudicial to the interest of Bangladesh;

(b)   any property which is in the possession or under the control of the Government under any law for the time being in force.  

Explanation.-“Person who is not present in Bangladesh” includes any body of persons or company constituted or incorporated in the territory or under the laws of a State which at any time after the 25th day of March, 1971, was at war with or engaged in military operations against the People’s Republic of Bangladesh. 

20.         According to definition it appears clearly that if any person who left his property or is not present in Bangladesh or whose whereabouts are not known or who has ceased to occupy, supervise or manage in person his property, during the period of  liberation in 1971 was at war with or engaged in military operation against the Bangladesh, his property under the definition shall be included as an abandoned property.

21.         On a careful perusal of the judgment of the Court of Settlement it is clearly revealed that the petitioner before the Court of Settlement produced 10 (ten) prosecution witnesses and exhibited so many documents.

22.         On the other hand the respondent Government neither produced any oral or documentary evidence for rebutting the prosecution explanation offered in the oral and documentary evidences regarding Noor Mohammod’s living in Bangladesh. Rather, from the trend of the cross it reveals that they would like to establish that claimants are not the legal heirs of the late Noor Mohammod. It is must be mentioned here that Noor Mohammod had filed the application on 17-09-1986 under section 7 of the ordinance LIV of 1985, the final gazette was published on 23-9-1986 including the property as abandoned property at page no. 9762 (58) being serial No.43. In respect of the legal heirs, the petitioners before the settlement Court filed the copy of succession certificate vide Succession case No. 631 of 1996, certificate being issued by a competent   Court which did not revoke till today. More so, they had deposited some certificate copies of the complaint case being No. 5 of 97 of the tribunal No. 1 of Bangladesh Bar Council and copy of passport of the Noor Mohammod. Noor Mohammod died on 10.02.1987. The leave petitioners were substituted by the settlement Court as the legal heirs. The government did not challenge those events before any court. 

23.         The Settlement Court found from the evidence on record that Noor Mohammod never left the Bangladesh, rather, on 04.02.1972 he shifted from the property in question for fear of his life to House No.7, Land No.4, Block-C, Avenuce-3, Section-11 of Mirpur, Dhaka. So, there is no question arises as to Noor Mohammod’s leaving the country. There is no iota of evidence on the side of the Government that Noor Mohammod had not shifted the house nor they had produced any oral and documentary evidences that Noor Mohammod left Bangladesh. It is further depicted from the record that Noor Mohammod could not manage or supervise the case property for a short period. The such type of absence from the case property does not take away his right and title in respect of case property as he did not left the country, rather had been trying to release the property. Therefore, the Court of Settlement rightly found that Noor Mohammod never left Bangladesh. It was also found by the Court of Settlement that Noor Mohammod had applied for passport and on 24.09.1979 a passport was issued in the name of Noor Mohammod as a permanent resident of Bangladesh. This passport proved that Noor Mohammod was a permanent resident of Bangladesh until death and lived in Bangladesh. He died on 10.02.1987 which has been clearly mentioned in a Death Certificate issued by the Local Commissioner and certificate issued from the concerned authority of the graveyard. Therefore, the Court of Settlement believed the prosecution story thoroughly examining the oral deposition and documentary evidence. The High Court Division without controverting the aforesaid unimpeachable evidence reversed the facts of finding of the Court of Settlement, as if, it is a Court of appeal which is not only beyond the jurisdiction under article 102 of the Constitution, rather, such exercise is colourable and fenciful which is deprecated by this Division. So, the High Court Division under article 102 could not act as a Court of appeal.

24.         This Division in the long line of catena has already held that the High Court Division only could interfere with the finding of the Court of Settlement, if could be shown that the tribunal had acted without jurisdiction or made findings upon no evidence or without considering any material evidence causing prejudice to a party or it had acted malafide or in violation of any principle of natural justice. The High Court Division has failed to appreciate any such lacking in the judgment of Settlement Court, even then, it set aside the judgment of settlement Court. 

25.         Noor Mohammod had filed the application on 17.09.1986 to the abandoned property cell of Ministry of Works for releasing the property vide a receipt No.3712 which has ultimately given rising to Settlement Case No.83 of 2003. So, it is crystal clear that the Late Noor Mohammad had filed the application within time as per provision of Ordinance No.LIV of 1985 though the case Number was recorded in the year 2003.

26.         It is, therefore, clearly depicted that the filling of case in the Court of Settlement by the Noor Mohammod not barred by law as opined by the High Court Division.  It is therefore obvious, the findings in the judgment of High Court Division as to non-consideration of evidence by the Settlement Court bears no significance. Ratherest, the Judgment of the High Court Division is so inappreciable which justifying calls for interference by this Division.

27.         The next ground is whether the High Court Division erred in failing to appreciate that sole respondent of the settlement case Secretary, Ministry of Housing and Public Works filed the writ petition without any delay acting with due diligence. On a careful scrutiny of the record of the Writ Petition No.2516 of 2011 it is apparent on the face of the cause title of the writ petition that the impugned judgment was passed on 03.05.2006 and an affidavit was sworn on 22nd day of March 2011; therefore, filing of the writ petition was not only delayed, ratherest, shockingly and unprecedented delay has been caused in invoking the special original jurisdiction under article 102 of the Constitution, though settlement case  was allowed on contest against the writ petitioner Government denying the material allegations, not only that, moreso,  they also cross examined all the P.Ws.

28.         Now this Division will look into the behaviorally attitude regarding seeking the redress under article 102 of the Constitution.

29.         Though no period of limitation has been prascribed by law for seeking redress under article 102 of the constitution. However, such relief must be sought as early as possible and must be shown due diligence. There is no special provision of privilege for the Government to explain the delay invoking constitutional jurisdiction. In the case in our hand, the Government even in the relevant paragraph of the Writ  Petition did not explain the knowledge about the judgment, filing of application and obtaining of the certified copy, nor cause any reason in the application for what cause it has not filed the writ application as early as possible after passing the judgment.

30.         As we have already noticed the date of judgment and swearing the date of affidavit for filing the writ petition, we cannot but to opine that there is no sufficient explanation for condonation of such inordinate delay. It is therefore, well established that the respondent Government was not vigilant for the filing the writ petition, probably it has acted in persuasion of the third party who filed the writ petition in the year 2006, even then, the Government had filed the writ petition in 2011 just before hearing and judgment of the High Court Division.

31.         In the facts, circumstances and instant situation of the case we cannot but to hold that inordinate delay in seeking relief can’t be justified according to explanation offered to condone the delay in accordance with law. Ratherest, it is clearly justifying to interference with the judgment of the High Court Division because the High Court Division in maintaining the writ application acted beyond the settle maxim of law in regard to condonation of delay.

32.         Accordingly we find substance in both the appeals. The appeals are hereby allowed. The judgment of the High Curt Division is set aside.
