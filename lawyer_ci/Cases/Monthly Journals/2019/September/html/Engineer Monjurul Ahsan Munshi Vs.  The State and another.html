Money Laundering Act, 2002 (15 of 2003)

Section 2 (Ttha) (Aa)

A company is always a different entity and a juristic person. The appellant being an individual and Managing Director of SBEL cannot merge into a single entity. We are, therefore, unable to accept that the appellant as an individual purchased the properties and is liable for owning/ possessing the same in the names of wife and son, or for concealment of its value. This is correct that he failed to produce any decision of the company to lend money to accused 2 for purchasing the flat at Gulshan. If any money is illegally shifted from a company’s fund, it may at best constitute an offence of money laundering as defined in section 29(Ttha) (Aa) of the Money Laundering Act, 2002 but cannot constitute an offence under section 27 of the Act V of 2004. It would also contribute in an illegality/irregularity under the Company Act and the other directors/share holders aggrieved thereby can raise the question in proper forum.            . . . (90)

Anti-Corruption Commission Act (V of 2004)

Section 27

An offence under section 27 of the Act V of 2004 continues so long the person sought to be prosecuted owns possesses the disproportionate property as defined therein. An abetment thereto also continues in the same way with his ownership/possession over the same. There is no scope to say after acquiring the disproportionate property, time of occurrences comes to an end. Evasion of income tax and offence of acquiring/ possessing/owning disproportionate property by illegal means as defined in section 27 of the Act V of 2004 are not the same. There might be some professionals/businessmen who legally earn a lot of money, but pay less income tax, it is an offence of evasion of tax or suppression of facts under the income Tax Ordinance, 1984. But if any public servant or an individual acquires property by illegal means, which is apparently disproportionate to his individual acquires property by illegal means, which is apparently disproportionate to his known/ legal sources of income and fails to satisfactorily account such property, an offence under section 27 of the Act V of 2004 would be committed. In the present case, the ACC failed to prove that the appellant as an individual acquired those properties for his own benefit, in benami of his son and wife and by illegal means, in such a case, we find no reason to convict him alone for especially when the co-accused thereselves claim it to be of their own. We are, therefore, not inclined to maintain his conviction and sentence.      . . . (91 to 93)

Md. Ruhul Quddus, J: This criminal appeal under section 10(A) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act, 1958(Act XL of 1958) is directed against judgment and order dated 09.10.2007 passed by the Special Judge, Court No. 5, Dhaka in Special Case No. 04 of 2007 arising out of Gulshan Police Station Case No. 101(3) 07 corresponding to GR No. 24 of 2007 convicting the appellant and sentencing him to suffer simple imprisonment for 3 (three) years under section 26(2) of the Durnity Daman Commission Ain, 2004 (Act V of 2004) read with rule 15Gha (5) of the Emergency Power Rules, 2007 (Rules, 2007) and further sentencing him to suffer rigorous imprisonment for 10 (ten) years under section 27(1) of the Act V of 2004 read with section 5(2) of the Prevention of Corruption Act, 1947 (Act II of 1947) with a fine of Taka 65,00,000/- (sixty-five lac) in default to suffer rigorous imprisonment for another one year and also confiscating the properties as scheduled thereto.

2.             The Anti-Corruption Commission (ACC) received an information that the appellant himself and in the name of the members of his family was in possession of property disproportionate to his known sources of income. It thus served a notice dated 18.02.2007 under section 26(1) of the Act V of 2004 asking him to furnish statement of wealth and that of his family-members within 72 hours from receipt thereof. At that time the appellant was in Bogra jail under and order of detention. He, however, furnished the statement of wealth through his representative on 25.02.2007. On receipt of the said statement and completion of the preliminary inquiry, the ACC lodged a first information report (FIR) with Gulshan Police Station, Dhaka on 23.01.2007 under sections 26 and 27(2) of the Act V of 2004 read with section 5(2) of the Act II of 1947, section 109 of the Penal Code and rule 15Gha (5) of the Rules, 2007 against the appellant, his wife Majeda Ahsan and two sons. namely, Rezwanul Ahsan and Rezviul Ahsan bringing allegation of concealment of properties worth Taka 3,03,56,157/- (three crore three lac fifty-six thousand one hundred fifty-seven) in total and of holding properties worth Taka 4,92,13,417.73 (four crore ninety-two lac thirteen thousand four hundred seventeen and seventy-three paisa), which were dispropor-tionate to their known sources of income. The material particulars of the allegations against each of the accused were specifically described in the FIR.

3.             The Investigating Officer (IO) investigated the case and submitted a charge sheet on 17.06.2007 against all the four FIR named accused under sections 26 and 27 of the Act V of 2004, 5(2) of the Act II of 1947, 109 of the Penal Code and rule 15Gha (5) of the Rules, 2007. According to the charge sheet, the concealed property was of Taka 3,13,93,199/- (three crore thirteen lac ninety-three thousand one hundred ninety-nine) and the disproporti-onate property, which was allegedly acquired by illegal means and beyond their legal sources of income was amounting to Taka 5,37,07,439.73 (five crore thirty-seven lac seven thousand four hundred thirty-nine and seventy-three paisa). The charge sheet was accompanied by a sanction of ACC. During investigation the accused including the appellant submitted revised statements to the ACC on 14.05.2007 in pursuance of order dated 06.05.2007 passed in Writ Petition No. 4441 of 2007.

4.             The case being ready for trial was sent to the Senior Metropolitan Special Judge, Dhaka and was registered as Special Case No. 30 of 2007. The learned Judge took cognizance of offence against the accused including the appellant by order dated 19.06.2007 and transferred the case to the Special Judge, Court No. 5, Dhaka for trial. On transfer the case was renumbered as Special Case No. 04 of 2007. The learned Special Judge, Court No. 5 by order dated 09.07.2007 framed charge against the accused under sections 26 (2), 27(1) of the Act V of 2004 read with rule 15Gha (5) of the Rules, 2007; sections 5(2) of the Act II of 1947 and 109 of the Penal Code and proceeded with trial. The charge so framed was read over to them, to which they pleaded not guilty and claimed justice.

Although 72 hours time was given in the notice, he got only 15/20 hours and was not allowed to meet anybody. In such a situation his youngest son somehow furnished an statement of wealth. Thereafter, he moved Writ Petition No. 4441 of 2007 in the High Court Division and obtained an interim order. In pursuance thereof, he furnished a revised statement of SBEL maintained with Rupali Bank. Initially the apartment was booked for his sister-in-law Masuma Haque, but as she declined, it was transferred in the name of his wife. He stated that the Mercedes Benz car belonged to his wife and she took loan for purchasing the same. The loan was repaid from the company and after repayment of all the installments, the company would take over the car. He further stated that the Nisan pick-up was purchased in the name of his wife. He proved among others, the audit reports of the companies for last seven years (exhibit-B series), demand letters of the creditor bank asking for repayment of loan (exhibit-C series), company’s resolution and other documents against car loan (exhibit-D series) and that against the loan for Pick-up (exhibit-E series), money receipts and bank statements showing payment against the flat at Gulshan (exhibit-1 series) and papers and documents in respect of shares in NCCBL (exhibit-L series).

49.         DW 2 Md. Kamruzzaman Bhuiyan, Vice-Principal of Jobeda Khatun Mohila College stated that the FDR of Taka 1,00,000/- kept with Debidwar post office actually belonged to the college. It was so done under a regulation of its Governing Body taken on 22.12.2005. Accused 1 was Chairman of the Governing Body. Masuma Haque contributed Taka 1,10,000/- for the purpose of giving stipend to the students, out of which Taka 10,000/- was already given as stipend and the remaining  one lac was kept in FDR account.

50.         After conclusion of trial, the learned Special Judge pronounced the judgment and order of conviction and sentence against the sole appellant as stated above and also confiscated four items of properties, namely, (1) Flat No. 6-J, House No. 20, Road No. 129, Gulshan, Dhaka worth Taka 55,98,927/-; (2) 3619 right shares in NCCBL worth Taka 3,61,900/- (3) Taka 2,24,000/- as canceled on account of price of Mercedes  Benz car, and (4) Taka 2,38,520/- as concealed on account price of Nissan pick-up those were scheduled to the judgment challenging the said judgment and order, the appellant preferred the instant criminal appeal. The appeal was taken up by another Division Bench and was allowed by judgment and order dated 23.06.2011 on the point of legality of notice dated 18.02.2007, challenging which the Government preferred Criminal Petition for Leave to Appeal No. 511 of 2011. The ACC also preferred Criminal Petition for Leave to Appeal No. 462 of 2013 against the selfsame judgment. The Appellate Division heard both the petitions together and disposed it of summarily by judgment and order dated 02.02.2015 sending back the case to the High Court Division for hearing afresh and disposal on merit on setting aside the impugned judgment and order. The appellant filed a Review Petition before the Appellant Division for reviewing the said judgment and order dated 02.02.2015, which was also dismissed.

51.         Mr. Ajmalul Hossain, Senior Advocate appearing for the appellant referring to Anti-Corruption Commission, Vs- Dr. Mohiuddin Khan Alamgir 62 DLR (AD) 290 submits that service of a notice under section 26(1) of the Act V of 2004 on an imprisoned person giving him only 72 hours time is an example of arbitrary action. A notice must allow a workable and reasonable time to a person who can freely go through necessary documents including income tax file and consult his lawyer to furnish a correct statement of wealth with all material particulars. In fact it is impossible even for a free man to furnish an accurate statement of wealth within 72 hours. Section 26 does not envisage a notice upon a person who is in detention and not expected to give the details of all his properties within a very short time. The person, upon whom a notice is served, must be afforded a fair and a reasonable opportunity to respond to the notice. A notice served upon a detenu in jail, away from his home, cannot also be said to be a fair and bonafide exercise of power.

52.         In the case in hand the appellant had to furnish the statement of wealth without consulting his Advocate or going through the necessary documents. It was quite usual that there would be some omissions and incorrect statements. Besides, the notice itself was without jurisdiction, for which the appellant moved Writ Petition No. 4441 of 2007 challenging the same and obtained a Rule with an interim order for furnishing a revised statement. Accordingly, he furnished the statement and the ACC accepted and considered the same in its investigation report (charge sheet) and never challenged the legality of the interim order passed in Writ Petition No. 4441 of 2007. On receiving the revised statements, the ACC could not locate any more undisclosed property of the accused. Under the circumstances, the appellant cannot be held liable for concealment of fact or making untrue statement because of some omissions and incorrect facts and figures given in the 1st statement of wealth.

53.         The learned trial Judge acquitted accused 2-4, wife and two sons respectively of the appellant and held him guilty for commission of offence under section 26(2) of the Act V of 2004 read with rule 15 Gha (5) of the Rules, 2007 for concealing Taka 2,24,000/- in mentioning the price of Mercedes Benz car, Taka 2,38,520/- in the price of Nissan pick-up and further held him guilty under section 27(1) of the Act V of 2004 read with section 5(2) of the Act II of 1947 for holding Flat No. 6-J. House No. 20, Road No. 129, Gulshan, Dhaka in the name of his wife and 3619 number of right shares in NCCBL in the name of his son Rezviul Ahsan. Whereas it is apparent on the face of record that the flat at Gulshan, Mercedes Benz car and Nissan pick-up belonged to accused 2 Majeda Ahsan, and 3619 right shares in NCCBL to accused 4 Rezviul Ahsan.

54.         Mr. Hossain then refers to section 5(3) of the Land Reform Ordinance, 1984 (Ordinance, 1984) and submits that if any property is registered in the name of any person, it shall be presumed that such person is the real owner of the property. In the present case all the confiscated properties were in the names of accused 2 and 4, and the prosecution failed to rebut the presumption of their ownership over the said properties. The learned trial Judge under misconception of law shifted the onus of proof on the appellant and most illegally held him guilty. In order to substantiate his submission on this point, Mr. Hossain refers to S.N Kabir Vs Fateman Begum and others 66 DLR (AD) 193 and Jay Dayal Poddar (deceased) through l.Rs and another vs Mst. Bibi Hazra and others. AIR 1974 SC 171.

55.         Mr. Hossain further submits that none of the prosecution witness stated that the flat at Gulshan, car and pick-up belonged to the appellant. It is rather evident from record that the flat, car and pick-up belonged to accused 2 Majeda Ahsan, who was acquitted by the trial court. It is also evident from the deposition of PW 39 read with exhibit-20 and that  of DW 1 that down payment against the flat was made from the account of SBEL, a private company having its own and known sources of income and accused 2 Majeda Ahsan was one of the shareholders and directors of the company and as such it cannot be said that the source of fund for purchasing the flat was illegal money. Similarly the shares in NCCBL, belonged to accused 4 Rezviul Ahsan, who was also acquitted. Both the accused 2 Majeda Ahsan and accused 4 Rezviul Ahsan were are tax payers and they themselves signed their statements  of wealth as well as the revised statements and submitted the same to ACC stating that the flat, car, pick-up and shares respectively belonged to them and the prosecution failed to disprove the same and as such there is no scope to convict the appellant on the ground that the appellant concealed any fact in respect of the flat, vehicles and shares or that those were acquired by his illegal means.

56.         Mr. Hossain also submits that after promulgation of emergency on 11.01.2007, the joint forces arrested the appellant on 03.02.2007 from his residence at Dhaka and thereafter he was put in detention under the Special Powers Act. While in detention, the Secretary of ACC issued a notice dated 18.02.2007 under sections 18 and 26 of the Act V of 2004 and served it upon him in prison asking him to submit the statement of wealth within 72 hours. Whereas all the Commissioners of ACC had resigned from office on 07.02.2007 and the Commission was reconstituted on 24.02.2007 and as such there was no Commission existing on 18.02.2007 within the meaning of section 3 read with section 5 of the Act. The Secretary issued the notice on his own without any satisfaction and decision of the Commission. It was a clear violation of section 26 of the Act. The authority on realising the error, tried to cover it up by inserting sub-section (2) in section 18 of the Act on 18.04.2004 by Ordinance No. 7 of 2007, which provides ex-post facto ratification of any acts done by the officials of ACC during the period from 07.02.2007 to 24.02.2007. But the question of jurisdiction goes to the root of the case. If any person acts beyond his authority and to the prejudice of any other person, such acts cannot be ratified or validated by post facto approval. The action would remain void. The notice dated 18.02.2017 issued by the Secretary of ACC was not issued on behalf of the Commission as envisaged under section 26 of the Act V of 2004, which provides that if the Commission is satisfied that a person has in his possession property beyond his legal/known source of income and acquired the same by illegal means, it can direct the person to submit descriptions of all his assets and liabilities by an order in writing. This section unmistakably provides that before issuance of such notice the Commission must be ‘satisfied’ that the person owns or is in possession of the property beyond his known legal source of income and has acquired it by illegal means. The satisfaction must be of the Commission itself, not of any other person or any of its officials. The relevant order to submit statement of wealth may be signed and sent by any of its officials, but the decision to issue such order must be taken by the Commission. For all the reasons the notice was without jurisdiction and void abinitio and initiation of the present case on the basis of such notice was also without jurisdiction.

57.         Mr. Hossain as s second line of argument lastly submits that the PWD is a Government office having its distinct duty and functions. The ACC cannot delegate its distinct duty and functions to PWD to assess the value of the property belonged to an individual. It is not the duty of PWD to assess the value of such property. The conviction and sentence imposed upon the appellant on an imaginary assessment by an office, which is not legally authorized to do so, is also illegal and liable to be set aside.

58.         Mr. Md. Khurshid Alam Khan, learned Advocate for the ACC on the other hand submits that the judgment passed in the case of Dr. Mohiuddin Khan Alamgir (ibid) having been reviewed in Mudud Ahmed vs the State and another 68 DLR (AD) 118 it has got no more referral value and cannot be considered for taking decision in any others case. Admittedly the appellant furnished a revised statement amending his earlier statement of wealth, which itself proved falsehood of the statement. This is good enough to convict him under section 26 (2) of the Act V of 2004. Mr. Khan refers to the relevant part of the impugned judgment as well as the evidence of PWs 1, 39, 49 and that of DW 1 and submits that it is apparent on the face of record that accused 2 Majeda Ahsan was a name lender to her husband. She was a simple housewife and whatever she owned/possessed actually belonged to her husband. Section 5(3) of the Ordinance, 1984 was incorporated for the purpose of maintaining the ceiling of land as prescribed in law and it is applicable for agricultural land in rural area only. But section 27 of the Act V of 2004 says that if a person in his own name or in the name of others holds property, which is disproportionate to his known/legal source of income and there are reasons to presume that such property is acquired by illegal means. He would be liable for commission of offence under the law and such property would be confiscated to the State. The latter being subsequent special law would prevail, especially when the subsequent special law is of criminal nature and its purpose is to combat corruption.

59.         Mr. Khan referring to State vs Faisal Morshed Khan and another 66 DLR (AD) 236 further submits that it has already been settled by our Appellate Division that “there may be a situation when there is no assessment of valuation by any competent authority of the  Government exercising power on that behalf and in such a case, the Anti-Corruption Commission has no other option but to take the assistance of the PWD officials in making assessment of the valuation of any property. Therefore, it cannot be said that the assessment of valuation made by the PWD officials does not have any evidentiary value in all situations.” In the present case the ACC satisfactorily projected and established that the appellant was holding/possessing property in the name of his wife and son, which was beyond his known source of income. Now the onus of proof is squarely on him (appellant) to rebut the presumption of guilt under section 27 (2) of the Act V of the 2004 read with section 7(1) of the Act XL of 1958. But he failed even to show that the property disclosed in the revised statement was consistent to his known source of income. Referring to the evidence of DW 1 Mr. Khan further submits that when accused 1 admits that he himself purchased the apartment at Gulshan and the Mercedes Benz car for his wife, the question of shifting its ownership to accused 2 does not arise. This contention gets support also from the conduct of the co-accused themselves. Their properties were confederated long back in 2007, but they have not preferred any appeal against the judgment and order, so far it relates to confiscation of their properties, whereas the trial was held in their presence.

60.         The learned Judge of the trial Court attentively, meticulously and intelligently discussed and considered each and every piece of evidence, discarded those which were unfounded, but accepted the credible evidences and arrived at a finding that the appellant was guilty of offence under the relevant provisions of law in respect of the confiscated properties, which is based on evidence and appears to be rational. There is no reason to disbelieve the evidence in respect of the confiscated properties. The appeal is, therefore, liable to be dismissed.

61.         Mr. Nazibur Rahman, learned Deputy Attorney General appearing for the State adopts the submissions advanced by Mr. Khan and supplements that in a case under section 27 of the Act V of 2004, the duty of ACC is only to raise a believable allegation of holding property against a person, which is disproportionate to his known/legal source of income and acquired by illegal means. After bringing such allegation and framing of charge thereon by the Court if the person fails to prove the contrary, he would be liable for commission of the offence. In the present case, the appellant hopelessly failed to prove contrary to the presumption of guilt that was established against him under section 27(2) of the Act V of 2004 read with section 7 (1) of the Act XL of 1958. The trial Judge rightly convicted and sentence him and there is nothing wrong to interfere with.

62.         We have considered the submissions of the learned Advocates of both the sides and gone through the evidence and other materials on record. It appears that charge was framed against all the accused including the appellant under sections 26 (2) and 27 (1) of the Act V of 2004 read with section 5(2) of the Act II of 1947, but the manner of occurrence so far it relates to false statement or concealment of wealth in either statement was not particularly mentioned. We also bring it into the notice of the appellant’s learned Advocate that whether a major property like the house at Gunaighar, Debidwar can be mistakenly omitted in the statement of wealth and whether this omission constitutes an offence under section 26(2) of the Act. In response thereto Mr. Hossain submits that actually it was an ejmali property and at the time to furnishing the revised statement he could not realize that this information should also be incorporated in the statement. The trial Judge, however, acquitted him of charge on that property. There is no scope to convert that acquittal on the fine argument of Mr. Khan, when the ACC did not prefer any appeal against such acquittal.

         In Anti-Corruption  Commission vs. Dr. Mohiuddin Khan Alamgir, 62 DLR (AD) 290 the Appellant Division unanimously observed:

         “44. Besides the notice dated 18.02.2007 was not a notice required by law, the notice directed the respondent No. 1, a detenu, to submit return of his assets within a period of 72 hours, is itself a worst example of arbitrary action on the part of the concerned authority. A notice must allow a reasonable time to check up the details of the assets of a person, if necessary, on examination of his records and after consultation with his lawyers and other concerned persons. Section 26 certainly does not envisage a notice upon a person who is in detention and he is not expected to give any details of his assets within the time specified. The person concerned must be afforded a fair and reasonable opportunity to respond to the notice, otherwise, it is no notice in the eye of law. A notice issued under section 26 of the Act to a detenu, away from his hearth and home cannot be said to be a fair and bonafide exercise of power.

63.         In the recent case of Moudud Ahmed vs the State and another 68 DLR (AD) 118 heard and disposed of with some other cases in a bunch, the Appellate Division observed:

         “61. Having meticulously examined the judgment reported in 62 DLR (AD) 290 we find that the observations made in Para 44 started with the word “ Besides” which according to Chambers Twentieth Century Dictionary means “ in addition” or “ moreover”  or “over and above” and according to Oxford Advanced Learner’s Dictionary means “ in addition to ” or used for making an extra comment that adds to what you have just said” In view of the foregoing discussions and findings the following observations made by this Division in paragraph nos. 44 and 45 were not necessary for the disposal of the Criminal Petition for Leave to Appeal No. 398 of 2009 rather it is a passing comment and they do not relate to the material facts in issue and no issue on such point of law was ever raised, deliberated upon and decided both in the trial court as well as in the High Court Division, moreover, in this Division no such issue was raised and deliberated upon and as such the following observations not being at all relevant in the disposal of the case on merit are obiter dictum not ratio decidendi of the case .“ (emphasis supplied)

64.         Although the observation made by the Appellate Division in 62 DLR (AD) 290 has been reviewed to some extent, it did not discard, overrule or set aside its observation made in the above quoted paragraph 44 of the same. It has, therefore, still got a strong persuasive value and can be considered in deciding any other case, where it would be necessary to dissolve the point in controversy.

65.         A notice served upon an imprisoned person under section 26 (1) of the Act V of 2004 giving him only 72 hours for furnishing statement of wealth is not workable. We should say it is impossible to comply with even for a free man. If a free man after getting sufficient and reasonable time files an statement of wealth, still there might be some unintentional mistakes, arithmetical and typographical error. If such notice recipient by a subsequent statement satisfactorily accounts that the mistakes were really unintentional and bonafide, why the ACC should be reluctant to accept it? This cannot be the purpose of law to prosecute a person taking advantage of some unintentional mistakes. But whether an amended/ supplementary/revised statement should be accepted by ACC, would depend on the fact and circumstance of a particular case. It cannot be mechanically accepted in each and every case as a routine work. In the present case, the notice was served upon the appellant in prison at a place away from home giving him only 72 hours to respond. This is correct that in the case of Moudud Ahmed (ibid) the observations of the Appellate Division made earlier in 62 DLR (AD) 290 as quoted above, has been held to be obiter dicta, but have not been overruled, set aside, discarded or deleted. Obiter dicta are actually remarks/opinion of the Court having strong persuasive and academic value, but not a decisive factor or necessary part of the judgment passed on the case at hand. Obiter dicta include, but are not limited to works introduced by way of illustration, or analogy or argument. Unlike ratio decidendi, obiter dicta are not the subject of judicial decision, even if they happen to be correct statements/interpretations of law. It does not mean that the rational comes out from such remarks/opinion would not be cited as a good ground for taking decision in any other fit and appropriate case, where that particular point is raised and debated. The court can always take aid of the principle canvassed in the obiter dicta in a fit case, though not bound to follow it, It is therefore, difficult to accept the submission of Mr. Khan that the observation made in Dr. Mohiuddin Khan Alamgir’s case (ibid) cannot be taken into consideration because of subsequent observation of the Appellate Division. Mr. Khan’s submission that by filing the subsequent revised statement of wealth the appellant himself proved the offence under section 26(2) of the Act V of 2004 is equally not acceptable.

66.         However, in the present case the appellant acted upon the notice by furnishing statement of wealth within the given time and subsequently furnished a revised statement of wealth in order to make correction to his 1st statement. The ACC accepted the revised statement and considered it in submitting the investigation report. Because of acceptance of his revised statement by the ACC, there would be no more grievances on his part against the absurd time frame given in the notice. It also appears from the case of Muded Ahmed (ibid) that the ACC gave ex-post facto approval to the notice dated 18.02.2007. For all the reasons, we do not think that legality of the notice still remains as a point to be decided in the present case.

67.         The Ordinance, 1984 is a law of civil nature and was passed to reform the land tenure, land holding and transfer. Section 5(3) thereof was incorporated in the statute actually for maintaining ceiling of land by prohibiting benami transaction irrespective of its rural or urban character.

68.         In S N Kabir vs Fatema Begum 66 DLR (AD) 193 it has been observed that benami transactions crop up multifarious litigations and the corrupt persons take its advantage for concealment of property acquired by illegal means.

69.         Section 27 of the Act V of 2004 is a subsequent special law of criminal nature, which provides punishment for holding property acquired by illegal means in one’s name or that of others for his own benefit. It is also compatible with the purpose of section 5 (3) of the Ordinance, 1984 as observed in S N Kabir’s case (ibid). In any view of the matter, section 5(3) of the Ordinance shall no override the provision of section 27 of the Act. We find it difficult to accept the submission of Mr. Hossain, learned Advocate for the appellant that any evidence supporting benami transfer shall be kept out of consideration even in a case of anti-corruption.

70.         The submission advanced by Mr. Khan that section 5 of the Ordinance, 1984 would apply only in the case of agricultural land in rural area has also been discarded in the S N Kabir’s case (ibid) as under.

         “There is no scope for reading the words ‘rural area’ in section 5 of the Ordinance. The preamble cannot be used to control the enactments themselves where they are expressed in clear and unambiguous terms. The aid of preamble can only be taken when the meanings of the words to be interpreted are not clear and unambiguous. The words ‘immovable property’ occurring in section 5 of the Ordinance include both agricultural and non-agricultural properties. There is no scope for encroaching upon the domain of legislature by importing the words ‘rural area’ in section 5 and additional of such word will amount to legislation by the Judiciary which is not at all permissible.”

71.         In State vs Faisal Morshed Khan and another 66 DLR (AD) 236 the Appellate Division already held that if there was no assessment of valuation by any competent authority of the Government exercising power in that behalf and in such a case, the Anti-Corruption Commission had no other option but to take the assistance of the PWD officials in making assessment of the valuation of any property. It is therefore, difficult to accept the submission made by Mr. Hossain that the PWD has no authority to assess the value of any property related to an anti-corruption case.

72.         Let us now examine whether the learned trial Judge was legally correct in convicting the appellant and awarding sentence upon him for holding the properties as mentioned in the schedule of the judgment.

73.         It was alleged in the FIR that information about the flat at Gulshan was not disclosed by accused 2 Majeda Ahsan in her 1st statement of wealth. Down payment of Taka twenty-two lac against the flat was paid from the current account (A/C No. 2737) of the appellant maintained with Rupali Bank. The flat was initially booked in the name of Masuma Haque, but finally purchased in the name of Majeda Ahsan. The appellant showed this flat in his wife’s name to conceal his illegal property. In charge sheet the IO contended that there was no scope under the law to acquire personal property by taking loan from any company and as such the explanation of the accused about acquisition of this property was not acceptable. It was also mentioned in the charge sheet that after considering the revised statement, this property was found to have been acquired by illegal means.

74.         The evidence of PWs 1 18-20, 39 and 49 were relevant in respect of ownership of the flat. It appears that PW 1, the informant stated that it was purchased from Amin Mohammad Foundation by accused 2 in the name of her sister Masuma Haque. Down payment of Taka twenty-two lac was paid by the appellant from his current account No. 2737 maintained with Rupali Bank Ltd, Ibrahim Mansion Branch. In cross-examination he denied the defence suggestion that the account with Rupali Bank was maintained by the company.

75.         PW 18 Zahura Akter, Manager of Credit Division, Amin Mohammad Foundation stated that the flat was initially booked in the name of Masuma Haque and ultimately transferred in the name of Majeda Ahsan. She did not say that the flat belonged to or purchased by accused 1. The documents seized from PW 18 (material exhibit-III) in presence of PW’s 19-20 also do not show that the appellant as an individual was its owner or pay master. PW 39 M A Wahab, an Officer of Rupali Bank Ltd stated that initial payment of Taka twenty-two lac was paid from current account No. 2737 of SBEL, maintained with Rupali Bank Ltd. Ibrahim Mansion Branch.

76.         PW 49 Md. Akter Hossain, IO of the case stated that the flat belonged to accused 2 and initially it was purchased in the name of her sister Masuma Haque. Down payment of Taka twenty-two lac against the flat was paid from the account of the appellant (A/C No. 2737, Rupali Bank Ltd.). He (PW 49) further stated that the appellant actually purchased it in the name of Majeda Ahsan and gave false information to ACC and concealed property worth Taka 55,98,927/-. In cross-examination he (PW 49) admitted that accused 2-4 disclosed all their properties in their revised statements of wealth, and that after considering the revised statements of wealth he did not find any undisclosed property.

77.         It appears that accused 2 in her revised statement of wealth (exhibit-23) disclosed Taka 54,30,695/- as price of the flat at Gulshan and stated that the amount was paid from the account of SBEL and other accounts. The flat was initially booked in the name of her sister Masuma Haque and subsequently it was registered in her name. The loan of Masuma Haque was being paid from rent of the flat.

78.         DW 1 Monjurul Ahsan Munshi (appellant herein) in his deposition mentioned Taka 54,30,695/- as price of the flat and stated that it was paid from the account of SBEL. Initially it was booked in the name of Masuma Haque, but according to her (Masuam Haque’s) desire it was ultimately transferred in the name of accused 2.

In the charge sheet it was found that according to BRTA documents its actual price was Taka 40,24,000/- and as such she concealed Taka 2,24,000/- in mentioning the price and submitted false information in her statement of wealth.

80.         The prosecution witnesses did not depose that the car belonged to the appellant or it was disproportionate to his known source of income or was acquired by illegal means or that he concealed the same. Among the witnesses, PW 1 did not say anything about the car. PWs 3 and 4, two officials of BRTA exhibited the documents related to the car, but did not say that the appellant owned the car. The documents seized from PW 3 in presence of PW 4 also do not show his ownership over the vehicle. PW 49, IO of the case stated that accused 2 Majeda Ahsan in her statement of wealth declared that the Mercedes Benz car was purchased at Taka 38,00,000/-, but it appeared from BRTA documents that its actual price was 40,24,000/- and as such she concealed information of Taka 2,24,000/- in mentioning the price. In cross-examination, he (PW 49) stated that the variation caused due to additional of Tax and Vat.

81.         In the revised statement she declared the car as her own, which she purchased at Taka 38,00,000/-. The appellant in his deposition as DW 1 supported the said statement and exhibited documents in support of the bank loan, account statements and resolution of the board of directors of the company.

82.         It was also alleged in the FIR that according to the statement of wealth, accused 2 Majeda Ahsan had purchased a Nissan pick-up for consideration of Taka 15,00,000/- taking loan of Taka 10,00,0000/- from bank and Taka 5,00,000/- from her own source. It was mentioned in the charge sheet that according to the documents of BRTA, its actual price was Taka 17,38,520/- and as such she concealed Taka 2,38,520 in mentioning the price, and furnished false information in her statement of wealth. None of the prosecution witnesses deposed that the pick-up belonged to the appellant or that he concealed any information. PW 1 did not say anything about the pick-up. PWs 3 and 4, two officials of BRTA deposed in the manner already stated. PW 49 deposed in support of the prosecution case to the extent of concealment of Taka 2,38,520/- in mentioning the price, but in cross-examination stated that the variation caused due to addition of Tax and Vat.

83.         In the revised statement of wealth accused 2 declared the pick-up as her own, which she purchased at Taka 15,00,000/-. The appellant in his deposition as DW 1 supported the said statement and exhibited documents in support of loan, account statement, resolution of the board etcetera.

84.         The FIR further disclosed that accused 4 Rezviul Ahsan was the owner of 4750 shares in NCBBL valuing Taka 4,75,000,00/- and at the material time he was holding 1878 shares, which was not disclosed in his statement of wealth. In the chare sheet it was stated that after collection of documents from NCCBL, it was found that on 29.12.1999 accused 4 purchased 4750 shares of NCCBL for consideration of Taka 4,75,000/-. He further paid Taka 3,61,900/- on 20.12.2005 for purchasing 3619 right shares of it. His total investment in respect of the said shares thus stood Taka 8,36,900/-, which he did not disclose in the statement of wealth. It was further stated in the charge sheet that after considering the revised statement, of wealth, it was found that 1817 shares valuing Taka 1,81,700/- were disclosed, but in fact he was holding shares of Taka 8,36,900 (IPD 4750 + right share 3619). The shares of Taka 6,55,200/- were thus concealed and acquired by illegal means.

85.         None of the prosecution witnesses stated that the shares in NCCBL belonged to the appellant or those were disproportionate to his known sources of income, or were acquired by illegal means. The evidences of PWs 1  33-35 and 49 are relevant in respect of the above said shares. Of them PW 1 Abu Sayed, the informant deposed that the shares valuing Taka 1,81,700/- were not disclosed in the statement of wealth furnished by accused 4 and he got information about those shares during inquiry. In cross-examination he disclosed his ignorance about the revised statement of wealth filed by accused 4. PW 33 did not say that the shares were owned or purchased by the appellant. He rather deposed indicating accused 4 to be the owner of those shares. The documents seized in presence of PWs 34 and 35 also do not connect him with its ownership. PW 49 with reference to the documents of NCCBL, deposed that accused 4 purchased its 4750 shares worth Taka 4,75,000/-. He (accused 4) paid Taka 3,61,900/- to NCCBL, for purchasing 3691 right shares. His total investment against the shares was Taka 8,36,900/-, which he did not disclose in his statement of wealth. In cross-examination he (PW 49) stated that he did not inquire about accused 4’s BO account and also did not seize or suspend the account. He denied the suggestion that if he had considered the BO account, would have found that accused 4 gave correct information in the statement of wealth about the said shares.

86.         Accused 4 Rezviul Ahsan himself signed the revised statement and submitted it to ACC stating that in the year 1999 he had purchased 5000 shares of NCCBL and subsequently sold some shares therefrom. The fund for purchasing the shares was taken from the company’s account.

87.         The appellant as DW 1 proved the documents, papers and report related to the shares in NCCBL as exhibit-4. In cross-examination, he stated that the shares were owned by accused 4 and their company paid money for purchasing the shares.

88.         From a total reading of the evidence and other materials on record, it appears that a major part of the consideration for purchasing the flat at Gulshan was paid from the current account of SBEL. The appellant was the Managing Director and the co-accused were directors and shares holders of the company. But it does not appear from any evidence that the appellant in the capacity of a private individual provided accused 2 and 4 with the money for acquitting the properties in their benami.

89.         The accused 2 and 4 were major tax payer, income earner and maintained their income tax files. They furnished their respective statements of wealth stating the properties to be of their own. If the properties were disproportionate to their known/legal sources of income and acquired by illegal means, they were supposed to explain it during the trial held in their presence. At the same time the ACC was also to show and prove that the appellant acquired the said properties in benami by illegal means and it was disproportionate to his known/legal source of income as the ACC itself claimed so. The trial Judge did not convict the accused 2 and 4 even for abating the appellant in owning/possessing the properties in their benami and acquitted them honorably. The ACC did not challenge the said order of acquittal and thus accepted the verdict.

90.         It further appears that learned Judge o the trial Court relied on the oral evidence of DW1  (appellant) and proceeded on the assumption that he himself that he himself purchased the flat, car and shares in the names of his wife and son. In so doing the learned Judge did not consider the above said documentary evidences, which speak of providing money from the account of the company and that the appellant was the Managing Director thereof. One’s oral evidence should not be considered in isolation and without keeping in mind his way of speaking. There are so many persons who sitting at the helm of affairs of a public office describe it as ‘his office’ or any duty performed by the office to have been done by him. It does never mean that any public office belongs to a private individual or to a particular public servant. Similarly we find Managing Director/Chief Executive of many companies, who describes the company as ‘his company’ and a procurement done by the company to have been procured by ‘him’. A company is always a different entity and a juristic person. The appellant being an individual and Managing Director of SBEL cannot merge into a single entity. We are, therefore, unable to accept that the appellant as an individual purchased the properties and is liable for owning/possessing the same in the names of wife and son, or for concealment of its value. This is correct that he failed to produce any decision of the company to lend money to accused 2 for purchasing the flat at Gulshan. If any money is illegally shifted from a company’s fund, it may at best constitute an offence of money laundering as defined in section 2 (Ttha) (Aa) of the Money Laundering Act, 2002 but cannot constitute an offence under section 27 of the Act V of 2004. It would also contribute in an illegality/irregularity under the Company Act and the other directors/share holders aggrieved thereby can raise the question in proper forum.

91.         The learned Judge committed further wrong in interpreting the word ‘abetment’ holding that in the present case it did not occur before or during continuance of the occurrence. Actually an offence under section 27 of the Act V of 2004 continues so long the person sought to be prosecuted owns possesses the disproportionate property as defined therein. An abetment thereto also continues in the same way with his ownership/possession over the same. There is no scope to say after acquiring the disproportionate property, the time of occurrences comes to an end.

92.         It is also to be kept in mind that evasion of income tax and offence of acquiring/ possessing/owning disproportionate property by illegal means as defined in section 27 of the Act V of 2004 are not the same. There might be some professionals/businessmen who legally earn a lot of money, but pay less income tax. It is an offence of evasion of tax or suppression of facts under the Income Tax Ordinance, 1984. But if any public servant or and individual acquires property by illegal means, which is apparently disproportionate to his known/legal source of income and fails to satisfactorily account such property, an offence under section 27 of the Act V of 2004 would be committed.

93.         In the present case, the four items of properties upon which the appellant was convicted, were in the names of accused 2 and 4. We have already discussed that the ACC failed to prove that the appellant as an individual acquired those properties for his own benefit, in benami of his son and wife and by illegal means, In such a case, we find no reason to convicted him alone for owning/ possessing the properties, which in official documents belong to the co-accused, especially when the co-accused themselves claim it to be of their own. We are, therefore, not inclined to maintain his conviction and sentence.

94.         The appeal thus merits consideration and accordingly, it is allowed. The impugned judgment and order dated 09.10.2007 passed by the Special Judge, Court No. 5, Dhaka in Special Case No. 04 of 2007 arising out of Gulshan Police Station Case No. 101(3) 07 corresponding to GR No. 24 of 2007 is set aside.

95.         Send down to lower courts record.
