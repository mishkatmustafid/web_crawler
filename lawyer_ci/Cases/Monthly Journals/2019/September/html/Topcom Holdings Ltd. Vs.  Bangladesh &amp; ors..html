Code of Civil Procedure (V of 1908)

Order 39, Rule 1

After institution of the suit, when it would appear to the Court that if the temporary injunction is not granted, the suit itself may be infructuous or great hardship and prejudice may cause to the party, then, in order to keep the subject matter of the suit intact or for the purpose of preserving the rights of the parties, temporary injunction may be granted for a limited period or till the disposal of the suit. The cardinal principles for granting temporary injunction are that the applicant must satisfy the Court that (i) there is an arguable prima facie case, (ii) balance of inconvenience is in favour of the applicant and (iii) the applicant shall suffer irreparable loss and injury not commensurable in monetary terms unless the other party is restrained. It is a well-settled principle that granting or vacating the temporary injunction is a discretionary power of any Court, and a discretionary power is required to be exercised only in favour of those litigants who come before the Courts with clean hands.          ...(16 and 47)

Interpretation of Statute.

What is lien:

‘Lien’ is a right of retaining any property, goods and money against the service rendered by any skilled/unskilled professional, or in exchange of or against the sale of any movable/immovable property. A lien is the right to retain property belonging to a debtor until he has discharged a debt due to the retainer of the property.     ...(25 and 29)

Interpretation of Statute.

What is Maritime claim:

When a ‘common law lien’ is created in connection with the dealings of a vessel, it may give rise to a ‘maritime claim’.                                                . . . (31)

Interpretation of Statute.

What is Maritime lien:

A lien on a vessel, given to secure the claim of a creditor who provided maritime services to the vessel or who suffered an injury from the vessel’s use. -Also termed tacit hypothecation. Although all ‘maritime liens’ are ‘maritime claims’, but all ‘maritime claims’ are not ‘maritime liens’. ‘Maritime lien’ is an important aspect of maritime law with the liability of contract that falls on ship and ships’ equipment. In other words, it is a privileged claim upon a ship for service rendered to, or injuries caused by the ship.       . . . (33 and 36)

Words and Phrases

Duties of an Advocate.

The learned members of the Bar that they, being the integral part of the Judiciary, should be more interested to dispose of the substantive matters pending before the Courts than showing their eagerness in obtaining the interim relief. Particularly, when any Court suggests that the substantive matter shall be heard on fast-track basis, the concerned Advocates should understand that while on the one side, the parties of the suit/case would get the aspired remedy, which would obviously include the interim relief, on the other, the Court would be able to reduce the number of backlog of cases. The learned Advocates should take into consideration the valuable time of the Courts that requires for adjudication upon the interim/interlocutory matters. Instead of compelling the Courts to spend time in dealing with interlocutory matters, if the learned Advocates extend their co-operation to dispose of the substantive matter, the Judiciary will be able, to some extent, to cope with the prevailing backlog.    . . .(54)

JUDGMENT

Muhammad Khurshid Alam Sarkar, J: By filing two separate applications, the defendant no. 2 (Director General of Food, Ministry of Food, Government of Bangladesh) and the defendant no. 5 (the PEC limited, an Indian Government entity) are praying to this Court for vacating the interim Order of Injunction passed by this Court on 28.06.2018, by which the defendant no. 2 has been restrained from making any payment to the defendant no. 3 (the Supreme Container Line, who chartered the Vessel MV RAINBOW FAIRY from the plaintiff of this suit). And, by filing another application the plaintiff is praying for temporary injunction against the defendant no. 2 restraining him to make any further payment to the defendant no. 5.

2.             The background facts of filing these applications are that Topcom Holdings Limited, who is the disponent owner of the vessel M.V. RAINBOW FAIRY, as the plaintiff, filed the instant Admiralty Suit against the defendants for realization of its outstanding freight/hire under the time-charterparty dated 18.01.2018 for an amount of USD 274,677.98, equivalent to BDT 2,32,10,289.31. At the time of admission of the suit on 28.06.2018, the plaintiff also filed an application for temporary injunction restraining the defendant no. 2 from making any further payment of the outstanding amount of freight to the defendant no. 3 till disposal of the instant suit. After hearing the aforesaid application, the same was allowed. Now, the defendant nos. 2 & 5 have come up with their respective applications for vacating the said Order of Injunction and the plaintiff has filed an application for modification of the Order dated 28.06.2018 to the effect that defendant nos. 2 & 8 shall be restrained from making any payment to the defendant no. 5 (i.e. at the time of admission, Injunction Order was passed against the defendant no. 3, now the plaintiff prays for passing the same Order against the defendant no. 5, instead of the defendant no. 3).

3.             Factual averments made by the respondent nos. 2 & 5.

         In the applications filed by the defendant nos. 2 & 5, it is stated, inter alia, that under the Government to Government (G to G) negotiations, contract no. DGF/PEC-01 dated 25.10.2017 was signed between the DG, Food, Government of Bangladesh (defendant no. 2) and the PEC Limited, an enterprise of the Government of India (defendant no. 5) to purchase 100000 Metric Tons (mt) non-basmati parboiled rice from India; that  pursuant thereto, the Letter of Credit (L/C) No. 033017010569 was opened by the defendant no. 2 with Sonali Bank Limited (defendant no. 8) on 01.11.2017 in favour of the defendant no. 5; that Bill of Lading was signed for and on behalf of the vessel M. V. RAINBOW FAIRY; that the freight has been paid in advance; that as per the contract dated 25.10.2017 signed by the Bangladesh Government and the Indian exporter, the ship (the plaintiff) with the rice arrived at the Chittagong Port on 23.05.2018 and after completing the formalities with the customs and other authorities, offloading of rice started on 02.06.2018 and ended on 21.06.2018 and, thereafter, the vessel sailed out from the Chittagong Port on 25.06.2018.

4.             It is further stated that the plaintiff did not submit the original copy of the charterparty or any certified copy thereof before this Court, the plaintiff has merely submitted a printed copy of the Form NYPE/93; that through the addendum no. 1, the plaintiff seeks to show that there is existence of the charterparty dated 18.01.2018, but it relates to a different vessel named M V ORCHID; that for the sake of argument, even if the said charterparty is taken to be for the vessel MV RAINBOW FAIRY, the said charterparty dated 18.01.2018 provides a “guarantee clause” of the performance of the defendant no. 3 and condition no. 6 of its addendum no. 1 provides that the disponent owner (the plaintiff) does not have any objections if the Supreme Container Line (defendant no. 3) receives freight payment on the basis of services performed.

5.             It is further stated that although only 5% of the total L/C money amounting to USD 262,762 currently is due to be paid through the L/C opening Bank, but this Court’s Order has stopped further exports and imports between the Bangladesh Government and the said Indian Government’s enterprise; that the defendant no. 5 is not being allowed to enter into new business/contract with the Bangladesh Government; that due to the Order of Injunction passed by this Court, the defendant no. 5 is to pay 12% interest as per the Rules framed by the Central Bank of India; that the defendant no. 5 failed to supply 2,00,000 mts of rice to Bangladesh as the Indian Bank did not provide loan facility because of the fact that the previous loan has not yet been adjusted and, consequently, the defendant no. 5 has been bearing interest incurred thereon on top of losing new business; that they have sent e-Mail and letter by registered post to the address of the defendant no. 3 with a request to contest this suit so that they may be relieved of the hassle they are facing in dealing with this suit owing to the alleged fault of the defendant no. 3, but till date they have not heard anything from the defendant no. 3. 

6.             Factual statements of the plaintiff:

         In the Written Objection filed by the plaintiff, it is stated that although the Fixture Note dated 18.01.2018 mentions the name of MV ORCHID as the performing vessel, however, the addendum no. 4 dated 19.04.2018 clearly shows that the above-named MV ORCHID was replaced by the plaintiff-vessel MV RAINBOW FAIRY as the performing vessel; that the plaintiff has  not been paid off its freight by the defendant no. 3; that the claim of the plaintiff requires to be either admitted or denied by the defendant no. 3 who is yet to appear before this Court.

7.             Submissions advanced and the arguments made by the learned Advocates for the respondent nos. 2 & 5:

         Placing the provisions of Section 5(1)(a) of the Admiralty Court Act, 2000 (shortly, the Admiralty Court Act) before this Court and, side by side, taking me through the Fixture Note and the Four Addenda thereto, the learned Advocate for the defendant no. 2, Mr. Md Syed Hasan Zubair and the learned Advocate for the defendant no. 5, Mr. Ashadullah, at the very outset submit that the factual contentions of these two defendants being the same, their submissions may be recorded as joint submissions. They, thus, jointly submit that in the light of the fact that Fixture Note with the four addenda were executed in India between the plaintiff and the defendant no. 3 and since the main grievance and allegation is only against the defendant no. 3 and, further, given the fact that the place of business of the defendant no. 3 is in Mumbai, India, the jurisdiction of the suit falls outside Bangladesh territory as per Section 5(1)(a) [aviv 5(1)(K)] of the Admiralty Court Act. They strenuously submit that the charterparty having been executed outside Bangladesh territory between two foreign parties, namely, the plaintiff and the defendant no. 3, and the other defendants of the suit admittedly being not party to the said charterparty, they were illegally made defendants to the suit by the plaintiff with a motive to make them scapegoat, so that the defendant nos. 2 & 5 become compelled to contact the defendant no. 3 towards ensuring the presence of the defendant no. 3 before this Court. They submit that since the defendant no. 5 has paid off the freight in advance to the defendant no. 3, the outstanding 5% L/C money lying with the defendant no. 2 is the payment against the rice purchased by the defendant no. 2 from the defendant no. 5. They argue that had it been the scenario that the defendant no. 5 would pay the freight to the defendant no. 3 out of this 5% outstanding L/C money, the plaintiff would have been justified to seek Order of Restraint upon the outstanding 5% money.

8.             They submit that since it is evident that the Form NYPE/93, which has been produced before this Court, was not executed by the aforesaid parties to the contract, it is not a contract at all and, thus, not binding upon the defendants of the suit. They further submit that, even for the sake of argument if the template of the Form NYPE/93 is taken to be a valid one, since the Clause No. 40 of the said prescribed Form NYPE/93 provides for arbitration in case of any dispute arising out of the said contract, the plaintiff is required to approach the arbitration forum and, hence, the present suit is not maintainable. They next submit that the contract was signed between Bangladesh and India for export and import of rice, which is the commercial dealing between Bangladesh and India, and the Order of Injunction in fact restrained the performance of international contractual obligations arising out of international trade. They submit that the Order of Injunction virtually stopped the payment of L/C money relating to international trade and non-payment of L/C money may cause serious repercussions on the international trade of the country inasmuch as payment of the L/C money cannot be stopped by way of injunction as per the provisions of various International Banking and Trading Laws, such as UCP-600, UCC, URC-522 and ISBP (International Standard Banking Practice).

9.             They submit that the ‘maritime lien’ contemplates against ship/vessel; not against the freight or damages and, that is why, ‘maritime lien’ compels the ship for payment of a claim founded upon the maritime law when a judicial proceeding is taken under the Admiralty Court Act against the vessel. He submits that since the vessel M V RAINBOW FAIRY left the Chittagong Port on 25.06.2018 after discharging the rice as per the Bill of Lading, and since the plaintiff filed the suit for charges and damages asserting the same as the ‘maritime lien’, institution of the suit for money decree in the name of the Admiralty Suit is a fraud upon the statute, as apparently the present suit is cunningly filed for avoiding Court fees of a significant amount of money which would have required for institution of a Money Suit.

10.         Submissions made by the learned Advocate for the plaintiff:

         On the other hand, the learned Advocate for the plaintiff places Section 3(2)(h) [aviv 3(2)(R)] of the Admiralty Court Act and submits that since the plaintiff’s claim has arisen out of hire of its ship, the Admiralty Court is the proper forum for adjudication upon the claim of the plaintiff. Then Mr. Saifur Rashid refers to Clause 23 of the Form NYPE/93 and submits that as per Clause 23 of the aforesaid Form NYPE/93, the plaintiff has a lien on all sub-freights and sub-hires of the vessel M V RAINBOW FAIRY, if the freight of the vessel is not paid in due time, and since the plaintiff did not receive its freight in time in spite of sending repeated reminders to the defendants, the plaintiff has the right to exercise lien upon sub-freight. In support of his submissions, he refers to the case of Care Shipping Corporation Vs Latin American Shipping Corporation reported in (1983) 1 All ER 1121 and the case of Mareva Compania Naviera SA Vs International Bulk Carriers SA reported in (1975) Vol 2 Lloyd’s Rep 509, CA.

11.         He next submits that Form NYPE/93 is a standard Form for using it as the charterparty. He, in an effort to place before this Court the nitty-gritty of the Form NYPE/93, goes on to contend that NYPE stands for the “New York Produce Exchange” and the Association of Ship Brokers and agents of USA Inc has been issuing the said Form since 06.11.1913 and because of the fact that the same was issued for the last time on 14th September 1993, it is known in the maritime world as “NYPE/93”. Mr. Saifur Rashid submits that although a template of the Form NYPE/93 has been filed before this Court without producing the signed copy of the charterparty, but since it is being used by all the stakeholders of the maritime industry, including the Baltic and International Maritime Council (BIMCO) and the Federation of National Associations of Ship Brokers and Agents (FONASBA), the plaintiff and its charterers/sub-charterer have also used the template of Form NYPE/93 as the charterparty.

12.         He lastly submits that since the plaintiff has not received the freight payment from the defendant no. 3, the defendant no. 5’s claim of making advance freight payment to the defendant no. 3 requires to be proved by way of adducing evidence and, therefore, presence of all these defendants are necessary for an effective and fair adjudication of the suit. He submits that if the money is released from the lien, this suit shall be infructuous and therefore, the facts and circumstances of the case fairly demand that an Order of Temporary Injunction should be passed against the Sonali Bank, Foreign Exchange Branch the L/C opening Bank  (defendant no. 8) and the Directorate General of Food (the defendant no. 2) restraining them from making payment of the outstanding 5% L/C amount to the defendant no. 5 (PEC Limited), till the plaintiff’s outstanding amount is cleared off or till disposal of the suit and, also, the two applications, filed by the defendant nos. 2 and 5 for vacating the Order of Injunction dated 28.06.2018 be rejected, otherwise, the plaintiff shall be seriously prejudiced and shall suffer irreparable loss and injury.

13.         Upon hearing the learned Advocates for both the sides and perusal of the relevant laws, it appears to this Court that since these applications are regarding granting or vacating temporary injunction, it would be useful if it be learnt as to under what legal provisions as well as under what factual circumstances a Court grants temporary injunction and when it may be vacated/varied/ modified.

14.         Section 6 of the Admiralty Court Act and Rule 3 of the Admiralty Rules, 1912 (Admiralty Rules) make it mandatory for the litigants to comply with the provisions of the Code of the Civil Procedure, 1908 (CPC) in drawing up, subscribing and verifying a plaint and Rule 35 of the Admiralty Rules mandates this Court to resort to the CPC for execution of a decree. The Admiralty Court Act and the Admiralty Rules, however, do not contain any prohibitory provision as to application of the other Rules of the CPC; rather Rules 51 & 60 of the Admiralty Rules herald that the provisions of the CPC shall be applicable where the Admiralty Rules do not contain any Rule to cover the situation and, more so, the last part of Rule 60 of the Admiralty Rules empowers this Court to pass any Order which the Judge may think fit and proper. It leads me to conclude that subject to the provisions of the Admiralty Rules, not only the provisions of the CPC but also any other Rules and Practice are applicable in the Admiralty Court. Since the Admiralty Rules do not contain any Rule to deal with the prayer for Restraint, Status quo, Stay and other necessary interim Order/Direction, obviously the provisions of the CPC should be resorted to adjudicate upon the prayers of the parties to this suit.

15.         Having obtained nod as to taking recourse to the provisions of the CPC, now I may look at the relevant provisions of the CPC for dealing with these applications. The relevant provisions are Order 39, Rules 1 & 4, which run as follows: 

Order 39, R. 1 (1) Case in which temporary injunction may be granted. (1) Where in any suit it is proved by affidavit or otherwise-

(a)                that any property in dispute in a suit is in danger of being wasted, damaged or alienated by any party to the suit, or wrongfully sold in execution of a decree, or

(b)               that the defendant threatens, or intends, to remove or dispose of his property with a view to defraud his creditors,

            the Court may by order grant a temporary injunction to restrain such act, or make such other order for the purpose of staying and preventing the wasting, damaging, alienation, sale, removal or disposition of the property as the Court thinks fit, until the disposal of the suit or until further orders.

Order 39, R.4 Order for injunction may be discharged, varied or set aside. Any order for an injunction may be discharged, or varied, or set aside by the Court, on an application made thereto by any party dissatisfied with such order.

16.         From a plain reading of the provisions of Order 39, Rule 1, all that I understand is that after institution of the suit, when it would appear to the Court that if the temporary injunction is not granted, the suit itself may be infructuous or great hardship and prejudice may cause to the party, then, in order to keep the subject matter of the suit intact or for the purpose of preserving the rights of the parties, temporary injunction may be granted for a limited period or till the disposal of the suit. The cardinal principles for granting temporary injunction are that the applicant must satisfy the Court that (i) there is an arguable prima facie case, (ii) balance of inconvenience is in favour of the applicant and (iii) the applicant shall suffer irreparable loss and injury not commensurable in monetary terms unless the other party is restrained.

17.         And, from a plain reading of Order 39, Rule 4, it appears to me that when any party would successfully register its dissatisfaction with the Court by way of filing an application about the temporary injunction already granted, it is the discretionary power of the Court either to vacate the Injunction Order completely or to modify it. 

18.         Now, let me see whether there is a prima facie arguable case in favour of the plaintiff for granting temporary injunction restraining the defendant nos. 2 & 8 from transmitting L/C money to the defendant no. 5 and/ or continuing with the Order of Injunction granted earlier on 28.06.2018 or the same is liable to be vacated as per the prayer of these applicants (defendant nos. 2 & 5). In order to adjudicate upon the issue as to whether there is a prima facie arguable case, I would, at first, go for examination of the following issues; (i) whether the money lying with the defendant nos. 2 & 8 is payment against L/C, and whether it is for payment of price of the rice purchased from the defendant no. 5 or the said money is for payment of freight to the defendant no. 3 by the defendant no. 5, (ii) whether the defendant nos. 2, 5 & 8 have been impleaded as the defendants of the suit lawfully or illegally and (iii) whether the plaintiff is competent to claim lien over the money to be paid by the defendant no. 2 to the defendant no. 5 for purchasing the rice. Further, since it is no more a res integra that the plaintiff cannot have a prima facie case where the suit ex-facie is not maintainable, I should venture scrutiny whether on the face of the plaint and the submitted papers the suit appears to be maintainable or not. For the examination of this aspect, I need to adjudicate upon the following issues; (iv) whether this Court lacks the jurisdiction in the backdrop of incorporation of the arbitration clause in the charterparty, (v) whether there is any other efficacious forum available for the plaintiff and (vi) whether the payment of the Letter of Credit can be stopped by an Order of Injunction of the Court.

19.         Although the issue nos. i, ii and iii appear to be interlinked with each other requiring dealing with these three issues together, however, I would be taking them up serially one after another. Admittedly, the money, which is currently lying with the defendant nos. 2 & 8 is L/C money. Evidently, the disputes have arisen out of the charterparty dated 18.01.2018 executed between the plaintiff and the defendant no. 3 and, as per the claim of the plaintiff, the defendant no. 3 is responsible for making payment for using the vessel for a specified time inscribed in the charterparty. It has been contended by the plaintiff that since the plaintiff has not received the hire of the vessel from the defendant no. 3 for the period during which the vessel carried the cargos of the defendant no. 5, the defendant no. 5 should pay the hire to the plaintiff out of the money lying with defendant nos. 2 & 8, as the same being the freight payment, the plaintiff has got lien over it. The plaintiff has sought to make out a case that the money lying with the defendant nos. 2 & 8 is not only the payment against the price of the purchased-rice, but it also includes the freight payment. Per contra, it has been contended by the defendant no. 5 that it had hired the vessel from the defendant no. 3 only for a particular voyage to carry the rice from India to Bangladesh, and the defendant no. 3 had agreed to engage with the shipper (Indian company) only on the condition that before sailing out of the Indian Port, the defendant no. 3 must be paid off its freight payment in advance and, accordingly, the defendant no. 5 have paid off the freight in advance. The logic behind advance freight payment by the defendant no. 5 to the defendant no. 3 is that, since by the charterparty the plaintiff has given full authority to the defendant no. 3 to receive advance from any sub-charterer, the defendant no. 5 did not see any legal impediment to pay full freight in advance in enjoying the service of the vessel.

20.         Following their above contentious arguments on this issue, I examined the relevant papers, namely (i) the template of the charterparty, i.e the Form NYPE/93 (as the original or photocopy of the charterparty dated 18.01.2018 has not been produced before this Court by the plaintiff), (ii) Fixture Note together with its four addenda and (iii) the Bill of Lading. Since all the above papers have been submitted by the plaintiff before this Court and the defendants are also not raising any question as to the authenticity of these papers, there is no impediment for me to rely on the same to come to a primary conclusion. It transpires that (a) the charterparty was sealed between the plaintiff and the defendant no. 3 and there is no mentioning of the names of the defendant nos. 2, 5 & 8 in any connection, not to speak of stating them as the parties to the charterparty, (b) by condition no. 6 of the addendum no. 1 of the charterparty, the defendant no. 3 has been categorically authorised by the plaintiff to receive freight payment from any person during the tenure of the charterparty, (c) Bill of Lading was signed for and on behalf of the plaintiff and it states that the freight was paid in advance and the same was received on behalf of the plaintiff and (d) from the e-Mail sent by the defendant no. 3 on 10.05.2018 at 17:31:20+530 to the shipper it is evident that the defendant no. 3 has acknowledged receipt of Indian Rupees 2,31,17,663/-.

21.         Therefore, without prejudicing this Court’s final findings of the fact to be arrived at upon taking evidence, I find that the defendant no. 5, following hiring the vessel from the defendant no. 3 for a voyage from Kandla Port of India to Chittagong Port of Bangladesh, has paid off freight payment in advance relying on the condition no. 6 of the first addendum to the Fixture Note to the defendant no. 3. Although it is an admitted position that the DG, Food of Bangladesh Government (defendant no. 2) is purchasing the rice from the Indian Government entity (defendant no. 5) on the basis of Costs, Insurance and Freight (CIF), but since prima facie it appears that the defendant no. 5 has paid off the freight payment in advance as per the terms of the charterparty to the defendant no. 3, the primary view of this Court is that the money lying with the defendant nos. 2 & 8 is the money for payment of price of the rice purchased by the Bangladesh Government from the defendant no. 5, not for making payment to the defendant no. 3.

22.         Now, I need to see why the plaintiff has impleaded the defendant nos. 2, 5 & 8 as the defendants in this suit, despite the plaintiff’s own admission that the defendant nos. 2, 5 & 8 are not the parties to the contract (charterparty). The apparent reasons behind it, so far surfaced from the above discussions, are that the plaintiff believes and asserts that with this outstanding 5% L/C money, the defendant no. 5 would make the freight payment to the defendant no. 3 and since it is the belief of the plaintiff that as per clause 23 of the charterparty, he got lien over the freight payment, this money should be handed over/transferred to the plaintiff by the defendant nos. 2 & 8, instead of letting the money to be remitted to the defendant no. 3 by the defendant no. 5. In other words, since this money would ultimately go to the Bank account of the defendant no. 3 through defendant nos. 2, 8 and 5, therefore, instead of handing over this money to the defendant no. 3, it should be transferred to the Bank account of the plaintiff and, as per the plaintiff, that is why the defendant nos. 2, 5 & 8 are necessary parties to this suit. Although from the documents submitted before this Court, it primarily appears to this Court that the outstanding 5% L/C money is not going to be used for the purpose of making any payment to the defendant no. 3, however, until the claim of the advance payment of freight is required to be proved through examining the relevant witness in this Court by exhibiting the payment receipts/documents in presence of defendant no. 5, and since the source of finance of the defendant no.5 is the defendant no.2 through defendant no.8, I hold the view that the presence of these three defendants, namely, 2, 5 & 8 are required in this suit.

23.         The above resolution leads me to take up the issue as to whether the plaintiff can claim ‘lien’ or ‘maritime lien’ over the outstanding 5% L/C money. For this purpose, I need to be acquainted with the statutory and dictionary meaning of the terms ‘lien’ and ‘maritime lien’.

24.         Although no Act of Parliament provides the definition of the term ‘lien’, Sections 170, 171 and 221 of the Contract Act, 1872 (the Contract Act) states about ‘lien’.

170. Bailee’s particular lien-Where the bailee has in accordance with the purpose of the bailment, rendered any service involving the exercise of labour or skill in respect of the goods bailed, he has, in the absence of a contract to the contrary, a right to retain such goods until he receives due remuneration for the services he has rendered in respect of them.

171. General lien of bankers, factors, wharfingers, attorneys and policy-brokers-Bankers, factors, wharfiners, Advocate of the Supreme Court and policy-brokers may, in the absence of a contract to the contrary, retain, as a security for a general balance of account, any goods bailed to them; but no other persons have a right to retain, as a security for such balance, goods bailed to them, unless there is an express contract to that effect.

221. Agent’s lien on principal’s property-In the absence of any contract to the contrary, an agent is entitled to retain goods, papers and other property, whether moveable or immovable, of the principal received by him, until the amount due to himself for commission, disbursements and services in respect of the same has been paid or accounted for to him.

(underlined by me)

25.         The above provisions of the Contract Act do not furnish the exact meaning of the word ‘lien’. However, the provisions suggest that ‘lien’ is a right of retaining any property, goods and money against the service rendered by any skilled/unskilled professional, or in exchange of or against the sale of any movable/ immovable property. ‘Lien’ is almost similar to ‘pledge’, which has been defined in Section 172 of the Contract Act in the following terms;

172. “Pledge”, “pawnor” and “pawnee” defined-The bailment of goods as security for payment of a debt or performance of a promise is called “pledge”. The bailor is in this case called the “pawnor”. The bailee is called the “pawnee”.

26.         Section 172 of the Contract Act defines ‘Pledge’ as bailment of goods as security for payment of a debt or performance of a promise. The civil law recognizes two kinds of pledge, viz the "pignus" (pawn) in which possession of goods is actually delivered to the creditor or pawnee and "hypotheca" (hypothecation) where possession of the goods pledged remained with the debtor with the obligation as per contract.

27.         Both in a pledge and in a lien the general property remains with the debtor and the creditor has only a special property. But the nature and extent of this special property in the two cases is quite different. A lien gives only a personal right to retain possession. The creditor holding this security cannot transfer it to any other person, nor can he himself enforce it by sale of his own motion, without the aid of judicial proceedings. A creditor holding a pledge may, on the other hand, transfer his interest to another, and he may himself enforce his security by sale without the aid of a Court.

28.         The Black’s Law Dictionary provides the definition of the word ‘Lien’ in the following expressions:

Lien: A legal right or interest that a creditor has in another’s property, lasting until a debt or duty that it secures is satisfied. Typically, the creditor does not take possession of the property on which the lien has been obtained.

29.         Thus, from the statute and the dictionary, all that I understand about ‘lien’ is that a lien is the right to retain property belonging to a debtor until he has discharged a debt due to the retainer of the property. So, in ‘lien’, (i) there must be a debtor and a creditor (ii) there must be a property, goods, money belonging to the debtor and (iii) the creditor has directly or indirectly control or possession over the property, goods, money. This is known as ‘common law lien’.

30.         In this suit, admittedly, the plaintiff is the creditor and the defendant no. 3 is his debtor; none of the defendant nos. 2, 5 & 8 is the debtor to the plaintiff, nor the property, goods, money of the plaintiff’s debtor (defendant no. 3) is in possession of the defendant nos. 2, 5 & 8. Therefore, there is no scope for the plaintiff to claim lien over this 5% L/C money. Had any property/goods/money of the charterer (defendant no. 3) been in the control/possession of the defendant no. 5, the plaintiff could have claimed a ‘common law lien’ over the same.

31.         When a ‘common law lien’ is created in connection with the dealings of a vessel, it may give rise to a ‘maritime claim’. The list of the ‘maritime claim’ has been provided in clauses (a) to (r) of Section 3(2) of the Admiralty Court Act, which is quoted below:

(a)    any claim to the possession or ownership of a ship or to the ownership of any share thereof or for recovery of documents of title and ownership of a ship, including registration certificate, log book and such certificates as may be necessary for the operation or navigation of the ship;

(b)   any question arising between the co-owners of a ship as to possession, employment or income of that ship;

(c)    any claim in respect of a mortgage of or charge on a ship as to share therein;

(d)   any claim for damage done by a ship;

(e)    any claim for damages by a damaged ship;

(f)     any claim for loss of life or personal injury sustained in consequence of any defect in a ship or in her apparel or equipment, or of the wrongful act, neglect or default of the owners, charterers or persons in possession or control of a ship or of the master or crew thereof or of any other person for whose wrongful acts, neglects or defaults the owners, charterers or persons in possession in the navigation or management of the ship, in the loading carriage or discharge of goods on, in or from the ship or in the embarkation or disembarkation of persons on, in or from the ship;

(g)    any claim for loss of or damage to goods carried in a ship;

(h)   any claim arising out of any agreement relating to the carriage of goods in a ship or to the use or hire of a ship;

(i)     any claim in the nature of salvage of life from a ship or cargo or any property on board a ship or the ship itself or its apparel, whether services were rendered on the high sea or within territorial waters or inland waters or in a port, (including any claim arising by virtue of the application by or under Section 12 of the Civil Aviation Ordinance, 1960, of the law relating to salvage to aircraft and their apparel and cargo);

(j)     any claim as to towage in respect of a ship or an aircraft;

(k)   any claim as to pilotage in respect of a ship or an aircraft;

(l)     any claim in respect of goods or materials supplied to a ship for her operation or maintenance;

(m) any claim in respect of construction, repair or equipment of a ship or dock charges or dues.

(n)   any claim for wages by a master or member of the crew of a ship or any claim for any money or property under the provisions of the Merchant Shipping Ordinance, 1983, hereinafter referred to as the Ordinance, or which is recoverable as wages in the Court of law;

(o)    any claim by a master, shipper, charterer or agent in respect of disbursements made on account of or for the purpose of a ship;

(p)   any claim arising out of an act which is or is claimed to be a general average act;

(q)   any claim arising out of bottomry or respondentia;

(r)    any claim for forfeiture or condemnation of a ship or of goods which are being or have been carried, or have been attempted to be carried, or for the restoration of a ship or any such goods after seizure, or for droits of Admiralty including any other power to grant reliefs as are provided under the provisions of the mentioned Ordinance or any other jurisdiction which was vested in the High Court Division as a Court of Admiralty immediately before commencement of this Act and any other jurisdiction as to any matter in connection with ships or aircraft which has by custom been exercised by the High Court Division as a Court of Admiralty.

32.         Apparently, the plaintiff’s grievance attracts clause (h) of Section 3(2) of the Admiralty Court Act inasmuch as the plaintiff has instituted this suit for recovery of his hire from the charterer (defendant no. 3) and, thus, prima facie there is an existence of ‘maritime claim’ against the charterer (defendant no. 3). 

33.         Since the plaintiff has sought to claim ‘maritime lien’ over the defendant’s money, it is incumbent upon me to see whether the above ‘maritime claim’ can be termed as the ‘maritime lien’. For the purpose of examining this issue, I need to be familiar with the meaning of the terminology ‘maritime lien’. Black’s Law Dictionary defines the expression ‘maritime lien’ in the following language;

Maritime Lien: (1831) A lien on a vessel, given to secure the claim of a creditor who provided maritime services to the vessel or who suffered an injury from the vessel’s use. -Also termed tacit hypothecation.

34.         Griffith Price, in his book The Law of Maritime Liens 1 (1940) describes about the terminology ‘maritime lien’ in the following manner;

“The maritime lien has been described as one of the most striking peculiarities of Admiralty law, constituting a charge upon ships of a nature unknown alike to common law and equity. It arises by operation of law and exists as a claim upon the property, secret and invisible. A maritime lien may be defined as: (1) a privileged claim, (2) upon maritime property, (3) for service done to it or injury caused by it, (4) accruing from the moment when the claim attaches, (5) travelling with the property unconditionally, (6) enforced by means of an action in rem.”

35.         The international convention for the unification of certain Rules relating to the maritime liens and mortgages in 1967 held at Brussels, “having recognized the desirability of determining by agreement certain Rules relating to maritime liens and mortgages agreed as follows”:

Article 4.-1. The following claims shall be secured by maritime liens on the vessel:

(i)                 wages and other sums due to the master, officers and other members of the vessel’s complement in respect of their employment on the vessel;

(ii)               port, canal and other, water way dues and pilotage dues;

(iii)             claims against the owner in respect of loss of life or personal injury occurring, whether on land or on water, in direct connection with the operation of the vessel;

(iv)              claims against the owner, based on tort and not capable of being based on contract, in respect of loss of or damage to property occurring, whether on land or on water, in direct connection with the operation of the vessel;

(v)                claims for salvage, wreck removal and contribution in general average. The word “owner” mentioned in this paragraph shall be deemed to include the demise or other charterer, manager or operator of the vessel.

36.         From the provisions of Section 3(2) of the Admiralty Court Act and from the definition of the ‘maritime lien’ as cited hereinabove, it is crystal clear that the terms “maritime lien” and ‘maritime claim’ have been given two distinctive meanings. While all of the claims catalogued in Section 3(2) of our Admiralty Court Act can be easily termed as ‘maritime claims’, but only few of them are recognized as ‘maritime liens’ as per the national laws and the international laws of today. It is the established principle across the maritime world that the parties to the charterparty can not by agreement confer status of ‘maritime lien’ which is by nature a mere ‘maritime claim’. That is to say, although all ‘maritime liens’ are ‘maritime claims’, but all ‘maritime claims’ are not ‘maritime liens’. ‘Maritime lien’ is an important aspect of maritime law with the liability of contract that falls on ship and ships’ equipment. In other words, it is a privileged claim upon a ship for service rendered to, or injuries caused by the ship.

37.         Apparently, in this suit there is a ‘maritime claim’ against the defendant no. 3 and, after completion of trial it would be clear as to whether there is any ‘maritime claim’ against the defendant no. 5. But in no way is there any ‘maritime lien’ against any of them. In view of my above prima facie finding that as of now apparently there is no ‘maritime claim’ against the defendant no. 5, hence there cannot be any question of ‘maritime lien’ against the defendant nos. 2, 5 & 8.

38.         I am, therefore, constrained to arrive at the conclusion that there is no prima facie case in favour of the plaintiff so far as against the defendant nos. 2, 5 & 8.

39.         Two cases have been referred to by the learned Advocate for the plaintiff in support of his prayer for granting/continuing with Order of temporary injunction. In the case of Care Shipping corporation Vs Latin American Shipping Corporation, reported in (1983) 1 All E.R. 1121, the issue was whether the owner of the vessel can claim lien over the cargos and freight payment for recovery of his hire due to the sub-charterer and it was held that the lien on sub-freights conferred by clause 18 included a lien on any remuneration earned by the charterers from their employment of the vessel whether by way of voyage freight or time chartered hire. This cited case was purely with regard to retaining hire and freight; it was not a L/C money. So, the ratio laid down in this cited case is not applicable in this suit. In the case of Mareva Compania Naviera S.A. Vs International Bulk Carriers S.A., reported in (1975) Vol.2 Lloyd’s Rep. 509, C.A., the owner obtained an Order of injunction on the charterer’s money, which the charterer had received as hire from his sub-charterer. The money was lying in the charterer’s Bank account. More importantly, it was not the charterer’s money against the sale of goods, as is the case in the present suit. Therefore, the facts of the cited case being different from the facts of the present case, I do not find the ratio of this cited case to be persuasive.

40.         In view of this Court’s conclusion that there is no prima facie case in favour of the plaintiff against the defendant nos. 2, 5 & 8, there is no need to examine the maintainability of this suit against the defendant nos. 2, 5 & 8. Nonetheless, in order to more effectively dispose of these applications, I intend to examine the issue nos. iv, v & vi. I would take up the issue nos. iv & v at first and, thereafter, the issue no. vi would be taken up for the reason to be noticed hereinafter. In order to deal with the issue nos. iv & v this Court should be acquainted with the relevant provisions of the Admiralty Court Act by which the jurisdiction of the admiralty matters has been vested in this Court. The relevant provision is Section 3 of the Admiralty Court Act, which is quoted below:

3. Admiralty jurisdiction of the High Court Division:

(1)  The High Court Division shall be a Court of Admiralty.

(2)  The Admiralty Court shall have jurisdiction to hear and determine any of the following questions or claims, namely;

(a)………………………………..…

to …………………………………..

(r)……………………………………..

(all the aforesaid clauses (a) to (r) have been quoted hereinbefore.)

41.         From a plain reading of the above laws, it is my understanding that Section 3(2)(h) [ধারা-3(2)(R)] of the Admiralty Court Act provides that any person is competent to invoke the jurisdiction of the Admiralty Court with a prayer for adjudication upon any question or claim which are enumerated in sub-sections 2(a) to 2(r) of Section 3 [3 avivi Dcaviv-2(K) †_‡K 2(`) ch©š—] of the Admiralty Court Act, and Section 3(5)(a) [aviv-3(5)(K)] of the Admiralty Court Act provides that all the provisions of Section 3 of the Admiralty Court Act shall be applicable irrespective of the facts that whether the vessel is a local or foreign one, whether it is registered or unregistered and whether the usual abode or permanent address is in Bangladesh or outside.

42.         With the above conclusion, a pertinent question crops up as to whether the provision of arbitration clause contained in the charterparty has any overriding power over the admiralty jurisdiction. In order to find the answer, I had to go through the relevant provisions of the Arbitration Act, 2001 (the Arbitration Act) and from a concurrent reading of the provisions of Sections 3 & 7 of the Arbitration Act, it appears that the jurisdiction of the Admiralty Court on a dispute, which is required to be resolved through arbitration to be held in Bangladesh, has been made subservient to the provisions of the Arbitration Act; meaning that if any of the provisions of the Arbitration Act allows the Courts to entertain legal proceedings for adjudication upon a particular issue, only for that purpose the Courts are competent to hear and determine the said legal proceedings. Although the provisions of Section 3 of the Arbitration Act stipulates that the provisions of the Act would be applicable only for those arbitration agreement which contain that the place of arbitration is Bangladesh, but there being no ouster clause as to application of the Arbitration Act for foreign arbitration, our Courts sometimes in a fit case entertain legal proceedings only in aid of the foreign arbitration tribunal. It means that if our Court is satisfied that by entertaining a legal proceeding, the Court is not going to be trying any dispute which is pending before the foreign tribunal; rather the Court’s interference is needed for the time being to help the said tribunal, our Courts, in that scenario, finds it appropriate to entertain a legal proceeding.  In this suit, the charterparty stipulates that the seat of the arbitration would be either in New York in USA or London in UK and, that is why, the provisions of the Arbitration Act are apparently not applicable in this suit and, therefore, there is no legal bar for this Court to try this suit. However, had any of the defendants already been in the arbitration forum, in that scenario, upon an application by any party to the legal proceedings in this Admiralty Court, the Court might have halted the proceedings pending before it till disposal of the arbitration. Given the present scenario of the suit that none of the parties have approached the arbitration tribunal, this Court appears to be well competent to entertain and dispose of this suit. 

43.         In other words, since the Arbitration Act of Bangladesh does not prohibit trying this suit, and in view of the averments made by the plaintiff that the defendant no. 3, upon taking hire of the ship from the plaintiff, sent the vessel into Bangladeshi territory for delivery of the cargos on board the vessel, which is primarily supported by the documents submitted by the plaintiff, I find that the plaintiff has been able to make out a prima facie case of ‘maritime claim’ against the defendant no. 3 and, accordingly, I hold that this Court has the jurisdiction as per the provisions of Section 3(2)(h) and 3(5)(a) of the Admiralty Court Act to hear and determine the claim. However, in the event of decreeing the suit in favour of the plaintiff, whether the plaintiff would be able to execute the decree in Bangladesh in the context of presently having no property attached herein this country- is altogether a different issue. It may be that upon obtaining a decree, the plaintiff may wait to attach any money of the defendant no.3 to be available in future in Bangladesh through any transaction by the defendant no.3 with any person/company/Bank, or the plaintiff may try to execute the decree in a foreign country with whom Bangladesh has reciprocal arrangement for execution of decree.   

44.         Apparently, the better forum for the plaintiff is to approach the arbitration tribunal in New York or London inasmuch as the award of the aforesaid forum is executable in most of the countries of the world, including India, the country of the defendant no. 3. Also, the plaintiff could have invoked the jurisdiction of the Admiralty Court in India in order to comfortably execute the decree, if it is passed in its favour.

45.         Now, I would take up the issue no. vi, namely whether the payment of Letter of Credit can be stopped by an Order of injunction by the Court. A Letter of Credit is an irrevocable guarantee given by the issuing Bank (L/C opening Bank) to the negotiating Bank to the effect that upon receipt of the clean shipping documents from the side of the negotiating Bank, the specified amount of money as contained in the L/C shall be remitted without being influenced by any objection raised by any party, except the objection of fraud if it is alleged to have been committed by the seller with regard to the documents sent by him through the negotiating Bank. It is the globally-honoured practice that payment of the L/C money shall not be stopped by the issuing Bank except finding fraud with the documents inasmuch as it has serious implications in international trade. If the buyer has any objection with regard to the quality of goods or timeous performance, it should be dealt with as per the prevailing law of the country, but the buyer or his Bank (issuing Bank) must not retain the L/C money for the purpose of settling down the contractual issues between the buyer and the seller. Therefore, while it is the settled principle of law that a buyer or his Bank is not competent to withhold the payment of L/C, stopping the payment of L/C at the instance of a third party would be outright illegal. In this suit, there is no allegation of fraud against the seller (the defendant no. 5) in sending the clean shipping documents to the buyer (the defendant no. 2) and, therefore, the defendant nos. 2 & 8 are legally bound to remit the L/C money to the defendant no. 5. In fact, in this suit there being no objection from the side of the buyer with regard to the goods, the buyer (defendant no. 2) and its Bank (the defendant no. 8) wish to send money to the seller (the defendant no. 5) at the earliest possible time. Therefore, the plaintiff, being not any party to the L/C or to the underlying contract to the L/C, is not competent to seek injunction upon the payment of L/C money.

46.         It means that the plaintiff’s suit against the defendant no. 2, 5 & 8 is though maintainable, temporary injunction cannot be granted upon the defendant nos. 2 & 8 restraining them from transmitting the L/C money to the defendant no. 5. And, if the temporary injunction has already been granted, the same should be vacated.

47.         Moreover, if the injunction is granted/continued, the inconvenience which would be caused to the defendant no. 5 is greater than that of the plaintiff in that while the plaintiff would have the option to recover the money from the defendant no. 3 by execution of the decree, if any, at a favorable time or the plaintiff would be able to obtain an award from the arbitration tribunal, the defendant no. 5 would be suffering serious hardship by not being allowed by the Banks of Bangladesh and India to enter into new business. Also, if injunction is granted/continued, the loss that is going to be suffered by the defendant no. 5 is apparently grave having enormous impact in his business given that he would be a Bank-defaulter, not only to the Banks of his country, but also to the foreign Banks. Since there is no prima facie case in favour of the plaintiff and the balance of inconvenience is also not in favour of the plaintiff and, further, there would be irreparable loss and injury to the defendant no.5, if injunction is granted/continued and, more importantly, the subject matter of injunction being the L/C money in which the plaintiff does not have any apparent ownership, this Court is not inclined to grant temporary injunction or to continue/extend the interim injunction already granted. However, it is a well-settled principle that granting or vacating the temporary injunction is a discretionary power of any Court, and a discretionary power is required to be exercised only in favour of those litigants who come before the Courts with clean hands. I would now briefly check whether these defendants, particularly the defendant no. 5, have approached this Court with clean hands.

48.         From the Order Sheets of this suit, it transpires that the defendant no. 5 entered its appearance in this suit on 17.01.2019 only when this Court on 14.08.2018 had directed the defendant no. 2 to communicate with the defendant no. 3 through e-Mail asking him to appear before this Court. It appears to this Court to be a bit mysterious that after sending an e-Mail to respondent no. 3 on 30.09.2018, although the recipient of the e-mail (defendant no.3) did not appear before this Court but the defendant no.5 appeared in this suit on 17.01.2019. The aforesaid event leads this Court to believe, albeit superficially at this juncture, that the defendant no. 3 is in touch with the defendant no. 5.

49.         In the light of the fact that as per the G to G contract, still a huge quantity of rice is yet to be exported by the defendant no. 5 to the Government of Bangladesh. Thus, law and equity demand that Order of temporary injunction may be vacated with the conditions that in course of trial of this suit if it is revealed that the defendant no. 3 has any financial transaction with the defendant no. 5 or the defendant no. 3 is in touch with the defendant no. 5 in any connection, the defendant nos. 1, 2, 5 & 8 shall be duty bound to bring the fact to the notice of this Court or to the knowledge of the plaintiff, so that an appropriate Order may be passed in time. Further, the defendant no.5 must submit some sort of security, which may be a Bank Guarantee to this Court or a bond to the defendant no. 2 or to the defendant no. 8 so that after trial of the suit, if it is surfaced that there was connivance of defendant no. 5 with defendant no. 3, the Bank Guarantee/bond may be used towards partial execution of the decree. However, the moment the defendant no. 3 would appear in this suit, all these three defendants shall be relieved of the above conditions. In view of this Court’s exercise of its discretionary power in favour of the defendant no. 5, it is expected that the defendant no. 5 shall show its bonafides in assisting this Court in effectively disposing of this suit either by depositing the above security or by producing the defendant no. 3 before this Court so that the conduct of the defendant no.5 of this suit does not appear to be dubious.  

50.         With the above findings of facts, examination of the laws and the conclusions arrived at on the issues framed by this Court, the applications filed by the defendant nos. 2 & 5 for vacating the Order of interim injunction passed on 28.06.2018 by this Court are declared to have succeeded, subject to compliance of the following conditions;

(i)     There shall be a standing direction (continuing mandamus) upon the defendant no. 5 that henceforth if the defendant no. 5 at any point in time receives any money from the defendant no. 3 in any connection, he must retain the same in his custody and inform this Court immediately.

(ii)   The defendant nos. 2 & 8 are directed to withhold payment of money if they ever receive any money from/for the defendant no. 3 and notify this Court immediately without fail.

(iii) The defendant no. 5 shall either furnish a Bank Guarantee of the equivalent amount of the money (i.e. the amount of money which is going to be released now) to this Court or in the alternative, the defendant no. 5 shall execute a bond in favour of the defendant no. 2 confirming that in course of completing the exportation of the rest of the quantity of rice to the defendant no. 2, the latter shall be competent to retain the equivalent amount of money at any point of time to satisfy any Order or decree passed by this Court.

(iv)  The learned Advocate for the defendant no. 5 is directed to remain present in this Court without fail whenever this suit would appear in the ‘Cause List’ so that the suit may be expeditiously disposed of.

(v)    The learned Advocate shall ensure the presence of any relevant witness of the defendant no. 5, if this Court intends to take his/her deposition as a Court Witness (C/W). In the event of failure to comply with this condition by the learned Advocate for the respondent no. 5, he shall be liable to furnish a written explanation before this Court.

51.         In the result, the applications filed by the defendant nos. 2 & 5 are allowed and the Order of interim injunction passed by this Court on 28.06.2018 restraining the defendant no. 2 from making any payment to the defendant no. 3 is vacated. Further, the application filed by the plaintiff praying for restraining the defendant nos. 2 & 8 from making further payment to the defendant no. 5 is rejected.

52.         If the defendant no. 5 wishes to file any Written Statement in this suit, he is directed to file it within 7 (seven) days from the date of receipt of this Judgment and Order.

53.         The suit is fixed for framing issues on 22.05.2019. Let the matter appear in the list under the heading for ‘Framing Issue’ on 22.05.2019.

54.         Before parting with this Judgment and Order, I find it pertinent to remind the learned members of the Bar that they, being the integral part of the Judiciary, should be more interested to dispose of the substantive matters pending before the Courts than showing their eagerness in obtaining the interim relief. Particularly, when any Court suggests that the substantive matter shall be heard on fast-track basis, the concerned Advocates should understand that while on the one side, the parties of the suit/case would get the aspired remedy, which would obviously include the interim relief, on the other, the Court would be able to reduce the number of backlog of cases. The learned Advocates should take into consideration the valuable time of the Courts that requires for adjudication upon the interim/interlocutory matters. Instead of compelling the Courts to spend time in dealing with interlocutory matters, if the learned Advocates extend their co-operation to dispose of the substantive matter, the Judiciary will be able, to some extent, to cope with the prevailing backlog.

55.         In this suit, after hearing the learned Advocate for the defendant no.5 on his application for vacating the Order of injunction, this Court had suggested that the suit shall be heard on priority basis by fixing the next date for framing issues. It was observed by this Court that if it surfaces upon examining the oral and documentary evidence that the suit is not maintainable against the defendant no. 5, then the suit would be proceeded only against the defendant no. 3. Had the learned Advocate conceded to the above suggestion of this Court, the defendant no.5 could have been permanently relieved of the hassle he is facing and on the other hand, this Court could have disposed of a substantive matter with the amount of time that required for disposing of these interim applications. Now, by getting the aspired relief in these applications, although the defendant no.5’s L/C money would be released subject to depositing the equivalent amount of security, he remains entangled with the suit.
