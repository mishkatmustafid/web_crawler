Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898


Section 401—

As the petitioner’s sentence of death was commuted to imprisonment for life on 11-12-91 he cannot claim the benefit of the order of clemency granted by the Acting President save and except treating his sentence for life imprisonment being for 20 years.

Shahnaj Rahman vs State 1 BLC 178


Sections 401, 402 & 402A—

As there is no scope for altering the conviction from one under clause 1(b) to clause 1(a) of the table of section 19 of the Act reducing the sentence on compassionate ground considering the age of the respondent who may approach the Government or the President, if so advised, for any relief that she may choose to pray.

State vs Miss Eliadah McCord 2 BLC (AD) 1


Section 403—

Two separate petitions of complaint were filed by the persons who received injuries and the first one was compounded but the second one was pending. As the offences under sections 323, 324 and 325 of the Penal Code can be compounded by the person or persons to whom the hurt is caused, the order of composition of the alleged offence suffers from legal infirmity and cannot operate as an acquittal of the accused persons as far as the second complainant is concerned and the doctrine of double jeopardy enunciated in section 403, CrPC is hardly applicable in this case.

Nurul Islam and others vs Md Velon Mia and another 1 BLC 252


Sections 403(1) & 561A—

In view of the provisions of section 403(1) CrPC and Article 35 of the Constitution the rifle as recovered from the accused persons during investigation of the earlier dacoity case, ended in conviction, there cannot be any separate proceeding and the prosecution against the accused persons in the present case is incompetent which is liable to be quashed.

Abdur Rashid and others vs State 1 BLC 180


Section 408—

A senior judicial officer should not have shown so much unwarranted agility in condoning the delay in filing the appeal without notifying the other side and thereby exercising his discretion not judicially but arbitrarily.

This sort of hot haste conduct of the learned Sessions Judge cannot be approved of. We take a serious exception to this and we give a note of warning and it is desired that he should bear in mind the golden principle of Audi Alteram Partem grounded on the principle of natural justice in dealing with such matter in future.

Minhaz A Chowdhury vs Manzurul Huq & another 1 BLC 18


Section 417(1)—

As it is a case of homicidal strangulation the trial Court committed no illegality in acquitting the accused persons from the charge of murder but the husband was liable for conviction for causing hurt on the upper lip of the wife.

State vs Yahiya alias Thandu & ors 1 BLC 185


Sections 417(2), 439 & 439A—

Against an order of acquittal passed by the appellate Court in a case instituted upon a complaint, the complainant shall have to file an appeal before the High Court Division under subsection (2) of section 417 and filing of revision shall be barred under sub-section (5) of section 439 of the Code.

Abdul Khaleque vs State 5 BLC 701


Section 423—

The High Court Division held rightly that the trial Magistrate had conveniently failed to consider the opinion of both the handwriting expert and the fingerprint expert to facilitate a judgment of acquittal and while setting aside the order of acquittal of the trial Court it has sent back the case on remand to the trial Court for a fresh decision and allowed both the complainant and the accused to examine further witnesses.

Bhulu Rani Saha & anr vs Pran Ballav Podder & another 5 BLC (AD) 122


Section 423—

While considering the sentence to be passed upon the respondent under clause 1(a) of section 19 of the Act the High Court Division was wrong in relying upon a statement made by the respondent recorded in connection with the Miscellaneous case started on a suo motu Rule as that statement was not part of the record of the appeal and thus irrelevant as far as the appeal was concerned.

State vs Miss Eliadah McCord 2 BLC (AD) 1


Section 423(1)(b)—

The trial Court awarded the maximum sentence of fine of Taka ten thousand but the Appellate Court reduced the same to Taka one thousand without assigning any reason and such reduction of sentence was not proper and legal and hence the judgment and order of the Appellate Court so far it relates to reduction of sentence is set aside and the sentence imposed by the trial Court shall be the sentence in this case.

Abdul Hamid vs Habib Ahmed and another 4 BLC 343


Section 426—

Considering the age, suffering in service, facing trial and surrendering before the trial Court soon after the delivery of judgment the convicted- petitioner was enlarged on bail for one year in a pending appeal against the judgment of sentence of 3 years.

Abul Hossain (Md) vs State 4 BLC 151


Section 426—

When the part-heard appeal was adjourned today fixing 57-96 on the prayer of the petitioner, there is no reason to enlarge him on bail because a convict- appellant is entitled to get bail during the pendency of his appeal only when it cannot be disposed of at an early date.

HM Ershad vs State 1 BLC 291


Section 426—

While disposing of an application for bail in a pending criminal appeal the High Court Division would avoid writing out a long judgment by issuing a Rule and it is required to exercise its discretion judiciously in either granting or refusing bail at the time of the admission of appeal. In the cases where bail is not granted it is desirable that the appeal be disposed of as expeditiously as possible.

Hasimuddin & another vs State 1 BLC (AD) 16


Section 426—

Bail in pending appeal— As the appellant Nos. 1 and 2 are full brothers and the appellant No. 3 who is more than 72 years old is their father and although there is allegation against the appellant Nos. 1 and 2 that they assaulted some of the PWs but there is contradiction in the evidence of PWs and in such facts and circumstances of the case the appellants are enlarged on bail for ends of justice.

Noor Alam Fakir & 2 others vs State 2 BLC 230


Section 426—

Bail in a pending appeal— It appears that convict petitioner No. 1 has been implicated in this case simply as an order giver and excepting this there is no other allegation against him. For which he is entitled to be released on bail.

Maharam Ali & another vs State 2 BLC 401


Section 428—

As the PW 1 in his evidence did not at all state that he had got a paper to show that the age of her daughter was 13 years and the prosecution had not filed any application for getting the school certificate admitted into evidence and also by calling headmaster of the school as a witness for which the power given by section 428, CrPC cannot be exercised.

Haren Halder vs Md Akkas Ali & ors 3 BLC 455


Section 436—

While no notice is required to issue at the preliminary stage of taking cognizance by issuing processes, in like manner, no notice to the accused persons is necessary in a revision case under section 436, CrPC directing against an order of dismissal without a notice to the complainant.

Sirajuddowla and others vs State and another 1 BLC 60.


Sections 438 and 439A—

Sessions Judges have been given revisional powers by section 439A of CrPC to make final orders but simultaneously their powers to make recommendation to the High Court Division for orders under section 438 has remained intact as the legislature has consciously kept section 438 alive even after the Sessions Judges have been invested with the powers under section 439A.

Abdul Ahad @ Md Abdul Ahad vs State 5 BLC 598


Section 439—

Although both the courts below made a concurrent finding on conviction of the petitioner on the facts alleged in the case, the same will not debar the revisional Court from interfering and examining the evidence on record for arriving at a proper decision of its own on this point as both the Courts below have failed to discuss and consider the evidence of the case.

Asiruddjn (Md) alias Asiruddin Sarker and others vs State and another 5 BLC 641


Section 439—

It is now well settled that merely because the Court deciding a revision against acquittal of the accused may arrive at a different conclusion on consideration of the evidence on record would not be justified in reversing the decision of the trial Court unless it is possible to demonstrate with certainty that none of the grounds upon which trial Court acquitted the accused is at all supportable, the same being manifestly wrong or perverse.

Ali Akbar vs State and others 3 BLC 465.


Sections 439A, 540 and 561A—

It cannot be approved as a universal principle that a party who has been unsuccessful in revision under sections 439A, CrPC is totally debarred from invoking the inherent power under section 561A, CrPC but insofar as the instant cases are concerned the trial Magistrate upon appreciating the material facts was not satisfied with the prayer for recalling the prosecution witnesses for cross- examination and there are no illegalities in those orders which warrant the exercise of jurisdiction under section 561A, CrPC.

Mirza Khorshed Alam and another vs Shahjahan Au and 3 others 2 BLC (AD) 155


Sections 439A and 561A—

The petitioner moved the Sessions Judge in revision and he having failed there has again filed an application before the High Court Division for quashing of the proceeding under section 561A of the Code of Criminal Procedure which he is precluded by law as because there was no lack of jurisdiction by the Sessions Judge to dispose of the case nor there was any erroneous exercise of jurisdiction by the court concerned. So this is only a camouflage for filing a second revisional application.

MA Mottalib vs Narayan Kumar Agarwala and others 3 BLC (AD) 62


Sections 439 and 417—

When complainant has been given a limited right of appeal against an order of acquittal under the amended sub-section (2) of section 417, CrPC on the ground of error of law then informant should also be given the right of preferring appeal alike the complainant and such right of appeal should be on the grounds of error of fact as well as law and the revisional Court also shall have the power to convert a finding of acquittal into one of conviction by amending the above provisions as there is no justification for restricting the appeal by the complainant only to the question of law after abolition of the jury trial long before.

Ali Akbar vs State and others 3 BLC 465


Sections 439A and 439—

The Sessions Judge is quite competent to decide and rectify any illegal order passed by the subordinate Courts in exercise of its revisional power under section 439A of the Code and as such the petitioners have no right to come straightaway to the High Court Division for vindication of their grievance bypassing the Sessions Judge.

Tajul Islam and others vs State and another 3 BLC 527


Sections 439A and 561A—

The facts as alleged in the petition of complaint constitute a prinwi facie criminal intention of cheating and deception in the mind of the petitioner which is still continuing because of the nonpayment of any part of the amount alleged in the complaint-petition and all these are the matters for decision at the time of trial after taking evidences and that after exhausting the remedy under section 439A the jurisdiction under section 561A, CrPC cannot be invoked.

Kamal Hossain vs Zahid Hasan & another 3 BLC 378


Sections 476 and 476B—

All that the appellate Court, as expressed by section 476B of the Code, can do is to direct the withdrawal of the complaint if made by the trial Court or itself make the complaint which might have been made by the subordinate Court following the provision of section 476. Sending a case on remand by the appellate Court amounts to acting beyond jurisdiction.

Khizir Hayat Khan Eusuf Zai vs Major (Retd) Md Muqtadir Ali and others 4 BLC (AD) 100


Sections 476(1) & 561A—

The learned Special Judge considering the depositions of the petitioner and the concerned Magistrate given in the Special case in question and exercising its jurisdicti6n under section 476(1), CrPC recorded its finding and rightly made a complaint to the District Magistrate Jhalakati wherefrom the magistrate concerned rightly started a complaint case and took cognizance of the offence under sections 193/212 of the Penal Code and hance the petition under section 561A, CrPC is a misconceived one.

Masudur Rahman (Md) vs State 5 BLC 286


Sections 476(1) and 561A—

When the learned Special Judge considering the depositions of the petitioners as PW 6 and PW 10 and the concerned Magistrate as PW 9 given in the special case exercised his jurisdiction under section 476(1), CrPC and recorded his findings and rightly made a complaint to the District Magistrate whereupon the concerned Magistrate rightly took cognizance of the offence under sections 193/212 of the Penal Code and such offence is not dependent upon the acquittal or conviction of the original accused persons for whom the false evidence was given or sought to be screened from punishment and the learned Special Judge need not wait for forwarding a petition of complaint till conclusion of the trial to observe the effect of such deposition and hence the application for quashing the proceeding is misconceived.

Masudur Rahman Mollah and another vs State 5 BLC 145


Section 488—

Provisions of the Ordinance have empowered the Court to entertain, try and dispose of any suit relating to five matters including maintenance but such power is not given to a Magistrate under section 488, CrPC but power of Magistrate has not been curtailed consequent to the establishment of the Family Court.

Rezaul Karim vs Rashida Begum and another 1 BLC 198


Section 488(1)(3)—

In view of the provisions of section 16(3B) of the Family Courts Ordinance, 1985 the executing Court may direct for civil imprisonment once again for failure to pay money of the subsequent installments and this power can also be exercised as a Magistrate while dealing with cases under section 488(I)(3), CrPC.

Maksuda Akhter vs Md Serajul Islam 5 BLC 595


Section 491—

Release of a girl from the judicial custody—Although the victim girl is neither an accused nor a witness in the criminal case but she is kept in judicial custody. Relying on the school certificate of the girl it is held that as soon as the girl attains the age of 18 years from 1-12-78 she must be released from the judicial custody on her own bond even if the criminal case is pending.

Hasina Begum vs State and another 1 BLC 315


Section 491—

When the liberty of a citizen is curtailed and his valuable right of freedom is taken away by an order of detention without serving it on him and such a fact of detention is brought to the notice of the High Court Division who has a constitutional duty cast on it by Article 102(2)(b)(i) of the Constitution and a legal duty in view of section 491 of the Code of Criminal Procedure to examine whether such person is being detained illegally. Although there is no specific provision in the Special Powers Act, 1974, to serve the order of detention on the detenu but it is essential that the detenu must know under what order he is being detained and this can only be done by serving a copy of the order on him.

MA Hashem vs Government of Bangladesh and ors 1 BLC 5.


Section 491—

As the father being the best well-wisher of an abducted minor daughter is entitled to custody and in her own interest she should be given in her father’s custody in spite of her refusal to go with her father who is trying to get the custody of his minor daughter from the Court of Magistrate upto this Division out of filial love, affection and welfare of the minor daughter.

Mongal Chandra Nandi vs Bangladesh and others 2 BLC (AD) 18.


Section 493—

Although this section provides for appointment of pleader in any court by a private person to assist the Public Prosecutor regarding enquiry, trial or appeal but the instant application for addition of party has been filed by the informant in the Miscellaneous case arising out of an application for bail is neither an enquiry proceeding nor trial nor appeal and hence such application for addition of party merits no consideration in spite of his interest in the result of the case in the absence of any laches or negligence from the side of the prosecution in the present case.

Rashel Kabir alias Roman vs State 4 BLC 346


Section 494—

As the order of withdrawal of the case against the accused opposite parties has been passed by the learned Additional Sessions Judge without assigning any cogent ground for which the impugned order is set aside.

Ayen Ali vs Shah Obaidul Mannan and others 3 BLC 572


Sections 496 and 497—

Since there is no inherent power of the High Court Division or the Court of Session to grant bail at any stage, the status in life, affluence or otherwise are irrelevant while considering the prayer for granting anticipatory bail.

KM Jahangir Alam vs State 3 BLC 564


Sections 496, 497 and 498—

A person seeking anticipatory bail does not require to appear before the High Court Division or the Court of Session as section 498 like section 496 or 497 does not require a person to place himself in custodian legis, to be dealt with in accordance with law. If such person is allowed anticipatory bail then upon furnishing bond he will subject himself really to one form of custody.

KM JahangirAlam vs State 3 BLC 564


Section 497—

When the Hon’ble Appellate Division granted bail to be effective upto 3 1-8-96 which is neither extendable nor can the trial Court enlarge the petitioner on bail. Cancellation of bail.

Zakir Hossain Khan and others vs SM Jillur Rahman and another 3 BLC (AD) 66


Sections 497 and 498—

Per Latifur Rahman J: All persons charged with non- bailable offences must be treated equally unless, of course, there are special circumstances which need special consideration in particular facts of a case. Granting of anticipatory bail is an extraordinary remedy and as such in the case of accusations of non-bailable offences anticipatory bail should not be granted and the person should be directed to seek bail under section 497 of the Code of Criminal Procedure. The discretion of granting anticipatory bail shall not be exercised in unscrupulous and frivolous petitions just to avoid the normal legal procedure. On vague and general allegations anticipatory bail should not be granted which will make a short circuit of judicial administration and will help the commission of further offences in the society.

State vs Abdul Wahab Shah Chowdhury 4 BLC (AD) 195


Sections 497(5) and 498—

When bail is granted by the Court of Session to an accused person in custody exercising its power under section 498, CrPC after hearing the parties, such bail can be cancelled only on some definite allegations of abuse of the privileges of the bail or on the ground that the accused to whom bail is granted in taking preparation to leave the jurisdiction of the Court but such bail cannot be cancelled on the ground that bail was wrongly granted.

Momtaz Begum vs State and others 5 BLC 348


Section 498-

The High Court Division gave some reasons of its own for exercising the discretion in granting bail to the respondent and the Appellate Division do not normally interfere with such exercise of discretion unless the discretion has been exercised in an arbitrary and capricious manner.

Bangladesh Bank vs Mustafizur Rahman and another 1 BLC (AD) 187


Section 498—

Per ATM Afzal CJ: The main circumstance as would entitle an order for extraordinary remedy of pre-arrest bail is the perception of the Court upon the facts and materials disclosed by the petitioner before it that the Criminal Proceeding which is being or has been launched against him is being or has been taken with an ulterior motive, political or otherwise, for harassing the accused and not for securing justice, in a particular case.

State vs Abdul Wahab Shah Chowdhury 4 BLC (AD) 195


Section 498–

Per ATM Afzal CJ: Anticipatory bail—When it can be considered—This prayer, extraordinary as it is, can only be considered when it appears to the Court that the purpose of the alleged proceeding as far as the accused is concerned is not what it purports to be but to achieve a collateral purpose by abusing the process of law, such as harassment, humiliation, etc. of the accused which cannot be permitted.

State vs Abdul Wahab Shah Chowdhury 4 BLC (AD) 195


Section 498—

Although charge was framed on 12-8-95 there has been no progress in the trial because of stay order granted by the High Court Division and such indefiniteness of trial is sufficient to allow the appellant to continue on the bail granted to him.

Hussain Md Ershad vs State 5 BLC (AD) 80


Section 498—

The three conditions are required to be satisfied in granting anticipatory bail, firstly, the arrest of the person on a criminal charge by the police without a warrant is imminant and certain, secondly, the absence of reasonable grounds for believing that the petitioner is guilty and thirdly, the existence of extra-ordinary circumstances necessitating exercise of the power which is given in section 498 with the word “may”.

KM Jahangir Alam vs State 3 BLC 564


Section 498–

All the three conditions for granting anticipatory bail are present in this case and as such the petitioner is allowed to remain on bail till the submission of the police report.

KM Jahangir Alam vs State 3 BLC 564


Section 498—

The revolver with bullets was recovered from the possession of co-accused and not from the possession of the petitioners and no charge sheet has yet been submitted though five months has already elapsed and that learned Sessions Judge under misconception that the revolver and bullets were recovered from their possession, refused to release the petitioners on bail for which the petitioners are enlarged on bail till submission of the charge sheet.

Mizanur Rahman Mijan vs State 3 BLC 307


Section 498—

It appears from the FIR that there is no overt act on the part of the petitioner and 5 FIR named witnesses gave statement under section 164 CrPC and except Kuti all others gave merely lump allegation against the petitioner. It further appears that by order dated 3-8- 96 the Magistrate ordered for further investigation and the said investigation is still pending and it is uncertain that when police will submit report after further investigation and when the case may be sent up for trial.

In the above facts and circumstances this court is inclined to exercise discretion in granting bail to the petitioner (who has been in custody since 19-12-1995).

Shahed Reza Shamim vs State 1 BLC 554


Section 498—

Anticipatory bail—It is contended that the petitioner No. 1 is a heart patient and had undergone a by-pass heart operation and a diabetic patient and a respectable person of the locality and physically so infirm that he could not be expected to be involved in the alleged occurrence of looting and damaging properties and the other petitioners have not abused or misused the privilege of bail after the bail was granted by the High Court Division. Considering the submissions the High Court Division allowed the petitioner No. 1 to remain on anticipatory bail till submission of the police report and since there is specific allegations against the other petitioners they were not allowed to continue their bail.

Nurul Islam and others vs State 4 BLC274


Section 498—

Considering the First Information Report, charge sheet and order sheets it is found that the accused-petitioner is not a First Information Report named accused and the police submitted charge sheet against him only on the basis of purported confession made by him and another accused to the police and hence the accused-petitioner is entitled to be released on bail.

Sultan vs State 4 BLC 516


Section 498—

The petitioner having not been named in the FIR but he was named in the charge sheet with the allegation that he might be involved in the killing because of land dispute and he has been in custody since 13-9-98. Considering the facts and circumstances of the case and that the petitioner has remained in custody since 13- 9-98 and he was not aware Of the occurrence, the petitioner was enlarged on bail.

Adhir Mondal vs State 5 BLC 177


Section 498—

As the Civil Surgeon opined the specialised treatment required for the patient which was not available in the jail hospital, a direction was given to shift the accused-petitioner to the Bangladesh Medical University. Dhaka from Central Jail, Jessore for proper treatment.

Selim (Md) @ Khokan vs State 5 BLC 661


Section 498—

Considering the submission of both the sides and perusing the said report of corruption inside the district Jail by the Jailor and employees of the said jail and the certificate issued by the Editor of the said weekly to the effect that the petitioner has been working as a staff reporter of the said newspaper, the petitioner was enlarged on bail. 

Zakir Hossain (Md) vs State 5 BLC 108


Section 498–

The alleged statement of a co-accused involving the respondent in the real planning for the occurrence has been belied by the confessional statement. Without mentioning anything more, the materials on record so far, are plainly insufficient to think that there are reasonable grounds for believing that the respondent has been guilty of the alleged offence of criminal conspiracy. The law permits granting of bail even in a case where there are such reasonable grounds for refusing bail in the case of any woman or any sick or infirm person. The respondent has not been granted bail upon these considerations but upon the view that there are no reasonable grounds for believing that she has been guilty of the offence alleged.

State vs Jobaida Rashid 2 BLC (AD) 75


Section 498—

As there is a delay of 22 days in lodging the First Information Report and the petitioner has been in custody for a long time, the petitioner is entitled to be released on bail.

Majibor vs State 5 BLC 671


Section 498—

As the delay of 20 days in lodging the FIR has made the prosecution case somewhat prima facie doubtful and fishy and that no confessional statement was made by the petitioner after remand and the allegations and averments made in the petition supported by an affidavit has not been controverted by a counter-affidavit, the petitioner who is a lady is entitled to be released on bail.

Jobaida Rashid wife of Khondaker Abdur Rashid vs State 2 BLC 135


Section 498—

Mere naming the petitioner in the charge-sheet without any prima facie material or the mere fact in the occurrence the head of the State with his family has been murdered or that it is a sensational case cannot be a ground for refusal of bail and in such circumstances, the petitioner is entitled to be released on bail.

Jobaida Rashid, wife of Khandakar Abdur Rashid vs State 2 BLC 191

Bail on humanitarian ground—As the petitioner is more than seventy-four years old and his one side of the body is paralysed and one of the co-accused is absconding and there is no certainty when the trial shall commence and his health is deteriorating day by day, the petitioner is entitled to be released on bail at least on humanitarian ground for ends of justice.

Syed Abdul Awal vs State 2 BLC 338


Section 498—

The allegation against the petitioner Nos. 1 and 2 is that they threw stones which struck on the head and chest of the deceased but it appears from the post mortem report that there is small abrasion on the occipital and parietal region and no injury on the chest of the deceased is found and it appears from the school certificate that the petitioner No. 1 was only 14 years old when the occurrence took place and they voluntarily surrendered before the Court and out of ten accused persons eight have already been enlarged on bail and in such circumstances the petitioner No. 1 is entitled to be released on bail and the petitioner No. 2 will continue on the same bail bond.

Asik Miah & ors vs State 2 BLC 512

Nazrul Islam vs State 2 BLC 645.

Section 526—

When the appellant or the State was given no opportunity of being ard to refute the allegations made in such petition which is against the principle of audi alteram partem and hence the volatile order of transfer of the case is set aside.

Section 526—

Mere fact of taking into custody of the accused during trial can never be a basis for making a prayer for transfer of the case unless there is other convincing and reasonable grounds for apprehension in the mind of the accused. Emran Ali (Md) vs Md Amir Hossain and Ors 4 BLC 234


Section 526—

Although the main cause of grievance has ceased to exist but all the seven accused persons are resident of Dhaka City and most of the witnesses are from Dhaka and even the complainant has no address at Rajbari for which in spite of changed circumstances the convenience of the parties demand such a transfer.

Shahida Khatun and another vs Golam Kibria & another 3 BLC (AD) 87


Section 526(1)(c)—

It is alleged that the petitioner has been threatened by the other side in open court to be picked up from the Court with no hope to return and that there is also pressure on his lawyer not to defend him in the case. Although it is difficult to assess the scale of threat to life or its existence or non-existence but presence of circumstances has created a reasonable apprehension in the mind of the accused that he will not get fair trial or that he may be denied the right to defend himself in the trial through his lawyer calls for transfer of the case for the ends of justice within the meaning of clause (c) of section 526 (1) of the Code.

Suman alias Faysal Ahamed vs State and others 2 BLC 578

Section 537—

The contention that section 537 of the Code applies only to disposed of criminal cases after conclusion of trial or hearing and has no application in respect of pending cases is not correct. Although the words “finding sentence” in the section relate to concluded trial or hearing when the word “order” does not relate to only concluded trial or hearing but also to order passed in a pending proceeding.

Shainpukur Holding Ltd vs Security Exchange Commission 3 BLC 148


Section 537—
