Arms Act|XI of 187K|

Sections 15 and 19A—That no licence is required to possess arms like dagger or gachi dao and those arms were not recovered from the accused petitioners within the area speci­fied by Gazette Notification by the Govern­ment as indicated in section 15 of the Arms Act. It was therefore necessary for the prose­cution to prove the existence of such notifica­tion under section 15 of the Arms Act. As such, no offence had been committed by the petitioners under the Arms Act and, as such, the cognisance taken by the Tribunal in the trial of the two petitioners under the Arms Act is without jurisdiction. AH Hossen @ AH and vs Bangladesh 12 BLC 379.

 

Sections 17 and 17A—Rule 43 of the Arms Rules, 1924 provides that a person, whose licence has been cancelled, can appeal to the executive authority immediately supe­rior to the authority who has issued the order of cancellation of licence. In the instant case, the petitioner without resorting to such Appeal, has rushed to this Court and invoked the Writ jurisdiction. The right to property is guaranteed in our Constitution subject to restriction provided by law. The instant writ petition is not maintainable for the statutory remedy of Appeal not having been exhausted by the petitioner prior to filing this writ peti­tion. Saleh Jahur (Md) i>s Government of Bangladesh 11 BLC 583.

 Section 18(A)—The granting of a licence to possess firearms granted by the authorities and that a citizen has common law or legal right to go for a licence to possess a gun or pistol and hence it is a discretion on the part of the Government (Authority) to grant arms licence to the citizen and to cancel them when the authorities deem it necessary for the security of public peace and the can­cellation of licence is an administrative order and the order by no imagination can be treat­ed as judicial order hence the making of a speaking order is not called for. SM Halim Reza vs Secretary, Ministry of Home Affairs (CiviUn BLC 597. 

Sections 19 and 19A—Section 19A, it appears, was introduced to punish the terror­ist. But the time has now changed and appli­cation of section 19A after the amendment of section 19 in 1991 as aforesaid has become redundant. Now section 19 itself provides for same punishment as of section 19A, as such, to avoid anomaly, in police report or in fram­ing charge, section 19A should go. The law­makers are there to look into the matter. Nurul Islam Babul vs State 11 BLC 217.

Section 19A—The law is settled on the point that had there been no support from the side of the public witness even then convic­tion can be given only on the basis of evi­dence of police personnel. In this case, it appears from the evidence on record that PW 1 (the informant) and PW 4 (police constable) have corroborated each other stating the occurrence of recovery of alleged arms and ammunition from the exclusive control and possession of the accused-appellants in a vivid and graphic manner. They came and deposed about the occurrence before the Court without any influence whatsoever and their depositions are also treated as evidence within the meaning of section 3 of the Evidence Act. Hekim alias Hakim vs State 12 BLC 496.

Sections 19A and 19(a)—When the arms were recovered the accused petitioner No. 1 was not brought at the place of occur­rence and the arms were recovered at the pointing of the co-accuseds and one Project Director opened the office of the accused petitioner No. 1, at the time of search, which shows others have access to the place of occurrence and hence the arms so recovered cannot be said to be in possession of the accused petitioners. Nurul Islam Babul vs State 11 BLC 217.

 

Section 19A and 19(f)—Point to be considered is, whether the seized arms and ammunition were recovered from possession and control of the convict appellant. Possession must be exclusive and conscious and control must be actual which is essential to constitute offence. Further to constitute offence under section 19A and 19(f) of the Arms Act there must be mens rea or guilty knowledge. In the case in hand, it appears from the evidence that in the place of occur­rence there is possibility of access of any one. The conviction and sentence of the appellant is based on the evidence of police party, that is, PWs 1 to 4 and not corroborated by the public witnesses PWs 5 and 6. It is now well settled proposition of law that evidence of police personnel are also evidence within the meaning of section 3 of the Evidence Act, 1872. There is no reason to discard their evi­dence if those do not appear falsehood. An accused may be convicted on the basis of unimpeachable evidence of police personnel who made search and seizure even if not sup­ported by the local witnesses.

There are contradictions and inconsisten­cies in the evidence of the PWs 1 to 4. Further before search the PW 1 did not call any local witness in compliance of provisions of sec­tion 103 of the Code of Criminal Procedure. The seizure list witnesses PWs 5 and 6 in their deposition stated that they did not see the recovery of the seized arms and ammunition. They further said that while they went to the police station for their own business the Investigating Officer took their signatures on the white paper. Further, it appears that PW 6 is an inhabitant of Tambalkhana, a different area who definitely cannot be the neighbour of the place of occurrence of Alipur. So, there are discrepancies, inconsistencies and contra­dictions in the evidence. But the learned Judge of the Tribunal failed to notice the aforesaid contradictions and inconsistencies in the evidence of PWs 1 to 4 at least.

The prosecution has miserably failed to prove the offence charged under section 19A and 19(f) of the Arms Act against the convict appellant beyond all reasonable shadow of doubt and, therefore, he is not guilty of the charge. Thus the impugned judgment and order convicting and sentencing the convict appellant cannot be sustained. Abu Sayeed vs State 14 BLC 379.

Sections 19A & 19(f)—The words "WRH ctrai «mH" was inserted in the column of the seizure list and PW 7 Mahbubur Rahman, the informant admitted in his cross-examination that there was no 'initial' on the said portion of seizure list, which creates a presumption that the word "www cirar <n<\\u" has been inserted subsequently in the seizure list and same was after thought act of police. The defence further put suggestion specifical­ly to the investigating officer that the alleged arms were not in order. PW 8 the investigat­ing officer in his cross-examination admitted that he did not send the arms to the expert for examination. This also creates a doubt as to the position of the arms, whether it was in order. To convict a person under section 19A and 19(f) of the Arms Act, the alleged arms must be a fire arms and workable one. Delwar Hossain vk State 16 BLC 32. 

Sections 19A & 19(f)—All the PWs arecorroborative to one another on material points regarding time, place, recovery of arms and ammunition from the possession of the accused. Thus the tribunal rightly found him guilty of the charges of unauthorized possession of arms and ammunition under sections 19A and 19(f) of the Arms Act. Shahin Alam vs State 16 BLC 115.

Sections 19A & 19(f)—The mode of recovery of arms disclosed in the FIR and the statements made by the witnesses to that effect, thus leaves the factum of recovery unclear and do not permit safe inference of recovery of arms etc. from the appellant. A close scrutiny of the prosecution case is clear­ly suggestive of the fact that the story set out in the FIR is utterly unbelievable in that the story of an armed man being hauled up by two unarmed women as is stated in the FIR and thereafter taking control of him with his arms and gathering people by shouting is incompatible with the ordinary course of events. Feroz vs State 16 BLC 189.

 

Section 19A and 19(f)—There is no evi­dence to the effect that the alleged incrimi­nating articles were recovered from accused-appellant Faridul in asmuch as the evidence of record clearly justify that some used wear­ing apparels were recovered from his control and possession without mentioning its coun­try of origin and in such facts and circum­stances the charge against accused-appellant Faridul under section 19A and (f) of the Arms Act and 25B of the Special Powers Act of 1974 finds no leg to stand upon. The seizure of the incriminating articles having not taken place as per law, it cannot be accepted that the incriminating articles (firearms and ammuni­tion) were recovered from accused appellant Nuruzzaman at all. Lastly, Exhibit 1 gives us a light to the effect that the same was pre­pared for the purpose of receiving award from the authority and, as such, the same cannot be the basis of conviction of the accused-appellants. Faridul Mollah (Md) vs Stnte 12 BLC 28.

 

Sections 19A and 19(f)—Though there is allegation that arms and ammunition have been recovered from the possession of the appellant but the Druto Bichar Tribunal has convicted the appellant under section 19A of the Arms Act. He has not convicted the appellant under section 19(f) of the Arms Act in failing to notice that section 19(f) of the Arms Act is a distinct offence. If he finds appellant guilty under section 19A of the Arms Act, why not he found the appellant not guilty under section 19(f) of the Arms Act. This judgment does not satisfy the mini­mum requirement of law. Syed Kaisarid Islam @ Kaisarul Islam vs State 12 BLC 420.

 

Section 19A and 19(f)—Prosecution case portrayed in First Information Report is that Swopan Kumar Biswas, Project Officer, Grameen Arthayan Kendra, having been posted with the information of snatching away of money from PW 16 Tahmina Khatoon, he along with Abdus Sabur Molla on a motorcycle hurried to the place of occur­rence and village people started hounding convict-appellant Alam and Abdus Sabur Molla followed convict-appellant Zakir Hossain. Swopan Kumar Biswas effected a seizure on convict-appellant Alam and Abdus Sabur Molla caught hold of convict-appellant Zakir Hussain. Swopan Kumar Biswas and Abdus Sabur Molla were the most competent persons to unveil prosecu­tion case but they have been withheld by prosecution without offering any explanation whatsoever for their non-production in Court as prosecution witnesses. Withholding of Swopan Kumar Biswas and Abdus Sabur Molla who had been very much important and vital witnesses in proof of criminality of convict-appellants was fatal to the edifice of prosecution case and a presumption against the trustworthiness of prosecution case in bridgment of convict-appellants in the felony arose. Zakir Hossain vs State 13 BLC 705

 

Section 19(f)—It is well scltled that if the evidence of police personnel is unim­peachable and seizure list witnesses make obliging statements to save the accused, the evidence of police personnel may be relied upon jnd conviction can be based on such evidence. The punitive measure in the Arms Act is only for sentence of imprisonment. There is no provision to impose fine along with sentence of imprisonment. By imposi­tion of fine, the Tribunal acted without juris­diction and, as such, the fine awarded to appellant is liable to be rescinded. Altaf Hossain vs State 13 BLC 675.

 

Sections 19t & 19(f)—Of the witnesses, eye witnesses, particularly, PWs 2, 3, 8 and 9 claimed to have seen the convict-petitioner fleeing away from the place of occurrence leaving seized arms and ammunitions. A ref­erence to evidence adduced by the star pros­ecution witnesses indicates that the convict-petitioner was aware of the case and he, deliberately, avoided the proceeding keeping himself in abscondence which disentitles him to get relief under section 561A of the Code of Criminal Procedure.
