Sections—6, 96 and 115

District Judge has no revisional jurisdiction in a matter over which he has no appellate jurisdiction.

All orders are not appealable. Appeal lies only against those orders which are enumerated in Order 43 Rule I C.P.C. Section 96 C.P.C. provides for appeal against original decree. Revision lies against non-appealable orders. Previously revision against non- appealable order lay only in the High Court Division. In case of an appealable order passed by a Subordinate Judge, an appeal shall lie to the District Judge where the value of the original suit in which or in any proceeding arising out of which the order was made did not exceed Tk 20,000,00 and to the High Court in any other case. It cannot be conceived that a Court which cannot exercise jurisdiction over a matter in appeal was intended to exercise its revisional jurisdiction. It appears to be inconsistent with the scheme of the Code of Civil Procedure and Civil Courts Act to invest the District Judge with concurrent revisional jurisdiction in a matter here no appeal lies before the District Judge. Hitherto the District Judge had no revisional jurisdiction, only the High Court Division had revisional jurisdiction. Section 1 15(2) has invested the District Judge with rvisional jurisdiction. In the circumstances e contention of concurrent jurisdiction apars to be inconsistent. The District Judge’s revisional jurisdiction under section 115(2) is restricted by his appellate jurisdiction as provided under section 21(1) of the Civil Courts Act. In other words, the District Judge can exercise revisional jurisdiction under section 115(2) only in respect of those cases here the value of the subject-matter does not exceed Tk. 20,000.00. In all other cases, it is High Court Division which exercises restriction under section 115(1). Apart from section 115(3) the High Court Division, may under section 115(1) exercise its revisional jurisdiction in any case and its jurisdiction is not controlled by sub-section (2) of section 115. In the instant case the suit has been valued at Tk. 1, 00,000.00. For the reasons stated above in was held that the District Judge had no jurisdiction to entertain a revisional application under section 115(2) against the impugned order passed by the learned Subordinate Judge. The instant revisional application was found to be maintainable and competent—Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 (V of 1908), Order 43 Rule 1—Civil Courts Act, 1887 LXII of 1887) Ss. 3, 6, 7, 8, 18, 19, 20 and 21.

Nurul Islam and others Vs. Md. Abdur Rashid, 1 BLD (HCD) 25.

The question of jurisdiction being a mixed question of fact and law, the parties cannot be precluded from urging the point at higher stage even if it was not taken at the initial stage in the trial Court.

Likewise one is not precluded from taking the point of jurisdiction at a higher stage even if it was not taken at the initial stage in the trial Court. Therefore, the learned Advocate for the petitioners cannot be estopped from rising the point of jurisdiction of the Civil Court in a revision case, though the same point was not taken in the trial Court where the trial was held and thereafter at the appellate stage.

Section—10

The, principle of consolidation—implication of—”shall not proceed in any suit,” —intention of the words.

The princip1 of consolidation implies that similarity or identity of the matter in issue in different suits by the same parties should be decided by the Court once for all. The object of consolidation is to avoid multiplicity of litigations between the same parties whenever the matter in issue is substantially or directly the same. The High Court Division in appropriate cases should interfere with the order of the subordinate Court refusing to consolidate suits as the principle of consolidation in no way conflicts with the purpose of section 10 of the Code of Civil Procedure. On the contrary, it ‘preserves and promotes it. Certain meaning of statute must not be imputed to the legislature to create situation leading to an absurd result and offending against common sense. On the contrary, it is the duty of the judiciary to give, as far as possible, an interpretation of law which inspires confidence in all responsible manners.

The words, ‘shall not proceed occurring in any suit” in section 10 C.P.C. are intended to bar separate trial -of any suit in which the matter in issue was also directly and substantially in issue in the previously instituted suit by the same parties in the same Court. But those words do not apply to simultaneous hearing of later and earlier suits after consolidation of the two suits inasmuch as the matter in issue in both the suits being directly and substantially the same.

Rezaul Karim Vs. Jahanara Begum, 11 BLD (HCD) 228.

Claim for dower and maintenance was within the exclusive jurisdiction of the Family Court and the claim for recovery of value of gold ornaments and personal belongings was not within the exclusive jurisdiction of that Court. Since part of the claim was within the exclusive jurisdiction of the Family Court, the suit filed by the plaintiff-opposite party in the Family Court was maintainable as there was no bar in filing the suit in that Court.

Under section 10 of the Code of Civil Procedure, the subsequent suit can be stayed. Under the provision of section 20 of the Family Court Ordinance, section 10 and 11 of the Code shall apply in respect of a suit in the Family Court.

Pending cases in any Court other than the Family Court relating to and arising out of the matters within the exclusive jurisdiction of the Family Court shall continue in that Court and shall be heard and disposed of by that Court. There is no bar under the said provision to withdrew or discontinue a pending case from any other Court and in filing a fresh t in the Family Court for the self-same relief ct The provision is to save the pending cases in any Court other than the Family Cwt relating to matters within the exclusive jurisdiction of the Family Court and not to bar a suit or a case to be filed and entertained by Family Court for the self-same claim.

Section -10

Preceding under a special law and ordinary—Further proceedings of suit under ordinary law, whether may be stayed on the ground of self-same subject-matter and par ties in the two kinds of proceedings.

The proceedings under a special law are different in nature and are not to be tagged with proceedings under any other ordinary law. It does not matter which one was instituted first. The special proceedings under the special law (P.O. No. 129 of 1972) shall proceed independently under the provisions of that special law, no matter whether there is any other pending proceeding under any other ordinary law instituted earlier or later than the special proceeding.

On the ground of self-same subject matter and parties in the two kinds of proceedings, further proceedings of the suit under the ordinary law may be stayed, but not vice versa. A subsequent suit filed under the ordinary law and an earlier miscellaneous case filed under a special law do not stand on the same footing. Therefore, section 10 C.P.C. cannot have any application in the case of two proceedings of different nature.

Ahmed Silk Mills Ltd. Vs. Bangladesh Shilpa Bank, 11 BLD (HCD) 398.

 

Section—11

Res-judicata

Two suits (title suit and partition suit) heard analogously and disposed of b’ one judgment—Appeal against the decree of partition suit only—Whether the decision in The title suit operates as res-jutdicata?

Application of the doctrine of res-judicata to a suit or appeal preferred therefrom is dependent on the nature of the. Suits and the common issues framed in the suits. If the suits are connected and the issues framed therein are also common so as to constitute virtually a single suit on the issues, in such a case the decree not appealed from will not operate as res-judicata.

But when the issue decided in the title suit was not directly and substantially in issue in the partition suit, the decision on the question of title in the title suit operates as a res judicata. The decision on the question of title given in the title suit could not be collaterally attacked in the appeal from the decree in partition suit.

 

Res judicata—Appilcation of the doctrine

The doctrine of res judicata, so far as it relates to prohibiting retrial of an issue, refers not to the date of commencement of the suit but to the time when the Judge is called upon to decide the issue. The rule is not limited to the Court of the first instance, but it also applies to the proceedings of the first and second appellate Courts. The main object of the doctrine is to prevent multiplicity of suits and interminable disputes between litigants.

Mokarram Ali and others Vs. Abdul Sama and others; 1 BLD (AD) 221.

Ref. A.I.R. 1927 (Lah) 289; (1951) SCR 178;37C.L.J. 184;11A11148;33A1151;29 Mad 333; 29 Mad 195; 15 Born 104; 7 PLR (Dac) 209—Cited.

Section—11

Res judicata by implication

To constitute res judicata, whether it is enough that the parties and the matter in issue m both the proceedings are the same — whether a final decision, even without a decree operates as res-judicata?

In order to constitute res judicata it is not enough that the parties are the same and the same matter is directly and substantially in Issue in both the suits or proceedings, but the important condition is that the matter must have been heard and finally decided in the f4rmer suit or proceeding. There can be a bar of res judicata if there is an executable judgment. A final decision, whether right or wrong operates as res judicata even though no decree is drawn up. It is not necessary that there should be express finding on the issue in question where a question has been necessarily decided in effect though not in explicit term. When a question is decided by necessary implication, it would also operate as res judicata—But where issue is left untouched and undecided, bar of res judicata will not operate.

Refazuddin Mondal Vs. Abdul Razzaque alias Rezaul Karirn and others, 11 BLD (HCD) 256.

Res-judicata

Claim for dower and maintenance is within the exclusive jurisdiction of the Family Court—Principle of res judicata—Application of section II of the Code of Civil Procedure in a suit in the Family Court—Pending cases relating to matters within the exclusive jurisdiction of the Family Court shall continue in that Court.

Claim for dower and maintenance was within the exclusive jurisdiction of the Family Court and the claim for recovery of value of gold ornaments and personal belongings was not-within the exclusive jurisdiction of that Court: Since part of the claim was within the exclusive jurisdiction of the Family Court, the suit filed by the plaintiff-opposite party in the Family Court was maintainable as there was no bar in filing the suit in that Court.

Under section 11 of the Code of Civil Procedure, the decision in one case will be binding in other case on the principle of res judicata. Under the provisions of the section 20 of the Ordinance, Sections 10 and II of the Code shall apply in respect of a suit in the Family Court.

Pending cases in any Court other than the Family Court relating to and arising out of the matters within the exclusive jurisdiction of the Family Court shall continue in that Court and shall be heard and disposed of by that Court. There is no bar under the said provision to withdraw or discontinue a pending case from any other Court and in filing a fresh suit in the Family Court for the self-same relief. The provision is to, save the pending cases in any Court other than the Family Court relating to matters within the exclusive jurisdiction of the Family Court and not to bar a suit or a-case to be filed and entertained by the Family Court for the self-same claim.

Abdur Rahman Vs. Shahanara Begum; 11 BLD (HCD) 329.

 

Section—11

Res-judicata when not attracted?

Plaintiff-petitioner a filed title suit earlier against third party for declaration of title to a portion of land covered by the deed of Hiba-bil-Ewaj and for eviction of the defendant of that suit in which his sister-defendants were impleaded as Performa defendants along with others—Suit decreed on contest against the principal defendant and expert against the rest including his sister-defendants—Whether former suit operates as resjudicata?

The entire issue involving the Hiba-bil-Ewaj is not res-judicata between the parties

Abdur Sattar Mir Vs. Morium Bibi, 12 BLD (AD) 296.

 

Sections—15 and 16

Venue of trial

Every suit shall be instituted in the Court of the lowest grade competent to try it. Suits to be instituted where subject- matter is situated.

Md. Mansur Ali Vs. Janata Bank and others, 11 BLD (HCD) 23.

 

Section—16

Territorial jurisdiction of a Court— When its bar may be waived?

The learned Munsif assumed jurisdiction bonafide out of a situation which was fraught with confusion— When a Court having the same pecuniary jurisdiction assumes jurisdiction under such circumstances the bar of territorial jurisdiction may be waived and a decree passed by the assuming Court may be left undisturbed—Such treatment shall by no means be construed as a farewell to the importance of the concept of territorial jurisdiction.

Mst. Rahela Khatun and others. Vs. Saraj Sarkar & another, 4 BLD (HCD) 24.

 

Section—20

Place of suing

Bank Guarantee—Place of payment not specified—Common Law rule that debtor must seek the creditor does not apply—Suit for the enforcement of the bank guarantee lies at the place where it was executed and issued or at the place of refusal to honor it—Contract Act. 1872 (1X of 1872), S. 49.

Janata Bank Vs. Shyampur Sugar Mills Ltd. and others; 1 BLD (HCD) 248.

Ref. C.A. 20 of 1978 (unreported) ; A.I.R. 1961 (Cal) 321; PLD 1956 Lah 341—Cited.

 

Section—20

Territorial Jurisdiction—Question of

Whether a case filed in a Court which had territorial jurisdiction at the time of filing the case could be disposed of by that Court even after the said Court subsequently was divested of such territorial jurisdiction—Transfer of the territory did not affect the vested jurisdiction in a case pending at the time of the transfer—The District Judge, Comilla did not lose his jurisdiction vested with the District Judge, Chandpur on and from 1.9.1984.

Razzaque Tapadar Vs. Taher Hossain Rusdi, 5 BLD (HD) 214.

Ref. AJ.R. 1943(FC) 24; AIR. 1950 (Hydrabad) 71—Cited.

 

Section—20(b)

Return of Plaint

When one of the defendants has its Zonal Office at Commilla the plaintiff was required to obtain leave of the Court at Comilla before filing the suit at Comilla—The cause of action for the short delivery of goods having arisen at Chittagong, the Court at Comilla has no jurisdiction to try the suit.—Moreover, documents of defendant No. 2 being at Chittagong and the witnesses to depose being from Chittagong it is proper that the suit be tried at Chittagong Court—Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 (V of 1908), Or. 7 Rule 10(i).

Islam and Co. Vs. Sarma Chemical Works Ltd; 6 BLD (HCD) 23.

Ref. 39 C.W.N. 174—Cited.

 

Section—20

Local limits of jurisdiction

When leave to sue outside a Court’s local limit units of jurisdiction is not accorded— Such leave cannot be granted on mere asking of the plaintiff and it can only be granted in the judicial exercise of Court’s discretion— Considering the facts and circumstances of the case the Court finds that all the relevant evidence will be available outside the present place of suing and it will cause undue delay and harassment to defendant No. 2 who also represents defendant No. 1 on whom lies the main responsibility for the alleged loss or short delivery of goods—If the plaintiff can establish its claim against them then liability of other defendants will arise— So leave to sue defendant Nos. 1 and 2 in Dhaka Court (when evidence will be available in Chittagong and Chalna) canot be granted. —Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 (V of 1908), Or. 7, R. 10.

Bangladesh Agricultural Development Corporation Vs. Moxey Seven (Chartering) Limited and others, 1O BLD (HCD) 100.
