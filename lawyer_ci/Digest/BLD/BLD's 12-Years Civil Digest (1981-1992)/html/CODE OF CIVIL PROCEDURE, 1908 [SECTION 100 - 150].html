Section—100

Practice and Procedure —Question of reassessment of evidence—When the appellate Court reversed the findings of fact without taking notice of the documentary evidence, the second appellate Court may reassess the evidence and come to its own finding on the point— Misreading of the judgment of the Courts below has led the High Court Division to an erroneous conclusion which requires reversal.

SK. Abdul Latif and another Vs. Abdul Malek Kazi and others, 4 BLD (AD) 15.

Ref. 19D.L.R. 912—Cited.

 

Section— 100

Comparison of hand-writing and signature by the Court—whether conclusion arrived at by comparison of handwriting or signature by the Courts below can be interfered with by the High Court Division in second appeal? —Comparison of signature with admitted signatures is a question of fact—High Court Division is not to interfere with the conclusion of the first appellate Court on such question— Comparison of signature is a perfectly legal mode of proving hand-writing and however inconclusive such proof may be it cannot be considered as an error of law to base a conclusion on such proof alone— High Court Division in second appeal is not competent to set aside a finding based on such comparison—Evidence Act, 1 872 (I of 1872), S. 73.

Abdul Matin Chowdhury Vs. Chapala Rani Sen and others, 5 BLD (AD) 172.

 

Section—100

Jurisdiction in Second appeal—The learned single Judge restored the trial Court’s judgment without disturbing the lower appellate Court’s finding—Jurisdiction of the High Court Division in second appeal is limited question of law, that is to say, whether the finding arrived at by the lower appellate Count is based on consideration of evidence or ii is the product of any misapplication or misinterpretation of law —The single Judge is not clear as to the ground for reversing the decision of the lower appellate Court—In the circumstances the appeal should be reheard keeping in view the question of jurisdiction in second appeal.

Sayet Ullah Vs. Askar Ullah being dead his heirs Jamaluddin and others, 5 BLD (AD) 333.

 

Section—100

Scope of Second appeal—Erroneous finding of fact is a different thing from an error or defect in procedure—There is no jurisdiction to entertain a second appeal on the ground of erroneous finding of fact, however gross and inexcusable the error may seem to be.

Abdul Gani Sonar Vs. Kinjor Uddin Sonar, 5 BLD (HCD) 37.

Ref. A.I.R. 1963(SC) 902—Cited.

 

Section—100

Finding of fact Whether can be interfered in second appeal—The High Courts Division in second appeal can interfere with a finding of fact only on the ground of an error of law or substantial error or defect in the procedure and as such finding of fact as to possession based on consideration of evidence on record cannot be interfered with in second appeal.

Doctor S.M. Amjad Hossain and others Vs. Ekias Au Mollah, 6 BLD (HCD) 63.

 

Section—100

Finding of fact—power to interfere — High Court Division’s power to interfere with finding of fact—It should not make interference on the ground that upon the documents and evidence placed before the lower Court, the High Court Division would have come to a different conclusion.

Sudhir Chandra Sil and another Vs. Hahi Mohan Das & ors., 7 BLD (AD) 288.

Ref. 3ODLR (SC)236; 35DLR(AD)2 16 = 3BLD(AD)342; 38 DLR(AD) 22; A.I.R. 191 8(PC) 92—Cited.

 

Section—100

Finding of fact—High Court Division’s power in second appeal to disturb finding of fact—A finding of fact to attain immunity from attack in second appeal must be based on evidence—A finding based on inadequate of evidence on which no reasonable conclusion can be reached can be interfered with— Misreading of evidence is a ground for interference but misappreciation of evidence is not—A finding inconsistent with the evidence calls for interference—If a decision of the lower appellate Court consists of oral and documentary evidence but part of the documentary evidence is misunderstood, it will not provide a ground for interference when the document does not form the preponderance of the evidence—High Court Division’s power does not include power to review the evidence to come to a finding of its own—It can give a finding not given by the lower appellate Court on the evidence on record.

Md. Naimuddin Sarder alias Namuddin Sardar Vc. Md. Abul Kalam Biswas alias Md. Abut Kalain Basiruddin alias Abdul Kalam Azad and another, 8 BLD (AD) 6.

Ref. 3 BLD (AD) 342.

 

Section—100

Finding of fact—When binding upon High Court Division? —Finding of facts arrived at by the lower appellate Court is binding upon the High Court Division if the former had before it sufficient evidence for consideration— The High Court Division in second appeal shall not set aside such findings although it may arrive at different findings of its own on assessment of evidence on record—There is no jurisdiction to entertain a second appeal on the ground of erroneous finding of fact although the error may seem to be gross or inexcusable.

Nur Mohammad and another Vs. Sultan Ahmed and another, 9 BLD (HCD) 351.

Ref: 17 DLR (SC) 47—Cited.

 

Section—100

Finding of fact —When such finding cannot be interfered in second appeal— Finding of fact of the first appellate Court is final if the finding is based on proper evidence —Erroneous finding of fact is a different thing from an error or defect in the procedure—There is no jurisdiction to entertain a second appeal on the ground of erroneous finding however gross or inexcusable it may seem to be.

Nazir Ahmed and others Vs. Hazee Md. Kalu, 10 BLD (HCD) 92.

Ref. 17DLR (SC)47; 17 Indian Appeals 122—Cited.

 

Section—100

Finding of the first appellate Court — In what circumstances to be rejected in second appeal? —Person in possession, whether should have actual physical contact?

If the finding of the first appellate Court cannot be supposed on the evidence on record, or if it has failed to take into account a material piece of evidence or if it does not reveal a logical basis for differing from the finding of the trial Court or is otherwise found to be arbitrary or capricious, it will have to be rejected in second appeal.

It is not necessary that the person possessing should have actual physical contact, it is enough if it is open and competent to him to assume such relationship with it, whenever he might chose. The physical-relation must be such as to enable him to control the use of the material object in question.

Muhammad Badsha Miáh and others Vs. Soleman Nessa Bibi and others, 11 BLD (HD) 259.

Ref: 21 DLR(SC) 448; 35DLR (AD) 216; 37DLR(AD) 205; 38DLR (AD) 201 (232); 21 DLR(SC) 450:—Cited.

 

Section—100

Occasioning failure of Justice

The term “occasioning failure of justice”, whether has a far reaching significance? — The plaintiff whether must not make the plaintiff whether must not make departure from pleading embodied in the plaint?

The term ‘occasioning failure of justice” has a far reaching significance. It invests the High Court Division with wide power not only to go into the question of law but also to the question of fact, in search of fact and to make scrutiny and analyse and if necessary to assess evidence for itself for its own satisfaction, to see to what extent such failure of justice has been perpetrated.

The plaintiff must not make any departure from the earlier pleadings made in the plaint to bring into existence any new claim or raise any new ground or plea which are quite inconsistent, incongruous or a departure from the earlier stand or pleas. The Court will not allow either party from making any departure from the earlier pleadings set forth in the plaint or written statements. Plaint and written statement are the foundation of the claim of the plaintiff and the defendant; any deviation from the earlier stand would surely land the other side into serious dislocation and by Order 6 Rule 7 of the Code of Civil Procedure, the same has been prohibited.

Bangladesh Vs. Md. Aslam and others, 11 BLD (HCD) 405.

Ref; 34 DLR278; 3ODLR(SC)4 1—Cited.

 

Section—100

Judgment of reversal

Lower appellate Court did not discuss evidence on record regarding possession— Whether can be interfered with?

It is clear that the lower appellate Court did not discuss the evidence on record with regard to possession and, therefore, its abrupt finding that the plaintiff had failed to prove his possession was rightly interfered with by the High Court Division, more so when the trial Courts finding on possession was arrived on the basis of evidence on record.

Shangsharer Nessa and others Vs. Matiur Rahman & others, 12 BLD (AD) 195.

 

Section—100

Misreading and non-reading of evidence

The High Court Division’s power to interfere with the findings of facts of the first appellate Court is limited to misreading and non-reading of material evidence and non- consideration of important documents affecting the merit of the decision. In all other cases findings of fact arrived at by the lower appellate Court, which are supported by evidence on record, are immune from attack in second appeal.

Fazlur Ali Tendal Vs. Fazal Miah and others, 12 BLD (AD) 203.

Section—103

When the lower appellate Court’s judgment is vitiated, whether the usual course for the High Court is to send back the case on remand or to make on exercise under section 103 of the Code of Civil Procedure.

When the lower appellate Court’s judgment is vitiated by misreading or misappreciation of evidence, the usual course for the High Court Division is not to send back the case on remand to the Court below but to make an exercise under section 103 of the Code of Civil Procedure on a fresh examination by itself of the evidence on record.

Section—141

Review—A substantive right

Power of review must be conferred by law—High Court Division is not empowered under the Constitution or any law to review its judgment in writ petition.

The provisions for review made in the Code of Civil Procedure cannot be made applicable to such special proceedings under the Constitution particularly when provisions of the Code of Civil Procedure have not been made specifically applicable to any such proceeding under Article 102. An application under Article 102 might involve civil disputes, criminal allegations, constitutional issues and administrative orders emanating from different authorities of the state and the Government and local authorities. If it is assumed that the power of the Court to review its judgment under the Code of Civil Procedure would be applicable in an application under Article 102 of the Constitution only when such application involves civil disputes between the parties in question, in such circumstances, it would lead to compartmentalizing the discretionary prerogatives and extraordinary jurisdiction of this Court under one specific provision of Article 102 of the Constitution, which could not be intended by the founding framers of the Constitution. The proceeding under Article 102 must be taken as a self-explanatory, self-contained, complete single proceeding which might be civil or criminal in nature involving constitutional or administrative issues from time to time. When a proceeding under Article 102 has no permanent and over-all bearing of a civil pro- ceding, any particular case from such proceeding which involves civil disputes cannot be selected for the purpose of invoking the powers under the Code of Civil Procedure to review judgments and orders. That would lead to fragmentation of an otherwise unified exhaustive proceeding under Article 102. The High Court Division is not empowered under the Constitution or under any other law to review its judgment made in application under Article I O2.—Constitution of Bangladesh, 1972, Article 102.

In re Mrs. Khohinoor Banu and another, 1 BLD (HCD) 155.

Ref. PLD 1970 (SC)1; PLD 1965 (SC) 55—Cited.

Section—141

Determination as to whether a proceeding is a civil proceeding or not

Civil Proceeding,—weather is a process for recovery of individual right or redress of individual wrong.

In order to determine whether a proceeding is a civil proceeding or not, it is necessary to see what the questions are raised and decided in the proceeding. If a proceeding involves the assertion or enforcement of a civil right, it is a civil proceeding. The provisions of the Code, other than the specially excepted ones, shall apply in the exercise of the High Courts jurisdiction in a civil matter, whatever may be the nature of that jurisdiction.

Civil proceeding is a process for recovery of individual right or redress of individual wrong, inclusive, in its proper legal sense, of suits by the Crown. Whether a proceeding is civil or not depends on the nature of the subject-matter of the proceeding and its object, and not on the mode adopted or the forum provided for the enforcement of the right. A proceeding which deals with a right of a civil nature does not cease to be so merely because the right is sought to be enforced by having recourse to the writ jurisdiction.

These were introduced for the first time in the Code of Civil Procedure of 1908 with the object of facilitation the exercise of rights by persons in whom they come to be vested by devolution or assignment, and being a beneficial provision should be construed liberally so as to advance justice not in a restricted or technical sense.
