Section—24

Transfer of suits or cases—Transfer of case on ground of apprehension regarding fair trial

The apprehension must have some objectivity—It is not merely an illusion or a stage of mental attitude—When from the materials on record the apprehension entertained by the defendant are found not altogether without foundation, a case for transfer is made out.

Haji Md. Elias and another Vs. Mrs. Suraya Rahman, 1 BLD (HCD) 147.

Ref. 53 C.W.N. 770; (1973) 11 BLR61— Cited.

 

Section—24

Violation of injunction—Transferee Court competent to try

Transferee Court competent to punish violation of injunction granted by the transferor Court—Court granting an order of injunction includes a Court before which the suit is subsequently transferred and the Court which is in seisin of the entire suit—Transferee Court to which the case is transferred under section 24 of the C.P.C. is competent to deal with the cases of breach of injunction and not to be confined with the Court granting the injunction—Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 (V of 1908); Or. 39 R. 2(3).

Dilip Kumar Chakravorty Vs. Abdul Muyeed Chwodhury and others, 3 BLD (HCD) 49.

Ref. A.I.R. 1914 Cal 815; 61 C.L.J. 543; A.I.R.l962Ca1519; A.I.R .1962 Pat. 443; A.I.R. 1923 Mad. 92; A.I.R. 1968 Punjab (Hariana) 374—Cited.

 

Section—24

Power of a transferee Court

In case of transfer of a case under section 24 C.P.C. the whole case is transferred and as such the transferee Court has seisin over the entire matter of the suit —When a case is transferred from one Court to another it means not only the transfer of the suit but the entire proceeding including the ancilliary proceeding and hence all the matters are to be disposed of by the transferee Court.

Dilip Kumar Chakravorty Vs. Abdurl Muyeed Chowdhury and others, 3 BLD (HCD) 49.

 

Section—24

Transfer from one Bench to another

Transfer of a suit from within the territorial limits of one Bench of the High Court Division by another Bench whether permissible—A Bench of the High Court Division has no jurisdiction to call for the records and withdraw a case lying in a Court beyond the territorial limits of that Bench—In view of the terriorial limits or jurisdiction transfer of such a suit is not permissible—Proclamation (Second Amendment) Order, 1982 (P.O. I of 1982), Para—44(3).

In view of the clear limitation of territory relating to the exercise of jurisdiction by the respective Benches of the High Court Division one Bench is not vested with the power and jurisdiction to call for the records and withdraw a case lying in a Court beyond the territorial limits of another Bench. One Bench is not authorized in law to direct a Court subordiante to the High Court Division within the territorial limits of any inother Bench of the High Court Division to do or not to do certain acts or things.

Mashaid Ali Vs. Begum Shakera Banu and others; 4 BLD(HCD) 91.

Ref. 35DLR I 18A.—Cited.

 

Section—24

Transfer of suit—When should be allowed

Ordinarily the plaintiff as the arbiter litis has the right to choose his forum and the Court should be very reluctant to disturb this right unless the defendant establishes a balance and preponderance of convenience in his favour.

Abdul Taher Vs. Abdul Kader and others; 4 BLD (HCD) 306.

Ref. AIR. 1925 (Lah) 189; A.I.R. 1944 Lah. 400; PD 1968 (Dac) 849; A.I.R.l961 Punj. 560—Cited.

 

Section—24

Transfer of suit by the High Court Division

Whether after the suspension of the Constitution of Bangladesh by the Proclamation of Martial Law on 24th March, 1982, the High Court Division can exercise the power of transfer it had under the suspended Constitution? Though the Constitution was suspended by the Proclamation of Martial Law on 24th March, 1982 by the Proclamation First Amendment Order of 1982 Schedule 4 was added whereby High Court Divisions power of transferring suits under Article 110 of the suspended Constitution was revived— The High. Court Division can exercise the power in regard to transferring suits as it could exercise under Article 110 of the Constitution—Constitution of Bangladesh, 1972, Article 110; Martial Law Proclamation First Amendment Order, 1982 (Proclamation Order No. I of 1982), Schedule clause 4(3).

 

Transfer of suit to the High Court Division—Public importance:

Whether there is general public importance to warrant the transfer of the suit —The point involved in the suit only concerns the parties and not the general public— So there is no point of general public importance— The petitioner failed to make out any case for transfer of the suit to the High Court Division on the point of general public importance— Constitution of Bangladesh, 1972, Article.110.

 

Power of the High Court Division in matters of transfer

Section 24 of the Code of Civil Procedure does not enjoin any condition for such transfer—Such transfer can be made by the High Court Division only upon some established principles and by exercise of proper judicial discretion—Balance of convenience and inconvenience of the parties, bias or embarrassments of the Court, intricate and complicated questions of law of general public importance and any other cogent grounds can only justify such transfer ensuring equal justice to both the parties.

Bangladesh Supreme Court Bar Association Vs. Khondker Abu Bakar and another; 5 BLD (HCD) 1.

Ref. 54 I.L.R. (Cal) 607; Ch. Div... (K.B.) Vol.1!, Page 205; D.L.C. Vol.11(1970) Page I 54;—Cited.

 

Section—24

Pending suits

Question of their transfer for disposal — Whether amendments raising their valuation shall affect disposal by the Courts other than where they are pending—Had the amendment been to the effect that all the pending suits of the Subordinate Judge’s Courts shall stand transferred automatically to the Courts of the Assistant Judges after promulgation of the Ordinance the position would have been different. Here, it is the discretion of the District Judge to transfer pending proceedings—It is true at the time of institution of the suits, the transferee Asstt. Judges were not competent to try them, but now by virtue of the amendment they have become competent to try them—what is needed is that the transferee Court must be competent at the time when the transfer is ordered—The amendment in question is not at al[ mandatory and in that view the impugned transfers have not occasioned any failure of justice—Civil Courts (Amendment) Ordinance, 1983 (II of 1983), S. 2 and 3.

Section—24(1)(b)

Power of withdrawal of appeal

Plea of loss of right of revision—The power conferred by the Code is unfettered and the contention of loss of valuable right of revision or appeal is no bar to the exercise of such power by the High Court Division.

Section—24(1)

Transfer of appeal by the District Judges

Whether a District Judge can transfer an appeal preferred to him from a decision of the Election Tribunal—The cumulative effefct of the relevant provisions in the Code of Civil Procedure and the Civil Courts Act is that the District Judge as referred to in the Local Government Ordinance has ample authority to transfer such an appeal to any Court subordinate to him —The expression “any’ before the expression “appeal” in section. 24(1) of the Code gives the District Judge an unfettered power of transfer of such appeal to any Court subordinate to him except the Court of the Assistant Judge—Local Government (Union Parishads) Ordinance, l983 (LI of 1983), S. 29(4)—Civil Courts Act, 1897 (XII of 1897) 5.22(l).

Section—24

Territorial Jurisdiction

Whether the Court has jurisdiction to try the Suit in respect of lands of 2 schedules (out of 4) which are beyond its territorial jurisdiction—Though the schedules of the plaint included some lands which have not fallen within the territorial jurisdiction of the Court, the Court does not suffer from lack of jurisdiction in view of the fact that the suit was transferred to it by the competent Court of the District Judge.

Section—24

Petition for transfer of a suit

Misquoting of the provision of law in the petition—By a sheer inadvertence the Advocate misquoted section 528 of the Code of Criminal Procedure in a petition seeking transfer of a civil appeal under the Code of Civil Procedure—For such misquoting it cannot be said that the petition could not be regarded as one seeking the intended transfer.

 

Transfer of case-Relevant provision for transfer

The Civil Court Act provides for no provision empowering the District Judge to transfer a case from one Court to another or to withdraw a case from any other Court to the Court of District Judge—Section 24 of the C.P.C. is the sole provision of law which empowers the District Judge to do the job—Civil Courts Act, 1887 (XII of 1887), S. 8.

 

Suo motu transfer of suit—Absence of notice of transfer

When transfer is made by the Court of its own motion no notice is necessary—In this case the transfer was made not on the Courts own motion but on the application of the respondent and as such notice was required to be given—So, in making the order of transfer without serving any notice on the opposite party the District Judge committed an error of law.

 

Transfer and withdrawal of case— Whether error of law in the order of transfer and withdrawal warrants interference? —For entitling the petitioner to have a revision of such order he must not only prove that there was an error of law but also that the error of law resulted in an error in the decision and also that it occasioned failure of justice— There is no cause of prejudice to the petitioner if the appeal is heard by the District Judge himself instead of by the Additional District Judge nor was there any submission on his behalf that he will have any cause of prejudice if the District Judge hears the appeal—Thus though the District Judge committed an error of law in making the impugned order his error did not result in any error in the decision occasioning failure of justice and as such there is no scope for revising the order—Code of Civil Procedure, l9oaV f l9.O8.. S. 115.

Md. Malik. vs. National Bank Ltd. 10 BLD (HCD) 225.

Ref. A, I.R. 1926 (All) 17; AIR. 1933 (All) 178: A.I.R. 1917 (Lab) 37; A.I.R. 1920 (All) 249; 20 DLR (Dhaka) 867 ;—Cited

 

Sections—24 and 151

Analoguous trial

Identical order under certain circumstances may also be passed by the District Judge and the High Court Division under section 24 of the Code of Civil Procedure in the interest of justice. In exceptional cases order for analogous trail may also be passed under section 151 of the C.P.C.

Salimullah Bahar Vs. Sharifa Khatoon and others; 10 BLD (HCD) 283

 

Section—24

Power of the District Court under section 24 C.P.C.

Whether administrative in nature—Lawyers, whether must always be vigilant and careful in drafting and pressing petitions in Court.

Power of the District Judge under section 24 C.P.C. is somewhat of an administrative and discretionary in nature. In suitable cases, the District Judge can even suo motu exercise this power in the interest of justice. Lawyers must always be vigilant and careful in drafting petitions and pressing the same in Court, particularly while imputing allegations of bias against a judge in an application for transfer of a suit or case.

Sree Bijoy Kumar Basak Vs. Narendra Nath Datta and others; 11 BLD (HCD) 325.

Section—34

Power of the Court to award interest

The Court is not bound by the stipulation in the contract itself in the matter of awarding interest it being completely within its discretion—The Court is free to award interest at such rate as it may think fit.

M/s. M.M. Ispahani Ltd. Vs. Sonali Bank and others, 4 BLD (AD) 242.

Sections—35 and 35A

Cost against a stranger to the proceeding

Provision of sections 35 and 35A of the Code of Civil Procedure cannot be referred to for determining the Court’s power to award costs in writ petitions— These provisions do not authorize normally awarding of cost against a person who is not a party to the suit—In exceptional cases the Court may award costs even against a stranger provided he is given an opportunity of being heard— Such hearing must be in the suit itself and not after its disposal—Awarding of costs is no doubt a matter of discretion of the Court, but the discretion must be exercised judicially and in accordance with law or at least the practice followed by the Court—Rules 14 and 15 of Chapter A in Appendix IV to the Rules of High Court of Judicature Vol. 1.

Habibullah Khan Vs. Shah Azharuddin Ahmed and others, 3 BLD (AD) 143.

 

Section—34A

Compensatory cost—Award of

Whether a party setting in motion a frivolous and vexatious writ proceeding by practicing fraud and suppressing the fact of his filing a case before the Election Tribunal should be saddled with cost?

The petitioner obtained the Rule suppressing the fact of his filing and pendency of an election petition before the Election Tribunal—Writ petition is not only frivolous but also vexatious and it was filed to harass respondent No. 5 and to cause financial loss to him for which the petitioner is liable to compensate respondent No. 5—Rule discharged with compensatory cost of Tk. 5000/- to be paid to respondent No. 5 by the petitioner.

Sections—47 and 144

Application for restitution is not an application for execution of a decree, but it is an independent application governed by Art. 181 and not Article 182 of the of the Limitation Act—Limitation Act, 1908(IX of 1908).

An application for restitution is an independent application, no matter its object is the recovery of the fruit of a decree passed earlier but subsequently reversed. Application for restitution is an application for which no limitation has been specifically provided for in the law and as such it attracts the residuary provision of Article 181 of the Limitation Act. A restitution application is not an application for execution of a decree and as such it is not governed by Article 182 but it is governed by Article 1 8 1 of the Limitation Act.

Shamuj Ali and others Vs. Kama-larma Bibi and others, 1 BLD (HCD) 131.

Sections—47 and 151

Review and inherent power of the Court —Absence of statutory alternative remedy against dismissal of objection petition under section 47 C.P.C.— Application under section 151 C.P.C. rejected on an erroneous view of non-applicability of section 151 C.P.C. and non-maintainability of the application thereunder.

The learned Musnif acted illegally and with material irregularity in refusing to restore the objection filed under section 47 of the Code of Civil Procedure.

The submission of the learned Advocate for the opposite parties is devoid of any merit. There appears to be nothing in the Code of Civil Procedure which specifically authorizes a party to make a second application in the facts of the case. In the absence of any alternative remedy available against the order of dismissal of their objection under section 47 of the Code of Civil Procedure, the petitioners, therefore, are entitled to take resort to the inherent power of the Court under section 151 of the Code of Civil Procedure for ends of justice. It is also a settled principle that where a mistake of a Court or Courts officer causes some injuries to a particular party, the power under section 151 of the Code cans be exercised for the purpose of giving necessary relief to aggrieved party even though there is an alternative remedy for doing the same.

The learned Munsif has acted illegally and with material irregularity in refusing to restore the objection filed under section 47 of the Code of Civil Procedure.

Boynuddin and others Vs. Azimuddin and others; 10 B.L.D. (HCD) 267.

Sections—96, 115 and Order 43 Rule 1

District Judge has no revisional jurisdiction over a matter in which he has no appellate jurisdiction.

A Court which cannot exercise jurisdiction over a matter in appeal is not intended to exercise its revisional jurisdiction. It is inconsistent with scheme of the Code of Civil Procedure and the Civil Courts Act to invest the District Judge with concurrent revisional juris-diction in matters where no appeal lies before the District Judge. The District Judge’s revisional jurisdiction under section 115(2) is restricted by his appellate jurisdiction as provided under section 21(1) of the Civils Court Act—Civil Courts Act, 1887 (XII of 1887), 5. 3,6,7,8, 18, 19, 20 and 21.

Nurul Islam and others Vs. Md. Abdur Rashid, 1 BLD (HCD) 25.

 

Section—96

Appeal by a stranger to the suit— Whether a person who is not a necessary party in the suit can prefer an appeal against the decree —The application of the petitioners for being added as a party in the suit was rejected on the ground that he is a stranger to the contract sought to be specifically performed and his subsequent application to be added as a party in the proceeding for setting aside the expert decree passed in that suit was also rejected —Such finding that the petitioner is not a necessary party in the suit would be binding on him and cannot be repaginated for the second time—It could not be said that the petitioner might properly have been a party in the suit or proceeding though he was not made a party in the suit and that he is not merely a stranger and his legal interest is adversely affected by the judgment passed in his absence—The petitioner could not be said to be or ought to have been made a party in the suit or proceeding and has not been made a party and thereby even as a stranger was not competent to file the appeal.

Md. Abdul Hoque Vs. Mst. Zainab Begum, 7 BLD (HCD) 158.

Ref. 21 DLR (SC) 50; (1958) IQ.B. 12(17)—Cited.

 

Sections—97, 151 and 152

Amendment of decree—When it can be done 7 —The Court can always amend the decree if there is a mistake that had crept in for bringing the decree in conformity with the judgment.

Ismail Ullah, being dead his heirs Bazidullah and others Vs. Sukumar Chandra Das, 6 BLD (AD) 251
