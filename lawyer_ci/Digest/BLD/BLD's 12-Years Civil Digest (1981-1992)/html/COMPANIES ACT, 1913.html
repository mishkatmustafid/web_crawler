Sections—32 and 131

Whether the Registrar can withhold acceptance if any return—Registrar has no option but to accept the filing made by the Company—Law does not authorise him to act as a mini—Court to impose his own judgment as to when a general meeting is due—It is his function to operate as a filing house and if he is of the opinion that any Company has violated the provisions of Section 76(1) of the Companies Act, he is certainly within his powers to initiate appropriate legal proceedings—He cannot, however, withhold acceptance of any filing.

Bangladesh Chemical Industries Corporation and another Vs. Registrar, Joint Stock Companies and another, 6BLD (HCD)311

 

Section—34

Shares can be transferred in accordance with the provisions of the Companies Act and the Articles of Association. Section 34 of the Companies Act contemplates two kinds of transfer of shares, namely, under an instrument of transfer duly stamped and executed by the transferor and the transferee and the transmission by operation of law.

Condonation of delay to hold Annual General Meeting: Application for condonation of delay to hold Annual General Meeting of the petitioner—not opposed even in pursuance of the direction of the Court—due to order of status quo passed by the learned Munsif, 1st Court, Chittagong. present Executive Committee elected on March 3, 1986 could not take over charge till August 8, 1986—the delay in holding the Annual General Meeting for the year 1987 within the prescribed time unintentional—no fault or laches of the members—circumstances beyond the control of the Company, members and officers in view of order of status quo passed by learned Munsif, 1st Court Chittagong, present Executive Committee elected on March 3, 1986 could not take over charge till August 8, 1986—the delay in holding the Annual General Meeting for the year 1987 within the prescribed time unintentional—no fault or laches of members—circumstances beyond control of company, members and officers in view of order of status quo passed by learned Munsif—contention convincing——no malafide intention not to hold the Annual General Meeting in due time—Sufficient ground for condonation of delay—delay condoned—Company permitted to hold the Annual General Meeting for the year 1987 by 30th June, 1989—petitioner directed to pay cost.

Section—76( 1)(2)(3)

Condonation of delay—effect of—Delay in holding Annual General Meeting was condoned by the Company Court of the High Court Division, granting permission to the Company to hold the Annual General Meetings for the defaulted years. But the petitioner challenged those meetings to be in gross violation of the provisions of section 76 of the Companies Act. The Court held.

(a) The next Annual General Meeting for the defaulted years was held within one year. Therefore, it cannot be said that the holding of subsequent meetings was illegal or bad.

(b) The condonation of delay has the effect of wiping out of the schedule for the Annual General Meeting provided as in section 76(1) of the Companies Act.

(c) A new schedule would start with the actual holding of the Annual General Meeting.

(d) The period of 15 months would be counted from the date when the Company held its last preceding General Meeting.

Precedent—judgment of the Division Bench is binding upon the Single Bench (Company Court)

Fazal Mahmud Vs. The Pioneer Printing Press Ltd. and others. 1OBLD (HCD)339

Sections—109A and 120

Company matter—Registration of mortgage deed—Question of extension of time for registration—Where the mortgage deed could not be registered within the statutory period of 21 days and the failure is bonafide and due to inadvertence on the part of the Company and due to such omission no prejudice is caused to the members of the Company or its creditors, the extension of the period for registration is necessary in order to protect the interest of the share—holders as well as the creditors—In that view of the matter, it is just and equitable to extend the period for necessary registration.

Sections—162 and 193

Winding up of a Company—Admission of an application by the creditor for such winding up when to be admitted—Mere filing of an application containing some allegations that the Company failed to pay its debts is not sufficient for its admission—Great care should be taken in admitting an application for winding up—It is likely to create alarm in the minds of all creditors of the Company, divert its customers away and to paralyse its business—A winding up order will not be made on a debt which is bonafide disputed by the Company—If there is genuine dispute the petition may be dismissed or stayed and an injunction may be granted restraining the advertisement of the petition.

Sections—162 and 163

Winding up of Company—Preliminary question—Whether the petitioner Corporation has got any locus standi to file the application under Sections 162 and 163 of the Companies Act and the petitioner being landlord comes within the purview of the term ‘creditior’ —

(a) Petitioner Corporation being. landlord comes within the definition of ‘Creditor’ and the petitioner has locus standi to maintain the application.

(b) Petitioner being admittedly landlord and the defendant Company being unable to pay its rent, the application is maintainable and the petitioner is entitled to get the relief.

(C) The respondent Company is liable to be wound up and accordingly it be wound up.

The substratum of a company will be deemed to be gone when (a) the subject matter of the company is gone, or (b) the object for which it was incorporated has substantially failed, or (c) it is impossible to carry on the business of the company except at a loss, that is, there is no reasonable hope of trading at a profit.

Certificate proceeding: The provisions of section 171 of the Companies Act cannot be a bar for continuance of certificate proceedings as the order of liquidation was passed in 1956 and the liquidator did not take any steps whatsoever in order to protect the alleged right of the bank in liquidation in respect of the property— In such circumstances the Court expressed the view that section 171 of the Companies Act cannot be attracted to the certificate proceedings which started long before the date of the order of liquidation.
