Constitution of Bangladesh — Preamble

Interpretation — Role of the Preamble — Preamble is a part of the Constitution and it has got special importance in the interpretation of the Constitution.

The preamble can only be amended by referendum and, therefore, is a part of the Constitution. (Para-48)

Few Constitutions have such a Preamble. Its amenability is rightly protected which can only be done by the people at a referendum. In Indian jurisdiction, the contention that Preamble should be regarded as ‘guiding star’ has been repelled (1950 S.C.R. 88(120, 198). Nor it has been regarded as a source of any substantive power (A.I.R.1960 (SC)845. [Para-501

It is needless to pursue the point further because it has been noticed already that in our Constitution the Preamble can only be altered by the People because our Constitution has proceeded from the people and it is not a rhetorical flourish. Our Constitution is not the result of the process of the Indian Independence Act, 1947 though we have taken inspiration from the wisdom of the past. Our is an ‘autochthonous’ Constitution. [Para—5 I]

 

Per S. Ahmed, J:

There is no dispute that the Preamble is a part of the Constitution. As to the use of the preamble of a statute, it cannot be used to modify the language of the statute if it is clear, plain and unambiguous giving one and one meaning. But if the language is not clear and the words used therein indicate or are capable of bearing more than one meaning, then the meaning which is nearest to the purpose of the statute is to be preferred. [1957 AC, 436 — ref] (Para-353)

Anwar Hossain Chowdhury Vs. Bangladesh, 1989 BLD(Spl)1

 

 

Articles — 1, 44 and 94 to 116

Unitary Character of State — The scope of having more superior Courts are keeping in line with the unitary character of the State.

Per B.H. Chowdhury, J:

The amendment purports to create territorial units which eventually may claim the status of federating units thereby destroying the very fabric of the unitary Republic. In other words, by sowing the seeds of regionalism the next step can be dismantling the fabric of the Republic. (Para — 238).

 

Per S. Ahmed, J:

It is not permissible to create a separate High Court under the Supreme Court as it will run counter to the, unitary character of the State opening a door for its ultimate disintegration As to the unitary character of the State, it is clear that in view of the homogeneity of her people having same language, culture, tradition and way of life, within a small territory the State has been so organised as a unitary State by its founding fathers leaving no scope for devolution of executive, legislative and judicial powers on different regions to turn into Provinces ultimately. (Para —361 and 362).

 

Per M.H. Rahman, j:

It has been repeatedly pointed out before us that because of the unitary character of our Republic, the Constitution makers devised only one Court for the whole of Bangladesh in the Supreme Court of Bangladesh comprising the Appellate Division and the High Court Division. In a unitary State there can be more than one Appellate Court like the High Court, but both in a unitary and in a federal State there must be one Court having full plenary power over the whole State ... Our founding fathers devised a composite Supreme court to emphasise the oneness of the country. (Para — 484)

Article — 38 with Preamble

Nature of the right under Article 38 — The rights under Article 38 of the Constitution to form association are fundamental for civic life so that citizens may, ‘prosper in freedom’.

Freedom of association — The right under Article 38 of the Constitution implies that several individuals having a community of interest can join together to form a voluntary association for furtherance of a common lawful object. Article 38 cannot, however, be invoked for support, sustenance or fulfillment of every object of an association.

Parliament is the supreme legislative authority subject to the constitutional limitations—One of the attributes of lcislative supremacy is its power to pass any law on any subject, not prohibited by the Constitution, both prospectively and retrospectively. It can equally validate, subject to Constitution, any earlier law found invalid either prospectively or retrospectively.

Mofizur Rahman Khan Vs. Govt. of Bangladesh, 2BLD(AD)120

Articles — 85 and 86

Prize Court Fund — Prize Court Fund consists of money payable into the Public Accounts of the Republic — Under the Rules of Business only the Ministry of Finance could move an application before the Prize Court with formal proposal and a working scheme for withdrawal of money from the fund. Article 85 of the Constitution provides that all public moneys which will form part either of the Consolidated Fund or the Public Accounts of the Republic is payable into such Fund or Account or can be withdrawn from under an Act of the Parliament or Rules made by the President. There is neither an Act of Parliament nor any rules by the President which govern the mode of payment of the money constituting the Prize Court Fund into the Public Accounts of the Republic. In the absence of any enactment or rules, which the Constitution has empowered the President to make for the regulation of payment of money belonging to the Prize Court Fund into the Public Accounts of the Republic, the only alternative left to the Government is to get an Act passed by the Parliament or have Rules the framed by the President pending such enactment — Prize Court Rules, 1939, Or. XXXIV.

Bangladesh Vs. Hon‘ble Judge, Prize Court, 2 BLD (AD) 147.

 

Article — 93

Ordinance-its force—In Article 93 of our Constitution the power of the President to promulgate an Ordinance has been given for a contingency when the Parliament stands dissolved or is not in session. Under clause (4) of Article 93 such Ordinance will have the force of law as an Act of Parliament, but this does not make it an Act of Parliament. An Ordinance has got the force of an Act of Parliament as long as it is in force i.e till the expiration of thirty days after it is laid before the Parliament at its first meeting following its promulgation. If the Ordinance making power of the President is construed to give the President power to erase a statute from the statute-book permanently then it will cause violence to the Constitution itself. The President may repeal a statute by an Ordinance, but such repeal shall take permanent effect only when it is followed by an Act of Parliament.

Suranjit Sengupta Vs. Election Tribunal, Syihet and another, 1BLD (HCD) 132

Article — 94

Decentralisation of Judiciary—Constitution of Bangladesh — Part VI — Supreme Court — High Court Division — Decentralisation of judiciary.

Per B.H. Chowdhury, J:

Decentralization of the judiciary is conceivable in so far as the trial Courts are concerned, but such attempt in the sphere of superior judiciary has been deprecated by the jurists far less in the case of the highest Court of the country.

Per M.H. Rahman, J:

It is not clear how the doctrine of doorstep justice could be applied to the High Court Division, virtually the last appellate Court where, except in a few cases of original jurisdiction, most of the Court’s business is done on the basis of records, written papers and submissions of the learned Counsels, and the parties or their witnesses are not required to cross the threshold for coming to Dhaka, except for giving power and for affirming affidavits. For many it is a classic case of harassment from pillar to post, particularly for the litigants from Syihet who had to move from Dhaka to Comilla, from there to Chittagong and then, from there to Sylhet.

Amended Article — 100(5)

Constitution of Bangladesh — Power given to the President to assign areas in relation to which each Permanent Bench shall exercise jurisdiction, power and functions offends the principle of separation of powers.

Per B.H. Chowdhury, J:

This clearly runs counter to the theory of separation of powers and cherished cannon of independence of the judiciary because it is the Chief Justice who is to initiate the proposal for holding sessions for declaring what cases or what class of cases shall be heard at the outlying areas. It is not for the Chief Executive to initiate such a proposal far less for assigning areas for Permanent Benches alternatively (as was done by the Proclamation dated l7th.June, 1986) for holding Sessions.
