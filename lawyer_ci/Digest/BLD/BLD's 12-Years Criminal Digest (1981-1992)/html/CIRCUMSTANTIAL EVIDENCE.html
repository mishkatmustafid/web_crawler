CIRCUMSTANTIAL EVIDENCE

Circumstantial evidence — It is well-settled that when all the evidence is cir­cumstantial, it is necessary that cumula­tively its effect should be to exclude any reasonable hypothesis of the innocence of the accused — The circumstances estab­lished in a case are to be incompatible with the innocence of the accused and are to be sufficient to exclude all possibilities of the innocence of the accused — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 3 Keshab Chandra Mistry and others Vs. The State 5 BLD (AD) 301Ref. 11 DLR (SC) 38

Circumstantial Evidence — In order to sustain a conviction on circumstantial evidence, such evidence must be complete and incapable of explanation of any other hypothesis than that of the guilt of the ac­cused — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 3 Shafiullah alias kala Mia Vs. The State 5 BLD(HCD) 129.Ref. AIR 1952 (SC) 343; AIR 1976 (SC) 917; AIR 1979 (SC) 826.

Circumstantial Evidence — Absence of reasonable explanation — There is no reason to disbelieve the evidence of son and wife of the deceased who are most natural and material witnesses as to the calling away of the deceased after which he was not found till his dead body was recovered — In the absence of any other reasonable explanation as to the safe de­parture of the deceased from the company of the appellants, no conclusion other than the guilt of the appellants can be drawn — Evidence Act (1 of1872) S 3 Anisur Rahman and others Vs. The State 6 BLD (AD) 79. Ref. 11 DLR (SC) 365; 16 DLR (SC) 261 and 33 DLR 320.

Circumstantial Evidence — When not sufficient for conviction — The accused can not be convicted merely on the ground that circumstantial evidence provides a ground for strong suspicion against the accused — If the circums­tantial evidence does not furnish con­clusive proof of the guilt of the accused, she cannot be convicted merely on the probability of having committed the offence — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 3 Alpana Rani Das Vs. The State 8 BLD (HCD) 114.

Criminal Trial — Circumstantial Evi­dence — Degree of probability required — Circumstantial evidence requires high degree of probability, sufficiently high from which a prudent man considering all facts that the life and liberty of an accused person depend upon his decision, is justified in holding that the accused has committed the crime — All facts forming the chain of evidence must point conclusively to the guilt of the accused and must not be capable of being explained on any other reasonable hypothesis. Abdul Wahab and another Vs. The State 8 BLD (HCD) 344.

Circumstantial Evidence — Whether circumstances by themselves were enough to connect the accused with the crime — It is proved beyond doubt that the deceased was last seen alive in the company of the accused, the latter are liable to offer satisfactory explanation as to the cause of death of the deceased — When circumstantial evidence led by the prosecution is quite incompatible with the innocence of the accused, the view that the circumstances themselves are not enough without anything more to connect the ac­cused with the offence is contrary to the time-honoured principle of law regarding circumstantial evidence — Evidence Act (I of 1872) S 3 Rezaul Huq and others Vs. The State 9 BLD (HCD) 218 Ref. 37 DLR (AD) 139; 16 DLR (SC) 261.

Circumstantial Evidence — Onus on the prosecution in a case based on such evidence — Circumstances taken cumu­latively should form a complete chain — Onus is on the prosecution to prove that the chain is complete — Prosecution can not derive any strength from the weakness of the defence — After the various links in the chain of circumstances are them­selves complete then a false defence plea may be called into aid to lend assurance to the court but not that the infirmity in the prosecution could be cured by a false defence — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 3 The State and others Vs. Badsha Molla 9 BLD (HCD) 257.Ref. 5 BLD (AD) 301; 5 BLD (HCD) 129; AIR 1952 (SQ343; AIR 1976 (SC) 917; AIR 1979 (SC) 190; AIR 1984 (SC) 1622.

Circumstantial Evidence — Charge of murder — No eye witness of the oc­currence to implicate circumstances the court has to guard itself against any tendency to substitute suspi­cion or to supply links which are missing to constitute the necessary chain of guilt — In evaluating the circumstances the Court must see whether the circums­tances proved by the proscution lead to the sole and certain inference of the guilt of the accused, whether they are con­sistent with the innocence of the accused and whether inference from the totality of circumstances precludes all reasonable possibilities of the accused being innocent.

The principle for appreciation of cir­cumstantial evidence — Each circum­stance reiied upon by the prosecution must be established by cogent, succint and reliable evidence, the circumstances must be of an incriminating nature or character — All the proved circumstances must unequivocably point to the guilt of the accused and exclude any hypothesis inconsistent with the innocence of the accused — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 3 Mostain Mollah and others Vs. The State 11BLD (HCD) 552.Ref. 1 SCC 530; 3 SCC 198 (202); AIR 1952 (SC) 343; AIR 1954 (SC) 660; AIR 1960 (SC) 29; 1952 SCR 1091.

Circumstantial Evidence — Last seen together — It is a well-settled law that 'last seen together' is a weak type of cir­cumstantial evidence on which a convic­tion can be based, where evidence is nec­essary to find a link between the accused and the murder — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 3 The State Vs. Sree Ranjit Kumar Pramanik 12 BLD (HCD) 284.
