Section    4(1 )(h) —    The word complaint has been defined in Sectic 4(l)(h)ofthe Code of Criminal Proce­dure as follows: "Complaint means the allegation made orally or in writing to a Magistrate, with a view to his taking action under this Code, that some person, whether known or un­known, has committed an offence, but it does not include the report of a police of­ficer."

 

Thus the report of a police officer has been specifically excluded from the defi­nition of the term 'complaint' Solicitor, Government of Bangladesh Vs. Mil Yasin Ali alias Gaddu 2 BLD (HCD) 131

 

Section 4(1 )(h) — Definitions of the term 'complaint' requires that in order to constitute a complaint an allegation re­garding the commission of an offence by known or unknown persons must be made either orally or in writing to a Magistrate — It does not say that such an allegation to a Magistrate would be channelled through a police officer On the contrary, the definition expressly mentions that it does not include the report of the police officer The State Vs. Aynuzzaman 7 BLD (AD) 100.

 

Section 4(1 )(h) — Cognizance of of­fence by Special Judge — Question of validity of his taking cognizance upon complaint made to a Magistrate — When the Magistrate sends the complaint, which is an allegation made in writing or verbally to a Magistrate, to the Special Judge with his comments and if cognizance is taken again by the latter, it cannot be said that the Special Judge has not taken cognizance on a complaint — Criminal Law Amendment Act (XL of 1958) S 41 — Code of Criminal Proce­dure (V of 1898) S 4(1 )(h). Sections 6(1) and 9 — Court of Ses­sion and Sessions Judge — Whether they mean the same thing — By establishing a Court of Sessions the Government consti­tutes a class of criminal court for the pur­pose of trial of criminal cases as a court of original jurisdiction — But by appointing a judge of such a Court, the Government does not constitute a court, it creates an office — The Court of Sessions is a criminal court but the Sessions Judge is an office and they are not inter-changeable — When the Government appoints an Additional Sessions Judge or an Assistant Sessions Judge it does not constitute an Additional or Assistant Sessions Court — There can be only one Judge of the such Sessions Court and that is the Sessions Judge — Code of Criminal Procedure (V of 1898) Ss 6 (1) and 9 Nurul Huq Vs. Eahauddin and others 9 BLD (HCD) 271 Ref. AIR 1955 (SC) 765; 17 DLR (SC) 392; (1973) 1 All ER 576 (PC).

 

Section 8 — Extension of time limit for pending cases — Ordinance No 37 of 1983 extended the period for conclusion of trial of the cases pending on the date of its commencement on 8.8.83 — Charge sheet in the present case was submitted on 16.6.1983 and the case record was in due course forwarded to the Sessions Judge for trial. As such the trial of the case was pending within the meaning of Ordinance No. 37 of 1983 which was intended to save such trials which could not be com­pleted within the time limit specified in Section 339C of the Code of Criminal Procedure The State Vs. Modhu Mirdha 8 BLD (AD) 123.

 

 

 

Sections 9(3), 29C, 31(4), 408 and 410— Sentence not exceeding 5 years passed by an Assistant Sessions Judge deemed to be an Additional Sessions Judge — In view of the full and unfettered operation of Section 408 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, any appeal against any judg­ment passed by any Assistant Sessions Judge deemed to be appointed as Addi­tional Sessions Judge under proviso to Sub-section (3) of Section 9 read with Sub-section (4) of Section 31 of the Code of Criminal Procedure involving conviction and sentence for a term not exceeding 5 years shall exclusively lie to the Court of Sessions and not to the High Court Division as the provisions of Section 410 of the Code do not apply and operate in such a case Shawkat Kayal Vs. The State 3 BLD (HCD) 250

 

Sections 9(3), 29C, 31(4), 408, 409, 410, 439A(3) and 538(2) — Forum of appeal against sentence of imprisonment not exceeding 5 years by an Assistant Sessions Judge deemed to be an Addi­tional Sessions Judge — Under the scheme of the law the Assistant Sessions Judge is deemed to be an Additional Sessions Judge only for a limited purpose for awarding higher sentence in a trial — If it were the intention of the law makers that the Assistant Sessions Judge should be deemed to be an Additional Sessions Judge for the purpose of appeal also, then Section 408 would have been accor­dingly amended — An appeal against such a sentence by the Assistant Sessions Judge shall lie to the Court of Sessions under Section 408 subject to the proviso thereunder Monir Hossain and another Vs. The State 4 BLD (HCD) 250 Ref. 36 DLR 93; 35 DLR 363.

 

Section 9(3) — Jurisdiction of the Assistant Sessions Judge deemed to be an Additional Sessions Judge — Whether jurisdiction is limited to trial of Sessions Cases only — By legal fiction he is to be treated as an Additional Sessions Judge not only for the purpose of trial of Ses­sions cases but also for the purpose of hearing appeals, revisions and references made over to him for disposal. Abdul Latif and other Vs. Nurjahan Begum and another 5 BLD (HCD) 316. Ref. 34 CWN (PC) 230: 121 IC (1930) 532; 1952 (AC) 109; AIR 1953 (SC) 244.

 

Sections 9(3), 29C, 31(4), 409 and 431A— Power of Assistant Sessions Judge deemed to be an Additional Sessions Judge — An Assistant Sessions Judge deemed to be appointed as an Additional Sessions Judge can exercise all the powers and discharge all the functions of an Additional Sessions Judge subject to the only limitation that such an Assistant Sessions Judge cannot pass a sentence of death — He is competent to hear revi-sional applications and appeals Nazir Ahmed and others Vs. Yonus Meah and others 5 BLD (BCD) 41.

 

 

 

Sections 9(3) and 408 — Forum of appeal against sentence of imprisonment not exceeding 5 years passed by an Addi­tional Sessions Judge deemed to be an Assistant Sessions Judge — The law­makers although amended Section 407 Cr.P.C. which deals with the forum of appeal from the sentence passed by Mag­istrates of the Second Class and Third Class did not amend Section 408 Cr.P.C relating to forum of appeal from the sen­tence passed by the Assistant Sessions Judge and Magistrates of the First Class — The consensus of judicial opinion is that notwithstanding the proviso to sub­section (3) of Section 9 of the Code of Criminal Procedure an appeal against an order of conviction and sentence not exceeding 5 years passed by such an As­sistant Sessions Judge shall lie to the Court of Sessions under Section 408 Cr.P.C. subject to the proviso to clause (b) and (c) thereunder when appeal shall lie to the High Court Division Shamsul lludit and others Vs. The State 6 (BLD) (HCD) 367.Ref. 35 DLR 362: 1983 BCR 298; 5 BLD 41; 36 DLR 93; 4 BLD 250; 37 DLR 19; 5 BLD 316; 5 BCR 286

 

Sections 9(3), 17(3), 28, 31(4) and 528(1) — Status and extent of jurisdiction of an Assistant Sessions Judge deemed to have been appointed as an Additional Sessions Judge — Such an Assistant Ses­sions Judge does not acquire the status of an Additional Sessions Judge — He only acquires the same jurisdiction as that of an Additional Sessions Judge in respect of trial of cases subject to such limitation as to passing of sentence — An Additional Sessions Judge is not subordinate to the Sessions Judge and they exercise co-ordi­nate powers but an Assistant Sessions Judge is subordinate to the Sessions Judge — There is only an added authority for him to try cases which were hitherto tri­able by the Additional Sessions Judge and to impose a higher sentence. Nurul Huda Vs. Bahauddin and others 9 BLD (HCD) 271.

Section 12 — Power of Upazila Nir-bahi Officer as Magistrate to dispose of pending cases — Upazila Nirbahi Officer though a Magistrate of the First Class he has been given limited judicial powers, namely, to take cognizance of an offence under Section 190 of the Code of Crimi­nal Procedure, to grant bail and to adjourn the hearing of pending cases when the Upazila Magistrate remains absent but he has not been given the power to hear and dispose of pending cases. Sections 33(1) and 386(1) — Whether a Magistrate can order for imprisonment in default of payment of fine when the of­fence is punishable with fine only — Section 33(1) authorises the Magistrate to award such terms of imprisonment in de­fault of payment of fine as is authorized by law in case of such defautl — Where the offence is punishable with imprison­ment as well as fine and where the offence is punishable only with fine, the imprison­ment in default of payment of fine shall be simple and the maximum term is six months — All courts including the court of the Magistrate got power to direct re­covery of fine when the offence is punish­able only with fine by any of the three methods, such as by issuing distress war­rants or by referring the matter to the Collector or by committing the offender to the prison The State Vs. Sections 107, 112 and 145 — Criminal proceeding for restraining a person from entering into the disputed land — It is different and independent from a proceed­ing for executing a bond for keeping the peace — An order restraining a person from entering into a land is not contem­plated in a proceeding for executing a bond to keep peace — To meet a situation arising out of a dispute over land the Magistrate is required to proceed under Section 145 Cr.P.C. — In the course of such a proceeding a Magistrate is competent to proceed under Section 107 Cr.P.C. to prevent the breach of the peace.

 

Criminal proceeding to bind down a person for keeping peace — Hearing of parties cannot be a substitute for an order to show cause as to why a bond should not be executed — Show cause according to the procedure provided in section 112 Cr.P.C. is mandatory and cannot be avoided even in the case of an emergency. Section 145(6) — Whether a fresh proceeding under Section 145 Cr.P.C. between the same parties over the same land maintainable — An order in such a proceeding is binding on the parties andan unsuccessful party cannot be allowed to start a fresh proceeding in respect of the settled matter — The purpose of such a proceeding is not to decide title between the parties in a dispute but to maintain status quo till the party found not in pos­session by the Magistrate comes up with a decree from a civil court in his favour — Till that is done the order of the Mag­istrate is final so far as the proceeding un­der Section 145 Cr.P.C. is concerned —' To allow the unsuccessful party to open it up afresh on the same grounds as to the right to possess would nulify the finality attached to the Magistrate's Order. Section 164 — Duty of the Magistrate recording confessional statement — It is imperative that the Magistrate recording the confessional statement must ask the maker thereof as to the motive of the con­fession and to ascertain the voluntariness of the same — The Magistrate must ask the accused person as to how long he was in police custody, whether any pressure was brought to bear upon him, whether he was subjected to torture or he was given any allurement or inducement — Besides that the Magistrate must inform the ac­cused that he is not bound to make any statement but if he makes any statement that might be used against him at the trial and that after he makes the statement he would be sent to judicial lock-up and not to the police custody — A Magistrate recording the confessional statement should endeavour to place the confessing accused at ease, dispel all or any lurking fear, inducement and hope from the mind of the accused to enable him to make of his own volition an absolutely free and voluntary statement according to the best dictates of his own inner conscience — Before proceeding to record a con­fessional statement the Magistrate must assure himself and satisfy his own judicial conscience about it and this satisfaction must flow out of the answers given by the accused to the questions put to him by the Magistrate — A mere statement of satisfaction by the Magistrate does not satisfy the test that the confession was voluntary and it may be relied upon — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) Ss. 24 and 28. Abdul Wahah and another Vs. The State 6 BLD (HCD) 390.
