Section 164 — Statement of witness — Use of — Such statement cannot be used as substantive piece of evidence of the truth of the fact stated therein — It can only be used in cross-examination of the witness to show that the evidence given in the Court was false but not to use it for showing that the statement recorded was true — The statement can not be used for filling up the lacuna or for strengthening the prosecution evidence which is other­wise weak. Asaddar Ali and others Vs. The State 9 BLD (HCD) 187.Ref. 1 BLD 433; 2 DLR (PC) 1; 8 DLR 404; 8 DLR (FC) 1; AIR 1941 (Cal) 401.

 Section 164 — Confessional statement — Value of — The accused having been kept in ' police custody for 3 days preceding the recording of his confession, his confession can not be considered as voluntary — The Court first has to find the confessional statement to be true and voluntary and then only he may place reliance on it — Extra-judicial confession made in presence of the police cannot be used against the accused — Uncorrobo­rated confessional statement has no evidentiary value and it cannot be used as evidence against other accused persons Rustom AH and others Vs. Sections 195, 476 and 561A — A criminal prosecution launched by a party to a civil suit against the other party alleg­ing offences alleged to have been commit­ted in relation to a proceeding in the Civil Court is not maintainable in law in view of the bar imposed by Section 195(1) Cr.P.C — Only the Court concerned can lodge a complaint before a competent Court — Moreover, so long the decree passed by the Civil Court remains in force it provides a good defence for the accused appellants in the criminal prosecution — Such a proceeding should not be allowed to be continued and should be quashed. Section 195(1)(C) — Whether sub­section 1 (c) of Section 195 Cr P C has been superseded by the Criminal Law Amendment Act. 1958 — The provision for complaint from Court as envisaged by Section 195(1)(C) Cr.P.C. has not been specifically done away with and as such the general provision of the Code remains unaffected — When an offence within the meaning of the Code is committed in or in relation to a proceeding before a Court, then the complaint shall have to be filed bv that Court — The provision for taking cognizance of an offence on a complaint from the Court has remained unaffected and as such if an offence falls within the ambit of Section 195(I)(C) Cr P C then cognizance can not be taken except upon a complaint from the Court — The Criminal Law Amendment Act, 1958 (Act XL of 1958) Md. —     Sections 197 and 561A — Code of Criminal  Procedure  and Labour Court In the Industrial Relations Ordinance, 1969 there is no enactment relating to the manner of investigation, enquiring into or otherwise dealing with offences and as such the provisions of the Code of Crimi­nal Procedure including Section 197 of the Code are applicable to cases triable by the Labour Court — Where a public servant is not removable from his office save by or with the sanction of the Gov­ernment, the Labour Court in that case cannot take cognizance of the offence without prior sanction of the Government _In the absence of prior sanction under Section 197 of the Code of Criminal Procedure the proceeding under Section 55 of the Industrial Relations Ordinance, 1969 is incompetent and as such the pro­ceeding is liable to be quashed — The In­dustrial Relations Ordinance 1969 — Section 55. Moqbul Ahmed and another Vs. HamidulBari 7 BLD (HCD) 108Ref. 25 DLR 192.

Section 197(1) — Sanction for prose­cution of a public servant — When the bar of sanction can be claimed — Being merely a public servant a person cannot claim the bar of sanction unless he acted as a public servant in the discharge of his official duty while committing the alleged offence. Sudhir Das Gupta and others Vs. Bhupal Chandra Chowdhury 8 BLD (HCD) 246Ref. AIR 1955 (SC) 309; AIR 1948 (PC) 128 (133).

Section 197 — Question of obtaining prior  sanction  to  prosecute  a public servant — The intepretation that such sanction of the Government in terms of Section 197 Cr.P.C could be obtained even after the charge-sheet was submitted but before the trial started is applicable in respect of sanction required under the Criminal Law Amendment Act, 1958 — Criminal Law Amendment Act (XL of 1958) S 6(5) Matiur Rahman Vs. The State and others 9 BLD (HCD) 144 Riff. 32 DLR (AD) 100; 35 DLR 249.

Section 199 — Prosecution for adultery — There can be no cognizance of the of­fence except upon a complaint by the hus­band or by some other person who had the care of such a woman — Penal Code (XLV of 1860) S 497. Nurul Huq Bahadur Vs. B. B. Sakina and another 5 BLD (HCD) 269.

Section 199 — Adultery and enticing of a married woman — Bar of taking cogni­zance in such offences — The bar against taking cognizance of such offences other­wise than upon a complaint by the hus­band is total and complete — Penal Code (XLV of 1860) Ss. 497 and 498. The State Vs. Aynuzzaman 7 BLD (HCD) 100.

Section 200 — Naraji petition — In­formant — Petitioner submitted a naraji petition stating that the police being influ­enced by the accused persons might submit a final report — The Sub-divisional Magistrate called for the case diary without passing any order on the naraji petition, which was for all practical purposes a fresh compliant — When the Magistrate accepted the final report and discharged the accused there has been an implied dismissal of the naraji petition — The Magistrate acted illegally in not giving any consideration to the fresh complaint before accepting the final report.

—  He was not bound to accept the final report and he ought to have considered the facts of the case and the naraji petition — The Magistrate is therefore directed to hold further enquiry into the matter. Syed Abdul Hannan Vs. The State 3 BLD (HCD) 156

Section 200 — Cognizance of an of­fence on complaint — A Magistrate is competent to take cognizance of an of­fence after examining the complainant alone — He commits no illegality in sum­moning the accused only after examining the complainant in the absence of any other witness. Gour Chandra Shamaddar Vs. The State 3 BLD (HCD) 216.

Section 200 — Examination of the complaint before taking cognizance of the offence by the Magistrate — Examination of the complainant under Section 200 Cr.P.C. is not a mere form but an intelligent enquiry into the subject-matter of the complaint carried far enough to enable the Magistrate to exercise his judgment as to whether there is sufficient ground for proceeding against the accused—       Allegations are to be scrutinized thoroughly and if the complaint is found to be false or it does not disclose the offence alleged and it is merely intended to harass the accused, then the Magistrate should not issue process — It is not also proper to make indiscriminate use of the police agency for investigating the complaint Shahjahan Vs. Atiqur Rahman 7 BLD (HCD) 164.

Sections 200, 537 and 561A — Non-examination of the complainant — When not curable — It is incumbent upon the Magistrate to examine the complainant on oath before taking cognizance of the of­fence alleged — It is however well-settled that non-examination of the complainant is an irregularity which is curable and that such irregularity can not always be a ground for quashing the proceeding unless such non-examination caused prejudice to the accused persons and occasioned a failure of justice. Md Jahar AH Vs. Afrajul Islam Chowdhury and others 9 BLD (HCD) 509. Ref. 25 DLR 471, 37 DLR 223 and 227; 17 DLR (SC) 626; 31 CWN 271

Sections 202(2A), 205B and 205C —Case triable by the Court of Sessions — The Magistrate cannot hold an enquiry under Section 202*(2A) Cr.P.C. after issu­ance of process against the accused. Abdul Bari Vs. The State 1 BLD (HCD) 52.

Section 202 — Enquiry — Whether more than one enquiry is permissible — The Magistrate has jurisdiction to direct holding of more than one enqiry into a complaint if the previous eqnuiry does not appear to him to be satisfactory — There is no bar in law in holding more than one enquiry. Nazimuddin Ahmed and others Vs.   The State and another 2 BLD (HCD) 205 Ref. 11 DLR9; 33 DLR 8.

Sections 202 and 203 — Naraji peti­tion — When it should be rejected — When upon perusal of the complaint or the evidence brought on record under Section 202 Cr.P.C. the essential ingredi­ents of offences alleged are absent or there are patent absurdities in the complaint or in the evidence, then the Magistrate should dismiss the complaint but when a prima facie case is made out, he should issue process against the accused — Whether evidences are sufficient for conviction or not can be decided at the trial and not at the enquiry stage. Amir Ali Bhuiyan Vs. The State and others 3 BLD (HCD) 256.

Section 202 — Participation of the ac­cused in an enquiry proceeding — It is now well-settled that in an enquiry under Section 200 Cr.P.C. a Magistrate has no jurisdiction to ask the persons complained against to take part in any manner in such an enquiry. Md Showkat Rabbani Vs. Md Showkat Osmani 3 BLD (HCD) 301Ref. ILR 49 (Mad) 918; ILR 39 (Cal) 1041; ILR 14 (Cal)141; 21 Cr L.J 621.

Sections 221 and 227 — Power of the Magistrate to frame charges — The Magistrate has power to frame charge un­der any appropriate Section of law other than the section mentioned in the charge sheet and he is never bound by the Sec­tions mentioned therein — The Court has wide discretion to alter or add a charge — It is always competent to frame proper charges for offences disclosed in the allegations or in the enquiry report or in the evidence and in proper cases it can amend charge at any time before pro­nouncement of judgment. Sections 225, 237 and 238 — Whether conviction of the accused under Section 381 of the Penal Code without framing a charge thereunder is maintainable — In the instant case the accused was charged under Section 408 of the Penal Code but he was convicted under Section 381 of the Penal Code although no charge was framed thereunder — As the accused petitioner had the ocassion to hear the evidence revealing all the elements of the offence under Section 181 of the Penal Code and he had all the opportunity to cross-examine the prosecution witnesses on the facts and circumstances appearing against him, it cannot be said that he was prejudiced in his trial for want of framing a charge under Section 381 of the Penal Code — Penal Code (XLV of 1860) Ss. 381 and 408 Mahabubul Aldm Vs. Sections 236, 237 and 403 — Prohibi­tion against double jeopardy — The sec­ond trial which the appellant is now facing is with respect to different offence — Since they occurred during the same transaction or arose from the same facts, the appellant should have been charged for all such offences in the previous trial. Sultan Mahmudul Hossain Vs. The State 5 BLD (AD) 203.

Section 238 — Conviction of an ac­cused for abetment of an offence in the absence of any formal charge though charged with the commission of the substantive offence — Law does not provide that in the absence of any formal charge an accused can not be convicted for abetment though he was charged with the substantive offence — It is only as a rule of prudence that an alternative charge for abetment is framed in case of any doubt — In the absence of any such alternative charge no miscarriage of justice has occurred nor the accused prejudiced when his attention was drawn by the Court to the relevant evidence proved against him in his examination under Section 342 Cr.P.C Abdur Rouf and others Vs. The State 6 BLD (HCD) 402.

Section 241A — Discharge of accused without assigning reasons — The Magis­trate is not authorised by law to pass an arbitrary order of discharge of an accused person — He must assign reasons and come to a clear finding that there is no prima facie case against the accused warranting framing of charges and that the charges are groundless — The prosecution can not be throttled by the Court arbitrarily but it must be given due opportunity to prove its case. Haji Azizur Rahman and others Vs. Syeedul Haque Chowdhury and others 7 BLD (HCD) 335.

Section 241A — It empowers the Magistrate to discharge the accused with­out framing any charge if he considers the allegations to be groundless — The pro­vision in Section 241A is not altoghether new in the Code of Criminal Procedure. Previously Section 253(2) Cr.P.C. pro­vided for discharge of an accused without framing any charge if the Magistrate considered the allegations groundless — Section 241A Cr.P.C. has been brought in by amendment by Ordinance No XL of 1982 — By this and other Ordinances, procedure for trial of summons cases by the Magistrate was made applicable to trial of all cases by Magistrates abolishing the distinction between erstwhile sum­mons cases and warrant cases. Nannu Gazi Vs. Awlad Hossain and others 11 BLD (AD) 110
