Section 439A — Revisional power can only be exercised where there is an ille­gality in the impugned order — The order of the Sessions Judge directing a fresh trial without finding any illegality in the order of the trying Magistrate can not be sustain in law. Habibur Rahman and others Vs. Wasefuddin and another 4 BLD (HCD) 169.

 

Section 439A — Practice and proce­dure — An order allowing maintenance allowance to the wife was allowed exparte but the order was set aside by an Additional Sessions Judge in revision and the case was remanded to the Magistrate to allow the husband opportunity to ad­duce evidence — The Appellate Division modified the said order of remand with a direction that if the husband failed to avail of the opportunity of adducing further evidence within six weeks the original order for maintenance would stand — The husband having failed to adduce further evidence within six weeks, the Magistrate affirmed his earlier order of maintenance — On revision an Assistant Sessions Judge deemed to be an Additional Sessions Judge set aside the Magistrates order and remanded the case to the Magistrate for rehearing — The High Court Division set aside the order holding that the Additional Sessions Judge has no jurisdiction to re-open the matter settled by the Appellate Division and that a second revision under Section 439A was not maintainable in the same Court — A Magistrate has got power under Sub­section (6) of Section 488 Cr.P C to hear and determine a case for maintenance exparte if the opposite party willfully neglects to attend the Court. Abdul Moneyem Chowdhury Vs. Md Shamsul Hussain Chomlhury 6 BLD (AD) 128.

 

Sections 439A, 439(4) and 561A_Inherent power — When cannot be in­voked — The extra-ordinary power of the High Court Division under Section 561A of the Cr.P.C. cannot be invoked by a per­son after becoming unsuccessful in an applicaton under Section 439A Cr.P.C. except for specific purposes set out in that Section Shafiqur Rahman Vs. The State 6 BLD (HCD) 69 Ref. 4 BLD (AD) 7.

Sections 439A and 561A — The in­herent jurisdiction of the High Court Di­vision under Section 561A is available even to a party who lost in revision before the Sessions Judge under Section 43 9A — But this power should be exercised sparingly and with caution and only in those cases where such exercise is essential and justified by the test specially laid down in the provision itself. Sections 464- 475 — Plea of insanity by the accused — Procedure to be followed — Onec the plea of insanity is validly taken by the accused and the same is prima facie found as such, the Court is under an obligation to take recourse to the provisions as laid down in Chapter XXXIV of the Code of Criminal Procedure as the Court is not expected to come to a definite finding as to whether the accused is suffering from insanity. Abdul Latif Vs. The State 7 BLD (HCD) 432 Ref. 11DLR5, AIR 1928 (Cal) 775.

 

 

Sections 476 and 561A — The judge issued a notice to a party in a case for making false statement — It being within his statutory jurisdiction, there was no abuse of the process of the Court — It is well-settled that when a Court acts, which is empowered by law, such act cannot be interfered with unless there has been a failure of justice — The application for quashing the proceeding is rejected. A. N. M. Rezwan Vs. Fariduddin Chowdhury 8 BLD (HCD) 224.

Section 491 — The High Court Divi­sion has jurisdiction under 491 Cr.P.C. to interfere with an order of detention passed under the Special Powers Act — Wherethe detaining authority does not comply with the mandatory provisions of the Special Powers Act, it cannot be said that the detention of the detenu is lawful and in that case the High Court Division has ample power under Section 491 Cr PC to interfere with an illegal detention though it purports to be under an enactment providing for preventive detention.

 

The bar provided in Section 34 of the Special Powers Act, 1974 does not pro­tect orders passed in bad faith or in abuse of the Act— Consequently when these is­sues are raised, the same are required to be investigated — The Special Powers Act (XIV of 1974) S34. Sultana Ara Begum Vs. Secretary Ministry of Home Affairs and others, 7 BLD (HCD) 138.Ref. AIR 1940 (PC) 150; 28 DLR 259.

 

 

Section 491 — Preventive detention — If a rule has been issued on an application under Section 491 Cr P C and the same is ready for hearing it is the duty of the Court to hear the matter and pronounce its decision as early as possible without waiting for the Advisory Board to report its opinion to the Government Dr. Md Habibullah Vs. Secretary, Ministry of the Home Affairs and others 9 BLD (HCD) 387.

 

 

Section 491 — Habeas Corpus— Di­rection of the nature of a Habeas Corpus and scope of an application under the Sec­tion — The words "illegally or improperly detained" used in the Section indicate that when a person has not been detained within the provision of any law it becomes an illegal detention — The expression 'improperly' as made in the Section has a wider connotation than the word "illegally" — The word "improperly" refers to cases in which although the forms of the law have been observed but there has been fraud on an Act or an abuse of the process of the powers given to the executive — In such cases it is the duty of the Court to step in aid of the detenu to a set him at liberty — The scope of Section 491 Cr.P C is wider than the relevant constitutional provision. Syeda Rezia Begum Vs. The People's Republic of Bangladesh and others 9 BLD (HCD) 441.

Section 494 — Withdrawal of a crimi­nal case According of consent for withdrawal is not a mechanical process but a judicial act and it should be exer­cised judiciously with due care and cau­tion after proper application of the mind — There must be materials on record to justify the order Abdul Mannan Talukder Vs. Section 494 — Withdrawal of a crimi­nal case ----- Consent for withdrawal is not to be given mechanically — The Court is to exercise its jurisdiction judicially before giving consent This implies that the Court will have to examine the materials on which the Government decides the withdrawal of case — It ,is open to the Court to see whether the Government's order for withdrawal was malafide or made for collateral purposes.
