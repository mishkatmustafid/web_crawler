CONFESSION

Confession — Confession of an accused though not a substantive evidence against other accused, the court may take such confession into consideration and can use the same to lend assurance to other evidence for the purpose of conviction of other accused — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) Ss. 3 and 30. Moharak Hossain Vs. The State 1 BLD (HCD) 286. Ref. AIR 1931 (Lahore)38; 62 IC 570; AIR 1941 (Oudh) 517.

 

Extra-Judicial confession — It is to betaken into consideration along with other evidence — Since other evidence has been found meagre, which also lacks indepen­dent corroboration, the confession of the co-accused, even if accepted as true, cannot be the basis of conviction of the appellant — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 30 Moharak Hossain alias Md Moharak Hossain Vs. The State 3 BLD (AD) 329.

 

Confessional Statement — Not legally proved nor marked exibit — Magistrate recording the confessional statement simply said that he observed all formalities Such confessional state-ment is not admissible in evidence Truth and voluntariness of the confessional statement must be venhed — In the absence of proof, confession could not be admitted in evidence nor could it be referred to by the Judge — Evidence Act (I of 1872) S 24 Mahidur alias Mahidul Islam and others Vs. The State 3 BLD (HCD) 162.

 

Conviction solely based on confession— Conviction solely based on confession is valid in law if such a confession is be­lieved to be true and voluntary.

 

Conviction of an accused solely based on the confession of a co-accused is not valid — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 24 and 30 Abdur Rashid and others Vs. The State 3 BLD (HCD) 206.

 

 

Retracted confession — When the retracted confession is found to be volun­tary and true and not obtained by threat, coercion and inducement, the same can be the basis for conviction — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 25. The State Vs. Abu Bakkar and others 3 BLD (HCD) 240 Ref. 31 DLR 3 12.

 

 

 

Oral confession — Question of verac­ity — When a confession does not indi­cate any injury referred to in the post mortem report being committed while murdering the victim and there being nothing in the alleged oral confession as to where, when and in what manner the murder was committed — Such oral confession not also supported by any independent witness cannot be believed — Evidence Act (1 of 1872). S 24 Sudhir and others Vs. The State 3 BLD (HCD) 293.

 

Conviction and confession of co-ac­cused — Conviction of an accused based on the confession of a co-accused can not be sustained unless it is supported by other evidence — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 30 Safar Ali Vs. The State 3 BLD (HCD) 325

 

Confession of a co-accused and re­tracted confession — The statement of a co-accused does not fall within the definition of evidence as it is not made on oath in presence of the person affected and its veracity is not tested by cross-examination — Though it is admissible, it is much weaker than the evidence of an approver — It is the established rule of evidence as well as rule of prudence that the confessional statement of a co-ac­cused shall not be used as the sole basis of conviction in the absence of independent corroborative evidence — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) Ss. 3 and 30.

 

Retracted confession — A retracted confession may form the sole basis of conviction of its maker but as against the co-accused the evidentiary value of re­tracted confession is practically nil and in the absence of corroboration it is uselessEvidence Act (1 of 1872) Ss. 24 -30 Amir Hossain Howlader Vs. The State 6 BLD (AD) 193 Ref. 76 IA 147; PLD 1960 (SC) 313; 12 DLR (SC) 156; PLD 1969 (SC) 89; SCMR (1970) Vol. Ill 351; PLD 1964 (SC) 65.

 

Confessional statement — Partly in­culpatory and partly ex-culpatory — The court can consider the inculpatory portion of the confessional statement

 

Retracted confession — In law it isimmaterial whether a confessional state­ment is retracted or not If the court thinks that the confessional statement is true and voluntary the same can form the basis of conviction against the maker himself — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 24. State Vs. Masudur Rahman alias Masud 4 BLD (HCD) 228.

 

Recording of confessional statement

—              Form of recording the same — con­fessional statement of the accused re­corded by the Magistrate on a plain piece of paper and signed by the Magistrate at the bottom — Usual form for recording confessional statement not used — Such confessional statement is not admissible in evidence — Defect is not curable even by subsequent examination of the recording Magistrate — Code of Criminal Pro­cedure (V of 1898) Ss. 164 and 364(3). Zaheda Bewa and another Vs. The State 5 BLD (HCD) 9. Ref. AIR 1936 (PC) 253; 20 DLR (Dhaka) 780.

 

— Retracted confession — The confes­sion made by an accused even if retracted can form the sole basis of conviction of its maker if the confession is found to be true and voluntary — Evidence Act (I of 1872) S 30.

 

 

— Whether confession of an accused can be used for corroboration — The con­fession of one accused cannot be used to corroborate the confession of another ac­cused for the purpose of convicting the other accused who have not confessed Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 30. Abdul Malik and others Vs. The State 5 BLD (HCD) 67.Ref. ILR 38 (Cal) 559; AIR 1937 (Bomb) 31; 76 IA 147; 12 DLR (SC) 217; 27 DLR (AD) 29; 60 IC 786 (Lahore); 10 DLR (Dhaka) 155.

 

Confession — Whether examination of the recording Magistrate necessary — When a confession has been recorded in accordance with law by observing all the formalities prescribed by law and the said confession is duly taken and tendered in the Sessions Court, it proves itself under Section 80 of the Evidence Act without calling the Magistrate who recorded it —The two requirements for admitting such a confession into evidence are that it was taken in accordance with law and that the identity of the accused has been satisfac­torily proved — The Privy Council de-pricated the practice of examining the Magistrate and Judges except in special circumstances such as those provided by Section 533 Cr.P.C — Evidence Act (I of 1872) S 80 — Code of Criminal Proce­dure (V of 1898) S 533. Md Emran All and others Vs. The State 5 BLD (HCD) 95 Ref. 3 DLR (Dhaka) 383; AIR 1929 (Cal) 617; AIR 1936 (Patna) 11; 12 DLR (Dhaka) 615; 30 DLR 320; AIR 1936 (PC) 253; AIR 1952 (SC) 159.

 

Confession of co-accused implicating other accused — Its effect — It is much weaker than the evidence of the approver — It is an established rule of evidence as well as rule of prudence that confessional statement of a co accused shall not be used as the sole basis of conviction in the absence of independent corroborative evidence — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) Ss. 3 and 30 Md Emran All and others Vs. The State 5 BLD (HCD) 95. Ref. 4 BLD (AD) 193.

 

Confession — What amounts to con­fession — The statement in order to amount to confession must either admit in terms of the offence or at any rate substantially all the facts which constitute the offence — The confession of a co-accused is not evidence against another co-accused whose guilt is under consideration — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) Ss 3 and 30 Abdul .Mil and others Vs. the  State 5 BLD (HCD) 137. Ref. AIR 1976 (SC) 1167; 25 DLR 41; 76 IA 147; 12 DLR (SC) 156; 27 DLR (AD) 29; 29 DLR (SQ271; 1952 SCR (Ind) 526

 

Retracted confession — It's Value — It is now well settled that conviction can be based on retracted confession alone if it is voluntary and true, though as a matter of paidence some corroboration may be asked for — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 30 Abdul J alii and others Vs. The State 5 BLD (HCD) 137.

 

Confession of co-accused — Whether evidence against other accused persons — Confession of a co-accused is not evi­dence within the meaning of section 3 of the Evidence Act — Confession of a co-accused may be taken into consideration along with other evidence — In the ab­sence of any other evidence a court can­not take into consideration the confession of a co-accused and conviction cannot be based on the sole basis of confession of a co-accused—Evidence Act (1 of 1872) Ss. 3 and 30. Mahmud Ali and others Vs. The State 5 BLD (HCD) 218 Ref. 76 TA 147; 12 DLR (SC) 156; 1952 SCR (Ind) 526; 27 DLR (AD) 29; 29 DLR (SC) 271; 4 BLD (AD) 193.

 

Confession by the accused — When can be relied upon — Confession means admission of certain facts which constitute an offence committed by the maker thereof who is charged with the offence and which is the subject-matter of the statement— Confession should be directly on thepoint admitting facts — No state­ment that contains self-exculpatory matter can amount to confession — Confession must either admit in terms of the offence or at any rate substantially all facts which constitute the offence — Evidence Act, (1 ofl872)S25.

 

Duty of the Magistrate recording confessional  statement   —It is imperative that the Magistrate recording a confessional statement must ask the maker thereof as to the motive of the confession and to ascertain the volun­tariness of the same and he must ask the accused person as to how long he was in police custody, whether any pressure was brought to bear upon him, whether he was subjected to torture or he was given any allurement or inducement — Besides that, the Magistrate must inform the accused that he is not bound to make any statement but if he makes any statement that might be used against him at the trial and that after he makes the statement he would be sent to judicial lock-up and not to the police custody — The Magistrate should endeavour to place the confessing accused at ease, dispel all or any lurking fear, inducement and hope from the mind of the accused which enable him to make of his own volition an absolutely free and voluntary statement according to the best dictates of his own inner conscience — Before proceeding to record a confes­sional statement the Magistrate must as­sure himself and satisfy his own judicial conscience about  it and this satisfaction must flow out of the answers given by the accused to the questions put to him by the Magistrate — A mere statement of satisfaction by the Magistrate does not prove that the confession was voluntary — Code of Criminal Procedure (V of 1898) S 164 — Evidence Act ( 1 of 1872) Ss. 24 and 28. Abdul Wahab and another Vs. The State 6 BLD (HCD) 390

 

Retracted confession — Its value — On consideration of the evidence on re­cord if it is found that a confession is vol­untary and true, conviction of the maker himself can solely be based upon the same, no matter whether it is retracted or not — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 30.

 

Confessional statement — Partly ex­culpatory and partly in-culpatory — When a confessional statement of an accused contains both exculpatory and inculpatory statements, whether it must either be accepted or rejected as a whole — In­culpatory part of the statement can be accepted rejecting the other part when there is corroborative evidence in support of the inculpatory part — Evidence Act (1 of 1872)S 30.

 

Confession of a co-accused — Con­viction of the co-accused can not be solely based on the retracted confession of an­other co-accused — The value of the re­tracted confession of an accused against a co-accused is practically nil and he can­not be convicted unless the said confes­sion is corroborated in material particulars by independent evidence on record — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 30. AH Ajgar and another Vs. The State 6 BLD (HCD) 436 Ref. 16 DLR (SC) 598; 11 DLR (WP) 58;4DLR311

 

Confessional statement against co-ac­cused — Confessional statement of the co-accused against another co-accused can be taken into consideration in a lim­ited manner — It cannot be made the foundation of the conviction in the absence of any other reliable evidence tending to prove the guilt of the accused—Such a confession may be used only in support of other evidence for corrobora­tion — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 30. The State Vs. Lalu Miah and another 7 BLD (AD) 212. Ref. AIR 1969 (SC) 422; 27 DLR (AD) 29; AIR 1952 (SC) 1; AIR 1939 (PC) 47; AIR 1952 (SC) 343

 

Confession its worth — It is no con­fession but a mere testimony by an ac­cused against the other accused when the maker does not involve himself in any part of the commission of the alleged offence—The prosecution should have examined the confessor as a witness instead of making him an accused so that the other accused could cross-examine him — If the direct oral evidence does not qualify to be trustworthy, the alleged confession can be of no avail for sustaining convic­tion — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 30 Ibrahim Motlah and others Vs. The State 7 BLD (AD) 248.

Value of retracted confession — Re­daction of confession is wholly immaterial if if is found that it was voluntary as well as true — The confessional statement being true and voluntary it is alone sufficient for conviction of the accused___Code of Criminal Procedure (V of 1898) S 164 — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 30  Bhagahan Chandra Chakma Vs. Confession — Value of— Whether the objection that the Magistrate before recording the confession did not inform the appellant that he would not be re­manded to police custody even if he did not make any confession, renders the con­fession unacceptable — There is no re­quirement of law to inform the accused as above — Of course, if the Magistrate has any reason to believe that the accused is apprehensive of the police, he may assure him as above, but that is not to say that if it were not said, the voluntariness of the confession would be' in doubt — Code of Criminal Procedure (V of 1898) S 164 Dipak Kumar Sarkar Vs. Retracted confession — Its value — A judicial confession, whether retracted or not retracted, can in law validly form the basis of conviction of the maker of the confession but as a matter of prudence and caution , which has become virtually a rule of law, the court does not generally consider it safe to base a conviction on a retracted confession alone without cor­roboration in material particulars — Evidence Act (I of 1872) S 30 '

 

Requirements of a confessional state­ment — The court must affirmatively satisfy itself that the statement is voluntary and true — There must, be me­ticulous observance of all necessary for- malities and precautions provided in Sec­tion 164(3) of the Code of Criminal Pro­cedure with minute particularity so as to ensure that the confession is absolutely free from the slightest tinge or extraneous influence — Before recording a confes­sion a Magistrate is bound to make a real and substantial enquiry as to the voluntari­ness of the confession — The Magistrate must put questions with a view to finding out the real object of the confession — Code of Criminal Procedure (V of 1898) S 164 Habibur Rahman alias Hahib Vs. The State 6 BLD (HCD) 210 Ref. 5 BLD 9, AIR 1936 (PC) 253.

Extra-judicial confession — Its value By its very nature it is a very weak type of evidence — It is unsafe to base conviction of an accused on his extra­judicial confession alone — The value of such confession as evidence depends on the veracity of the witnesses to whom it was made — In a case of such confession material corroboration by evidence of unimpeachable character is required — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 24 The State and others Vs. Statement of accused before police

 

— When it is admissible in evidence — Such statement as far as it leads to the discovery of the dead body is a relevant fact for finding out the clue to the crime and hence admissible in evidence — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 27 Hazrat Ali and another Vs. Confessional statement — When can be the sole basis for conviction — Conviction can be based solely on the confession of the maker of it if it is found voluntary and true though the rule of pru­dence may require some sort of corrobo­ration in the given facts — It is not necessary that each and every circum­stance regarding the maker's complicity must be corroborated — It is enough if the general trend of confession is substan­tiated — The court has to examine the confession in the light of the materials on record and the broad probabilities of the case.

 

Retracted Confession — Once a confession if found to be true and volun­tary, conviction can be based solely on the confession even if it is retracted — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 2.4. Confession by a co-accused — Re­tracted during trial and partly inculpatory and partly exculpatory — Whether such confessional statement can be relied upon and upto what extent — When the con­fessional statement of a co-accused is found to be voluntary and partly exculpa­tory and partly inculpatory, the exculpa­tory part being improbable, contrary to reason and ordinary human conduct and as such false, is liable to be rejected and the inculpatory part can be relied upon even if the confessional statement is subsequently retracted by the accused during his trial — Inculpatory part of such a confessional statement can also be used against other co-accused jointly tried for the same offence — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) Ss 22-30 Ahul Kashem ami another Vs. The State 10 BLD (HCD) 309. Ref. 7 BLD (AD) 212; 39 DLR (AD) 117

Confession by co-accused — Section 30 of the Evidence Act contemplates that a confession made by a co-accused in a joint trial for the same offence affecting himself and others may be taken into con­sideration — In other words, the con­fession of a co-accused may lend assu­rance to the other evidence on record but the confession of a co-accused cannot lend any additional assurance to substantive evidence — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 30 Babor Ali Molla and others Vs. The State 11 BLD (AD) 256.

The State 11 BLD (AD) 270.
