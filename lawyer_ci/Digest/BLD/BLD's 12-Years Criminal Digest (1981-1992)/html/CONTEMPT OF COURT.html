Contempt of Court — Sessions Judge rejecting prayer of an accused for a dis­charge warrant — Suit against the Judge claiming compensation — Plaint contain­ing statements imputing unfitness, corrup­tion, malafide, misuse of official power and malice — If the plaintiff had any grievance against the rejection of the prayer for discharge warrant, his remedy was to move the superior court but filing of the suit and that too containing scandalous statements against the Judge constitutes contempt of court

 

Characterization of remarks made in a judicial order as "malicious" in an appli­cation for leave to appeal is clear con­tempt.

 

Contempt of Court — Appeal against rejection of plaint in a suit filing of which constitutes contempt of court is calculated to re-open the matter and is nothing but continuation of the suit — Filing of the appeal constitutes contempt of court al­though the memorandum of appeal does not contain any contemptuous statement.

 

Contempt of Court — Duty of the drafting and filing lawyers — Lawyers should exercise great care and caution in drafting and filing application or plaint in the Court and they are to see that it does not contain anything reflecting upon the integrity of the Court.

 

Judiciary is the upholder and preserver of the liberties and rights of the people and nothing should be done to lower the judiciary in the estimation of the people Nobody should be allowed to obstruct the administration of justice, more so by law­yers, who are the integral part of the court and it is the part of their duty to uphold the dignity and prestige of the Court.

 

Contempt of Court — Expression of view by the referring judge — Judge making reference to the High Court Divi­sion should only narrate the facts and send the materials in support thereof leaving other materials for disposal by the High Court Division — Expression of views by the referring judge about the sentence to be imposed is depricated — Contempt of Courts Act (XII of 1926) S 2. The State Vs. Abdul Majid Munshi and others 1 BLD (HCD) 278.

 

Contempt of Court — Punishment for disobedience to Court's order by a public servant — The affidavit showed defiant justification of his role — It is time that the penal law of the land be applied in such cases for deterrent impact upon the public servant who chooses to disobey the orders of the Court — Punishment under the Contempt of Courts Act is not stringent enough for such glaring cases of wilful disobedience — The delinquent should be prosecuted under Section 217 of the Penal Code — Penal Code (XLV of 1860 ) S 217. Southern Fisheries Ranong Corporation and others Vs. king Fisheries Industries and others 1 BLD (HCD) 487.

 

Contempt of Court — Qtiesuon of apology — An apology however un­qualified does not by itself purge con­tempt — It can only be considered in mitigation of the offence.

 

Position of Lawyers   in relation to contempt — Lawyers are officers and integral parts of the Court — They owe a duty not merely to the clients but a/so to the court — The lawyers should exercisepetitions, plaints, etc and must avoid which contain allegations reflecting upon the integrety of the judiciary Contempt of Court — Notice issued to the Magistrate in a pending case amounts to interference with the admini­stration of justice — Notice asking him to pay to the Advocate or his client damages amounts to blackmailing and makes contempt all the more serious Contempt of Court Act (XII of 1926) The State Vs. Sharafat Hossain Chowdhury and another 2 BLD(HCD) 168.

 

Contempt of Court — Meaning of contempt — It may be constituted by any conduct that brings authority of the Court into disrespect or disregard or undermines its dignity and prestige — Scandalizing the Court is the worst kind of contempt — In making imputations touching the impar­tiality and integrity of a Judge or making sarcastic remarks about his judicial com­petence is a also a contempt.

 

The essence of contempt is an action or reaction amounting, to an interference with or obstruction to or having a tendency to interfere with or obstruct due administra­tion of justice.

 

Practice and procedure in a contempt proceeding — Nature of — It is quasi-criminal in nature and the contemner is entitled to the benefit of doubt — Rule as to proof of guilt of the contemner must he strictly observed as the Court is both the prosecutor of judge.

 

Bias - It denotes a departure from even-handed justice — It is well-settled that not only bias but even a real likeli­hood of bias demands that the cause or matter should not be heard by the judge concerned

Dignitary and authority of the court— These are to be protected by all par­ties, lawyers and other officers of the Court — The judges are themselves re­quired to protect and maintain their own dignity by proper conduct and behavior in and outside the court — They must regulate their behavior and dealings in such a way that there is no occasion of speculation or criticism about their free and fair mind and impartiality — They must guard their position zealously but they should not be touchy about it.

 

Discourtesy to the Court — It is not every act of discourtesy to the court by the counsel that amounts to contempt — Absence of the counsel on the hearing or judgment may amount to discourtesy but not contempt. Moazzam Hossain, Deputy Attorney -General Vs. The State 3 BLD (AD) 251 Ref. 5 DLR (PC) 540.

 

Contempt of Court — Deliberate and intentional disobedience of Court's order amounts to contempt of court but casual, accidental or unintentional disregard of the court's order may not constitute con­tempt of court — It is not a single act or omission for which the respondent has been charged — It is not his case that he misunderstood the order of the court or that there was any ambiguity therein — Had he any genuine intention to act ac­cording to Court's order he could have sought clarification during the long period In the circumstances there is hardly any scope for holding that the violation were committed unintentionally or it was due to misunderstanding of the Court's order.

 

Nature of contempts of courts pro­ceeding — Though a contempt proceed­ing is quasi-criminal in nature, the con­temner is not like an accused in a crimi­nal case, since he may file affidavit or make statements on oath in refutation of the allegation against him. Mahbubur Rahman Sikder and others Vs. Mujibur Rahman Sikder and others 3 BLD (AD) 317. Ref. 1944 ILR (Lahore) 196.

 

Contempt of Court — Absence of the Advocate on the date of hearing without permission of the Court and making de­liberate false statement regarding illness for not appearing on the dates fixed for the case and sending telegram and letter direct to the Court seeking adjournment All these acts constituted willful dis­obedience, discourtesy and utter contempt of court The State Vs. Moazzam Hossain, DAG 3 BLD (HCD) 232.

Clause 'h'-Martial Law Regula­tion No. 1 1 1 of 1983 Article IV. Kalilur Rahman Vs. Mr. .lamsed Ali and others 3 BLD (HCD) 298.

 

Action for violation of Court's order__The Appellate Division passed order for keeping a vessel under attachment — Whether the High Court Division is com­petent to take action for violation of such order — After the order was passed by the Appellate Division in the civil mis­cellaneous petition, the appeal before the High Court Division was rendered infruc-tuous — The impugned order which may be argued to have merit seems to have been passed without any competence to do so — Its legality or illegality therefore loses all significance — Anxiety to do justice can not have free play so as to en­able the court to overcome the barrier of competence Md Muzzaffar Hussain Vs. King Fisheries Industries Ltd. 4 BLD (AD) 237.

Contempt of Court — Obstructing course of justice by an executive officer — An executive officer himself exercising judicial powers in criminal matters has the temerity to obstruct the course of justice by such acts which exhibit scant respect for a court and as such rightly convicted for contempt of court.

 

Apology — Tendering apology to purge contempt of Court — Whether apology tendered by the contemner in ap­peal should be accepted — The applica­tion tendering apology is all too late in the day after every conceivable defence has been taken — Acceptance of apology tendered not at the earliest opportunity showing sincere regret would amount to opening and not closing the door of scan­dalizing the court — In this case the ap­pellant committed contempt of court — He ought to have thrown himself at the mercy of the Court as soon as the rule for contempt of court was served on him — After he was convicted for contempt of court he proceeded with the appeal — His action cannot be condoned as the same is not only humiliating to the presiding officer of the Court but it also amounts to clear defiance of the majesty of law Abdul Karim Sarker Vs. The State and another 6 BLD (AD) 210. Ref. PLD 1970 (SC) 350; AIR 1983 (SC) 1151; AIR 1984 (SC) 1374

 

Contempt of Court — Upazila Nirbahi Officer is guilty of contempt of court for sitting by the side of the Munsif-Magistrate and telling him publicly as tohow to conduct a criminal case and also for asking the bench officer of the Court not to work in the Court of Munsif -Mag­istrate — These are contemtuous disre­gard of the dignity of the court of law and gross interference with the working of the Court — The sight of an executive officer lecturing a member of the judiciary in the open court and creating hindrances in his smooth functioning is too much to be allowed with impunity — The execu­tive arms of the Government will not be allowed to attack or deface the honour, dignity, majesty and independence of the judicial organ of the state — If they are allowed then in no time the remaining civilised fabric of our society will collapse The UNO has no supervisory, advisory or administrative jurisdiction over the Munsif-Magistrate. The State Vs. Abdul Karim Sarkar 6 BLD (HCD) 284.  

 

 

 

Contempt of Court — Unconditional and unqualified apology — Apology, however unqualified and sincere, does not necessarily purge the contemner — Apology may be considered in awarding lenient sentence. Magistrate, Baliadanga Upazila Vs. F. R. Chowdhury, Former SP, Thakurgaon and others 6 BLD (HCD) 301.

 

Violation of stay order — Contempt when lies — Since the appeal from which the contempt petition has arisen has been dismissed, the petitioner has no legs to stand upon — As to the merit in the petition, at the time of admission the ap­pellant was satisfied by obtaining a short order of stay with respect to the High Court's Order — Effect of this court's order on the orders passed by the High Court Division or on the respondents does not appear to be clear and leaves scope for wrong understanding — Having re­gard to the circumstances, it would not be reasonable to entertain the application — The Contempt of Courts Act (XII of 1926) S 2 Charanawip Bhumihin Krishi Samahaya Shamity Limited Vs. The Deputy Commissioner, Cox's Bazar and others 8 BLD (AD) 62.

 

Contempt of Court in an Aribitration Proceeding — The High Court Division is to exercise the jurisdiction in respect of contempt committed before Courts sub­ordinate to it or before itself — As the Arbitrator is not a Court at all there can be no question of his being subordinate to the High Court Division — The opposite parties can not therefore be proceeded against by the High Court Division on an allegation of contempt of the Arbitrator — The Contempts of Courts Act (XII of 1926) S 2(1) Begum Lutfunnessa Vs. Nizamuddin Ahmed and others 8 BLD (HCD) 357. Ref. 38 DLR (AD) 172; 20 DLR 599; 25 DLR 440; 26 DLR 255

Contempt of Court — Contempt com­mitted by writing a letter to the Editor imputing collective dishonesty to the en­tire class of Judges — Contemner No-1, real or fictitious, can not escape the proc­ess of law — He has committed a gross contempt of court — Contempts of Courts Act (XII of 1926) S 2 M. A. Monir Vs. The State 8 BLD (HCD) 434

 

Contempt of Court — When apology is not acceptable — The contemner police officer did admit the orders of the Magistrate upon which the contempt pro­ceeding had arisen and at the same time he admits his guilt and tenders uncondi­tional apology — The question of apology arises only when the contemner uncondi­tionally confesses his guilt and then ten­ders apology — In the present case there was no proper apology previous orders to him and thereby threw all decorum and decency to the wind.

 

Involvement of a police officer as con­temner is a case for added concern — The police are not only the law enforcing agency of the administration but they are also the arms of the Court — The court can enforce its orders and decisions only through the police — If the police flout the orders of the court then the whole legal system is bound to collapse — The contemner committed contempt of the worst type and his apology is really no apology in the eye of law — Contempts of Courts Act (XII of 1926) S 3 Md. Abdur Razzaque, Magistrate Vs. Sheikh Serajul Islam OC, Sailkupa 9 BLD (HCD) 298

 

Contempt of Court — Failure to act in aid of the Supreme Court may lead to contempt — Since the delay in imple­menting the court's advance order of re­lease of the detenus cannot be explained in terms of any provision of the Jail Code or in terms of Government's instruction, the contemners have acted not in aid of the Supreme Court in releasing the detenus but in aid of the Home Ministry in facilitating the making and serving of a fresh order of detention — The con­temners have thus disobeyed the orders of the Court and are guilty of the Contempt of Court — Contmept of Courts Act (XII of 1926) — Constitution of Bangladesh, 1972 Art 112 Amit Kumar Banik and another Vs. Manjurul Karim, Jailor, Dhaka Central Jail and another 10 BLD (HCD) 5.

 

Contempt of Court — Effect of con­tempt to be taken into consideration — In contempt matters the intention of the contemners is not relevant — It is the effect of the contemner's action which is to be taken into consideration in deciding whether a contempt was committed or not — The effect of the contemner's action is that the Court's order has been flouted — The Court cannot be a silent spectator in this state of affairs. Mrs. Tahera Nargis Syed Vs. Mr. Shamsur Rahman, DIG of Prisons, Dhaka Central Jail 10 BLD (HCD) 73.

 

Contempt of Court — Apology — When a contemner tenders apology as an act of contrition, the Court must weigh the apology in awarding punishment — If the apology is found to be a real act of contrition, no action need be taken and a word of warning may be enough but if the apology is qualified, hesitating and sought to be used as a device to escape the con­sequences of the contemner's actions, it must be rejected — Contempt of Courts Act (XII of 1926) Mr. Shamsur Rahman, Deputy Inspector General of Prisons, Dhaka Central Jail Vs. Mrs. Tahera Nargis Syed and another 12 BLD (AD) 150.

 

The contempt proceeding — Nature of — A contempt matter is in the nature of a criminal proceeding and as such the contmner stands in the position of an accused. The allegation of contempt, therefore, must be proved by the prosecution and in the case of any doubt its benefit shall go to the accused — If no evidence is led, the allegation must fail ex­cept in a case of contempt committed in the very presence of the judge — In other cases, such as where an order of the Court has been violated elsewhere, then the act of violation is to be proved by evidence — In such a case the Court must act on the evidence and cannot re­cord a conviction by mere perusing the affidavit and counter affidavit of the con­testing parties. Md Fazlur Rahman Molla Vs. Bangladesh and another 12 BLD (AD) 220.

 

Contempt of Court — Necessity of knowledge — As soon as the executing Court comes to know of the stay order either by receiving communication of the Court passing the stay order or from an affidavit from one of the parties of the proceeding or in other way, it will stay hands till further order and, if he does not do so, he will be liable for contempt of court — No action for contempt can be taken against an executing court if it car­ries on execution in ignorance of the order of stay — Where there is no justification for doubting the authenticity of an order of the superior Court, it is the duty of the subordinante Court to carry out that order and it cannot take up the plea that as the order had not been officially commu­nicated, it was at liberty to ignore it.

 

Apology is to be offered unequivocally and at the earliest opportunity — Abe-lated apology hardly shows the contrition which is the essence of the purging of the contempt — One who has the courage of his conviction may, however, take the risk and run the gauntlet of proving that he is not in contempt.

 

In deciding a serious question as to whether there is a contmept of court or not the accuser as well as the judge of the accusation is to act with as great circum­spection as possible making all allowances for errors of judgment and difficulties the contemner might have suffered in the facts of the case — It is only in a clear case of contemptuous conduct, not explainable otherwise, that the contemner must be punished.

 

When an Advocate's information is dis­regarded by the Court, a reason must be given so that the exercise of discretion may be scrutinised by the superior Court — Contempt of Court Act (XII of 1926) Chairman, Kushtia Co-operative Indus­trial Union Ltd Vs. Md Mujibur Rahman and others 12 BLD (AD) 227.

 

Law of Contempt — In a contempt matter a Court should not be touchy and no one should be held guilty of contempt of court unless there is a wilful disrespect to a court's order with full knowledge of it.

 

A person in judiciary must be deemed to be acquainted with the law of contempt and his ignorance of law is no excuse. Ayub AH Mahalder Vs. Md Shajahan and another 12 BLD (HCD) 54
