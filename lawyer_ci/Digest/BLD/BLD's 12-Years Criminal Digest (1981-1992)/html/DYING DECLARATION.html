DYING DECLARATION

 

Dying declaration — The declaration having been made immediately after the victim was assaulted and while he was in dying state there is no reason to disbelieve it as in such condition and at such time he is not supposed to make a statement falsely implicating the accused — If believed, dying declaration can form the basis for conviction — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 32 Tuku Miah Vs. The State 3 BLD (HCD) 193.

 

Absence of the name of the accused in the dying declaration — Whether the accused can be convicted relying on the FIR. — The name of appellant Amjad Hossain was not mentioned in the dying declaration although his name appears in the FIR. — Since the conviction of the appellants has been based on the dying declaration and the name of appellant Amjad Hossain is not mentioned therein, his conviction and sentence cannot be maintained — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 32. Amjad Hossain and others Vs. The State 5 BLD (AD) 63

 

 

 

Dying declaration — Omission about dying declaration in the FIR. — Effect of FIR. is an information relating to the commission of cognizable offences re­ported to the police by any person with the object of putting the police in motion for investigation — Absence of dying declaration in the F.I.R. does not by itself render the dying declaration unacceptable_Mere omission of some details in the FIR. does not detract from its cor­rectness — Code of Criminal Procedure (V of 1898) S 154 — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 32. Majihar Rahman and others Vs. The State 5 BLD (HCD) 110Ref. PLD 1978 (Lah) 1285; PLD 1977 (SC) 529; PLD 1971 (Lahore) 883

 

 

 

Dying declaration — Whether the ap­pellant is entitled to be acquitted when part of the dying declaration relating to co-accused was disbelieved — In this case the FIR. was lodged on the same day and the Magistrate recorded the victim's dying declaration on the same day — The evidence of PW1 who rushed to the victim immediately after the occurrence appears to be convincing — The trial court's acceptance of the plea of alibi of co-accused Abdur Rashid does not destroy the dying declaration — Evi­dence Act (1 of 1872) S 32(1). Abdul Hakim alias Lokman Hakim Vs. The State 8 BLD (AD) 152

 

 

 

Dying declaration — The expectation of imminent death is not a requirement for treating declaration as a dying declaration _Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 32. Nurjahan Begum Vs. The State 8 BLD (HCD) 389.

 

Dying declaration — Its credibility — Where the dying declaration is the sole basis of conviction, utmost care must be taken by the Court in considering the surrounding circumstances and compare them with the statement in the dying declaration for safe dispensation of justice It is imperative to determine not only that the dying declaration was actually made but also that it is true and genuine ___There is no denying the fact that a dying declaration having being made on the apprehension of approaching death, has a degree of sanctity under the law but in view of the state of affairs now prevailing in our society it is necessary to find out that the dying declaration was true, because truthfulness of the deceased during his life time is of no less considera­tion for the purpose — Evidence Act (I of 1872) S 32 Abdul Majid Vs.The State 8 BLD (HCD) 459.Ref. 22 DLR (SC) 16.

 

 

 

Dying declaration — Question of its credibility — The dying declaration not being a voluntary statement of the decea­sed but made in response to questions put by the doctor and the questions not being recorded by the doctor, it deserves to be ignored as it was not a voluntary statement of the deceased — It is a matter of great puzzle to see that a sinking man could make a statement with such completeness as would put a man in his full sense to shame — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 32 Kashem All Vs. The State and others 9 BLD (AD) 13.

 

Dying declaration — Way of making such a declaration — A dying declaration may be recorded by any person who is available — It may be written or it may be verbal, it may also be indicated by signs and gestures, in answer to questions, if the person making it is not in a position to speak.

 

Dying declaration — The worth that it commands — The legislature in its wisdom has put a dying declaration at par with the evidence on oath for the reason that a man under the apprehension of death is not likely to speak falsehood and involve innocent persons in preference to his assailant — That is why particular sanctity is attached to such statement and it is admissible as substantive evidence — But the Court must take care to see that the statement is genuine — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 32.

 

Dying declaration — Its utility as evidence — There is no wrong is treating a dying declaration as First Information Report, for prior to this no other information was lodged with the police regarding the assault and injury of the victim — Had the victim survived her statement would not have attained the sanctity of a substantive evidence but would remain just a First Information Report — When an injured person lodged an FIR. about his injuries at a time when there was no apprehension of his death therefrom but subsequently he succumbed to his injuries, the statement served both the purposes of dying declaration and FIR. — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 32 — Code of Criminal Procdure (V of 1898) S 154 Nurjahan Begum Vs. The State 10 BLD (AD) 61.

 

Dying declaration — Where it is highly doubtful and its probative value is little or nil — where the alleged dying declaration is the only piece of evidence against an accused and it is not free from reasonable doubt — Such a dying dec­laration cannot be accepted to form the basis of conviction — If the dying state­ment is found essentially true and genuine the Court can act upon it — Evicence Act (1 of 1872) S 32 .Shamsur Rahman alias Shamsu Vs. The State 10 BLD (AD) 251
