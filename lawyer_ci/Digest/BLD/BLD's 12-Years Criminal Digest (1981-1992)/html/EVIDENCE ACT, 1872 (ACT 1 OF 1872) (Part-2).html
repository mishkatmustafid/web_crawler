Section 30 — Ex-culpatory confession __When the maker of a confession does not involve himself in the commission of the alleged offence, it is no confession at all — It is at best a statement against the other accused — In such a case the prosecution should examine the confessor as a witness instead of making him an accused in the case — If is the direct oral evidence does not qualify to be trustworthy, the alleged confession can be of no avail for sustaining conviction. Ibrahim Mollah and others Vs. The State 7 BLD (AD) 248. Ref. PLD 197.1 (SC) 469; PLD 1959 (SC) 377; PLD 1962 (SC) 320; PLD 1963 (SC) 157.

Section 30 — Value of retracted confession A  judicial confession, whether retracted or not, can validly form the basis of conviction of the maker of confession but as a matter of prudence and caution, which has become virtually a rule of law, the Court does not generally consider it safe to base a conviction on a retracted confession without corrobora­tion in material particulars. Hahihur Rah m a alias Hahih Vs. The State 8 BLD (HCD) 210. Ref. 5 BLD (HCD) 9; AIR 1936 (PC) 253.

Section 60 -Hearsay evidence Generally speaking the statement or declarations of persons not called as witnesses but repeated bv a third person are excluded from evidence as hear say — But when it is proposed to establish by evidence nor the truth of the statement but the fact that if was made, there can be no objection in receiving the same in evidence S. Section 60 — Hearsay evidence — It is hearsay and inadmissible when the object of the evidence is to establish the truth of the statement but it is not hearsay and is admissible when it is proposed to establish not the truth of the statement but the fact that it was made Shaflulla alias Kala Miah Vs. The State 5 BLD (HCD) 129.

 

Section 64 — Admissibility of news­paper report — Probative value of such report — Although a newspaper report is. admissible in evidence without formal proof yet the paper ipso facto is no proof of its contents — The newspaper report cannot be treated as proof of the facts stated therein Nurul Islam and others Vs. The State 7 BLD (HCD) 193Ref. AIR 1925 (Lahore) 299, AIR 1961 (Punjab) 215; AIR 1951 (SC) 1376.

 

Sections 64 and 65 — Best evidence - The requirement of law is that the best evidence should always be given — Primary evidence is the best evidence which affords the greatest amount of certainty of facts in question — Unless it is shown that the production of the best evidence or the primary evidence is out of a party's power, no other proof of fact is generally admitted — The oral testimony as to proof of shortage of stamp and stationary is inadmissible in evidence when the documentary evidence to prove the same was available and purposely withheld Sk. Abdul Majid Vs. The State 7 BLD (HCD) 413.

 

Section 80 — Examination of the Magistrate recording confession — When the confession has been recorded in accordance with law by observing all the formalities prescribed by law it becomes admissible in evidence under Section 80 of the Evidence Act without calling the Magistrate who recorded it — The two requirements for admitting such a con­fession into evidence are that it was taken in accordance with law and that the identity of the accused has been satisfactorily proved. Section 101 — Burden of proof— The prosecution must prove every link in the chain of evidence — Where the vital link in the chain of evidence is broken the accused cannot be convicted Mil Nasir alias Nasir Vs. The State 9 BLD (HCD) 502.

Section 106 — Special Knowledge — Onus of proof— If the maid servant had died in any other circumstances other than by kicking, it was within the special knowledge of accused Mokles Mridha on whom lav the onus to prove the circumstances leading to the murder of victim — He was the master of the maid servant and it is in his house that she suddenly died although she got no ailment prior to her death and the accused hurriedly burried her — The conduct of the accused is highly incriminating. Section 106 — Special Knowledge — Section 100 of the Evidence Act is attracted only in exceptional cases where a relevant fact is pre-eminently within the knowledge of the accused — In such cases the accused is to prove a fact which is essentially within his knowledge — Of course, the burden of the accused is lighter than that of the prosecution, because the Court is to consider his plea only after, and not before, the prosecution leads evidence for sustaining a conviction.
