EVIDENCE ACT, 1872 (ACT 1 OF 1872)

 

Section 3 and 30 — Confession of a co-accused — It is well settled that the confession of an accused is not sub­stantive evidence within the meaning of Section 3 of the Evidence Act — But it is also well-settled that although the con­fession of a co-accused cannot be treated as substantive evidence against the co-accused and cannot be the sole basis for conviction of another accused but still then along with other evidence the Court may take into consideration such confession and can use the same to lend assurance to other evidence. Mobarak Hossain Vs. The State 1 BLD (HCD) 286.Ref. 27 DLR (AD) 29; 12 DLR (SC) 156, 217; II DLR (SC) 38 & 81, AIR 1931 (Lahore) 38.

 

Sections 3 and 30 — Confession of a co-accused — The statement of a co-accused does not fall within the definition of evidence as it is not made on oath in presence of the person or persons affected and its veracity is not tested by cross-examination — Though it is admissible, it is much weaker than the evidence of an approver — It is the established rule of evidence as well as rule of prudence that the confessional statement of a co-accused can not be accepted as the sole basis of conviction in the absence of independent corroboration Amir Hossain Howlader Vs. The State 4 BLD (AD) 193.Ref. 12 DLR (SC) 156; PLD 1960 (SC) 313; 76 IA 147.

 

Section 3 — It is well-settled that where all the evidence is circumstantial it is necessary that cumulatively its effect should be to exclude any reasonable hypothesis of the innocence of the accused — The onus always remains with the prosecution to establish the guilt of the accused beyond reasonable doubts — To base a conviction on circumstantial evidence, circumstances established in the case must be incompatible with the innocence of the accused and must be sufficient to exlude all possibilities of their being innocent Keshab Chandra Mistry and others Vs. The State 5 BLD (AD) 301.Ref. 1 I DLR (SC) 38.

 

Section 3 — Circumstantial evidence — To base a conviction on circumstantial evidence it is necessary that circumstances proved in the case must be complete and incapable of explanation of any other hypothesis other than the guilt of the accused Shafiullah alias Kala Mia Vs. The State 5 BLD (HCD) 129.Ref. AIR 1922 (SC) 343; AIR 1976 (SC)917; AIR 1979 (SC) 190, 826.

 

Sections 3 and 30 — Confession — In order to constitute confession a statement must either admit in terms of the offence, or at any rate, substantially all the facts which constitute the offence — The confession of a co-accused is not evidence against another co-accused whose guilt is under consideration and material witnesses as to the calling away of the deceased after which he was not found till his dead body was recovered.

 

—    In the absence of any other reasonable explanation as to the safe departure of the deceased from the company of the accused-appellant, no conclusion other than the guilt of the accused can be drawn Anisur Rahman and others Vs. The State 6 BLD (AD) 791. Ref. 11 DLR (SC) 365; 16 DLR (SC) 261;32DLR320.

 

Section 3 — Benefit of doubt — In a criminal case the accused is always entitled to the benefit of doubt but it is a reasonable doubt which is considered to be a ground for giving its benefit to the accused — A faint doubt, which has no reasonable basis, is not contemplated in law. The State Vs. Tayeh Ali and others 7 BLD (AD) 265.

 

Section 3 — Circumstantial evidence _Merely because the accused had called and taken away the victim in the previous evening it will be most unreasonable to thing that none but the accused abetted the murder which took place so many hours after the taking away of the victim __It is the fundamental principle of ^he criminal jurisprudence that the circums­tantial evidence should point inevitably to the conclusion that the accused and the accused only were the perpetrators of the offence and such evidence should be incompatible with the innocence of the accused. Hossain Ali and another Vs. The State 7 BLD (HCD) 80.Ref. 33 DLR 402; PLD 1956 (SC) (Ind) 286.

 

 

Section 3 — Circumstantial evidence — The accused can not be convicted merely on the ground that the circumstantial evidence provides a ground for strong suspicion against her — If the circumstantial evidence does not furnish conclusive proof of the guilt of the accused she cannot be convicted merely on the probability of having committed the offence Alpana Rani Das Vs. The State 8 BLD (HCD) 114.

 

 

Section 3 — Circumstantial evidence _If it is proved beyond doubt that the deceased was last seen alive in the company of the accused, the latter are liable to offer satisfactory explanation as to the cause of death of the deceased — When the circumstantial evidence led by the prosecution is quite incompatible with the innocence of the accused the view that the circumstances themselves are not enough without anything more to connect the accused with the offence is contrary to the time-honoured principle of law regarding circumstantial evidence Rezaul Huq and others Vs. The State 9 BLD (HCD) 218.Ref. 37 DLR (AD) 139; 16 DLR (SC) 261; PLD 1956 (SC) (Ind) 286.

 

 

Section 3 — Circumstantial evidence _The circumstances proved in the case taken cumulatively should form a complete chain and the onus is on the prosecution to prove that the chain is complete — The prosecution cannot derive any strength from the weakness of the defence — After the links in the chain of circumstances are themselves complete. then a false defence may be called in aid lending assurance to the Court, but not that the infirmity in the prosecution case could be cured by a false defence. The State and others Vs. Bailsha Mullah 9 BLD (HCD) 257. Ref. 5 BLD (AD) 301; 5 BLD (HCD) 129; AIR 1952 (SC) 343; AIR 1976 (SC) 917; AIR 1979 (SC) 190; AIR 1984 (SC) 1622

 

Sections 3 and 5 — The injuries found by the doctor who held the post-mortem examination on the deadbody of the deceased do not tally with the injuries alleged to have been inflicted on the deceased as mentioned by the eye- witnesses __Glaring inconsistencies between the injuries on the deadbody of the deceased as found by the doctor and the evidence of the prosecution witnesses about the inujries caused by the appellants led to the conclusion that the occurrence did not take place in the manner as alleged by the prosecution. Kudu and others Vs. The State 10 BLD (HCD) 236.

 

Section 5 — Appreciation of evidence _When concurrent findings of facts are liable to interference — A careful examination of the deposition of the witnesses reveals that the evidence of the vital witnesses was not sifted and assessed according to rules of evidence by either of the courts below — The circumstances in which victim Majid received the fatal injury remained a mystery — The pro-ecution has failed to prove the charges against the accused appellant — The

Appellate Division set aside the order of conviction and sentence and acquitted the accused Shahjahan Biswas and others Vs. The State 9 BLD (AD) 18.

 

Section 5 — Appreciation of evidence _If PW 2 had actually seen the killing of his mother he would have certainly raised hue and cry to attract the neighbours and he would not have waited for half an hour to see that the assailant safely escape — On examining PW4, one of the two eye witnesses, the doctor found some abrasions on his body which could be easily fabricated for the purpose of a case __The crucial uestions why the appellants abducted him and released him after 4 days knowing that the release meant they would be implicated in a case of capital sentence — Omissions and due-con­tradictions of these two eye-witnesses were not given consideration by the courts below — The contradictions and omissions in the evidence of PW4 makes the defence case probable and as such the appellants are entitled to acquittal as a matter of right Ahul Kashem and others Vs. The State 9 BLD (AD) 122.

 

 

Sections 5 and 9 — Appreciation of the evidence relating to recognition of the accused — Recognition of the accused by the focus of the torch light when the focus was directed towards the witness is doubtful — Recognition of an unknown accused by moonlight is very much doubtful, rather it is impossible. Jamir alias Jamiruddin Vs. The State 9 BLD (HCD) 474.

 

 

Section 8 — A relevant fact — The fact that the person affected (being dead and not examined) made a complaint relating to the crime shortly after its commission is relevant and admissible under Section 8 of the Evidence Act. S. M. Quamruzzaman Vs. The State 1 BLD (HCD) 107. Ref. AIR 1954 (Mad) 215.

 

 

Sections 8 and 9 — Abscondence of the accused — The mere fact of abscondence of the accused is of little importance if there is nothing beyond that — The importance of the fact of abscondence of the accused will depend on the facts of each case — In certain cases abscondence may indicate guilt of the accused unless he explains his conduct Mobarak Hossain Vs. The State 1 BLD  (HCD) 286.Ref. AIR 1931 (Lahore) 38; 62 IC 570, AIR 1941 (Odh) 517.

 

 

Sections 8 and 27 — Incriminating object — If an incriminating object is recovered in consequence of an infor­mation given by the accused it may be allowed to be given in evidence — It is also admissible in evidence as an act or conduct of the accused Khasru alias Khorshed Vs. Tlie State 3 BLD  (HCD) 318.

 

 

Section 8 — Abscondence of the accused — Abscondence of an accused under given circumstances may be corroboration of the direct evidence of eye witnesses connecting the accused with the crime — But the abscondecne of an accused cannot be treated to be corroboration of the confessional state­ments of another accused so as to base thereon the conviction of the absconding accused Amir Hossain Hawlader Vs. The State 4 BLD  (AD) 193.Ref. PLD 1969 (SC) 89; PLD 1974 (SC) 65; The Supreme Court Monthly Report (1970) Vol III, 351.

 

 

Section 8 — Abscondence of the accused after the commission of the offence — Abscondence by itself is not an incriminating matter, for even an innocent person sometimes absconds to avoid harassment by the police — In some cases a person with guilty knowledge also absconds — It is the facts and circumstances of the case which decide whether abscondence is due to any guilty knowledge or to anv intention to avoid harassment by the police The State Vs. Lain Miah and another 7 BLD (AD) 211.

 

 

Section 9 — Identification of accused — The accused was not recognised during the commission of the dacoity and no T I Parade was held — Recognition of the accused in the thana hajat at the instance of the OC is of no value Mahidur alias Mahidul Islam and ohters Vs. The State. Ref. 3 BLD (HCD) 162.

 

 

Section 9 — Value of T.I. Parade — The evidence of identification held at the stage of investigation can be used only to corroborate the substantive evidence given by the witnesses in the court — The value of identification in a T.I. Parade depends on the effectiveness of precau­tions taken against the identifying witness having any opportunity of seeing the accused to be identified in the parade. Hajrat All and others Vs. The State 5 BLD (HCD) 151.Ref. AIR 1972 (SCD) 283; AIR 1973 (SC)2190 AIR 1974 (SC) 791.

 

Section 9 — Evidence of identification of the accused — Evidence on identification per see is a weak type of evidence and it is not to be readily believed — Conviction may be based on the identification evidence alone if it is established beyond doubt that the witness correctly recognized the accused and that there was sufficient light to facilitate such recognition — It will be unsafe to base conviction on identification evidence alone unless such evidence is corrobo-rated by circumstances which may indicate that the accused took part in the commission of the offence Tafazzal Haque and another Vs. The State 6 BLD (HCD) 418

 

Section 9 — Value of T.I. Parade — In the absence of the Magistrate the defence obviously could not cross-examine him with regard to the proceedings of the T.I. Parade and as such the value of the T.I Parade is almost reduced to nil — T.I. Parade was held after the witness had seen the accused — So the identification of the accused in the T.I. Parade is of no consequence. Shahidullah and another Vs. The State 7 BLD (AD) 27.

 

Section 24 — Confessional statement not legally proved and not marked exhibits _The Magistrate recording the con­fessional statements simply stated in his evidence that he observed all the formalities and said nothing beyond that __He did not say that the statements were recorded according to the statements of the accused or that they were read over to the accused or that they signed them — In the absence of such proof the confessions could not be admitted in evidence nor could they be referred to by the Judge Mahidur alias Mahidul Islam and others Vs. The State 3 BLD (HCD) 162.

 

Sections 24-30 — Confession — It is well-settled that as against the maker himself his confession, whether judicial or extra-judicial and whether retracted or not retracted, can validly form the sole basis of his conviction if the court is satisfied and believes that it was true and voluntary and it was not obtained by inducement, coercion or torture — It is also well settled that in the absence of any supporting evidence the evidence of a co-accused cannot be the basis of the conviction of another accused Ahdur Rashid and others Vs. The State 3 BLD (HCD) 206.

 

Section 24 — Oral confession — It's veracity — When a confession does not at all indicate any injury referred to in the post-mortem report being committed while murdering the victim and nothing being said in the alleged oral confession as to where, when and in what manner the murder was committed, such a confession cannot be believed without independent corroboration. Sudhir and others Vs. The State 3 BLD (HCD) 292.

 

Sections 24-30 — Retracted confession—Even a retracted confession if believed to be true and voluntary may form the sole basis of conviction of its maker but as against the co-accused the evidentiary value of a retracted confession is practically nil. Amir Hossain Hawlader Vs. The State 4 BLD (AD) 193.Ref. 12 DLR (SC) 156; PLD 1960 (SC) 313; PLD 1969 (SC) 89; PLD 1974 (SC) 65; 76 IA147.

 

Section 24 — Confessional statement—Partly inculpatory and partly ex­culpatory — The Court can consider the inculpatory portion of the confessional statement and exclude the exculpatory portion of the confessional statement.A confession, whether retracted or not, can form the basis of conviction of its maker if it is found true and voluntary The State Vs. Masudur Rahman 4 BLD (HCD) 228.

 

 

Section 24 — Confession — Con­fession means admission of certain facts which constitute an offence committed by the maker thereof and it should be directly on the point admitting facts — It must either admit in terms of the offence or at any rate substantially all facts which constitute the offence.

 

 

Duty of the recording Magistrate —The Magistrate recording the confessional statement should endeavour to place the confessing accused at ease, dispel all or any lurking fear, inducement and hope from the mind of the accused to enable him to make of his own volition an absolutely free and voluntary statement according to the best dictates of his own inner conscience — Before recording a confessional statement the Magistrate must assure himself and satisfy his own judicial conscience about it and his satisfaction must flow out of the answers given by the accused to the questions put to him by the Magistrate Abdul Wahah and another Vs. The State 6 BLD (HCD) 390.

 

Section 24 — Extra Judicial Confession __The extra-judicial confession made before the dafadar, who gave the accused some beating is not admissible in evidence Dipok Kumar Sarkar Vs. The State 8 BLD (AD) 109.

 

Section 24 — Confession of co-accused — It cannot be used against other co-accused Yusuf Ali and others Vs. The State 8 BLD ( HCD) 361.

 

Section 24 — Extra-judicial confession __By its very nature it is a very weak type of evidence and as such it is unsafe to base a conviction on an extra-judicial confession — The value of such a confession depends on the verasity of witnesses to whom it was made and it requires    material  corroboration by evidence of unimpeachable character. The State and others Vs.    Badsha Molla.9BLD (HCP) 257. Ref. 6 DLR 246.

 

Section  24 — Confessional statement __Conviction can be based solely on the confession of its maker if it is found true and voluntary, although the rule of prudence may require some sort of corroboration — It is not necessary that each and every circumstance regarding the maker's complicity must be corroborated _It is enough if the general trend of confession is substantiated — The Court has to examine the confession in the light of the materials on record and the broad probabilities of the case.Once a confession is found to be ture and voluntary conviction can be based solely on it even if it is retracted. Hajrat Ali and another Vs. The State 10 BLD (HCD) 38.Ref. 12 DLR (SC) 156; 16 DLR (SC) 598; 19 DLR 818; 31 DLR 3 16, 32 DLR 227; 1 BCR 105; SCR 1979 Vol. 3, Page 417.

 

Section 24-30 — Confessional State­ment — Partly inculpatory and partly exculpatory — When a confession is found to be voluntary and partly exculpatory and partly inculpatory, the Court may reject the exculpatory portion and accept the inculpatory portion thereof __Even if subsequently the confession is retracted, the Court can rely on such a confession and the inculpatory part of such a  confessional statement may be used against the other co-accused jointly tried for the same offence. Ahul Kashem and another Vs.  The State 10 BLD (HCD) 309 Ref. 7 BLD (AD) 212; 39 DLR (AD) I 17.

 

Section 25 — Retracted confession — When the retracted confession is found to be true and voluntary and not obtained by threat, coercion and inducement the same can be the basis for conviction The State Vs. Abu Bakkar and ohters 3 BLD (HCD) 240Ref. 31 DLR 3 12.

 

Sections 25, 26 and 27 — Admissibility in evidence — Any statement made to a police officer is inadmissible in evidence under Section 162 Cr.P.C. and any confession made to a police officer is inadmissible under Sections 25 and 26 of the Evidence Act — But section 27 of the Evidence Act is an exception to those three sections — When any fact is deposed to as discovered in consequence of any information received from any accused during police custody, so much of such information, whether it amounts to a confession or not as it relates distinctly to the fact thereby discovered is admissible in evidence — In the instant case the first part of the statement that the accused "had killed Haji Yeasin Ali and five others" in admissible though the second part of the statement that they buried the dead bodies in the tarmaric field is admissible — This is so admissible in view of the fact that the truth of this statement made to the police has been sanctified and guaranteed by the discovery of the dead bodies from the place pointed out by the accused — Code of Criminal Procedure (V of 1898) S 162 Mohammad Siddiqur Rahman and others Vs. The State 7 BLD  (AD) 93.

 

 

Sections 25 and 26 — The validity of a confessional statement how far affected by the presence of a dafadar — When part of the confessional statement is made before the arrival of the dafadar and the remaining part of the confessional statement is made after his arrival the earlier part of the statement will not be affected since it was made when the accused was not in police custody When a confessional statement is voluntary and true and it agrees with the established facts of the case it can be safely accepted.

 

Language of the confessional Statem­ent — Confessional statement should be recorded in the words of the prisoner — But it is not correct to say that a confession not recorded exactly in the prisoner's own words is inadmissible in evidence Nowsher AH Sarder and others Vs. The State 7 BLD (AD ) 324.

 

 

Sections 25 and 27 — Confession made to a police officer — Section 25 of the Evidence Act excludes from evidence a confession made by an accused to a police officer under any circumstances but Section 27 provides an exception to the prohibition imposed by Section 25 to the extent that any information even by way of confession made in police custody, which relates distinctly to the fact discovered, is admissible in evidence against the accused Bashar AH and others Vs. The State 12 BLD (HCD) 225.

 

Section 27 — Admissibility of information furnished by accused — The information given by the accused that he concealed his wife's dead body in the latrine-well while being in the custody of the police is admissible in evidence as this information was followed by the recovery of the deadbody therefrom Dipok Kumar Sarkar Vs. The State .8 BLD (AD) 109

 

Section 27 — Statement of accused before police when admissible in evidence- Such statement as far as it leads to the discovery of the dead body or other incriminating materials is a relevant fact for finding out the clue to the crime and hence admissible in evidence Hazrat AH and another Vs. The State 10 BLD (HCD) 38.Ref. 32 DLR 227; 7 BLD (AD) 93.

 

Section 30 — Confession of a co-accused — The confession of a co-accused though not a substantive evidence against the other accused, the court may take such confession into consideration to lend assurance to other evidence for the purpose of conviction of other accused Moharak Hossain Vs. The State 1 BLD (HCD) 286.Ref. 27 DLR (AD) 29; 12 DLR (SC) 156 and 217; 11 DLR (SC) 38 and 81.

 

Section 30 — Extra-judicial confession _It may be taken into consideration along with other evidence — Since other evidence is meagre and it lacks independent corroboration, the confession of the co-accused even accepted as true cannot be the basis of conviction of the accused appellant Mil. Mohiiruk Hossain Vs. The State 3 BLD (AD) 329.

 

 

Section 30 — Conviction on the confession of a co-accused — Conviction of an accused solely based on the confession of a co-accused is not valid in law Abdur Rashid and others Vs. The State 3 BLD (HCD) 206.

 

 

Section 30 — Conviction of an accused on the confession of a co-accused — conviction of an accused based on the confession of a co-accused cannot be sustained unless there are other evidences on which the conviction can be based. Safar All and others Vs. The State 3 BLD (HCD) 325.

 

 

Section 30 — Retracted confession — The confession made by an co-accused even if retracted can form the sole basis of conviction of its maker if the confession is found to be true and voluntary.

 

Confession of a co-accused — Under certain circumstances the confession of a co-accused can be taken into considera­tion against other accused persons as well __But the confession of one accused cannot be used to corroborate the confession of another accused for the purpose of convicting the other accused who have not confessed Abdul Malik and others Vs.
