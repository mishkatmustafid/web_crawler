SANCTION

Public Servant — Sanction to prosecute Sub-Inspector of police and constables — Sub-Inspector of police and constables being removable by the Inspector General and the Superintendent of police, they are not such public servants as are not removable save by or the sanction of the Government — No sanction under Section 197 Cr.P.C. is necessary to prose­cute them, even though the police officer and the constables involved might have committed the offence while acting or purporting to act in the discharge of their official duty — Code of Criminal Proce- dure (V of 1898) S 197. Rokeya Begum Vs. Shafiqur Rahman and the State 1 BLD (HCD) 82. Ref. AIR 1943 (FC) 18; AIR 1964 (SC) 269

Purpose of Sanction for Prosecution

—   The provision for sanction is intended to protect officers of the Government from harassment by unncessary prosecu­tion — No prosecution shall lie against the public servants without prior sanction from the appropriate authority — If the Board of Revenue had reason to believe that the concerned Customs Officers acted bonafide and they had no 'mens rea' the Board could have refused sanction _Criminal Law Amendment Act (XL of 1958) S 6(5) A. K. Reazaul Karim Vs. The State 4 RLD (AD) 125

Cognizance and trial without sanction

—   Chargesheet was not accompanied by sanction for prosecution of the appellant who is admittedly a public servant — The Special Judge also did not write for sanction to the appropriate authority and as such the taking of cognizance by the Special Judge was not valid in law — Criminal Law Amendment Act (XL of 1958) S 6(5) A S. S. Mohammad AH Vs. The State 5 RLD (AD) 185. Ref. 35DLR (AD) 324.

Criminal Trial — Sanction for prosecution of a public servant — When bar of sanction can be claimed — Being a mere public servant a person cannot claim the bar of sanction unless he acted as a public servant in discharge of his official duty while committing the alleged offence — Code of Criminal Procedure (V of 1898) S 197(1). Sudhir Chandra Das Gupta and others Vs. Rhopal Chandra Chowdhury 8 RLD (HCD) 246 Ref. AIR 1955 (SC) 309; AIR 1948 (PC) 128 (133).

Cognizance   by   special   judge —Question of validity of his taking cogni­zance upon a complaint made to a Magis­trate — A complaint made orally or in writing to a Magistrate will be a complaint and when the Magistrate sends that complaint to the Special Judge along with the deposition of witnesses recorded by him and his comments for the Special Judge to take cognizance and if on that cognizance is taken again it cannot be said that the Special Judge has not taken cognizance on a complaint — Criminal Law Amendment Act (XL of 1958) S 4(1) — Code of Criminal Procedure (V of 1898) S 4(h).

Criminal trial — Question of obtaining prior sanction to prosecute a public ser­vant — The interpretation that such sanction of the Government in terms of Section 197 Cr.P.C. could be obtained even after the submission of the charge sheet but before the trial had started could be made applicable in respect of sanction required under the Criminal Law Amendment Act (XL of 1958) S 6(5) — Code of Criminal Procedure (V of 1898) S 197 — Penal Code (XLV of 1860) Ss. 406 and 409 Matiar Rahman Vs. The State and others 9 BLD (HCD) 144. Ref. 32 DLR (AD) 100; 35 DLR 249

Prosecuting an approver — Sanction for prosecution — The contention that without the sanction of the High Court Division the prosecution of the approver was illegal has no substance for the prosecution is attracted only where after forfeiture of parden the approver is wanted to be prosecuted for giving false evidence — In this case since the ap­prover has not been prosecuted for giving false evidence but he has been prosecuted only for committing dacoity in respect of which the pardon was granted, no such sanction was required — Code of Crimi­nal Procedure (V of 1898) S 339(3). Angur Vs. The State 9 BLD (HCD) 485.

Sanction for prosecution is mandatory— Objection as to sanction when to be taken — Sanction for prosecution is a mandatory provision of law in the trial of a public servant — But objection as to sanction is to be raised during the trial or during the appeal and that having not done it cannot be raised for the first time before the Appellate Division — More­over the record shows that the order of sanction was there in the record and it was perused by the learned Special Judge as recorded in the order sheet and as such the objection as to sanction was not tenable at this stage — Criminal Law Amendment Act (XL of 1958) S 6 — Penal Code (XLV of 1860) S 409 read with 5(2) of Act 11 of 1947. Liakat Ali alias Md Liakat Ali Vs. The State 10 BLD (AD) 165.

Sanction to prosecute is the discretion of the authority — Its importance — The sanction to prosecute is a very important matter and it constitutes a condition precedent to the launching of the prosecu­tion — The Government has an absolute discretion in granting or withholding sanction — The authority is merely to see that the evidence discloses a prima facie case against the person sought to be prosecuted — The sanctioning authority can refuse sanctions on any ground which commends itself to them.

Where facts are not referred to on the face of the sanction nor is it proved by evidence that they were placed before the sanctioning authority, the sanction is in­valid — Criminal Law Amendment Act (XL of 1958) S 6 — Code of Criminal Procedure (V of 1898) S 197 — Penal Code (XLV of 1860) S 467. Md Abdul Hakim Vs. The State 12 BLD (HCD) 400. Ref. 1962 PLD (Dhaka) 623; AIR 1948 (PC) 82.

Jurisdiction of Senior Special Judge— The Senior Special Judge is the only judge who has power for taking cogni­zance of an offence under the Criminal Law Amendment Act, 1958 though there may be other Special Judges within the same territorial jurisdiction — Criminal Law Amendment Act (XL of 1958) S 4(1 )(2) and Section 5(1 )(e) of Act II of 1947 H. M. Ershad Vs. The State 12 BLD (HCD) 610.

Sanction when not needed — No sanction is necessary for prosecution of a public servant who has ceased to be a public servant. H. M. Ershad Vs. The State 12 BLD (HCD) 610.
