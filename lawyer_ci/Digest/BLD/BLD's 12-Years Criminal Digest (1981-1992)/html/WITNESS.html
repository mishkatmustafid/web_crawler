WITNESS

Witness — Relationship of witnesses — Whether the evidence of such wit­nesses can be relied upon — The fact of relationship is no ground to discard the testimony of witnesses in a case unless there are internal marks of falsehood in their testimony — Mere relationship is no ground to disbelieve a witness if other­wise found trustworthy — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 146 Majibur Rahman and others Vs. The State 5 BLD (HCD) 111).Ref. 21 DLR 575; 24 DLR 217.

 

Non-production of witnesses named in F.I.R. and Chargesheet — Non-produc­tion of witnesses named in the F.I.R and charge-sheet weakens the prosecution case Abdul Bahar alias Abu I Hahar and others Vs. The State 5 BLD (HCD) 84.

 

Relationship of witnesses — Enemity with witnesses — Relationship and enemity of the witnesses are not the grounds for discarding the evidence of the witnesses — Enemity cuts both ways and it cannot be ground for disbelieving the positive evidence of an eye witness — Evidence Act ( 1 of 1872) S 118 Mahitullah PK. and others Vs. The State 3 BLD (HCD) 277.

 

Non-Examination of F.LR. and Charge-sheet witnesses — Effect of — The pro­secution is not bound to examine each and every witness named in the FIR and the charge-sheet — In the absence of any material to show that these witnesses were witnesses about the actual occur­rence, no adverse inference can be drawn against the prosecution for non-exami­nation of such witnesses — Code of Criminal Procedure (V of 1898) S 265G. Siddique Ahmed alias Md Siddique and others Vs. The State 5 BLD (HCD) 202.

 

Tendering of Prosecution Witness —A witness may be tendered by the prose­cution if his evidence is not of much im­portance or his evidencewill make unnec­essary addition to the evidence already adduced but an important witness should not be tendered — Tendering of witnesses from motive is depricated Shamsul Huq and others Vs. The State 6 BLD (AD) 216

 

Belated examination of witnesses by the Investigating Officer — The evi­dentiary value of such witnesses — Since the witness was examined bv the Inves­tigating Officer W; months after the F.I.R. the evidence of the witness was left out of consideration — Code of Criminal Procedure (V of 1898) Ss 161 and 167 The State Vs. Mokbul Hossain 6 BLD (HCD) 34.

 

Sole witness — Situations may arise where only a single person is available to give evidence in support of a disputed fact — In such a case the court has to weigh carefully such a testimony and if it is satisfied that the evidence is reliable and free from all taints which tend to render oral testimony open to suspicion, the court can act upon such testimony.

There being no material on record to show that the lone witness has any motive to bring false allegation or that he was acting at the instance of other persons, his credibility cannot be doubted — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 134 Shahidullah and another Vs. The State 7 BLD (AD) 27.

 

Witnesses of search and seizure of arms — A judge may disbelieve a witness of search and seizure if it appears to him that the witness is making obliging state­ments in favour of the accused to save him from punishment — A judge may convict an accused solely on the basis of the unimpeachable evidence of the officer who   made   the   search   and seizure disbelieving the seizure list witnesses — But in the present case the witnesses of search and seizure cannot be disbelieved as it seems probable that the planting of pipe gun was managed by the enemy of the appellant — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 134 — The Arms Act (XI of 1878) S  19A   Nurul Islam  Vs. The State 8 BLD (HCD) 106.

 

Non-examination of F.l.R. named witnesses — Witnesses who are named in the FIR and who came to the place of the occurrence shortly after the dacoits left, renders the prosecution case shaky and doubtful J amir alias Jamiruddin Vs. The State 9 BLD (HCD) 474.

 

Interested eye-witnesses — There cannot be any bar for a person to witness an occurrence and give evidence merely because the accused has brought a case against him — His evidence however re­quires to be tested with other evidence and circumstances Shah Alam and others Vs. The State 10 BLD (AD) 25 Ref. 12 DLR (SC) 289; 14 DLR (SC) 159.

 

Conviction on the sole testimony of a single witness — It is well-settled law that a conviction may be based on the sole testimony of a single witness, if believed, and mere interestedness is no ground to reject the testimony of a witness when it is found that his evidence is trustworthy and free from doubt — It is equally settled that when a conviction rests on the testimony of a single witness, who is interested and partisan in nature, the court, apart from requiring corroboration of the said testimony subjects the same to maximum closest scrutiny. Abu Taker Chowdhury and others Vs. The State 11 BLD (AD) 2.

 

Interested witness — A police officer being an interested witness in the prose­cution case and his testimony having not been corroborated by any independent and disinterested witness in material particu­lars, the prosecution case fails. Gaziur Rahman Vs. Tlte State 11 BLD (HCD) 11.

 

Conviction on the evidence of a single witness — It is true that it is the quality and not the quantity that has to be seen in the evidence — Even on the basis of evi­dence of the single witness conviction of an accused can be maintained provided that the single prosecution witness is wholly and fully reliable — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 134 Md Joynai Ahedin Vs. The State 11 BLD (HCD) 70.

The State 11 BLD (HCD) 70.Ref. 29 DLR (SC) 221; 4 BCR (AD) 114.

 

Chance witnesses — When there is a background of enemity and the witnesses are chance witnesses and not natural wit­nesses and natural and independent wit­nesses were not produced by the prosecu­tion, it is unsafe to rely on the chance witnesses to connect the accused with the crime Nowsher Mollah and others Vs. The State 11 BLD (HCD) 295.

 

Sole Witness — The conviction of an accused can be based even upon the evi­dence of a single witness, if believed, but that witness must be wholly reliable and he must be found to be above suspicion of interestedness, incompetence or subordi­nation — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 134. Ashrafuddin Vs. The State 11 BLD (HCD) 435.

 

Interested and partisan witnesses —In a case where bitter enemity between the parties is admitted, it is required as a rule of prudence that there should be some such corroboration of the evidence of the interested witness or witnesses as may inspire confidence in the mind of the court as to the truth of the prosecution case. Abdul Mannan and others Vs. The State 12 BLD (AD) 87.

 

Interested witness — It is not safe to convict an accused on a charge of murder on the dubious, tainted and interested evi­dence. Abdul Gafur Vs. The State 12 BLD (AD) 90.

 

Solitary witness — The conviction of an accused can safely be based on the solitary evidence of an eye-witness if he is found full, complete and self-contained — It may not receive corroboration from other witnesses but when it stands fully corroborated by the circumstances of the case and the medical evidence on record, the Court can safely rely on it — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 134. Abdul Hai Sikder and another Vs. The State 12 BLD (AD) 180.

 

Number of witnesses — Law does not require a particular number of witnesses to prove a case and conviction may be based on the testimony of a solitary wit­ness — But rule of prudence, if not rule of law, requires corroboration of such evidence Muslimuddin and others Vs. The State 7 BLD (AD) 1.

 

Relationship of Witnesses — The question of their credibility — Ordinarily a close relation will be the last person to screen the real culprit and falsely impli­cate an innocent person — Relationship far from being a ground of criticism is often a source of guarantee of the truth. The State Vs. Mizanul Islam alias Dablu and another 8 BLD (HCD) 317.

 

Uncorroborated testimony of inter­ested witness — When the informant of the case is highly interested in the result of the case, his evidence, unless corrobo­rated by independent and unipeachable evidence, no reliance can be placed on it. Mati Mia Vs. The State 12 BLD (HCD) 126

 

Number of witnesses — Under Section 134 of the Evidence Act no particular number of witnesses is neces-sary to prove a case — The quality of the statement of the witnesses has to be looked into and it has no reference to the number of witnesses — Evidence Act (1 of 1872) S 134 Safiuddin and others Vs. Minhazuddin Chowdhurv and another 12 BLD (HCD) 301.

 

Testimony of a single witness — It is unsafe to find the accused persons guilty under Section 147 of the Penal Code rely­ing on the uncorroborated testimony of a single witness. Dr. Abdul .hilil Clwwdhury ami others Vs. The Suae 12 BLD (HCD) 467 Ref. 3441DLR.

 

Evidence of the prosecutrix — It is a settled principle of law that a prosecutrix should be corroborated by independent evidence or circumstances in order to base conviction upon her evidence, particularly when the prosecutrix is an adult woman Abdul Kashem Vs. The State 12 BLD (HCD) 513. Ref. 19 DLR (SC) 259; 10 DLR 532; 14 DLR 821; 12 DLR (SC) 165.
