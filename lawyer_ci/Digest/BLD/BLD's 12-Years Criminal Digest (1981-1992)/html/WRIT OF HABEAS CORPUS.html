WRIT OF HABEAS CORPUS

 

Habeas corpus — Court Martial — Scope of enquiry and interference in habeas corpus — jurisdiction to interfere with the decision of Court Martial is narrower than in the case of other Courts or Tribunals — Constitution of Bangla­desh, 1972 Art. 102(2)(b). Jamil Huq (Ex-Captain) Vs. Bangladesh and other 1 BLD (AD) 488.

 

Ref. 44 CWN 709; (1969) 1 All ER 208; AIR 1946 (Lahore) 103; 7 DLR (FC) 253; (1949) 1 All ER 242; (1949) 1 All ER 373; 259 US 326; 344 US 137; 33 DLR (AD) 143; 1 BLD (AD) 378; 1 BLD (AD) 307.

 

Supervisory jurisdiction under Art 109— Court Martial is not a Court sub­ordinate to the High Court Division — Proceedings of such a Court cannot be interfered with on the ground of proce­dural irregularities — The writ jurisdiction will be attracted if the proceedings are coram non judice or malafide and the Writ of Habeas Corpus will be available for securing the release of the prisoner who has been committed by a Court Martial in such circumstances — Constitution of Bangladesh, 1972, Arts. 102(2)(5), 109 and 114. Jamil Huq (Ex-Captain) Vs. Bangladesh and others 1 BLD (AD) 488.

 

Detention on vague and unspecified allegations — Detention on vague, in­definite and unspecified allegations with­out giving sufficient materials to enable the detenu to make effective repre­sentation to the appropriate authority is illegal — Special Powers Act (XIV of 1974)S 3.

 

Order of detention and specific criminal case — Whether a valid order of detention can be made on the grounds for which specific cases were started against the detenu — When a specific case has been started in relation to the alleged activities of the detenu, the same activitycould not in all fairness be taken as a reasonable basis for making an order of detention — Such grounds are not legal and valid and render the order of detention illegal — Special Powers Act (XIV of 1974) Ss. 2 and 3.

 

Whether a detenu can be detained on the basis of a subsequent valid order of detention when he was kept under illegal detention earlier without any valid order of detention — Subsequent valid order of detention can not validate a previous illegal detention order — Detenu having been kept under illegal detention without any order of detention at all, his subse­quent detention, even if it was a valid order, made the entire detention illegal.

 

Failure to furnish to the detenu the grounds of detention within the specified time — Failure to furnish to the detenu the grounds of detention within the specified time makes the detention illegal.

 

If the grounds of detention are not served on the detenu within 15 days of the detention, his detention becomes illegal on the expiry of 15 days.

 

Grounds of detention to be placed before the Advisory Board — Section 10 of the Special Powers Act provides that the Government shall within 120 days from the date of detention order place before the Advisory Board constituted under Section 9 of the Act grounds on which the order has been made and the representation, if any, made by the person affected by the order — Failure of the Government to do so renders the detention illegal — Special Powers act (XIV of 1974) S 10.

 

Whether a detenu can be detained on the basis of an order of detention when on the same date the order of his earlier detention was revoked by the same authority — The Government passed two inconsistent orders on the same date, one directing the revocation of the detention and his immediate release and the other for his fresh detention — This exposes the fact that on the one hand the Government felt satisfied that there was no cause for his detention and on the next breath the Government expressed its satisfaction that he should be detained — This establishes that the Government failed to apply its judicial mind in the matter of deciding the detention of the petitioner — Special Powers Act (XIV of 1974) Ss. 2,3, and 10. Syedur Rahman Khalifa Vs. The Secretary, Minsitry of Home Affairs and others 6 BLD (HCD) 272.

 

Ref. 20 DLR 1005; 28 DLR 117 and 48; 31 DLR (AD) 1; 30 DLR 423; 27 DLR 245 and 576; 33 DLR 110; 18 DLR (SC) 250; 18 DLR (Dhaka) 214

 

Non-communication of the grounds of detention — After the detention the detaining authority is bound to commu­nicate to the detenu the grounds of his detention — This communication must be either at the time of detention or within 15 days from the date of detention so that the detenu is able to make an effective representation to the authority against the order of detention — The service of detention order under Section 3 of the Special Powers Act incorporating vague, indefinite, evasive and uncertain grounds can not be treated as sufficient commu­nication of the grounds of detention — Such detention order does not enable the detenu to make any effective representa­tion against the order of detention — Non-communication of the grounds of detention makes his detention illegal — Special Powers Act (XIV of 1974) S 8 (1) and (2).

 

Grounds of detention to placed be­fore the Advisory Board — The grounds of detention must be placed before the Advisory Board within 120 days from the date of detention required under Section 10 of the Special Powers Act — The non-observance of this provision of Section 10 renders the detention illegal — Special Powers Act (XIV of 1974) S 10.

 

Whether the High Court Division has jurisdiction to interfere with an order of detention passed under the Special Powers Act — The High Court Division has jurisdiction under Section 491 Cr.P.C to interfere with an order of detention passed under the Special Powers Act where the detaining authority does not comply with the mandatory provisions of the Special Powers Act — It cannot be said that the detention of the detenu is lawful under the provisions of the Special Powers Act — In that case the High Court Division has jurisdiction under Section 491 Cr.P.C. to interfere with an illegal detention though it purports to be under an enactment providing for preven­tive detention — Code of Crimina Pro­cedure (V of 1898) S491(3).

 

The bar to jurisdiction of the Courts — The bar provided in Section 34 of the Special Powers Act does not protect orders passed in bad faith or in abuse of the act — Consequently when these issues are raised, the same are required to be investigated and Section 34 can not stand as a bar in such investigation — Special Powers Act (XIV of 1974) S 34. Sultana Ara Begum Vs. Secretary, Ministry of Home Affairs and others 7 BLD (HCD) 138.

Duty of the court under Section 491 Cr.P.C. — The duty of the Court under Section 491 Cr.P.C. is to see that the or­der of detention is not malafide or that the grounds given are not colourable, vague, irrelevant or evasive — The Court has got powers to examine the reasonableness of the authority's satis­faction as to detention of a person — Code of Criminal Procedure (V of 1898) S 491. Sultana Ara Begum Vs. Secre­tary, Ministry of Home Affairs and others 7 BLD (HCD) 138.

 

Detention — Whether its validity is to be decided in a writ petition challenging the detention — Whether the Rule becomes infructuous on the revocation of the  order  of detention  and  a fresh application is to be filed to challenge the subsequent order of detention — It is not the question of order of detention, it is the detention itself of the detenu which is the subject matter of the Rule — In case of detention the Court is to satisfy itself that the person is not being held in custody without lawful authority or in an unlawful manner — The scope of enquiry is not in any way fattered by procedures of a Writ of Habeas Corpus or the Rule laid down under the various Habeas Cropus Acts — The Court is to see that the executive authority had before it sufficient materials upon which a reasonable person would have come to the conclussion that the requirements of law were satisfied — The constitutional obligation is to see whether the person is being held without unlawful authority or in an unlawful manner — Only a valid order of detention could be continued by a subsequent order of de­tention which, of course is to be valid — An illegal order cannot be continued by a subsequent valid order of detention.

 

The only point to be determined in the writ petition is whether at the time of hearing of the Rule there is a valid order of detention before the High Court Di­vision so that it may satisfy itself that the detenu is not being held in custody without lawful authority or in an unlawful manner — The fact of detention and not the date of order of detention is the material consideration — In case of continued detention the aggrieved peti­tioner has got a running grievance.

 

Detention — If found malafide — It is well settled that malafide vitiates every­thing — The statement of the Home Minister when puts in juxtaposition with the grounds of detention the conclusion becomes obvious that the order of de­tention was passed for collateral purposes — The Constitution of Bangladesh, 1972, Art 102(2)(b). Mrs. Sajeda Parvin Banu Vs. Government of Bangladesh and others 8 BLD (AD) 129.

Service of grounds of detention on the detenu — Order of detention con­taining the object of detention can be considered to be service of grounds of detention — Whether service of grounds of detention beyond 15 days of the order of detention is valid and legal — When the order of detention does not contain the allegations for which detention order was made enabling the detenu to make an effective representation against his deten­tion the same cannot be considered as service of grounds of detention — Grounds of detention must be served upon the detenu within 15 days of his detention — Failure to furnish the grounds of detention within the required time makes the detention illegal — Even subsequent valid order of detention can­not validate the vital illegal detention — Special Powers Act (XIV of 1974) Ss. 3 and 8 Syam Sundar Rajgharia Vs. M. A. Salam Additional District Magistrate, Chittagong and others 8 BLD (HCD) 127.

Nexus between the order of detention and the grounds of detention and the satisfaction of the detaining authority —The order of detention must co-exist with the facts and materials of detention as contanied in the grounds of detention — There is no nexus between some of the reasons shown in the order of detention and the facts and reasons for detention as disclosed in the grounds — There is divergence between the order of detention and the grounds of detention and such divergence can be interpreted as showing both lack of application of the mind by the detaining authority as well as callousness towards the person detained for which the order of detention is liable to be struck down — Since the grounds of detention are vague and indefinite the detention cannot be sustained in law — The materials disclosed in the grounds of detention will not satisfy a man of average prudence that the detention is warranted — Special Powers Act (XIV of 1974) Ss. 3 and 8.

 

Detention and service of grounds by the Additional District Magistrate —Whether the Government can pass an independent order of detention without serving fresh grounds — The Government can either approve the order passed by the District Magistrate or Additional District Magistrate or pass an independent order of detention — The order dated 23.2.87 cannot be called either an order extending the earlier order of detention passed by the Additional District Magistrate or an independent order as no fresh grounds of detention were served upon the detenu — Since the order of detention by the Government has no legal foundation, all subsequent orders extending detention cannot be valid orders — Special Powers Act (XIV of 1974) Ss 3 and 8.

 

Executive Orders — All executive orders of the Government shall be ex­pressed to be taken in the name of the President — Rules of business of the Government as well as the provision of the Constitution provide the same — Some of the orders of detention were made and signed in the name of the President and the others in the name of the Government — Orders made and signed, if do not conform to the provision of the Constitution and Rules of business, then they become executive orders without authority — The Constitution of Bangladesh 1972, .Art. 56(2). Md Khair Ahmed Vs. Bangladesh and others 8 BLD (HCD) 157.

Detention on the ground of public safety — Whether permissible — The Special Powers Act, 1974, only empowers the Government to detain a person only to prevent him from doing prejudicial acts — There is no provision for making a detention order merely in the interest of public safety — Special Powers Act (XIV of 1974)S 3.

 

Prejudicial Act — Whether non­payment of loan taking from the Shilpa Bank is a prejudicial act — Prejudicial act also means any act which is intended likely to prejudice the economic and financial interest of the state — The act of intentional non-payment of loan has prejudiced the economic or financial interest of the state and as such it amounts to a prejudicial act — Special Powers Act (XIV of 1974)S2.

  

Detention order for default in repay­ment of loan from the Shilpa Bank is a prejudicial act but such non-payment cannot be a ground for preventive detention — Preventive detention is meant for preventing some one from doing any prejudicial act in the future — There is no authority to order detention for default already made in repayment of the loan and as such non-payment though a prejudicial act has already been done and it cannot be prevented by detention — Prejudicial act already done is an offence for which criminal case can be filed — When a specific criminal case has already been filed detention on the self­same allegations cannot be ordered — Special Powers Act (XIV of 1974) Ss. 3 and 16. Order of detention — Nexus between satisfaction and order of detention — It is well settled that there must be a rational nexus between the satisfaction of the detaining authority and the order of detention — Special Powers Act (XIV of 1974) Ss. 2(f)iii, (v), (vii) and (viii).

 

Grounds of detention to be placed before the Advisory  Board — Thedetention   as   become   illegal   as the Government did not place the grounds of detention  before  the  Advisory Board within 120 days from the order of de­tention — The Constitution of Bangla­desh,  1972, Arts. 31, 32 and 33 Special Powers Act (XIV of 1974) Ss 10-12    Mrs.   Shahana   Begum Vs. Communication of the grounds of detention — Communicating the grounds of detention in a leisurely manner is not permissible — The provision made in the Special Powers Act "but not later than 15 days from the date of detention" does not give a general licence to the detaining authority to cause service of grounds of detention in a leisurely manner within 15 days in every case delibaretely to deprive the detenu of his valuable legal right to make an effective representation to the appropriate authority at the earlier possible opportunity or to move the Court at once — Constitution of Bangladesh, 1972, Art. 33(5) — Special Powers Act (XIV of 1974) S 8(2).

 

Report of the Advisory Board and decision of the Court — If the Rule has been issued by the Court and if the same is ready for hearing it is the duty of the court, in view of the provisions of the Code of Criminal Procedure and the Constitution, to hear the matter at once giving opportunity to the parties to make their submissions and pronounce its de­cision as early as possible without waiting for the Advisory Board to report its opinion to the Government — Code of Criminal Procedure (V of 1898) S 491 — Constitution of Bangladesh, 1972 Art. 102.

 

Grounds of detention must have ra­tional probative value — Grounds of detention served on the detenu and the materials before the detaining authority must have rational probative value — Special Powers Act (XIV of 1974) S 3(1).

 

Preventive detention of a foreigner —In the case of a citizen of a foreign country preventive detention is uncalled for — Special Powers Act (XIV of 1974) S 3(1 )(b) Dr. Ma\ Habibullah Vs. Secretary, Ministry of Home Affairs and others 9 BLD (HCD) 387.

Habeas Corpus — Scope of an appli­cation under Section 491 of the Code of Criminal Procedure — The High Court has discretion in the matter as regards the nature of the order to be passed and that it may pass any order any time  — The words "illegally" or "improperly" used in the Section means that when a person is not detained within the provision of any law it becomes an illegal detention — The words"improperly" refers to cases in which although the forms of the law have been observed but there has been a fraud on the Act or an abuse of the process of the powers given to the executive — In such cases it is the duty of the Court to step in and order the detenu to be set at liberty — The scope of Section 491 Cr PC is wider than the constitutional provision — Code of Criminal Procedure (V of 1898) S 491 Syeda Rezja Begum Vs. The People's Republic of Bangladesh and others 9 BLD (HCD) 441.

 

Fresh order of detention — When it is illegal — The Government revoked the order of detention of the detenu but instead of releasing him passed a fresh order of detention under the Special Powers Act —i This fresh order of de­tention without releasing the detenu from the jail is illegal — Special Powers Act (XIV of 1974) S 3(1 )(a).

 

Detention for over six months —Validity 'of such detention without ap­proval by the Advisory Board — It is clear from the provision of the constitu­tion that if the Advisory Board does not report within six months that there is sufficient cause for the detention, the Government can not detain the detenu beyond six months.

 

The case of the detenu must be placed before the Advisory Board within six months. The case of the detenu was not placed before the Advisory Board within six months of his detention as required by Section 10 of the Special Powers Act — The order of detention is therefore in contravention of the constitutional provi­sion — The Constitution of Bangladesh, 1972, Art. 33(4) — Special Powers Act (XIV of 1974) Ss. 3 and 10. Manowara Begum Vs. Secretary, Ministry of Home Affairs and others 9 BLD (HCD) 467.

 

Detention order — Time lag between prejudicial activity and detention order — Obligation of the detaining authority to explain the delay — In the present case there is a delay of over 2 years in making the detention order after the incidents of prejudicial activity — The time lag being so wide and there being no explanation as to why the detention order was passed after such a long time, the detaining authority is held to have failed to furnish a rational nexus between the grounds of detention and the passing of the order of detention and as such the same is declared illegal — Special Powers Act (XIV of 1974) S 8 Mahbuba Waited Vs. Govern­ment of Bangladesh and others 10 BLD (HCD) 19

Order of detention — Successive orders of detention passed keeping the detenu in custody after previous orders are illegal — As soon as the illegality of the order of detention is communicated to the respondents, the order of detention loses its force and becomes an illegal order unless appealed against and a stay is obtained — The order of detention pur­porting to continue an illegal detention cannot be sustained — Third order of detention falls through on this ground alone — Special Powers Act (XIV of 1974)S 3.

 

Grounds of detention — Repetition of the same grounds in support of sub­sequent orders of detention which have been declared illegal — On the ground of serving an illegal ground of detention also the detention becomes illegal — The grounds which were once declared illegal in the same cause cannot acquire legality by repeated use — They suffer the same fate — Special Powers Act (XIV of 1974) S 3 and 8.

 

Fresh grounds of detention — Service of fresh grounds and the question of their validity — Within the specified time of 15 days the detaining authority can add, vary or amend any ground but after its expiry they have no legal authority to add or substitute any ground — Service of fresh grounds served beyond the specified time illegal — Such recourse to illegal grounds is an act of patent malafide — Special Powers Act (XIV of 1974) Ss. 3 and 8

 

Repeated   orders   of detention —Whether every order need be specifically challenged — Respondents may pass any number of orders during the period of one's detention but it is not necessary for the petitioner to challenge every order as long as the detention is under challenge — The Constitution of Bangladesh, 1972, Art. 102 — Special Powers Act (XIV of 1974) S 3 Mrs. Alam Ara Huq Vs. Judicial   Review   of Detentoin —Subjective satisfaction of the detaining authority and the objective satisfaction of the Court — The need for judicial review when arises — A conflict between the constitutional mandate and the arbitrary and colourable exercise of power by the detaining authority calls for the exercise of judicial review by the High Court Division to strike down actions which are "malafide, colourable exercise of statutorypower or action taken upon extraneous or irrelevant considerations or action taken on no ground or without application of mind" to deprive a citizen of his personal liberty in an unlawful manner — The order of detention must bear a rational nexus between the satisfaction of the detaining authority and the order of detention.

 

Grounds of detention — These being vague, indefinite and lacking in material particulars as to time, place and manner to conform to or to co-relate with the order of detention preventing the detenu from making proper representation to the appropriate authority have been held to be illegal — Further more, for individual isolated acts of crimes viz theft, dacoity or hijacking or collection of tolls, detention law cannot be applied to deprive the constitutionally guaranteed right of liberty of a citizen.

 

Section 3(1) — Initial order of de­tention valid upto certain period — Within that period no valid fresh order of detention was passed — After the expiry of the first order, the detention becomes illegal — Subsequent order of detention even if valid, is liable to be struck down on the ground that it proceeds on an illegal detention — Order of detention not co-relative to or co-extensive with the factum of detention, the detention is held illegal.

 

Maximum period for which a person may  be  held  in  detention  — The Government can hold a person in deten­tion for a maximum period of 120 days — The Government is bound to place the case of detenu before the Advisory Board within 120 days — Non-compliance with this mandatory provision of law renders the order of detention illegal and void — Special Powers Act (XIV of 1974) S 3(1) — The Constitution of Bangladesh, 1972, Article 32 — Code of Criminal Pro­cedure (V of 1898 ) S 491. Grounds of detention — Section 8 of the Special Powers Act, 1974 provides that the detaining authority must furnish the grounds of detention to the detenu within 15 days from the date of making the order of detention — The detaining authority having failed to comply with this mandatory provision of law to furnish the grounds of detention to the detenu so as to enable him to submit any effective representation to the detaining authority against his detenu, the detention of the detenu becomes illegal and without any lawful authority — Special Powers Act (XIV of 1974) Ss 3(2) and 8(2) Haji Md Joynal Ahedin Vs. Bangladesh and others 10 BLD (HCD) 364

Authority of the Government to detain a person — Under Section 3(1 )(a) of the Special Powers Act, 1974 the Govern­ment is empowered to detain any person if it is satisfied that with a view to preven­ting him from doing any prejudicial act, it is necessary to make an order directing that such a person be detained.

Detention on vague and indefinite grounds — No person can be detained on vague and indefinite grounds as the same do not enable him to make an effective representation to the detaining authority.

 

Pendency  of a  criminal   case —Pendency of a criminal case against the detenu cannot be the reasonable basis for detaining him under Section 3 of the Special Powers Act, 1974. Mrs. Sultana Begum Vs. The Secretary, Ministry of Home Affairs and others 11 BLD (HCD) 449.

Specific Criminal Case — When there is a specific criminal case against the detenu on certain allegation, the self-same allegation can not constitute a ground for detention of the detenu under the Special Powers Act Sree Utpal Kumar Dutta Vs. The Government of Bangladesh and others 12 BLD (HCD) 375.
