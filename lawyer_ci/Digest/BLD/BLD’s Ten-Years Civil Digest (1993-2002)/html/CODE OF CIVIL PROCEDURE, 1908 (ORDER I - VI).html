Order I Rule 10

Necessary Party or Proper Party

In the absence of any material to show that the lessees are in possession of the disputed property upon renewal of their lease by the Government on payment of rent, such lessees cannot be held to be necessary parties in a suit in which the lessor government is a defendant.

Babor Ali Pramanik and others Vs. Mesar Ali Pramanik and others, 14 BLD (AD) 235.

Ref: 38 DLR (AD) 308; 39 DLR (HCD) 325; 38 DLR (HCD) 325; A. 1. R. 1953 (SC) 73.—Cited.

 

Order I Rule 3, Order I Rule 10(2)

In the absence of any specific provision in the Non-Agricultural Tenancy Act as to who are to be made parties in an application under Section 24, the procedural law, namely Order 1, Rule 3 or Order 1, Rule 10(2) C.P.C. will govern the field.

The co-sharers are not necessary parties in terms of Order 1, Rule 3 C.P.C. nor are they proper parties unless the co-sharers themselves pray to the Court to add them as parties on the ground of non-receipt of notice or lack of knowledge or any other acceptable ground.

In a proceeding under section 24 of the Act, it will be presumed that all co-sharers have received the requisite notice under section 23(I) of the Act unless any co-sharer prefers to rebut that presumption.

Golam Mohammad Vs. Sree Sailendra Nath Kanangoe being dead his heirs: Sree Biman Behari Kanangoe and others, 13 BLD (AD) 68.

Ref: Hosnera Begum and others Vs. Mosammat Anowara Begum and others. 37 DLR154; Nelly Zaman Vs. Giasuddin Khan, 34 DLR (AD)221; Shafique Mia Vs. Meherunessa and another, C.P. No. 10 of 1974 (digested in Bangladesh Supreme Court Digest, vol. Page236)—Cited.

 

Order I Rule 10

Transposition of Party

There is no bar in transposing the petitioners into the category of the plaintiffs in the instants partition suit on the facts and circumstances for complete and proper adjudication of the questions in dispute in the suit and to avoid multiplicity of litigations between the parties.

Md. Abdul Jalil Shah and another Vs Rahima Khatun and others, 19 BLD (HCD) 169.

Order I, Rule 10

The Court may at any stage of the proceeding order that the name of a party improperly joined, whether as plaintiff or defendant, be struck out and that the name of any person, whose presence before the Court is considered necessary for effectual and complete adjudication of the questions involved in the suit, be added.

Makbulur Rahman and others Vs. Halima Textile Mills Ltd. Employees Pro vi- dent Fund Trust, Comilla, and others, 14 BLD (HCD) 429.

Order I Rule 10(2)—Necessary Party

In a suit for ejectment of a tenant third party who is not claiming tenancy under the plaintiff is neither a necessary nor a proper party. The presence of Dhaka City Corporation as a defendant in the suit is neither necessary nor proper in order to enable the Court effectively and completely to adjudicate upon and settle all questions involved in the suit.

Noor Mohammad Vs. Md. Noor Hossain and another, 22 BLD (HCD) 502.

 

Order I Rule 10

The question of title cannot be determined in a suit for ejectment of a monthly tenant. The petitioner, who is not a tenant under the plaintiff, wanted to be added as a defendant in the suit on the plea that the suit property is a vested property and the plaintiff has no title therein. In such circumstances, the petitioner was riot found to be a necessary party.

Additional Deputy Commissioner (Revenue) and Assistant Custodian, Vs Md. Mozammel Haque, 16 BLD (AD) 237.

 

Order I Rule 10(2)

In view of the fact that the petitioner have purchased a substantial portion of the suit property from the plaintiff by a registered sale deed and they are allegedly in possession thereof, it is evidence that their vital interest are involved in the suit and as such their presence in the suit appears to be just and proper in the interest of justice.

Md. Zahirul Haque Chowdhury and another Vs Sree Sankor Lal Deb and others, 19 BLD (HCD) 10.

 

Order I Rule 10(2)

Court may add parties at any stage of the proceedings

Under Order 1 Rule 10(2) of the Code the Court may, at any stage of the proceedings. either upon or without the application of either party, may strike out or add parties whose presence is considered necessary in order to enable the Court to effectually and completely adjudicate upon and settle all the questions involved in the suit.

In the present case, the learned S.C.C. Judge allowed the respondents prayer to be added as defendants in the suit claiming themselves as the joint owners-in-possession of the Suit premises. Since upon judicious considerations of the relevant facts of the case and in exercise of the authority under Order 1 Rule 10(2) C.P.C. the learned S.C.C. Judge made respondents as defendants in the suit for enabling it to effectually and completely adjudicate upon all questions involved in the suit, the impugned order does not suffer from any legal infirmity.

Musammat Umme Kulsum Vs. Shamiyana Rahman and ors, 18 BLD (AD) 13.

 

Order 1 Rule 10(2)

In a suit for specific performance of contract it is not permissible to adjudicate on the question of title of stranger to the contract who need not therefore be made a party to such a suit.

Muzaffor Ali and another Vs Messrs Monwora Hospital and others, 19 BLD (HCD) 484.

Ref: 43 DLR(AD)107; 43DLR (HC) 160—Cited.

 

Order I Rule 10(2)

Court may strike out or add parties

In view of the fact that there are specific allegations in the plaint against the petitioner, the truth or otherwise of which can only be decided at the trial on taking evidence, there is no justification to strike out the name of the petitioner from the plaints. The High Court Division and the trial Court rightly refused the prayer of the petitioner for striking out his name from the plaint.

Ghulam Rahman Vs. National Bank Limited, 16 BLD (AD) 250.

 

Order I Rule 10

Transposition of parties

When the right to sue survived on the death of the sole plaintiff, respondent Nos. 1 and 2, who were proforma defendants in the suit, became entitled to proceed with the suit as heiresses of the deceased plaintiff on transposition as plaintiffs from the category of defendants. If respondent Nos. 1 and 2 succeed in cancelling the two disputed Hibabil- Ewaj deeds, it will be for the trial Court to make a suitable declaration in respect of the ownership of the properties covered by the two deeds amongst the heirs and heiresses of the deceased plaintiff. The existence of other heirs of the deceased-plaintiff cannot stand in the way of substitution and transposition of respondent Nos. 1 and 2.

Ramizuddin Miah Vs. Jarimunnessa and others, 15 BLD (AD) 52.

 

Order I Rule 9

Misjoinder and Nonjoinder of Parties

It is a settled law supported by a catena of decisions that one of the co-owners of a joint property can alone bring a suit for ejecting a trespasser from the property owned by him and others either jointly or as co-owners, the basis being that as a co-sharer he has a right to hold every inch of the joint property until a partition takes place. A suit at the instance of some co-sharers in exclusive possession of a joint property for recovery of possession against a trespasser is quite maintainable in law. In such a case the failure to implead other co-sharers as parties to the suit will not affect the plaintiff’s right to recover possession from the trespassers.

The uniform judicial opinion is that a Suit by only one co-sharer in exclusive possession of a joint property warding off an invasion to the rights of all co-sharers of a joint property is maintainable in law.

Baroda Sundari Paul and others Vs. The Assistant Custodian, Enemy Property (Land and Buildings), Comilla and others, 15 BLD (AD) 95.

 

Order I Rule 10

Transposition of a party

In view of the averments made in the plaint itself that the proforma defendants have identical interest in the suit land as co-owners with the plaintiffs, the pro-forma defendants prayer for transposing them as co-plaintiffs con not be resisted. They should be made co plaintiffs by way of transposition for complete and effective adjudication of the suit.

Md. Motahar Hossain and others Vs. Moenuddin Sk. and others, 15 BLD (HCD) 330

 

Order I Rule 10 and Order XXII Rule 4

To maintain an application under Order 1 Rule 10 C.P.C. for being added as parties or an application under Order XXII Rule 4 of the Code for substitution the applicants must show that they have subsisting interest in the estate left by their predecessor-in-interest. Such an application can succeed only when right to sue survives. In the absence of any personal interest of the deceased in the subject-matter of the proceeding, his successors do not come within the ambit of legal representatives as contemplated in Rule 4 of Order XXII of the Code.

Bainchitala Junior High School Vs. Md. Abdur Rashid and others, 17 BLD (HCD) 513.

Ref: PLD 1974 (Lahore)312; 22 DLR 500; 42 DLR48 1; 32 DLR(AD) 96—Cited.

 

Order I Rule 3

Necessary party and proper party

A ‘necessary party’ is one whose presence on the record is enjoined by law or in whose absence no effective decision can at all be given and in the absence of that person the suit cannot be said to be properly constituted. In such a case the suit is liable to be dismissed.

A ‘proper party’, on the other hand, is one whose presence is not essential in the constitution of the suit but whose presence is desirable in order to effectively and completely adjudicate on the matters involved in the suit. If the omission to implead a necessary party is not corrected by the plaintiff, the suit is liable to be dismissed [per Latifur Rahman, J, delivering the dissenting judgment.]

Barada Sundari Paul and others Vs. The Assistant Custodian, Enemy Property (Land and Buildings), Comilla and others, 15 BLD (AD) 95.

 

Order 1 Rule 10(2)

Courts power to add or strike out parties

Order 1 Rule 10(2) of the Code of Civil Procedure authorises the Court to add or strike out a party in a suit at any stage of the proceeding. Under certain circumstances a lessee has a right to be impleaded in the suit as a defendant as the interests of the lessor and the lessee may not always coincide.

Sree Gobinda Chandra Karmakar and others Vs. Sree Shanti Ranjan Talukdar and others, 16 BLD (HCD) 6.

Ref: 31DLR 107; 38 DLR(AD) 308; A.I.R. 1958 (SC) 886—Cited.

 

Order I Rule 10

Addition of Parties

An application for being added as a party in a pending suit must be a bonafide one and it can be allowed only when it appears to the Court that the presence of the applicant is necessary for effective adjudication of the suit. The applicants having set up a different genealogy and claiming title and possession in a large area of the suit land on the basis of purchase during the pendency of the suit their interest in the land purchased by them will not be affected if the suit is decided in their absence. Moreover, parties cannot be allowed to obtain documents in respect of the suit land during pendency of the suit in the names of their minor sons and daughters, married or unmarried, to set up a different case, at the peremptory hearing stage of the suit, from the one they had set up earlier with the object of lingering the litigations. The order of addition of parties is set aside and the written statement, if any filed by the added Opposite parties, is rejected.

Aynaddin Howlader Vs. Maniruzzaman (Mani) Minor, 16 BLD (HCD) 391.

 

Order 1, Rule 10(2)

Additional of parties

It clearly lays down that the Court can direct addition of a party under two circumstances, namely (1) when the Court finds that he ought to have joined either as a plaintiff or as a defendant, in which case he becomes a necessary party; but he has not been joined so, or (2) whose presence may be necessary in order to enable the Court effectually and completely to adjudicate upon and settle all questions involved in the suit, in which case he becomes a proper party.

In the instant case the plaintiff has sought for declarations that she is the absolute owner of the suit property and that it is not an abandoned property and also prayed for recovery of kha possession. Defendant No.2 being an allottee under defendant No. 1 and being in possession of the first floor of the building is a proper party to the suit and the order of the trial Court making him a party is a proper order that calls for no intereference.

Mrs. Jahanara Begum Vs. Govt. of Bangladesh. 16 BLD (HCD) 464.

Ref: 31 DLR107; 43DLR 169; 45 DLR (AD) 120; 38 DLR(AD)308;--Cited.

 

Order I Rule 8

Suing in representative capacity

Under Order 1 Rule 8 of the Code of Civil Procedure a representative suit can be filled with the permission of the Court. The Court shall in such case give, at the plaintiffs’ expense, notice of the institution of the suit to all concerned. Absence of a regular permission of the Court renders the proceeding untenable in law.

Shaikh Tabibur Rahman and others Vs. Shaikh Nazrul Islam and others, 16 BLD (HCD) 12.

 

Order I Rule 10

Necessary parties in a partition suit

In a partition suit even a third person claiming title to the suit property adversely to the plaintiff and the defendants is a necessary party. Under exceptional circumstances, addition of necessary parties can be allowed even after the passing of the preliminary decree.

Abdur Rahman and others Vs. Abdus Sattar and others, 17 BLD (HCD) 90.

 

Order I Rule 10(2) and Order VI Rule 17

State Acquisition and Tenancy Act, 1950, Section—96(2)

The object of Rule 10(2) of Order I of the Code of Civil Procedure is to enable the Court at any stage of the proceedings to add any person as a party thereto when it is found necessary for effectual and complete adjudication of all questions involved in the suit case. The expression ‘at any stage of the proceeding’ confers a wide discretion on the Court in the matter of addition or striking out of parties.

A belated prayer for addition of a necessary party under Order 1 Rule 10(2) or for amendment of pleadings under Order VI Rule 17 of the Code by itself is no ground to refuse such a prayer if such addition or amendment of pleadings be found necessary for complete and effectual adjudication of the matters in issue.

Section 96(2) of the S.A.T. Act also requires that all necessary parties should be brought before the Court for complete and effectual adjudication of the matter before it.

Mst. Hasina Khatoon and another Vs Md. Samsur Rahman and others, 17 BLD (HCD) 177.

Ref: 33 DLR (AD) 113---Cited.

 

Order II Rule 2(3) and order VII Rule 11

The question that came up for consideration before the Appellate Division is as to whether a subsequent suit for a particular relief is available to the plaintiff when he failed to incorporate the said relief in an earlier suit between the same parties in respect of the same transaction. When the earlier suit came up before the Appellate Division it was found that the present claim of taka 7 lacs was made in a concealed manner in paragraph 21 of the plaint of that suit. The learned counsel appearing for the defendant-petitioner submitted at that time that the plaintiff-respondent could not get taka 7 lacs in that suit without suing for account and his submission was accepted. The defendant-petitioner cannot now turn round and take up an opposite stand to plead that the present suit for that particular relief is not available to the plaintiff in view of the provision of Order II Rule 2 C.P.C. In the facts of the case, it cannot be said that the present suit comes within the mischief of Order VII Rule 11 C.P.C. for rejection of the plaint.

Sonali Bank Vs. Gazi Abdur Rashid, 15 BLD (AD) 93.

 

Order III Rule 2(A)

Evidence Act, 1872, Section—85

Order 3 Rule 2(A) of the Code provides that the recognised agent of parties by whom appearance and acts may be made or done includes holding power of attorney but such power of attorney must be executed according to the provision of section 85 of the Act otherwise a person holding a power of attorney is not legally permitted to represent his principal under Order 3 Rule 2(A) of the Code.

In the instant case it has been found that the power of attorney in question has not been executed in terms of section 85 of the Act and as such the petitioner is not entitled to the benefit of Order 3 Rule 2(A) of the Code.

Ramesh Chandra Chowdhury @ Das Vs Naresh Chandra Das @ Chowdhury, 20 BLD (HCD) 35.

 

Order III Rule 4(2)

Where a person is no longer the appointed advocate of a party, it is incumbent upon the court of the subordinate judge to communicate an order to the parties about the arrival of the records of the case after restoration by the High Court Division.

Order V Rule 20

Substituted service

When the Court decides to effect substituted service under Rule 20 of Order 5 of the Code of Civil Procedure it must direct that the summons be served by affixing a copy thereof in some conspicuous place in the court house and also in some conspicuous part of the house in which the defendant is known to have last resided or carried on business or personally worked for gain, or in such other manner as the court thinks fit.

The order for effecting a service by affixing a copy of the summon in a conspicuous part of the court house and also at some conspicuous place of the last known residence of the defendant is obviously for wide publicity. In addition, the Rule empowers the court to effect substituted service by any other manner it thinks fit and proper. When these mandatory requirements of law are not complied with, it must be held that there was no proper service of summons.

China Bala Saha Vs. Abdul Bari and others, 15 BLD (HCD) 5.

 

Order V Rule 17 

When the summons is returned under Rule 17 of Order 5 C.P.C. it is incumbent upon the Court to make an enquiry under Rule 19 of Order 5 of the Code and to record a distinct declaration of due service, if satisfied, or to order fresh service. When the service return has not been verified by the declaration of the serving officer, his examination by the Court is essential before recording due service.

Sharif Ullah Patwary and others vs. Jharna Dhara Chowdhury and another, 14 BLD (HCD) 257.

 

Order V Rule 17

When service of summons is effected by affixing a copy of the summons on the outer door or some other conspicuous part of the residence of the defendants and there are allegations of fraudulent suppression of the summons, a duty is cast upon the court in a suit for setting aside the ex-parte decree to determine the question of due service of summons. The failure of the appellate court to decide this important question of fact is not warranted by law.

Anowar Hossain (Chunnu) and others Vs. Abdur Rashid Howlader and others, 15 BLD (HCD) 442.

 

Order V Rules 17, 19 and 19A

When summons is returned under Rule 17 of Order 5 C.P.C. Rule 19 requires that the Court has to examine the serving officer on oath touching his proceeding and to ascertain by enquiry in the matter as to whether summons has been duly served or not and make a declaration to that effect.

Rule 19A of Order 5 C.P.C. provides that a declaration made and subscribed by the serving officer shall be received as evidence of the facts as to the service or attempted service of summons in which case examination of process server is not essential.

In the instant case in the absence of any declaration by the serving officer order of the learned Munsif reading ‘notice has been served’ does not satisfy the requirements of law as to due service of notice and as such it must be held that there has been no due service of summons. The ex-parte decree is accordingly set aside.

Sk. Haider Ali Vs. Abdul Wahab Gazi and others, 16 BLD (HCD) 316.

Order V Rules 17, 19 and 19A

When summons upon the defendant is effected by hanging and it is returned under Rule 17, the Court is required to examine the serving officer on oath, if the return has not been verified by affidavit. Although Rule 19A of Order V provides that a declaration made and subscribed by the serving officer is to be received as evidence of the facts as to the service of summons the Court has the power to examine the serving officer on oath for being satisfied regarding due service of summons. At any rate, the Court cannot accept the service of summons mechanically.

Abdul Wahab Gazi Vs. Sheikh Haider Ali and others, 16 BLD (AD) 53.

Order VI, Rule 17

Before allowing prayer for amendment, it is to be seen whether the amendment sought for is merely a formal addition of certain facts or whether the same has bearing on the nature or character of the suit and of it is found that it will have the effect of making out a new case for the plaintiffs having direct bearing on the merits of the case, such amendment should not be allowed under Order 6, Rule 17 of the Code of Civil Procedure.

Md. Ismail Vs. Motassim Ali Chowdhury 13 BLD (HCD) 172.

Order VI Rule 17

Amendment of pleadings

It is a well recognised principle that once an amendment of the plaint is allowed such amendment relates back to the date of the institution of the suit. But the Court will be reluctant to allow an amendment which would have the effect of altering the nature of the suit or it would take away a legal right accrued to the defendant by lapse of time. Since the suit was not time-barred at the time of its institution and no legal right accrued to the defendants by lapse of time since the filing of the suit, the proposed amendments should be allowed.

Doon Valley Rice Limited Vs. M. V. YUE YANG, 16 BLD (HCD) 469.

 

Order VI rule 17

An application for amendment of the plaint should not be rejected on the ground that the preliminary decree was already drawn up, and that re-opening the suit and adducing further evidence will cause inordinate delay in disposal and hardship to the parties.

Md. Hanif Ali v. Hajera Khatun and others, 22 BLD (HCD) 217.

 

Order VI Rule 17

Order VI rule 17 of the Code provides that the Court may at any stage of the proceedings allow either party to alter or amend his pleadings in such manner and on such terms as may be just and necessary for the purpose of determining the real question in controversy between the parties. The proposed amendment would settle the question whether during the pendency of the suit the plaintiff was dispossessed by defendant No.1 from the suit land. This will end all pending controversies between the parties and will not amount to a change in the nature and character of the suit.

Shahajadpur Central Co-operative Bank Ltd. Vs. Md. Majibur Rahman and others, 18 BLD (AD) 81.

 

Order VI Rule 17

Since all rules of the Court are intended to secure the proper administration of justice, it is essential that they should be made to serve and be subordinate to that purpose so that full powers of amendment may be enjoyed and s such it should always be liberally exercised. The only limitation in allowing an amendment of the plaint is that the proposed amendment should not change the fundamental character and nature of the suit. The settled law is that amendment of pleadings may be allowed at any stage of the proceeding for the purpose of determining the real of controversy between the parties.

A suit for simple declaration of title and recovery of possession and/or partition is essentially based on a common claim of the plaintiff which may call for different relief or reliefs in different situations and as such the adding of a relevant new relief by way of amendment does not change the nature and character of the suit when the claim of the plaintiff basically rests on the same foundation. In the instant case by adding a relief for partition in a suit for declaration of title and possession there is no alteration to the foundation of the suit and the fundamental character of the suit remains the same and as such the proposed amendment was rightly allowed.

Abdul Motaleb Vs Md. Ershad Ali and others, 18 BLD (AD) 121.

Ref: AIR 1922(PC)249: ILR 48 Cal 832; 31 DLR (AD) 133; 13 DLR (SC) 191; (1893) ILR 2OCal. 805; AIR 1933 Rangoon 247; 36 DLR (AD) 253; A.I.R.1971 Mad. 469; Sundaram Iyer in re. 1963-1 Mad. L.J.133; A.I.R.1963 Mad. 217(FB); AIR 1976 Karnatak 39; ILR Madras Vol.1(1876-78)333—-- Cited.

 

Order VI Rule 17

Under Order 6 Rule 17 C. P. C. the court has discretion to allow amendment of the pleadings if such amendment does not, alter the nature and character of the suit. But such discretion must be exercised judicially. The Order must reflect the application of due judicial mind of the court on proper consideration of the application for amendment and the objection filed against it.

M. S. Begum and Company Ltd Vs. Rupali Bank and others, 14 BLD (HCD) 515.

 

Order VI Rule 17

Amendment of pleadings- Rule 17 of Order VI of the Code of Civil Procedure has two distinct parts. The first part may be called a discretionary provision which gives discretion to the Court to allow amendment of pleadings while the second part may be called a mandatory provision which directs the Court to allow all such amendments as are necessary for determining the real questions in controversy between the parties.

When the effect of the proposed amendment does not change the fundamental character of the suit, though the proposed amendment introduces a new case of acquisition of title, such an amendment cannot be refused. A plaintiff can set up as many roots of title as possible and these may be even conflicting and inconsistent.

Samarendra Nath Roy Chowdhury Vs. Abdul Jabbar and others, 14 BLD (HCD) 229.

Ref: 54 C.W.N. 445, A.I.R. 1964 (Cal) 57-Cited.

 

Order VI, Rule 17

A new defence story having a remarkable departure from the stand taken in written statement will prejudice the Plaintiff in his suit.

Showkat Ara Begum Vs. Md. Wazuddin and others, 14 BLD (HCD) 61.

 

Order VI Rule 17

Since the defendant-petitioner accepted the order of amendment of the plaint and consequent thereto filed an additional written statement, the order does not warrant interference.

Mst. Jajia Khatun v. Md. Tajuddin alias Sukur Mamud and others, 22 BLD (HCD) 363.

Ref: PLD 1960 (Azad J & K) 70; 49 DLR 315.

 

 

Order VI, Rule 17

The guiding principle for amendment of pleadings is that it is to be made for the purpose of determining the real questions in controversy. It applies to the amendment of the plaint as well as to the amendment of the written statement. The Court can take into account subsequent events necessitating amendments by addition of new relief for doing complete justice.

Md. Nazrul Islam Majumder Vs. Tahamina Akhtar alias Nahid and another, 14 BLD (HCD) 299.

Ref: A.I.R. 1960 (Cal) 278; A.I.R. 1970 (Raj) 77; A.1.R. 1963 (Mad) 217 (FB), A.I.R. 1960 (Ker) 93-Cited.

 

Order VI Rules 1 and 2

The purpose of pleadings is to pinpoint the matters of controversy between the contending parties for enabling them to meet their respective claims. If at the trial the plaintiff fails to prove some of his allegations that does not necessarily mean that the suit shall fail. The fate of the suit depends on the overall evidence and materials on record.

Sonali Bank Vs. Mr. Hare Krishna Das and other, 16 BLD (HCD) 159.

 

Order VI Rule 17

Amendment of pleadings

Under Order 6 Rule 17 C.P.C. the Court enjoys a wide discretion in allowing amendment of pleadings at any stage of the proceedings provided that the proposed amendment does not change the nature and character of the suit or of the pleadings no does it cause undue prejudice to the opposite party. Amendment is also allowed when it appears to the Court that it is necessary for complete and effectual adjudication of the suit or the case. Law, however, does not permit an applicant to take a completely inconsistent and contradictory defence under the garb of a relief under section 151 of the Code of Civil Procedure.

 

Order VI Rule 17

The decree passed by the trial Court finally stands and was not superseded or wiped out in any way. The amount allowed by the trial Court by way of amendment under Order 6 Rule 17 of the Code for additional claim was part of the award and that deposit of the sum awarded is a statutory precondition to file an application challenging the award and such deposit does not amount to actual payment.

The University of Dhaka Vs. Associated Engineers and Drillers, 21 BLD (HCD) 527.

 

Order VI Rule 17

Amendment of Pleading

For effectually and completely adjudicating upon and-setting all questions involved in the suit, the Court may allow amendment of the pleadings at any stage of the suit, provided it does not change the nature and character of the suit. A belated prayer for amendment is no ground to refuse it.

Anwar Hossain Vs Shahadat Hossain and others, 17 BLD (HCD) 442.

Ref: 32 DLR 63; 27 DLR(AD)129 Cited.

 

Order VI Rule 17

Amendment of pleadings

Amendment of pleadings is essentially a matter of discretion of the Court necessitated by the urge to completely and effectually resolve all the controversies between the contending parties. The power of amendment is intended to be exercised for advancing and not for frustrating the cause of justice in appropriate cases, the Court has power to allow amendment of pleadings even at a belated stage provided it does not change the nature and character of the suit or of pleadings.

Abdul Hakim Vs. Secretary, Public Works and Urban Development, Dhaka and others, 18 BLD (HCD) 170.

 

Order VI Rule 17

Amendment of pleadings

For advancing the cause of justice a plaintiff deserves full opportunity to place his case against all the parties involved by stating all the facts he wants to allege against them and by seeking all possible remedies. It is for the plaintiff to do whatever he likes with the later suit. The direction to hold separate trial does not mean that the plaintiff must pursue the second suit or else his first suit will be strictly limited within its original frame.

The plaintiff as the ‘arbiter litis’ of his suits has the liberty to pursue or not to pursue any one of the two suits. The two suits being inextricably linked together can be amended as the plaintiff wishes to lay stress on either the first suit or the second suit having regard to the fact he is obliged to pursue the two suits basically for the same remedy. The trial courts order for amendment of the plaint is restored.

Mohammad Khaledur Reza Chowdhury Vs. Saleha Begum & others, 17 BLD (AD) 86.

 

Order VI, Rule 17

When a plaint is amended the suit is generally to be taken out from the list of peremptory hearing in order to enable the defendants to file an additional written statement, if they so want.

Md. Habibur Rahman and others Vs Md. Abdur Rashid and others, 21 BLD (HCD) 227.

 

Order VI Rule 17

Amendment of Pleadings

A suit for declaration of title and a suit for redemption of mortgage are by nature and procedure wise different and one cannot be substituted for the other. Pleadings in the two suits have to be different. Ordinarily an amendment of the plaint seeking to convert a suit for declaration of title with prayer for confirmation/recovery of possession into a suit for redemption of mortgage would never be permitted because in that case the nature and character of the suit will be entirely changed. In the instant case there was no prayer even for amendment of the plaint.

The High Court Division acted illegally in holding that there was no bar in treating the suit as one of redemption of mortgage and thereupon directing the trial Court to pass a decree declaring the title of the plaintiff etc. in respect of the mortgaged land (1.60 acres) for which it must be set aside but other part of the judgment remanding the suit to the trial court for coming to a finding as to whether the plaintiffs acquired title will be upheld (no further oral evidence will be permitted in the retrial except as may be necessary for proving any document).

Mohd. Eusuf and others Vs Haji Golam Bari and others, 17 BLD (AD) 113.
