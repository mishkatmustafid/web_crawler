Section-167(5)

The amended provision of section 167(5)- the provisions of section (5) of section 167 Cr. P. C. is undoubtedly a procedural law. The Criminal Procedure Code (Second Amendment) Act, 1992 (Act XLII of 1992) did not make any provision that the amendment brought in section 167, although a procedural law, will operate prospectively- operates retrospectively.

Kali Pada Dutta Vs. Subal Chandra Dev & Anr 6 BLT (AD)-244

Section-167 (5)

Accused cannot claim the privilege of bail as a matter of right—Section 167 (5) of the Code of Criminal Procedure enjoins that if investigation is not concluded within 120 days from the date of receipt of the information relating to the commission of the offence, the court, if satisfied can release the accused on bail. BLT the accused cannot claim the privilege of bail as a matter of right. In the face of peculiar circumstances, delay in completion of investigation of the case is no ground to release the accused-petitioner on bail.

Major (Retd) M. Khairuzzaman Vs. The State 6 BLT (HCD)-130

The responsibilities of the investigating agencies in general

Section-233, 234,235 and 239

Joinder of charges and Misjoiner of charges

Sections-233, 234, 235 and 239 of the Code are exception to the general rule that for every distinct offence there shall be a separate charge and every such charge shall be tried separately. The object of the exceptions is to avoid the necessarily of the same witnesses giving the same evidence over and over again at different trials. Misjoinder of charges is an illegality which is not curable under Section-537 of the Code of Criminal Procedure.

Abdul Jalil Vs. The State 9 BLT (HCD)-5

A B Siddikur Rahman Vs. A M Harunur Rashid & Ors 3 BLT (AD)-64

Section- 344

The hearing of case No. 33 was concluded and 25.9.1993 was fixed for judgment but the delivery of judgment has remained pending, on the ground of pendency of the counter case No. 34 which it appears, is still in the midst of examination of witnesses-long thereafter the informant in Case No. 33 moved the Sessions Judge in revision on 8.3.1995 against the aforesaid order dated 25.9.1993.  The  learned  Sessions Judge rejected the revision petition holding that the same was hopelessly time barred. Then the informant  came  upto the  High Court Division with an application under section 561A Cr.P.C. which was summarily rejected by a Division Bench.

Held: The Sessions Judge of the High Court division pught to have given a direction to the Magistrate for concluding the trial in case No. 34 quickly as the other case No. 33 was awaiting Judgment for nearly two years- it does not give credit to the Sessions Judge to have ignored the aforesaid facts and to have disposed of the revision petition wrongly by a mere fiat that it was hopelessly time-barred.

Mantasir Billah Vs. Bepari Abdur Rab & Ors 3 BLT (AD)-185

Section-344(1)

An accused person may be remanded if it is likely that in the interest of investigation into an offence further evidence is to be obtained and Magistrate is not precluded from handing over the accused to the police for the purpose of investigation in a case or some other cases in which the accused is involved. Order of remand dated 26.2.2005 for a period of 10 days and, thereafter, on 9.3.2005 for a period of another 10 days with a gap of three days are not liable to be declared to have been made and passed illegally, arbitrarily and malafidely. Petitioner is not at all entitled to be favoured with an order that orders of remand are illegal, arbitrary and malafide.
