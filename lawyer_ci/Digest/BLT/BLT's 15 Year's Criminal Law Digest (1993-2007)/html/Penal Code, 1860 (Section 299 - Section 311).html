Section-299

Mens rea—True it is that the facts appearing form the evidence giving a dismal picture of the occurrence and the conduct of the accused, on its own do not satisfy the test of legal insanity. But his previsous history of mental illness from time to time and his treatment at different places prior to the occurrence and the subsequent medical reports and opinions of the physicians, found in the records, show that the accused had been suffering from Schizophrenia. Although, no medical expert was examined in this regard but it is well known that a schizophrenic may look sombre or even apparently sober all the time but suddenly can commit acts of violence and such schizophrenic fits may not even last long always but sometimes only for few moments. But unfortunately, in this case, no Pshychiatrist was examined in Court in order to highlight the probable mental condition of the accused when he killed the victim, on the basis of the past history of mental illness of the accused and also on the basis of observations made on the accused in the Sher-e-Bangla Nagar Hospital and at the Pabna Mental Hospital no effort whatsoever was taken to ascertain the mental condition of the accused when the offence was committed in spite of revelation of his earlier history of mental illness. This is a failure on the part or the prosecution to prove the mental element of the accused when he committed the crime, Rather, the circumstances of mental illness of the accused from time to time preceding the occurrence, absence of any motive or reason to kill an unconcerned and innocent victim, his unconcerned attitude when he was apprehended immediately after the occurrence, subsequent diagnosis by the physician that he had been suffering from schizophrenia, all these facts and circumstances taken  together raise  a doubt  about the presence of mens rea, the essential ingredient of the offence of murder, in mind of the accused. This gives rise to reasonable doubt as to the guilt of accused within the meaning of Section 2 of the Penal Code.

State Vs. Abdul Barek & Ors. 10 BLT (AD)-8

 

Section- 304 Part-1

The whole dispute centres round on election matters and none of the accused appears to be criminal and murderer in the ordinary sense of the term- The incident took place as a result of election revelry between the two candidates. There may be other motives for inflicting such blows by the accused upon deceased. It further appears from the evidence of the prosecution witnesses that each of the I accused appellants has dealt only one blow by their knife or dagger. It is difficult to hold that by which blow death was caused or whether each of the blow is sufficient in the ordinary course of nature to cause death of deceased or whether as a cumulative effect of all injuries sustained by the deceased had died. Although the injuries are on the vital parts of the body inflicted by sharp cutting weapons never the less as the prosecution says that each accused dealt one blow each, therefore, in our view, the offence cannot be called a murder as defined in section 300 of the Penal Code of the necessary ingredients of murder appear to be missing. It is a manslaughter not amounting to murder. Therefore, the offence comes under section 304 Part 1 of the Penal Code.

Aynul Sheikh and Anr. Vs. The State 14 BLT (AD)- 33

 

Section- 304 Second Part

On scrutiny and careful analysis of the evidences by the prosecution side, it appears to us that accused- appellant Shamsul Hoq did not foresee that throwing of brick towards victim Kastura Bibi would cause death to her and he had no intention to cause death or to cause such bodily injury as was likely to cause death. No doubt, victim Kastura Bibi sustained violent blow on the abdominal wall by the brick thrown towards her chest and got senseless but death occurred when accused Abdul Hoque (since deceased) pressed her on the neck resulting her spontaneous death. Death would not have occurred if accused Abdul Hoq would not have played the part of pressing victim Kasture Bibi on her neck. The accused appellant Shamsul Huq had no premeditated intention to kill the victim kastura Bibi and the intention to kill is lacking in this case. But, although, he had no intention to kill the victim, he must be attributed the knowledge that such throwing of brick was likely to cause her death and the act done by him was both rash and indiscreet. We are of the opinion that accused Shamsul Hoq did not commit culpable homicide amounting to murder under section 302 of the Penal Code but committed culpable homicide not amounting to murder and the charge under section 302 of the Penal Code fails but we find that the accused- appellant Shamsul Hoq is liable for commission of offence punishable under section 304 Part II of  Penal Code and he can be convicted thereunder.

The rash or negligent act mentioned section 304B means the act which is the immediate cause of death and not any act or omission which can, be said to be a remote cause of death.
