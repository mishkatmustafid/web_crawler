Product of misreading and non considering the material evidence on record

The Court of appeal below by the impugned judgment affirmed the decision In the given facts and circumstances of the case and the uniform decisions of the highest Court, I have no hesitation to hold that the above quoted finding of the Court of appeal below is product of misreading and non considering the material evidence on record inasmuch as in this case the plaintiffs had produced their kabala, S. A. Khatian and rent receipt in the trial Court to show their continuous possession but the Courts below did not consider the same. - I am inclined to hold that both the Courts below erred in law in passing the judgments under challenge without following the correct principle relating to the appreciation of the evidence and materials on record and the same has resulted in an error in the impugned decisions occasioning failure of justice.

Section-115(l) read with Evidence Act, 1872 Section-102 Legal necessity under Hindu law—onus

 

The onus of establishing legal necessity is on the transferees. Again if the transactions are impeached the burden lies on the transferees to prove either that there was legal necessity in fact or that proper and bonafide enquiries were made by the transferees as to the existence of necessity and they did all that was reasonable to satisfy themselves as to the existence of the necessity. Thus it is seen that the transferees must establish both, but that may always prove one of these two alternatives. So it can not be argued that if the transferees failed to establish the first alternative, had happened here, it was incumbent on the court to investigate into the other alternative. The two considerations being alternative, in my opinion, one exclude the other. Here the first alternative has been found not to have been established and that was quite sufficient, and so there was no necessity to enquire into the other alternative. —It appears from the depositions of the recipient of the deed that it was not a deed covered with minimum legal necessity as defendant sold for maintaining of her day to day affairs of family. It appears that Chinta Haran Debnath died on 30th Magh, 1372 B.S. and the impugned deed of sale was registered on 17.04.1969 i.e. in early part of 1374 B.S. It is also on evidence that out of 5 of the daughters of Chinta Haran Debnath, 4 has already been given marriage and for the remaining daughter, Chinta Haran Debnath has made sufficient arrangement. It appears from the depositions of defendant's witnesses that none speaks for selling of the property to defendant No.l for legal necessity. The woman necessity means a kind of pressure which the lawdescribe as serious and sufficient. As such on an over all consideration of the Hindu Law it appears that defendant failed to prove the requirement of legal necessity for transferring the suit land as such we find substance in the rule.

 

Had Md. Nazim Hossain & Ors Vs Hafiz Ullah & Ors 21 BLT (HCD) 386.

Section 115(2) read with Specific Relief Act, 1877; Section 9 Proper Judgment of reversal

 

From the averments of the plaint it appears that the plaintiff's cause of action to institute the suit arose when he was dispossessed from the suit land on last part of Falgoon, 1379 B.S., that is, first part of March, 1973. The suit was instituted on 22.08.1973, that is, within 6(six) months of dispossession and the trail Court decided the issue No. 2, that plaintiff instituted the suit within 6(six) months of his dispossession from the suit land. On examination of evidence of the plaintiffs witnesses it appears that all the 4 P.Ws have categorically stated that the plaintiff from the time of his father was in possession in the suit land till dispossession in the last part of Falgoon, 1379 B.S. Trial court found the issue No.2 as to dispossession in favour of the plaintiff which clearly indicates that prior to dispossession the plaintiff was in possession of the suit land. So, I find that the plaintiff could well prove his possession in and dispossession from the suit land. The trial Court committed error of law in dismissing the suit occasioning failure of justice. The Rivisional Court assessing the evidence available on record rightly allowed the revision and decreed the suit by the impugned judgment and order. The District Judge or the Additional District Judge as rivisional Court in exercise of power conferred by section 115(2) of the Code of Civil Procedure, amended by the Law Reforms Ordinance, 1978, could rectify any illegality committed by the inferior Court even if such defect is not pointed out by either party. I find that trial Court dismissed the suit without assessing the evidence on record but the rivisional Court rightly and legally interfered within setting aside the judgment and decree passed by the trail Court.

 

Nannu Mia Vs. Mohan Basi Bhoumik & Ors. 18 BLT (HCD) 36.

Section 115(4) read with Order VI Rule 17

 

Whether in Civil Revision, an application for amendment of the plaint is maintainable

 

High Court Division rejected the application for amendment of the plaint on the view that the amendment, if allowed, would totally change the nature and character of the suit. -Held; it is clear that by the proposed amendment the plaintiffs did not seek any change as to the form of the suit from one of redemption of mortgaged to any other form and thus the suit remains as it was, i.e. one for redemption of the mortgaged property along with the other prayers. Mere omission/deletions and insertion of some new facts as indicated herein before in no way can be construed to change the nature and character of the plaint or the suit. It further appears to us that the amendment sought for it necessary to decide the core question involved in the suit as to whether the plaintiffs are entitled to redeem the mortgaged property being the heirs of late Sirajul Islam Khan who was the Managing Director of Plaintiff No.l company. In rejecting the prayer for amendment of the plaint the learned Judges have not at all spelt out as to how the amendments sought for would change the nature and character of the suit. The learned Judges in a very casual manner observed  that  the  amendment if allowed would totally change the nature and | character of the suit. In conclusion, we hold that the learned Judges of the High Court Division erred in law in rejecting the prayer for amendment of the plaint and we are inclined to allow the prayer.

 

Salahuddin Khan & Ors Vs. Md. Abdul Hai Bahar & Ors 21 BLT (AD) 137.

Also in the revisional application no ground relating to non-joinder of the party was raised. It appears for the first time in the course of hearing of the Rule question of non­joinder of the party was raised. -The settled principle of law is that a suit shall not fail for reason of mis-joinder or non-joinder of the parlies. This Division in the case of Safaruddin and others Vs. Fazlul Huq and others reported in 49 DLR(AD) 151 has held, "no suit can fail by reason merely of non­joinder of parties. Order 1, Rule 9 of the Code of Civil Procedure makes this legal position clear". The High Court Division was in error in entertaining submission as to the matter of non-joinder of the parties made for the first time in the course of argument before it inspite of not taking any ground in the revisional application. Since the defendants in the background of the material as relied upon before the High Court Division had the opportunity before the trial Court as well as in the appeal it; take the plea that the suit was bad for delect of parties and they having had not done so before the courts below they thereby in fact waived the objection as regard the non­joinder of parties, (Order 1, Rule 13 of the Code of C i v i 1 Proced ure)
