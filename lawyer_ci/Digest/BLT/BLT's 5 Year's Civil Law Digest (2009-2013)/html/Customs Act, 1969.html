Customs Act, 1969

 

Section 2(b) "appropriate officer"

 

From the adjudication order, it appears that the coast guards authority in its letter dated 20.6.2007 stated that after seizure of the oil tanker namely "O.T. NEW STAR" Mr. Munir Chowdhury, Magistrate, First Class, Chittagong Port, examined the same in the laboratory of Padma Oil Co. Ltd. On examination it is found that the seized furnace oil was pure furnace oil. Based on such examination report the said Mr. Munir Chowdhury awarded a fine of Tk.50,000/-each to the Chief Engineer and the Captain of the mothervessel -Mr. Munir Chowdhury is not an appropriate officer under the Customs Act rather he is the Magistrate of Chittagong Port completely, a different authority. As such the ex-parte examination report has got no legal basis.

 

Md. Rafiqul Islam & Anr Vs Customs, Excise and VAT, Appellate Tribunal & Anr 17 BLT (HCD ) 379.

diesel oil/dirty furnace oil are not prohibited or restricted items. To attract the definition of the smuggling it must be proved that the items found in the oil tanker were prohibited items by notification in the official gazette. It is well settled principle of law that to bring the charge of smuggling under section 156(1 )(8) of the Customs Act, the Customs Authority must show that the items are prohibited items in Bangladesh and there should also be smuggling under section 2(s) of the Customs Act, 1969. In the present case, as it appears that no single item of smuggled goods were found in the seized oil tanker.

Customs Act, 1969 (as amended) Section- 18 read with Section -19

 

Section 18 of the Customs Act speaks of a rate of duty which could be upto 100% or value on that but section 19 speaks of exemption as the Government deem fit proper in the public interest and in that view of the matter these two sections do not control each other.

The High Court Division held that then only difference between Hannan's case 42 DLR(AD) 167 and the instant case are that in Hannan's case there were special circumstances which were was narrated and in the present cases, no such circumstances were narrated but it is found from the materials on record that the government has exercised the discretion entrusted upon it under section 20 of the Customs Act whereas in Hannan's case exemption was made under section 19 of the Act which is a general power of exemption by the authority and not a special power of the authority as in the present case.-Held; We are of the view that the High Court Division on proper consideration of the materials on record and the law applicable, arrived at a correct decision and accordingly the decision does not call for any interference.

 

M/s. R.M. Corporation Ltd. Vs. Customs, Excise and VAT Appellate Tribunal & Ors 20 BLT (AD) 31

 

Section 25(1) read with Customs Valuation (Fixing Price of Imported Goods) Rules, 2000 Section - 23(2) read with Pre-shipment Inspection Rules, 2012; Section 22(4)

 

Held; Applying the relevant provisions of both the PS I Rules and the Valuation Rules read with those of the Act relied upon by the Petitioner, and given the evidently arbitrary assessment of the CRF valuation, this Court finds that as mandated in law the Respondents shall have to reevaluate the valuation of the Goods under Rule 22(3). Given this Court's satisfaction that as warranted in the facts and circumstances the Customs Authority can presently indeed arrive at a final decision as to the valuation of the Goods applying the standards and principles as elaborately enunciated above, there is found no justifiable ground to Order instead a mere provisional assessment of the Goods. This Court, accordingly, directs the Customs Authority to strictly so proceed, thereby, necessarily allowing the Petitioner to pay the Customs duties as assessed by the Respondents under Rule 23(2) in accordance with the Valuation Rules read in the context of Section 25(1) and (2) of the Act and on the basis of the declared/invoice/transaction value by way of final assessment.

Section-47

 

Vessel is liable to discharge the cargo if permission to break bulk cargo is obtained irrespective of tin.' arrest order.

 

Whatever be the reason stated by the learned Advocate the actual facts remain that the said part discharge has been made under the provision of the customs Act 1969. Although the vessel is now under arrest yet the vessel will be liable to discharge the entire cargo in accordance with the Provisions of Customs Act 1969 since no breaking of bulk is allowed without the permission of Customs Authority under Section 47 of the Customs Act 1969. Since the vessel obtained the permission for breaking the bulk it became liable to discharge the entire cargo as mandated by the Customs Act 1969. But as the plaintiff has not yet submitted the original shipping documents, the plaintiff will not be entitled to get the delivery of the said consignment till the plaintiff submits the original copy of the shipping documents.

 

L.M.J. International Ltd. India Vs M.V. BK ACE & Ors 19 BLT (HCD) 211.

 

Section 73

 

"Discharge" of Cargo is nothing but "unloading" the same in the port

 

Although the provision speaks of 'may' but the comprehensive reading of the other provision regarding discharge of cargo indicates that it is a mandatory provision that after obtaining permission for bulk breaking, the cargo must be unloaded within a specified period. The term "discharge" used in the Customs Act 1969 is nothing but "unloading" of the cargo into the classified area of the Port.

 

L.M.J. International Ltd. India Vs M.V. BK ACE & Ors 19 BLT (HCD) 211.

 

Declaration of Rio Principle-10

 

The United Nations Conference on Environment and Development was of the view that one of the fundamental prerequisites for the achievement of sustainable development was broad public participation in decision making. Furthermore, the Conference recognized, in the specific context of environment, "the need for new forms of participation" and "the need of individuals, groups and organizations to participate in environmental impact assessment procedures and to know about the participation in (pertinent) decisions". The Conference implicitly linked the notion of real participation in the right of access to information by nothing that "Individuals, groups and organizations should have access to information relevant to environment and development held by national authorities, including information on products and activities that have or are likely to have a significant impact on the environment, and information   on   environmental   protection measures". The link between participation and   information   can   also   be   found   in Principle 10 of the Declaration of Rio.

 

Metro Makers & Developers Ltd Vs. BELA 21 BLT (AD) 240.
