Limitation Act, 1908

 

Suit for recovery of money

 

If the defendants fail to make payment within the stipulated time, the plaintiff is required to file the suit for recovery of the outstanding loan with interest within a period of three years from the date of expiry of the last date of payment of installments as per exhibit-'Cha'. The last date for payment expired on 30th June 1973 and the period of limitation reckoned from that date which expired on 30lh June 1976. Therefore, the plaintiffs claim for recovery of money is hopelessly barred by limitation.

 

Agrani Bank Ltd. Vs Habib Oil Mills (Bangladesh) Limited & Ors 19 BLT (HCD) 01

 

Section -5

 

It is a settled principle of law that a litigant should not suffer for negligence of the tadbirkar or the lawyer as on them the litigant has to depend. A party wishing to take advantage of section 5 of the Limitation Act, 1908 must satisfy the court that it had not been negligent or had been prosecuting the case with due diligence and care. Excusing delay has to be approached from the point of view of the petitioner's conduct.

 

Goutom Mudi & Ors Vs. Moron Mudi & Ors 17 BLT (HCD) 203

 

Section -5 Delay of 1428 days

 

In the instant case the petitioners were fully depended on their tadbirkar and for his negligence the said delay of 1428 days was caused in preferring the civil revision against the impugned judgment and decree. It appears to me that the delay caused was not for the fault of the petitioners rather it Was due to tadbirkar of the petitioners. For that reasons the petitioners should not suffer inasmuch as the door of justice should not shut out to them. The petitioners instituted the suit for declaration of title and recovery of khas possession and accordingly they should get a chance to agitate their grievance before this court.

 

Goutom Mudi & Ors Vs. Moron Mudi & Ors 17 BLT (HCD) 203

 

Section -5

Revisional application -The delay of 1155 days

 

There is a long standing practice that a revisional application is to be filed within the period of 90 days prescribed by law for appeal and that this Court may in its discretion entertain an application made for condonation of delay in a suitable case where there is no negligence or laches on the part of the petitioner.

 

 

Govt: of Bangladesh & Ors Vs Engineer M.M. Shawkat Alt &Ors 17 BLT (HCD) 360

 

Section-5

 

Leave was barred by 68 days -The appellants Advocate Mr. Abu Backkar entrusted his clerk Md. Abdur Razzaq to check up the fortnightly and the daily cause lists which was his usual duty but unfortunately the said Abdur Razzaq missed the case in the list. Consequently the Criminal Revision was heard by the High Court Division in the absence of the Appellants Advocate and that the Rule was discharged on 5.8.74. The appellants came to know of the disposal of the Rule from their surety Mr Mohan Kanti Ghose, Advocate, Judge's Court, Chittagong on 7.11.74 who received a notice from the Sub-Divisional Magistrate, Chittagong on the same date for producing in Court in obedience to the direction of the High Court Division. Prior to that date the appellants had no knowledge of the disposal of the Rule as contended by the learned Counsel for the appellants.

 

Held; Having gone through the application for condonation of the delay and heard the learned Counsel who was very frank in his explanation we were inclined to take a lenient view, in the special facts and circumstances of the case, for the delay which was not due to any fault of the appellants but was due to carelessness and irresponsibility on the part of the Advocate's clerk and the Advocate upon whom trust and responsibility were reposed. Accordingly, the delay was condoned.

Section - 5 read with Bangladesh House Building Finance Corporation Order, 1973 (P.O. No.VII of

1973); Article 27(10)

 

Whether an application under Section 5 of the Limitation Act is maintainable in an appeal under the provisions of House Building Finance Corporation Order, 1973

 

No doubt the Bangladesh House Building Finance Corporation Order, i 973 is a special enactment but only for being a special enactment it can not be said that the applicability of the Limitation Act is prohibited in filling appeals under this Order. -We are of the view that in the enactment where there is no prohibition for entertaining any application filed under the provision of the Limitation Act is made, the court can condone the delay in filing appeals entertaining the application for condonation of delay under the provision of the Limitation Act.

 

Laila Begum & Anr Vs. House Building Finance Corporation & Anr 20 BLT (HCD) 131.

 

Section-5 read with Section-14

 

Delay of 2050 days in filing the appeal against the Order passed in Arbitration Case—for filing of an appeal from an award of arbitrator a time limit has been made under Section 7(f) of (Emergency) Requisition of property Act, read with Rule 9(16) of (Emergency) Requisition of Property Rules, 1948.

 

Held; In the instant case we find the award was passed by the Arbitrator and appeal there from lies to the High Court Division and there is a special limitation provided under (Emergency) Requisition of Property Rules, 1948, for preferring appeal, that is 60 days and relying on the decision reported in 43 DLR (AD)28, we are of the view that there being a special limitation provided in the law, we are unable to condone the delay and admit the appeal as prayed for in the rule.

 

Govt. of Bangladesh & Ors Vs. A.Y. Mashiuzzaman & Ors 21 BLT (HCD) 179.

 

Section -14

 

With due diligence another civil proceeding

 

The facts revealed shows the petitioner is misled by wrong advice. It is quite clear from the chronological happenings of the case that petitioner was always prosecuting with due diligence another civil proceeding with the same cause of action. The wording in Section 14 of the Limitation Act is not only "from defect of jurisdiction" but also "or other cause of a like nature". The case in hand comes within the later wordings of Section 14 of the Act. All the facts of the case if taken into a narrow campus than it is seen that petitioner honestly in good faith, in due case and attention for relief travel from pillar to post in whatever forum as was advised render the Court to apply provision of Section 14 of the Limitation Act.

 

Abdur Rashid Akanda Vs. Md. Raisuddin & Ors 21 BLT (HCD) 01.

 

Section - 14 read with Section - 29(2) with The Local Government (Upazilla Parishad) Ordinance, 2008 Section - 13(4) with The Local Section - 65(3)

 

Evidently, the High Court Division was not correct in coming to the finding that the Local Government (Upazilla Parishad) Ordinance, 2008 and the Local Government (Upazilla Parishad) Rules, 2008 being special laws and the period of limitation prescribed under section 13(4) of the Ordinance and Rules 65(3) being different from that prescribed in the Limitation Act, any proceeding thereunder is excluded from the operation of Section 29(2) read with section 14 or from any other provisions of the Limitation Act, and therefore, the benefit of Section 14 or any other provisions of Limitation Act is not available in computing the period of limitation in filing any election tribunal case or election appeal therefrom and thus in holding that the election appeal filed before the Election Appellate Tribunal was barred by limitation. We make it clear again that the provisions contained in sections 4, 9-18 and 22 of the Limitation Act are applicable in determining the period of limitation prescribed by any special law for filing any suit, appeal or application where such special law does not expressly exclude the applicability of the provisions of these sections of the Limitation Act. The remaining provisions of the Limitation Act shall not apply in any proceeding under any special law.

 

Abdul Waltab Sheikh Vs. Md. Kamal Hossain & Ors 20 BLT (AD) 282.

Article-113

 

9/2 years had elapsed from the time of contract till the filling of the suit

 

In our view the period of limitation was never crossed as the action of the Vendor from time to time effectively gave a new lease,of life to the agreement -In the facts of the instant case it is clear that, although it was stipulated in the agreement that the contract for sale should be completed within a period of one year, the breach of such condition was sanctioned by the payment of the liquidated damages in the sum of Tk.5,00,000/-. Therefore, time was definitely not of the essence of the contract. It must also be noted that the one month's ultimatum is an unilateral stipulation and it cannot be said that the Purchaser agreed to it. Even if the period of one month from 8.7.93 is taken to be the basis for performance of the agreement, then failure on the part of the Purchaser to conclude the sale in that period makes the agreement voidable. Even if at that stage time became of the essence of the contract, it is apparent that the Vendor did not take any action on or after 8.8.93 to cancel the agreement. On the contrary, her acceptance of Tk.15,00,000/-of the consideration money on 15.02.94 has negated the aspect of time being of the essence of the contract and effectively extended the period of limitation. Moreover, according to section 55 of the Contract Act( if it was the intention of the parties that the time should not be of the essence then the contract does not become voidable, but the promisee becomes entitled to receive compensation. Hence, it is our view that the Vendor is only entitled to receive compensation for the delay in performance of the agreement by the Purchaser.

 

Mahua Khair Vs. Amena Begum All Ispahani 21 BLT (AD) 192

 

Article 113 read with Transfer of Property Act, 1882 Section-53A

Held; As it has been noticed already in the present case the plaintiff-opposite parties entered into bainapatra (Ext.l & Ext.6) to purchase the suit land and got delivery of possession of the suit land immediately after execution of the bainapatra in their favour. The provisions of section 53A of the Transfer of Property Act have created a bar against enforcement of any right of the transferor in respect of the property in question which he would have apart from the agreement. In view of the provisions of section 53A of the Act the vendor or his successors in-interest are prevented frpm dealing with the property provided the vendee has performed his part of the contract and in pursuance thereof has taken possession of some immovable property. I, therefore, find there is a good deal of substance in the contentions raised by Mr. Abul Kalam Mainuddin, the learned Advocate for the opposite parties that in the facts and circumstance of the case the present Plaintiff-opposite parties are entitled to protect there own possession by invoking the provisions of section 53 A of the Transfer of Property Act and by instituting a suit as has been done in the present case and thus the provision of section 113 of the Limitation Act has no manner of application in the present case

 

Ayesha Khatun Vs Nasiruddin Ahmed & Ors 21 BLT (HCD) 336
