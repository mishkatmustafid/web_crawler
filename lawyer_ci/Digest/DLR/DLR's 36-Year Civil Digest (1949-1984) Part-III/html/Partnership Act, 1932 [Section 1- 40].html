Partnership Act

S. 4.  Partnership business—Accounts in the bank.

Mere fact that the bank account of a business was in the name of proprietor and his manager will not show that they were partners.

Hubert James Vs. Gulam Hussain Paksuma (1949) 1 DLR (PC) 5.

 

Cause of action arising after death of partner—Suit by firm not competent.

When the suit was instituted one of the partners in the firm was dead and the cause of action had also arisen after his death. It was held that the suit was i incompetent at the time of its institution and no amendment of plaint could be permitted after limitation for the suit had expired.

Shujaul-Mulk vs. Firm Abdul Ghafoor, PLD 1964 Pesh. 110—PLR 1964(2) W.P. 235. (Bashir-ud-Din, J.)

 

Creation of partnership—Oral agreement— Valid.

The Partnership Act does not prescribe any special mode for the creation of a partnership which can validly come into being even upon an oral agreement between the partners.

Usman vs. Haji Omer Haji Ayub, PLD 1966 Supreme Court 328. (Hamoodur Rahman, J).

 

Ss. 4, 6—Partnership—How may be determined—Facts and circumstances must be looked into.

The question whether there is a relation of partnership in any particular case will depend on the facts and circumstances brought out in each case or in the words of section 6 “regard shall be had to the real relation between the parties as shown by all relevant facts taken together.”

Essa E.H. Jaffar vs. Nishat Ltd., PLD 1962 (W.P.) Karachi 603 =PLR 1962(2) W.P. 929 (DB). (Kharal, J).

 

—S. 5— Relation of partnership—How arises.

The relation of partnership under the Partnership Act arises from contract and not from status.

Noor Hossain Vs. Commr. Income-Tax, PLD 1964 Dacca 373=PLR 1962 Dacca 1419 (FB). (Chowdhury, J,).

 

S.6, Expl. I—Joint lease of agricultural land—Joint management and sharing of profits—Not enough to prove partnership.

The land in dispute was taken on lease jointly by two persons. It was contended that they were partners on that score.

Held: The respondent only alleged that the land in dispute was obtained by the parties jointly with a view to share profits. That by itself cannot make this adventure as of partnership and will not make the relationship of the parties as that of partners. According to section 6 of the Partnership Act in determining whether a group of persons is or is not a firm, or whether a person is or is not a partner in a firm, regard shall be had to the real relation between the parties; as shown by all persons. Holding a joint or common interest in that property does not of itself make persons partners. Thus, in the absence of any allegation of the respondent that the lease was obtained by them as partners or that the land in dispute was to be cultivated in order to carry on a partnership business, and further in the absence of any case of the appellant that the lease was obtained in partnership and the parties were carrying on any partnership business, it is difficult to appreciate how the question of partnership can arise in the present case. It, however, appears that if the co—owners of the land or the co—lessees of the land are shown not to have any interest in the land other than of receiving their share of its income that by itsself would not make them partners.

Mobarak Ali Shah vs. Muhammad Sadiq, PLD 1961 (W.P.) Karachi 265 (DB) (Wahiduddin, J)

 

S. 8—One transaction—If partnership can be for one transaction only.

Under S. 8 a partnership can be for one transaction or one adventure only.

Muhammad Zakria vs. Mazahar Ali, PLD 1961 (W. P) Karachi 213 (DB) (Wahiduddin, J).

 

Ss. 11, 44—Agreement between parties—Should be subject to Partnership Act—Court’ power to dissolve partnership cannot be taken away by agreement between partners.

Section 11 of the Partnership Act, which provides for the determination of rights and duties of partners, by agreement between them, has been made subject to the provisions of Partnership Act, meaning thereby that no such agreement can override the provisions of the Partnership Act, but would be subordinate to it. No limitation, however, is placed on the power conferred by section 32 of the Act, on a partner to retire from the partnership, by agreement with the other partners, but this does not mean that such an agreement between them can override any other provisions of the Partnership Act, specially those of section 44, which permit a Court to dissolve a firm under certain conditions. Sections 32 and 44 of the Act being both independent provisions of law, one cannot override the other and hence, the argument that an agreement entered into between the partners permitting one of them to retire, would take away the power of the Court to dissolve such a partnership under section 44 of the Act, has no force.

Muhammad Yaseen Vs. Mahammad Yasin, PLD 1961 (W.P.) Lahore 468. (Masud, J.) (Rel A I R 1917 P.C. 116).

 

S. 14—Goodwill—Place of business is always part of goodwill.

The place of business of a firm is not invariably a part of its goodwill.

Peshoton vs. Peerozshaw, PLD 1962 (W.P.) Karachi 293=PLR 1962 (I) WP. 880. (Qadeeruddin, J).

 

S. 14—Rent paid in form of services—Interest in property—Forms part of assets of firm.

If a lease is created by agreement of the parties in which rent is paid in the form of service rendered to the owners of the property, it is interest in the property and would form a part o the assets of the firm in terms of section 14 of the Partnership Act.

Peshoton vs. Peerozshaw, PLD 1962 (W.P.) Karachi 293=PLR 1962 (I) WP. 880. (Qadeeruddin, J).

 

Scope—Does not override provisions of Registration Act.

The provisions of Section 14 of the Partnership Act which describes the property of the firm, and section 46 of the same Act which deals with the right of partners to have business wound up after dissolution, do not override the mandatory provision of the Indian Registration Act.

Zainab Bai vs. Ibrahimji, PLD 1962 (W.P.) Karachi 209 =PLR 1962 (1) W.P. 530. (Faruqui, J.).

 

S 17(a): Partnership firm may be reconstituted when one of the partners dies, resigns, or ceases to be a partner and the other partners may continue the business.

The Eastern Agencies Vs. Alhaj Mafizuddin (1968) 20 DLR 1056.

 

S. 18: It is true, a partner may, subject to the provisions of the Partnership Act, bind the firm itself. But in this case a specific provision has been made to the effect that while applying for registration under section 26—A of the Act, the application shall be signed by all the partners. Section 18 of the Partnership Act cannot override the provision of another Central Act, namely, 26A of the Income Tax Act and compel the Income Tax Authorities to accept the application filed by some of the partner. The Partnership Act is a general law which will go era the business transaction of the firm but cannot over—ride a specific provision made in section 26A of the Income Tax Act. In fact this specific provision shall override the general provisions made in the Partnership Act.

M/S Essential Industries Dacca Vs. Commissioner of Income Tax (1968) 20 DLR 1204.

 

Ss. 18, 19, 22—Partner signing agreement as “of E & Co”—Deemed to have signed on behalf of partnership.

When a partner R. of the E & Co. signed a contract as “of R of E and Co.” It was held that in the circumstances of the case he would be held to have signed the contract on behalf of the Company.

Hassanally Shah Vs.  Engineering & Co. PLD 1966 Kar. 52 (DB). (Inamullah, J).

 

S. 19(2)(b): Partnership entered into by two firms—Deed of partnership signed by one partner of one firm on his own behalf alone, and by one partner of the other firm on behalf of his firm— bigger partnership not formed.

S.A. Rahim & Ors. Vs. Commr. I Tax Punjab (1959) 11 DLR (SC) 289.

Secs. 19(2)(f)—Power of transfer of immovable properties.

It is clear from section 19(2)(f) of the Act that in the absence of any usage or custom of the trade to the contrary, the implied authority does not include the power to transfer immoveable properties belonging to the firm.

In the absence of an express authority two of the partners of the firm had no authority to transfer the immoveable properties of the firm even though the deed of sale was executed on behalf of the firm. 2 PLR (Dac.) 187.

 

S. 20—Partner—Public may presume every partner has authority to act on behalf of other partners—Liability of other partners for act of a partner.

The public have a right to assume that every partner has authority from his co-partners to bind the whole firm in contracts made according to the ordinary usages of the trade. In a trading concern a partner has implied authority to accept and draw bills on behalf of the firm. Even if the authority to do so has been cancelled and such cancellation has not been brought to the notice or knowledge of a discounting bank, the bank is entitled to recover as against the other partners also.

Suleman Ibrahim vs. Eastern Rice Syndicate, PLD 1966 (W.P.) Karachi 289. (Ghulamally, J.).

 

—A firm can only be made liable for what is done by one of it’s members. on the supposition that the action in question was authorized by the other members. Now, as by law they arc held prima facie to authorize all acts done in carrying on the business. of the firm in the usual way, they cannot escape liability for any act of this character unless they can show that the apparent authority to do it did not exist, and was known not to exist. But when it is sought to make the firm liable for some act not prima fade authorized by it. an actual authority by it must be shown; and if this cannot be done, no case is made out against the firm, however, ignorant the person seeking to charge it may have been of what was authorized and what was not. In the case now supposed the firm did not mislead him; and if he was misled by the representations of the partner with whom he dealt, his remedy is against that partner; just as when an agent untruly represents his authority, a person dealing with him acquires no right against the principal but must look to the agent for indemnity.

Aziz-ur-Rahman vs. Bahawalnagar Corp: Commn. Shop Ltd., PLD 1962 Baghdadul—Jadid 20=PLR 1962 (2) W.P. 359 (DB). (Bashiruddin, J).

 

Opening of account in the name of individual partners, will bind the partnership firm.

Sachindra Kumar Saha vs. Eastern Banking Corporation Ltd. (1967) 19 DLR 200.

 

S. 25—Firm liable—Suit may be brought against firm or partners—Ail partners need not be impleaded.

If the firm is liable, then it is left to the plaintiff to make his choice as to whether he wants to sue all the partners of the firm or some of them. Under Order XXX, rule 1, Civil Procedure Code, a plaintiff is not debarred from making his choice in the above mentioned matter. The rule is a permissive one and does not make it incumbent on a plaintiff to sue a firm in the name of the firm. The liability of the partners of a firm being joint and several, any one of the partners could be sued with reference to the liability of the firm.

Nazar Mohd. Vs. Shafiq Ahmad, PLD 1963 (W.P.) Lahore 23 N.A. Mahmood, J.)

 

S. 30—Minor—Cannot create partnership—May be admitted to benefits of partnership.

A minor cannot create a partnership but it cannot be said that by being merely admitted to the benefits of a partnership he also becomes a partner. There is no reason why the inclusion of the minors should render the constitution of the firm itself invalid when there were at least two adult partners, besides the minors, who could lawfully have entered into the partnership. Under section 26—A of the Income—Tax Act two conditions have only to be satisfied, firstly, that the firm has been constituted under an instrument of partnership, and, secondly, that the instrument has specified the shares of the partners. If these conditions are satisfied, the firm is entitled to registration.

Commr. of I-T. vs. Amin Match Works, PLD 1964 Supreme Court 337=(1964) 16 DLR (S.C. 183. (Hamoodur Rahman, J).

 

—Minor and partnership

A minor cannot be admitted into partnership, but he can be admitted to the benefits of partnership under section 30 of the Partnership Act with the consent of all partners for the time being.

Maya Ray vs. Mrs. Dorothy Watts. (1954) 6 DLR 285.

 

The minor becomes a partner unless within six months of his attaining majority or of his obtaining knowledge that he had been admitted to the benefits, whichever date is later, he elects by public notice not to become a partner of the firm.

The consequence of the attainment of majority of a partner in a partnership firm is that the contract so far as it seeks to make him a partner, and as such, make him personally liable for the act of the firm, is not effective. But the partnership agreement as between the adult is valid, the minor would be deemed to have been admitted to the benefits of the partnership. Ibid.

A contract entered into by a minor is void. But the doctrine of the minor’s incapacity is a doctrine for his protection by preventing others from injuring him by taking advantage of his incompetence.   Ibid.

Partnership is an agreement and agreement with a minor is void. No doubt under sec. 30 of the Partnership Act a minor can be admitted to the benefits of partnership, but in order that section 30 may apply there should be a partnership already in existence and cases where a minor became a partner from the very inception of partnership are beyond the scope of sec. 30. 1955 PLR (Lah) 588.

 

—Minors acquire interest in partnership business by right of inheritance—To such a case section 30 not applicable.

Commissioner of Income Tax, Vs. Msi. Safiya Bai (1979) 31 DLR (AD) 41.

 

S. 30(1)—Minor—When may be admitted to benefits of partnership—Status of such minor.

For the applicability of S. 30 (1), two things are required, namely: (1) the existence of a valid partnership on the benefits of which a minor may be admitted, and (ii) the mutual consent of the partners to admit a minor to the benefits of that partnership.

The minors admitted to benefits of partnership are only beneficiaries under that section and not partners. Although a partner, for the purposes of Income Tax Act, includes a minor who has been admitted to the benefits of partnership, a partner within the meanings of the Partnership Act does not include a minor.

Amin Match Works vs. Commissioner of Income Tax, PLD 1964 Dacca 295 (DB). (M.R. Khan, J).

 

Secs. 30(1) and 11: A minor may be included in partnership and when included it means he has been admitted to the benefits of partnership and he cannot be made liable personally, his liability is limited to the extent o his share in the partnership—Technical defect that the minor has been described as a partner if the document instead of showing him as ‘admitted to the benefits of partnership is no ground to refuse registration of a firm under section 26—A of the Income Tax Act.

Messrs Ittehad Textile Mills Multan Vs. Commissioner of Income Tax, (1969) 21 DLR (WP) 325.

 

S. 37. Outgoing partners’ right in the partnership—asset of the partnership—Principles which govern the assessment of the outgoing (or deceased) partner in the assets of the partnership.

The outgoing partner has either to claim a share in the profits made since he ceased to be a partner in so far as that may be attributable to the use of his1 share of the property of the firm or to claim interest at the rate of 6% per annum.

The principle accepted is that the deceased or the outgoing partner has rights over all the assets of the partnership and that this right is in the nature of an unascertained interest in every single item of asset thereof. He cannot be regarded merely as a person entitled to a particular sum of cash ascertained upon the striking of balance. If the continuing partners choose to carry on the business they obviously utilize the assets of the partnership and are thus placed in a fiduciary position with regard to the share of the outgoing partner in those assets. This makes them liable to account for whatever profits are attributable to such user.

Usman vs. Omar Haji (1966) 18 DLR (SC) 322.

 

Existing partners entitled to profits attributable to their personal skill or assets supplied by them alone— or it may be that the profits have been earned by reason of the fact that the remaining partners themselves provided further assets and further capital by means of which the business became a profitable one. Under this system of accounting, if insisted upon by the outgoing partner under section 37 of the Partnership Act, it will be t open for the remaining partners to claim to be recompensed for the skill and labor employed by them in the conduct of the business after dissolution and they will also be entitled to prove what portion, if any, of the profits was obtained not by reason of the use of the partnership assets but in some other manner. Ibid.

—Outgoing partner’s option under the section regarding his share—Courts cannot substitute its own option in place of that of the outgoing partner. Ibid.

 

S. 39 (dissolution of a firm).

Retirement of a partner from a partnership firm and dissolution of the firm are quite distinct.

Retirement of a partner from a firm and dissolution of the firm are quite distinct and a clear distinction exists between the two concepts, inasmuch as the consequences flowing from each are entirely different.

Liu Ying Ping Vs. Leon Fang Ai (1984) 36 DLR (AD) 273.
