S. 42.When a firm is dissolved:

Where a disputed partnership was to carry out one or more adventures or undertakings and in the absence of a contract to the contrary, the firm is dissolved, by the completion thereof under section 42 of the Partnership Act, where it is not made out that hr partnership was to continue even after the completion of the adventure or under staking. 7 PLR (Dac) 23.

 

S. 42. Prosecution of the suit, when partnership dissolves.

Partnership (consisting of 2 persons) is dissolved due to retirement of one of the partner during the pendency of the suit—Remaining partner competent to proceed with the Suit and upon decree passed therein competent to execute the decree in the name of the partnership.

Menon Trading Co. Vs. Hajee Gaffar Hajee Habib Janoo (1968) 20 DLR 41.

Sec 43 and 69: Dissolution by notice of partnership at will—Effect of non-registration of a firm.

An oral agreement was arrived at between the appellant and respondent that they would carry on jointly the business of military contracts on the basis of equal share in profits and losses, and the business was carried on till the end of March, 1946, when all war contracts ceased.

A suit thereafter was brought for a declaration of dissolution of partnership and rendering of accounts. The existence of partnership was denied and the liability for rendering accounts too. Maintainability of the suit was also questioned.

Held— (1) Since the partnership was confined to a particular class of business venture and it term mated automatically with the termination of the venture neither section 43 nor section 69 of the Act would be a heard to the maintainability of the suit for accounts of a dissolved firm.

(2) No notice was necessary under section 43 of the Act to sustain a claim for accounts of a partnership in certain ejmali contracts which on completion of the business terminated in March, 1946.

Sudhangshu Bimal Biswas Vs. Md. Mustafa Chowdhury; (1960) 12 DLR (SC) 259.

 

It is now well established that the mere fact that after a particular date no further business was done will not amount to dissolution of the partnership.

S.44 — Agreement between parties that they would not have recourse to litigation—Court has power to dissolve partnership.

Even if the partners had agreed that any of them would be at liberty to retire, by giving notice of this fact and would not have recourse to litigation for having the partnership dissolved, the power of the Court to order dissolution did not remain unaffected, nor was the right of the partners to claim dissolution taken away by such an agreement.

Muhammad Yaseen Vs. Muhammad yasin PLD 1961 (W.P.) Lohore 468. (Masud, L)

 

—Grounds for dissolution—What may Be.

The order of dissolution was based on three grounds. Firstly that the Managing Director was guilty of misconduct in so far as he had been utilizing the assets of the partnership for his personal needs, secondly, that although the partnership was carried on for more than two years not a pie in the shape of profits, was paid to the financing partner,

and thirdly, that the relations between the parties having become strained, on account of criminal litigation, it was just and equitable to dissolve the firm. Ibid

 

—Partner guilty of misconduct was not entitled to sue for dissolution of partnership

A partner who is himself guilty of misconduct was not entitled to sue for dissolution of partnership. Ibid

 

—Ss. 46, 47 & 48. After the dissolution a partner may sue or be sued in firm’s name provided the cause of action arose before its dissolution.

Merely because the firm was dissolved during the pendency of the suit will not render the suit untenable.

M/S Memon Trading Co. Vs. M/S. Hajee Gaffar (1965) 17 DLR 677.

 

Ss. 63, 69—Change of name and membership of firm—Suit filed for cause of action arising prior to change—Intimation of change given to Registrar after filing of suit— Suit competent.

There was a change in the name and membership of the firm and a suit was brought by it on a cause of action which arose prior to the change. It was argued that as the intimation of the change had not been sent to the Registrar regarding the change at the time of the filing of the suit; the suit did not lie.

Held: Section 63 of the Partnership Act under which an intimation with regard to the change in the members of the firm and of the name of the firm has to be given to Registrar does not prescribe any time limit for sending such intimation. The mere fact that intimation about this change was given to the Registrar subsequently does not alter the situation. The firm running under the name and style of Messrs Jumani Brothers, therefore, was fully competent to file a suit against the applicant and by filing such a suit there has been absolutely no contravention of the provision contained in section 69 of the Partnership Act.

Muhammad Zaman Khan vs. Jumani Bros., PLD 1961 (W.P) Karachi 19. (DB) (Khamisani, J).

 

—S 69. Applicablity of provision of section 69 when the suit is one for accounts of dissolved firm.

Where on an interpretation of the plaint it is evident that the suit was for the accounts of a dissolved firm, the provisions of section 69 would not be a bar for the maintainability of the suit.

Sudhanjshu Birnal Biswas Vs. Md. Mustafa Chowdhury. (1960) 12 DLR (SC) 259

 

S. 69— Change in members and name of firm—Does not debar firm from filing suit on cause of action which accrued prior to the change.

Where some new partners were introduced in a firm with a change in the name of the firm, and the new firm brought a suit under the new name.

Held: There is in law no restriction in regard to some of the members of the firm leaving the firm and some new members being taken in their place.

There is also no restriction for the changing of the name of the firm. Merely because the name of the firm has been changed or some of the members of the firm have left and some new members have been taken in it does not necessarily mean that the firm has been dissolved and that a new firm has been set up. The firm Messrs Jumani Brothers & Co. which has ultimately come into being as Messrs Jumani Brothers is not a new firm but is the same firm continuing under a different name. Therefore the suit was maintainable.

Muhammad Zaman Khan Vs. Jumnani Brothers, PLD 1961 (W.P.) Karachi 19 (DB). (Khamisani, J).

 

—Loan made to person to earn profits on his business—Does not constitute partnership with him. Where two persons advanced a loan to another without interest on the hope of making profit in his business of paddy.

Held: That was not sufficient to constitute them his partners.

Muhammad Z.akria vs. Mazahar Ali, PLD 1961 (WP) Karachi 213 (DB) Wahiduddin, J).

 

—Partnership agreement not registered—Suit for accounts or realization of assets on dissolution is not barred—Suits by or against firm not permitted.

Non-registration of the firm under section 69 of the Partnership Act does not affect the validity of the partnership or prevent any of the partners from suing for the dissolution of the firm or for accounts or the realization of the property of a dissolved firm. This section only bars a Suit for enforcing a right arising out of a contract against either the firm or any past or present member of it or against any third party.

Usman vs. Haji Omar Haji Ayub, PLD 1966 Supreme Court 328. (Hamoodur Rahman, J).

 

S. 69— Suit by partner in individual capacity— Not for benefit of the firm— Suit not barred under the Act.

Section 69 of the Partnership Act. 1932 applies to those cases only where the plain shows that the dispute in respect of which the suit is brought was for the benefit of the partnership and the plaintiff has either brought the suit for the benefit of the partnership or has based his claim in respect of a transaction which was entered into between the partners and the defendant. Where a suit, on a claim arising out of a contract, was brought by one person alone and there was no allegation in the plaint that the benefits of the contract in dispute also accrued to some other persons, it was held that the suit was not barred under section 69 of the Partnership Act, 1932.

Jaffar Khan vs. Muhammad Achar, PLC) 196 (W.P) Karachi 335 (DB). (Wahiduddin, I).

 

S. 69: Partnership defacto.

Partnership—Not necessarily involved in mere promise of share of profits in lieu of work. 1955 PLR (Lah.) 588.

Partnership—in fact but, legally defective, and failing to produce legal effects of partnership agreement— May give rise to equitable rights. Ibid.

 

—Suit against a 3rd party will not lie unless the partnership is registered.

Eastern Union insurance Co. Ltd. vs.  Yusuf & Co. (1967) 19 DLR 69.

 

S. 69(1)(2): Suit by a member of dissolved firm. Then a suit for recovery of damages is not one by the firm but by one of its members of the dissolved firm—such a suit at the instance of such a member is not hit by sub—sections (1) and (2) of section 69 of the Partnership Act.

Where the deed dissolving the partnership authorizes one of the members of the partnership firm to institute a suit such a member is entitled to institute such a suit and the provisions of sub—section (1) and (2) of section 69 do not stand as a bar.

M. Ismail & Sons Vs. Trans Occeanic Steamship (1965) 17 DLR 269.

 

Scope—All rights assigned by partners of dissolved firm to one partner— Suit by latter not hit by S. 69(1) (2).

Where one of the partners of the dissolved firm was at the time of dissolution atoned to realize their assets and meet the liabilities of the dissolved firm, and he brought a suit for realizing the assets of the firm. It was held that as it is not a suit by the firm but by one of the members of the dissolved firm at the instance of the plaintiff, it cannot be held to be hit by sub-sections (I) and (2) of section 69 of the Partnership Act.

M. Ismail & Sons vs. Trans Oceanic Steamship Co. Ltd., PLD 1966 Dacca 296(DB). (Hasan, J).
