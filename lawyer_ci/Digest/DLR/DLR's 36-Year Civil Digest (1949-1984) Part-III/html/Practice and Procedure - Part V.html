Practice and Procedure

 

Objectionable observation quashing of — In view of the facts and circumstances the findings imputing malafide on the appellant cannot be sustained. Therefore, the objectionable findings and observations made in the judgment of the High Court Division are quashed.

Habibullah Khan Vs. S.A. Ahmed (1983) 35 DLR (AD) 72.

 

Pay Commission’s recommendation—must be notified in official gazette to create a legal right.

Manager Vs. Abdus Sattar (1979) 31 DLR 58.

 

Plaintiff to prove his case to get a decree—Plaintiff to prove his case to enable him to get a decree—Absence of the defendant no ground to award decree in plaintiffs favour.

Kiron Chandra Das Vs. Sirajul Hoque Patwari (1982) 34 DLR 225.

 

Point not taken in High Court— cannot be raised in Supreme Court.

A point not taken in the High Court which is involving a question of fact cannot be permitted to be raised in the Supreme Court.

S.M. Ismail & Co. vs. Chief Cotton Inspector (1966) 18 DLR (SC) 267.

 

Prize Court—Proceedings in.

Since no other rule could be found in the Rules of Business as empowering the Ministry of Law to exercise the functions relating to financial matters which are reserved under the Rules of Business to the Ministry of Finance, in the absence of such a rule the Ministry of Law cannot initiate the proceeding in question in the Prize Court.

Bangladesh Vs. Prize Court, (1982) 34 DLR (AD) 212.

 

Prize Court fund: Constitutional provision—Constitutional provision regarding Consolidated Fund or Public Accounts of the Republic—Prize Courts Fund.

The Constitution provides that all public moneys which will form, part either of the Consolidated fund or the Public Account of the Republic is payable into such Fund or Account or can be withdrawn from it under an Act of Parliament or Rules made by the President.

Bangladesh Vs. Hon’ble Judge, (1982) 34 DLR (AD) 212.

 

Procedure—Defect of— Question of prejudice—Party prejudiced must raise question—Not to be raised suo motu by Court—May be waived by party concerned.

A question of defect of procedure is always a question of prejudice. When there is no point made of any defect of procedure by the party there is any prejudice to the party…….There is no ground for raising this question suo motu by the Supreme Court.

An objection as to procedure has force only if prejudice be caused unless there be some strict statutory provision which vitiates proceedings in case of non-compliance.

With respect to such defects in procedure the settled rule is that they can always be waived and had there been any defect in procedure it would surely have been waived by the fact that no objection was ever taken at any stage.

Zafarullah Khan Vs. Custodian of E. Property (1965) 17 DLR (SC) 126.

 

Procedural law, amendment of will have retrospective effect.

Since the provisions of the Code of Civil Procedure is nothing but a procedure, an amending section will have retrospective operation.

Abdul Gafur Sardar vs. Wazedali Talukdar (1967) 19 DLR 33.

 

Proceedings of one kind treated as those of another kind.

Ordinary Courts have no objection to treat proceeding of one kind as proceedings of another kind, if dint becomes necessary for doing justice between the parties.

Court did not allow him to contest the service of notice as the point had not been taken either before the Custodian or in the concise statement.

Ata Ullah vs. Custodian of Evacutive Property (1964) 16 DLR (SC) 298.

 

Publication necessary before notification comes into force

It is well settled principle that publication of some reasonable sort is essential before a notification or Act comes into force.

Mohammad Faizullah & others. Vs. Govt. of Bangladesh (1981) 33 DLR 16.

 

Publication of some reasonable sort—It is well settled principle that publication of some reasonable sort is essential before a Notification or Act comes into force.

Mohammad Faizllah Vs. Govt. of Bangladesh (1981) 33 DLR 17.

 

Question of law when dependent upon prior determination of fact.

Principle that a question of law can for the first time be raised even before the High Court Appellate Division has no application if the determination of a question of law is dependent upon prior determination of a question of fact.

Hafizuddin Ahmed Vs. Mahbubul Huq (1983) 35 DLR (AD) 174.

 

Railway administration—Power of the General Manager re: the recommendation of Selection Board.

The General Manager is not bound to accept the recommendation of a Selection Board nor is he incompetent to revoke an earlier order whereby he accepted the recommendation of the Selection Board but cancelled it later on.

Md. Seraj Mia Vs. D.G. Railways. (1960) 12 DLR 638.

 

Registration of document of sale after payment of consideration and taking of actual possession.

Generally after payment of the full consideration and execution of the kabala (where it is stated that the land has been given in possession of the transferee) the transferee takes possession of the land and he never waits for getting back the registered kabala from the registration office.

Satya Ranjan halder vs. Surendra Nath (1961) 13 DLR 338.

 

Re-hearing and re-considering— difference between—Federal (Supreme) Court can change its opinion. The distinction between an application for the rehearing of a decided case and reconsidering in a subsequent case a question of law previously decided must be borne in mind. The Federal Court on who rest the ultimate responsibility of interpreting the law of the land is like Privy Council, entitled to change its opinion and take a different view from the one it has hitherto held.

Mirza Akbar Ali Vs. Mirza Iftekhar Ali (1956) 8 DLR (FC) 15.

 

Re-opening of a matter—The Privy Council consistently refused to re-open a matter, on an application for re-hearing, except where the law had been applied on a mistaken assumption of or by inadvertence the Order—in—Council did not give effect to the intention of the Board as expressed in the judgment. Ibid.

 

Relinquishment of expectancy

Relinquishment of expectancy—by a Muslim heir, for consideration received of his right not to claim his right nor to claim his share of inheritance when it opens in future does not operate as a complete transfer, but in equity in can be enforced as an agreement to sell when it comes into the possession of the heir—Application of this principle depends upon the facts and circumstances of each case.

Quamiridin Vs. Mst. Aisha Bi (1956) 8 DLR (WP) 86.

 

Revenue Court’s order holding plaintiff to be tenant can be challenged in civil court

A person who’s Suit to contest his liability ejectment has failed and who has been held by the Revenue court to be a tenant can set up in defense his title to the land in a subsequent suit in a civil court for his dispossession. (1955) 7 DLR (FC) 90.

 

Reversioner refusing to sue—next competent person.

If the nearest reversioner without sufficient cause refuses to institute proceedings, or he has precluded himself by his own act or conduct from suing or has colluded with the person who had the prior right to bring suit, or concerned in the act alleged to be wrongful, the next respective reversioner would be entitled to sue.

Hajee Yar Ali Khan Chowdhury Vs. Mobarak Ali Chowdhury (1955) 7 DLR 6.

 

Review of ML. Court’s judgment—Ateration of review forum does not affect parties

Review of M.L. Court’s Judgments—Previous review Authority is changed for another review authority. A Review of Judgment passed by M.L. Court offers no right of hearing or the persons personal appearance; as such it is not a substantive right which could not be altered by amendment whereby a former review authority is changed by appointment of another review authority—in the absence of any right of personal, appearance etc. the persons concerned is not prejudiced by mere alteration of review forum.

Khandker Moshtaque Ahmed, Vs. Govt.of Bangladesh, (1981) 33 DLR 348. [Reversed by 34 DLR (AD) 222]

 

Review of judgment referred in Reg 4.

In the matter of review of Judgment referred to in Reg. 4, the Government has to exercise its mined judicially and that should be apparent on the face of record which is lacking hem Appellate Division held it otherwise.   Ibid.

 

Revisional jurisdiction—exercise of.

Orders passed in exercise of revisional jurisdiction should be clear and precise.

Abdul Jalil Munshi Vs. Abu Bakr Siddique (1983) 35 DLR (AD) 42 (44).

 

Revival of suit when not proper.

Where a suit was struck out and was re-listed after a lapse of nine years and having regard to the length of the delay and in consequence of setting aside the sale of Land as against a bonafide purchaser for value who had been in occupation of the land during the whole period and had apparently altered the buildings thereon—such a suit ought not to have been re-listed.

John Obojo Agbeygbe Vs. Festus Makene Ikomi (1953) 5 DLR 219 (PC).

 

Rules of procedure—compliance with—imperative.

Enactments regulating procedure in courts are imperative and not merely directory. If, for instance, an appeal from a decision is given with provisions requiring the fulfillment of certain conditions such as giving notice of appeal and entering into recognizance, or transmitting documents within a certain time, a strict compliance would be imperative and noncompliance would be fatal to the appeal.

Muhd. Hakim Mia Vs. Mafizuddin Dhupi, (1959) 11 DLR 438.

 

Rules and forms under an Act—Rules and forms under the authority of the Act can be referred to for the purpose of the interpretation of the Act.

Aryyasrhan Insurance Co. Vs. Nurul Islam Chowdhury (1958) 10 DLR 510.

 

Rules of evidence in Muhammedan Law—The Muhammedan Law relating to rules of evidence is no longer in force, having been superseded by the provisions of the Indian Evidence Act.

Khirodin Nessa Vs. Sekander Biswas (1952) 4 DLR 626.

 

Same person cannot both be plaintiff and defendant in the same suit.

A man cannot sue himself. If he is present in one capacity in the suit, his entire involvement in the subject—matter of the suit falls for determination. He need not enlist in the opposite camp for a complete adjudication of his conflicting interests.

Madan Chand Gouthy Vs. Mohammad Nurul Huda. (1979) 31 DLR 372.

 

Sovereign authority—suing of

One independent sovereign power is not the agent of another independent sovereign power as far as municipal courts are concerned unless specifically such a state of agency is, for a particular purpose, assumed and the court is given jurisdiction.

Bengal Store Vs. Dominion of Pak. (1954) 6 DLR 594.

 

Stamps when not cancelled—Stamps not cancelled before filing is no ground to reject the instrument.

Anath Bandhu Guha Vs. Sudhangsu Sekar Dey (1979) 31 DLR (AD) 312.

 

Standing Rules and Orders—of Shop Employees of the Bats Shoe Co. Ltd. Clauses 21(8) (XII) and 21(A).

The plaintiff was found guilty of misconduct under clause 21(B)(XI1) of the Standing Rules and Orders for absenting himself beyond seven days after the expiry of period of leave granted to him. But inspite of the said finding the plaintiff has been dismissed under clause 21(A) though it has been stated in the order that in order to give the benefit of provident fund the order has been passed under clause 21(A).

No doubt the relationship between the parties is that of master and servant. But in view of the Standing Orders and Rules governing the services of the shop employees, the rules must be strictly followed in terminating the services of an employee. The Service Rules have been framed by the company to be binding both on the employer and the employee.

Abdul Majid Vs. Bata Shoe Co. (1978) 30 DLR 262.

 

Statute creating liability or right—Where statute creates a liability or a right and at the same time prescribes a special or particular method or machinery for enforcing it, then that method or machinery alone must be followed and none others.

Almas Khatun Vs. Ezhar Mia, (1956) 8 DLR 82.

 

Stay of the operation of an order for moving the High Court.

Granted on condition that petitioner must deposit in court Th. 8000/— which shall be returned to him in the event of his success and in case of failure the Opp. party shall get the same.

Held: Order passed on such condition is illegal.

Modern Talkies Vs. Chowrangee Cinema (1976) 28 DLR 416.

 

Stranger purchasing the interest of a cotenant—Where the interest of a co-tenant is purchased by a third person, such person becomes a tenant in common with the other co-tenants and the possession of such a co-tenant cannot, in the absence of clear proof to the contrary, be adverse to such purchaser.

Syed Abul Fazal Vs. Nirod Bala Deb Roy (1956) 8 DLR 181.

 

Subordinate Court’s Order in ignorance of any order of superior Court— not ipso facto null and void—If any step is taken by any subordinate Court haying ordinary normal jurisdiction In ignorance of any order of a superior Court, the subsequent orders passed by j.he subordinate Court are not ipso facto null and void. But they will stand unless they are either recalled or are set aside.

Rani Harshamukhi Vs. Maharaja Shashi Kanta, (1955) 7 DLR 473 (495 r.h. col.)

 

Subordinate legislation when struck down—Subordinate legislation struck down in conflict with an Act of Parliament.

A subordinate legislation can be struck down as being invalid if it conflicts with an Act of Parliament under which it is made. It may also be declared unconstitutional if it is seen to violate any provisions of the Constitution.

Kundeswari Ausudhalaya Vs. Bangladesh (1976) 28 DLR 63.

 

Superior Court’s Order—inferior Court’s duty in respect thereof—Although an ex parte order of slay is a valid order, it does not ipsofacto nullify all the proceedings of the Court of the first instance passed subsequently. If the Court comes to know of the orders of the superior Court, it should always act on the orders of the superior Court and the question is not a question of communication only but is one of knowledge of the Court and ibis knowledge may be direct or indirect.

Rani Harshamukhi Vs. Maharaja Shashi Kanta (1955) 7 DLR 495.

—As soon as a subordinate Court is satisfied of the existence of such an order of a superior Court, it must act on it. Ibid.

 

Superior Court’s power to sit in judgment on a matter which is also before a subordinate Court for trial—Decision on a particular question by the High Court while disposing of a case when the same question between the same parties was also pending adjudication before a subordinate Court, not a valid ground for the High Court to refuse to decide that question.

Dr. A.N.M Mahmood Vs. Syndicate, University of Dacca (1966) 18 DLR 357.

 

Surety’s liability enforceable when judgment—debtor fails to pay.

Suret’s liability to pay money he undertook to pay is enforceable only when Judgment—debtor failed to pay the same or it was found not realisable from him.

Yusuf Ali Mondal & ors. Vs. Soleman Ali Biswas (1981) 33 DLR 286.

 

Suspicion cannot take the place of proof—A strong amount of suspicion which attaches to the plaintiff’s case does not amount to proof.

Misri Lal Nayak Vs. Mst. Surji (1957) 9 DLR 375 (PC).
