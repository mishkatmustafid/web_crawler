S. 10(a)(b) Advance payment of rent for a premises hit by S. 10(b) of the Act—Agreement for advance payment of rent hit by S. 23 of the Contact Act.

Clause (a) of section 10 of the Premises Rent Control Ordinance prohibits the acceptance of any sum of money over and above the rent payable by a tenant. This clause has also made illegal even any claim or invitation of an offer for the payment of such some of money in the form of premium, salami or fine either when a tenancy is being created or renewed, or during its continuance. Under Clause (b) no one is allowed to claim or receive a payment of more than one month’s rent in advance.

Agreement for lease was void under section 23 of the Contract Act as its object was not lawful and that it having not been registered was unenforceable and also inadmissible in evidence.

The agreement to pay Tk. 19,200/- by way of advance rent was undoubtedly a part of the consideration of the transaction as defined in section 2(d) of the Contract Act. This contravenes the positive statutory mandate as provided in section 10(b) of the Premises Rent Control Ordinance.

In view of the unenforceability of the agreement the position is that the appellant is a monthly tenant who is liable to ejectment if he makes default in payment of rent as provided in section 18(5) of the premises Rent Control Ordinance.

Shamsuddin Ahmed Vs. Mohd. Hassan. (1979) 31 DLR (AD) 155.

S. 15(1): Rent Controller’s competency in alteration of rent previously fixed by him.

The Rent Controller has been expressly authorized by section 15 (I) of the Ordinance to fix the standard rent of any premises at the amount per annum which is equivalent to fifteen per cent of the market value of the premises to be assessed in such a manner as may be prescribed.

In the present case the Rent Controller has exactly done so by altering the monthly rent fixed under the previous enactment from Rs. 35/- to Rs. 125/- and therefore, fixing of the rent in the manner is permissible under the law.

The word ‘modification’ or ‘alteration’ has been used in the proviso in its usual and ordinary sense and it will be wrong to interpret these words prescribing different meaning thereto.

Abdul Maleq Vs. Alhaj A. K. Md. Hussain (1970) 22 DLR 737.

S. 18 (1)(e) — ‘Bonafide require’ explained: The expression “bonafide require” means a feeling of genuine need and not colorable pretext. The use of the term ‘require’ indicates something more than a mere wish or fancy and it involves an element of need, that is to say, it does not connote a mere subjective phenomenon, but has also an objective Import. The use of the qualifying word ‘bonafide’ along with the word ‘require’ tries to limit the scope of the subjective aspect of the landlord’s feeling of need for his house, although the word ‘bonafide’, the equivalent of which is ‘honestly’ is essentially a subjective concept. The Legislature seems to convey by using the words ‘bonafide require’ a kind of genuine feeling of need and not a colorable pretext.

The expression, therefore, connotes an honest feeling of need for the house and in ultimate analysis it is a question of fact which is to be determined on the basis of the facts and circumstances

Ss. 18(5) and 19: Tenant not entitled to get protection from eviction unless he pays rent to the landlord within the stipulated time as provided in section 18(5) and 19 of the Act—Once default is made that cannot be cured by subsequent deposit of rent with the Rent Controller.

The tenant is bound to pay rent to the landlord or to deposit the same in the treasury within the stipulated time under sections 18(5) and 19 of the Ordinance (i.e.) by the 15th day of the month next following that for which the rent is payable or deposit such rent in case of refusal by the landlord to accept the rent, within a fortnight of the date on which the rent so remitted is returned to the tenant by the defendant through postal authority.

But in the present case the defendant deposited rent for the months of February to September, 1958, with the permission of the Rent Controller on the 6th October, 1958.

Held: Once a defaulter always a defaulter. If once a default is established it cannot be cured by subsequent deposit of the rent with the Rent Controller. A tenant not having paid the rent to the landlord in terms of section 18 (5) or not having deposited the same with the Rent Controller according to the provisions of section 19, is not entitled to claim a protection from eviction.

M/S East Bengal Paper Traders Association Ltd. Vs. Md. Waziullah (1969) 21 DLR 572.

 

—Tenant’s liability to pay rent—If landlord improperly refuses to accept the rent, the steps which tenant must rake for protection against eviction.

To sum up, the tenant must pay rent in terms of the contract, and in the absence of the contract within fifteenth day next following month on which the rent falls due, and if the landlord refuses to accept the rent tendered by the tenant the tenant must remit the rent within the aforesaid fifteenth day of the following month by money order. If the rent remitted by money order is refused, the tenant can deposit the rent with the Rent Controller within a fortnight from the date of the receipt of the undelivered rent sent through the postal authority, and when these conditions as prescribed in the Ordinance are fulfilled by the tenant, he gets the protection, otherwise the protection from ejectment on ground of default in the payment of rent granted by the Ordinance is clearly taken away by the express language of sub-section (5) of section 18.

S. 21 [S. 23 of E.B. Premises Rent Control Act (16/1953)

Provisions for realization of repair costs under the section is merely an alternative method.

The suit premises was in a bad condition; thereupon the Municipality took up the matter and directed the defendant by a notice to reconstruct the suit house according to a plan approved by the Municipality and other necessary things so as to avoid interference with free flow of water with refuses.

The landlord-defendant, on getting these notices approached the tenant-plaintiffs and asked them to take up the matter with the Municipality and execute all necessary works of repairs.

The plaintiffs accordingly undertook the repairs as in case of default the structure would be demolished and they would lose their business which they were carrying on in the suit premises.

The defendant not having paid the costs of repairs incurred by the plaintiffs the latter sued him for recovery of the costs.

Held: Section 23 of the E.B. Premises Rent Control Act is to be read subject to the provisions of other Acts. This section provides machinery within the Act for realizing the dues of repairs carried out by the tenant from the landlord but it is not the exclusive method. It must be regarded as an alternative method in addition to other methods permissible by law. Moreover the section refers to any repairs. It was not a question of mere repairs but it was a question of support of the suit premises as required to be altered by the Municipality

Kazi Md. Ishaque Vs. Girindra Chandra Bose, (1968) 20 DLR 707.
