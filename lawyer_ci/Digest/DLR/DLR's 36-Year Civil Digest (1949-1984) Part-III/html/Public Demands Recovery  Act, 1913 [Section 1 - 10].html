S. 3(6): Trespassers of the Government d or fishery cannot be proceeded against under Article 7

Demand under Article 7 of Schedule I of the Public Demands Recovery Act must be payable to the Collector by a person holding any interest in the land.

Possessory right may be a right in certain circumstances but a trespasser’s possession is not such a right as contemplated under Article 7 of Schedule I of the Act.

So the demand which can be made by the Collector must be in pursuance to a contract enforceable in aid therefore the Governments right to realise compensation for use and occupation against a trespassers does not come under Article 7 of Schedule I of the Act nor it is public demand as defined in subsection (6) of section 3 of the Act..

Province of East Pakistan Vs. Mahim Chandra (1962) 14 DLR 213.

 

S. 4: Certificate against dead person void and also the sale there under—A certificate was issued in the name of one Jagadish Chandra Ganguly by the Certificate Officer of Mymensingh and was transferred to the Certificate Officer of Dacca for execution. The Certificate debtor was in fact one Jagadish Chandra Banarjee. It is admitted that Jagadish Chandra Banarjee died in 1946 and the certificate was filed in 1956. After receipt of the peon’s report the Certificate Officer of Dacca substituted the heirs of Jagadish Chandra Binarjee and the sale was held.

Held: The certificate issued is void having been issued against a dead person. The Certificate Officer of Dacca could only return the certificate to the Certificate Officer of Mymensingh for necessary correction. As this has not been done in the present case not only sale but also the certificate proceedings pursuant to which the sale is made is absolutely void.

Yar Ahmad Vs. Province of East Pakistan, (1968) 20 DLR 1103.

—S. 4. Party to Certificate Proceeding.

The plaintiff purchased the share of the defendants in a holding in a money sale and the landlord’s. fees and notice were duly sent to the landlord before the confirmation of the sale on 29.9.34.

Thereafter, in a certificate sale under the Public Demands Recovery Act against the recorded tenants only in which the plaintiff was not impleaded as a party, the holding was put to sale and purchased by one M and 2 others on 8.4.35.

Thereafter, in a suit by the plaintiff, he gave up his title in the land to the defendants by a compromise decree. The holding was again sold in execution of a rent decree and was purchased by the plaintiff, the defendants not being made parties to the rent suit or the execution proceeding. An application by the defendants for setting aside the sale under sec. 174 of the Bengal Tenancy Act was dismissed on the ground that they had no interest and, therefore, no locus standi. The plaintiff then attempted to take possession of the land but was resisted by the defendants. Hence the present suit for declaration of title and possession.

Held: The plaintiff not being made a party in the Certificate sale or suit sale, his title in the land remains intact and went to the benefit of the defendants under he compromise decree. Even if it be conceded that the plaintiff had no title at the time of the compromise decree, still, after subsequent acquisition of title under the rent sale, he would be estopped by the principle of ‘estoppel by the conduct” from taking such a plea and re-asserting his title to the land he previously gave to the defendants under the compromise decree.

Also held: The applications under sec. 174 of the Bengal Tenancy Act was neither a suit nor a continuation of a suit, and did not operate as res judicata against the defendants and in favour of the plaintiff. The appeal was dismissed.

Upendra Ch. Acharya Vs. Prazap Ch. Sutradhar. (1949) 1 DLR 18.

—Sale void when it is held for several certificate debts consolidated together:

Where a sale is held under the Public Demands Recovery Act not in respect of one particular certified-debt but in respect of several certificate debts all consolidated together, such a sale is not a sale in accordance with the Public Demands Recovery Act and as such is void.

Chittagong Engineering & Electric Supply Co. vs. Certificate Officer (1965) 17 DLR 405.

 

S. 7. Provisions of section 7 explained: Service of notice and copy of certificate served on certificate debtor.

Section 7 says that when a certificate has been filed the Certificate Officer shall serve upon the certificate-debtor in the prescribed manner a. notice in the prescribed form. After service of the notice, when a period of 30 days has elapsed, and no objection has been taken in terms of the Act, the Certificate Officer under section 14 shall proceed with the attachment id sale, or sale without previous attachment of any property or attachment of the decree, or arrest and detention of the certificate-debtor as the case may require.

The prescribed manner is to be found in rules 2 to 9 contained in Schedule H of the Act. A reading of the rules shows that there must be an attempt to serve the notice directly on the certificate-debtor in. But there is also the alternative provisions for service, by way of substituted service, if the attempt personal service fails that is contained in Rules 6 (a) (b)

Golam Hafez Mia Vs. Khadem Ali Mia (1971) 23 DLR 9.

 

—Decision in the case of Md. Muslim Ali Molla held to be wrong.

The decision in Mohammad Muslem Ali Molla’s (PLR 1957 Dac. 6) which says that rule 6(b) cannot be resorted to for service under section 7 of the Act is not correct. For substituted service of notice under section 7 of the Act, both the alternative clauses (a) and (b) of rule 6 can be resorted to, according to the facts of exact case. Ibid.

 

—Consequences following from service of notice under section 7.

On the service of notice under section 7 the consequences following from it are first: any private transfer of any immovable property in the district where certificate is filed is void, and secondly, the certificate dues become a charge upon the immovable property of certificate-debtor throughout the country. Ibid.

—Requirements to comply with the provisions embodied in sections 7, 8, 9, &10.

It is necessary to refer to the sections mentioned above namely, 7.8, 9 and 10. By section 7 when certain public demands were in arrears, the Collector of the district was to make under his hand a certificate (in the Form 2 given in the said Act) for the amount of such arrears remaining unpaid and was to cause the same to be filed in his office. By section 8 such certificate should, as regards the remedies for enforcing the same, have the force and effect of a decree of a civil court. Under section 10 such certificate having been filed in the office of the Collector, he has to issue to the Judgment- debtors a copy of such certificate and a notice in form No. 4 as given in the Act, and such certificate from the time it was served would bind all the immovable properties of the debtor if it has been attached under section 274 of the Code of Civil Procedure (Old). Such certificate u/s 19 of the Act is to be enforced in accordance with the provisions of the Civil Procedure Code as a demand for money.

Shafiqur Rahman Vs. Certificate Officer (1977) 29 DLR (SC) 234.

—Certificate Officer to whom a certificate is possesses under section 37 all the powers of the certificate Officer under sections 4 and 6, but exercise of such powers is subject to the provisions of section 9.

The Certificate Officer to whom a certificate is sent under section 12(2) has under section 37 all the powers of the Certificate Officer who filed the certificate under sections 4 or 6 and may decide all questions arising between certificate-holder and certificate-debtor relating to the making, execution, satisfaction and discharge of the certificate. But the power so vested in him is exercisable subject to the statutory exception embodied in section 9(2). Ibid.
