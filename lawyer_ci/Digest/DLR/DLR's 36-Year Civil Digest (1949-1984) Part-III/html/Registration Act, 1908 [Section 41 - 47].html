S. 32. Invalid registration-fresh deed by legal representatives.

Registration is invalid when presented by a wrong person—vendee entitled to a fresh deed of sale by the legal representatives of a deceased vendor even though the registration of the original document is invalid. Court competent to direct legal representatives to execute a fresh deed of sale in terms of the original deed.

Hafiz Ahmed Vs. Obedur Rahman (1955) 7 DLR 263.

 

—S. 32. Assign—A person does not become an assign until the document is registered and his assignment perfected—does not include a person who claims under a document sought to be registered.

The word “assign” has not been defined in the Registration Act.

In view of the fact that in section 32 of the Registration Act a person claiming under a document and an assign has both been separately referred to, it appears that the legislature had in mind two different categories of persons, namely, a person claiming under a document and a person who is an assign dehors the document sought to be registered.

Therefore the word “assign” in the Registration Act has been used in the strict legal sense, in the sense of a person in whom the transfer has been validly and fully vested and does not and should not include a person claiming under the document sought to be registered.

Consequently, a person claiming under a document does not become an assign until the document is registered and his assignment perfected.

Kabbad Mi vs. Abdul Rashid (1961) 13 DLR 326.

S. 35. Registration of a document upon admission of its execution—Whether execution was under duress or misrepresentation does not fall within the scope of the section.

Under section 35 the Registering Officer shall register the document if the executants or his representative, assign or agent admits its execution by the executants. Registration is obligatory on mere admission of execution. Admission of execution for purpose of registration is merely admission of the signature or mark affixed to the document. Allegation of the cxccuiant that she signed under fraud and misrepresentation does not stand in the way of registration once the execution, i.e., the putting of signature or mark is admitted.

Md. Jafar Ali vs. Md. Abdul Majid (1967) 19 DLR 40.

S.35: Document validly presented for registration—Registration cannot be refused.

When the document was admitted for registration and the Registrar satisfied he by admission of the executants that the deed was duly executed by them, the Registrar had no jurisdiction to refuse registration of the document.

Mst. Hasina Begum Vs. Province of East Pakistan, (1972) 24 DLR 6.

 

S. 35(1)(c): Registration-Admission of execution of document for registration after the death of the executants by or on behalf of all his legal representatives.

The Legislature having intended that admission in a case where the document is presented for registration should be by and on behalf of each one of the executants, it is difficult to appreciate why, lithe executants be dead, It is sufficient for any one of his legal representatives to admit execution on his behalf.

The singular number used in clause (c) also includes the plural and therefore where there were more representatives than one, all the representatives must join in the admission before it can be duly registered under the said Act.

Kobbad Ali vs. Abdul Rashid (1961) 13 DLR 326.

Where there is neither possession of the property alleged to have been sold, nor any proof of the payment of consideration mere registration of the sale-deed does not operate to pass title to the vendee.

—It cannot be Laid down as a general rule that mere registration of instrument without reference together circumstance operates to transfer the property.

—Purpose of enacting this section is check fraud.

The underlying principle behind the provisions of section 47 of the Registration Act is that a person having executed a document in favour of another must not have the opportunity to deprive the latter of the benefits arising out of that document by simply delaying registration. It is to guard against this that the section has been enacted and it has nothing to do with the time when the transaction embodied in the document is legally effect. Ibid.

 

—Date of execution of documents of sale of same property, between persons claiming title on the basis of documents executed by the same vendor— and not the date of registration—confers title to the property.

The contention was that a purchaser who has perfected his title by having his document registered acquires an indefeasible title as against anyone claiming under a document, registered on a later date, even though that might have been executed earlier.

Held (overruling this contention): A document that has been registered relates back to the date of its execution so that the question of title as between two contestants claiming on the basis of successive documents executed by the self same vendors must be determined with reference to the title of the latter on the date of execution and not of registration there-

Jainullah vs. Anu Mia (1963) 15 DLR 77.

—Section 47 is intended to safeguard the interest of a bonafide vendee under an instrument, which though duly executed, is awaiting registration by ensuring that he is not deprived of the property in case his vendor turns round to take advantage of the fact that the said instrument is still unregistered and executes a fresh kabala and gets it registered purporting to convey the self-same property in favour of another.

In the case of successive transfers of the selfsame property in favour of different persons by a common vendor by registered documents, the dispute as to the precedence of one document over the other has to be determined in accordance with the principles laid down in section 47. Ibid.

 

—S. 47. Defendant’s kabala was executed earlier than the plaintiff’s kabala which though executed later was, however registered before the defendant’s kabala— Held: Execution of the defendant’s kabala being earlier in point of time will have precedence over the plaintiff’s kabala.
