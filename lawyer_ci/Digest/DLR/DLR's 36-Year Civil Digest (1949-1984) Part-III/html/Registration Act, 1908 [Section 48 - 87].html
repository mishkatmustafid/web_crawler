Registration Act

S. 49—Unregistered deed used as evidence of a contract.

Proviso to section 49 of the Registration Act allows an unregistered deed which is required to be registered to be used as evidence of a contract in a Suit for specific performance or as evidence of part performance of a contract for the purpose of section 53A of the Transfer of Property Act.

Girindra Chandra Datta Chowdhury Vs. Kumud Behari Roy (1952) 4 DLR 623.

 

—Unregistered instrument cannot have the effect of creating any interest in the land.

An unregistered instrument cannot have the effect of creating any interest in the land which can be created by a registered instrument only. It cannot be used for the purpose of proving that the party setting it up acquired any title to the property.   Ibid.

 

—Endorsement requiring registration but not registered on a lease, admissible for collateral purposes. Khan Md. Biswas Vs. Chittaranjan Sen (1955) 7 DLR 60.

 

—S. 49. Unregistered document received in evidence.

An unregistered document could be received in evidence—in evidence in a suit for specific performance.

Dula Mia Vs. Haji Md. Ibrahim (1956)8 DLR 616=8 PLR (Dac.) 326.

(See also under section 17, above.) 8 PLR (Dac) 394.

 

—An unregistered document (in this case a lease) may be used for the purpose of showing the nature of possession though not of proving title.

Ahmad Shah Khan Vs. Abdul Barkat (1959) 11 DLR 427.

—If an endorsement on a lease is not admissible in evidence for want of registration, for the purpose of proving that a certain plot was included in the document it is admissible for collateral purpose. such as to prove what land the lessee was holding on the basis of the lease or to explain the possession of the lessee and the extent of the land in his possession on the basis of the lease.

Khan Md. Biswas Vs. Chittaranjan Sen (1955) 7 DLR 60.

 

—S. 49: Unregistered document relating to land though is not admissible to prove title to the land can however be relied on for a collateral purpose.

A document which is inadmissible u/s 49, not being registered is admissible for consideration for collateral purposes, as for example, to show possession of the properties by some persons who claim possession thereto and the nature of such possession.

In the present case though exhibit V not being registered which relates to transfer of immovable property cannot operate ipso facto as a document of title, it can be relied upon to show parting of possession by plaintiff and acquisition of exclusive possession by defendant. As more than 12 years have passed since the exclusive possession remained with the defendant, the right of defendant in the properties could by exhibit ‘L’ has matured into one of perfect title.

Dwijendra nath Pathak Vs. Abdallah Al-Mahmud, (1973) 25 DLR 480.

 

—Lessee put in possession but the dead of lease not registered—Lessee entitled to use unregistered lease deed as shield to prevent transferor from enforcing any right except those arising under such lease.

Pak. Employees Co-operative housing Society Vs. Anwar Sultana, (1969) 21 DLR (WI) 345.

 

—S.49: A document though required to be registered under the Registration Act but not registered, can yet be admitted for collateral purposes, such as to prove the nature of possession—Such a document cat be used to prove acquisition of title by adverse possession.

Khaliur Rahman Vs.Province of E. Pakistan (1977) 29 DLR 239.

Secs. 58 & 59: Formalities about registration of the document and the consequence that follows such registration-All formalities after registration including its explanation by the Sub-registratar complied with.

Endorsement of the Registrar on the document — Its presumptive value, rebuttal thereof.

The endorsement of the Registrar would be a among presumptive evidence of the fact that the document was explained to the executants who admitted his execution and receipt of consideration and that the whole proceeding and the endorsement made therein were regular and in order and the said endorsement could only be rebutted by the plaintiff by adducing strong evidence proving the allegation that the executants was not in his sense at die time of the execution of the deed or that any fraud was committed upon the Sub-registrar by an impostor.

Haji Kari Abdur Rahman Vs. Abdur Rahim Gazi (1983) 35 DLR 132(133).

 

Ss. 58 and 60: Registrar’s Endorsement on the deed would be a relevant evidence of execution.

Even without the statement of the Sub-Registrar his endorsement on the deed would be a relevant evidence of execution of the document.

The endorsement of the admission of the parties to a document made on a deed by registering officer was evidence under section 60, Registration Act, 1908, of the fact of the execution and that the mere fact that it was in accordance with section 35 that the Registrar questioned the parties as to whether they had executed the deed or not did not mean that the endorsement did not fall under section 58 when that section expressly provided for such an endorsement. PLR (1960) I (WP) 573.

 

S.60—Certificate by the Registering Officer that the document is “registered”-Its consequence:

Section 60 of the Registration Act only provides that when a certificate containing the word “registered” is endorsed by the Registering Officer on the document, the document is admissible for the purpose of proving that it was duly registered in the manner provided by the Act and that the facts mentioned in the endorsements referred to in section 59 occurred as mentioned therein.

Md. Jafar Ali Vs. Md. Abdul Majid (1967) 19 DLR 40.

[See under section 47 the case of Ata Ullah Vs. Custodian, Evacuee Property 16 DLR 299 above]

 

—Section 60: Title passes when conditions of sec 60 are fulfilled.

Document cannot be regarded as registered before the certificate by the Registering Officer is endorsed thereon and signed, sealed and dated in terms of section 60 of the Registration Act.

Julfu Molla Vs. Noab Ali (1975) 27 DLR 442.

—Where registration lakes place in favour of a 3rd party after the title and passed to the auction- purchaser in a revenue sale—No interest accrued by the registration. Ibid.

 

—S.74: Enquiry by the Sub-Registrar under section 74 of the Registration Act as regards execution is merely a departmental duty and has no legal validity.

Md. Cherag Ali -Vs- Dullas Khan & ors. (1953) 5 DLR 17

 

Ss. 76-77. Refusal of registration under section 76-Remedy by suit lies under section 77.

For refusal of registration of a document which is covered by Section 76 of the Registration Act, provisions have been made in section 77 thereof for a suit to be filed by the aggrieved party in a Civil Court. Therefore, the Registration Act has provided for adequate remedy against such refusal for registration by the registering authority.

Azimuddin Bhuiya -Vs- District Magistrate (1968) 20 DLR 355.

 

S. 77. Registrar’s order dismissing an application under section 73 is no bar to a suit under section 77 when the plaintiff had no intention of abandoning his application.

Nabadwip Ch. Karmakar -Vs- Madhu Sudan Karmakar. (1955) 7 DLR 235.

 

—Suit falls under the Section when execution of the document is admitted—question whether document is valid or forged etc., is not within the scope of a suit under this section.

Rakisuddin Fakir -Vs- Narsh Ch. Chakrabarty (1956) 8 DLR 648.

 

S.77. Extension of time, beyond 30 days—

Where a party having  led to present a document for registration, within 30 days from (late of the decree under section 77 of the Registration Act, applies to the civil court for extension of time, the court cannot extend time when the final decree has been passed anti the Court IS not in session of the case.

Sur. Rai Kishori Das -Vs- Ram Kamal Kaibarta Das (1957) 9 DLR 106.

 

—Document not presence within 30 days from the date of the decree- Registering Officer may refuse registration—Decree means final decree. Ibid.

—A suit to compel the registration of a document does not lie outside the provisions of section 77 of the Registration Act. 1953 PLR (Lah.) 210.

 

—S.77: The clause “person claiming under the document” means the person named in the deed in favour of whom the document was executed and does not include the real owner of a benami deed.

Rokeya Khatun Vs. Shahab Ali Molla (1970) 22 DLR 766.

—Person claiming to be the real owner of a benami dead—incompetent to present the deed for registration as well as to’ file a suit under section 77 of the Registration Act, 1908.   Ibid.

 

Secs. 77 and 82. Criminal proceedings under section 82 and civil suit under section 77 - points involved in both are common criminal proceedings to be stayed pending the disposal of civil suit.

Haji Majibur Rahman Vs. State (1957) 9 DLR 693.

 

Secs. 81-83. Offences committed before District Sub-Registrar or a Sub-Registrar.

The offences committed before District Sub- Registrar or a Sub-Registrar arc specially made punishable under sections 81, 82 and 83 of the Registration Act. No sanction under section 195, Criminal Procedure Code, for prosecution in respect of an offence committed before such an officer and falling within clauses (b) and (c) of sub-section I of section 195, Criminal Procedure Code, can be given by him but in respect of offences within clause (a) of subsection (1) of section 195, Criminal Procedure Code, he can lodge a complaint only as a public servant.

Dr. Abnoy Ch. Acharya -Vs- Faruk Ahmed Chowdhury (1953) 5 DLR 454.

 

S.82—Prior permission of Inspector-General of Registration or Registrar or Sub-Registrar mandatory before charging a person.

Abdul Gaffar Munshi and others. Vs. The State (1983) 35 DLR 76.

 

Ss. 83 and 83(i)— Section 83(i) of the Registration Act is no bar for lodging of initiating complaint by private person under section 82 of the said Act.

Barujan Bibi Vs. The State (1969) 21 DLR 881.

 

S. 83. Prosecution for an offence—

Section 83 deals only with the prosecution of an offence under the Act which comes to the knowledge of a Registering Officer in his official capacity who can institute any prosecution under the Act.

Sushil K. Paik -V.s- Crown. (1955) 7 DLR 254.

—The section has no application to cases in which the offences arc committed under the Act but do not come to the knowledge of the Registering Officer and therefore, in cases of this nature a private person can start prosecution without permission of the authority referred to in the section. Ibid.

 

S.83: Prosecution by a private party—not barred.

Section 83 of the Registration Act does not bar a prosecution by a private person and permission is not necessary br the institution of a prosecution by a private individual for an offence under section 82 of the said Act.

Section 83 of the Registration Act has no application whatever to cases in which offences are committed under the Act but do not come to the knowledge of the Registering Officer. The section enables a prosecution to be commenced by or with the permission of the authorities specified therein where an offence comes to the knowledge of a Registering Officer in his official capacity.

Prior permission of the Registration Authority is essential because it may so happen that on enquiry by the departmental chief innocence of an officer in respect of offence attributed to him may have been established and yet he may be prosecuted in Criminal Court on private complaint. It will defeat that object if it is interpreted to permit private prosecution without sanction.

Shaikh Rehenuddin Vs. Shaikh Abu Bakar Siddique (1973) 25 DLR 75.

 

S.87. Even if it is contended that there was any defect or irregularity in the execution of the document by an assign, such a detect or irregularity can be said to have been cured under section 87 of the Registration Act.

M Nuruddin Mollah -Vs- Shaikh Abbas & ors. (1957)9 DLR 6708—8 PLR (Dac.) 401.

 

S.87. An admission of execution by a wrong person does not affect the jurisdiction of the Court.

An admission of execution by a wrong persons does not affect the jurisdiction of the Court and therefore cannot render the registration null and void, it being a mere defect in procedure, and if this defect is a bonafide one, it does not, by reason of the provisions of section 87 of the Registration Act, invalidate the registration itself.

Kobbad Ali -Vs- Abdul Rashid (1961) 13 DLR 326.
