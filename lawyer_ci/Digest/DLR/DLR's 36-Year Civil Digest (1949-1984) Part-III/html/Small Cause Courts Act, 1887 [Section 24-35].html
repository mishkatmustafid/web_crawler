Appeal against suit—when does not lie.

In order to hold that so far as the Small Cause Courts Act is concerned no appeal would lie against the decision of the Court of first instance. The test, as provided in section 27 read with sec. 25 of the Provincial Small Cause Courts Act, is’ that the suit in question should not only be in the nature of Small Cause Courts suit but it must also be decided by a Court of Small Causes.

In the absence of a Munsif invested with Small Cause Courts power, a suit which is otherwise as a Small Cause Courts suit will have to be tried as an ordinary money suit and as against such a decision an appeal will lie.

—Judgment of the Small Cause Court—what it should contain.

Duty has been cast on the Provincial Small Cause Court to make its judgment intelligible, and to show that it had at least grasped the question to be decided by giving, even though very briefly, the reasons upon which its conclusion is based. To say merely that it has considered the evidence and it is satisfied that a particular plea cannot be believed is not sufficient.

Alauddin Mia & anr. Vs. Abdul Latif & ors. (1957) 9 DLR 357.

S. 25—High Court’s power of interference u/s. 25.

The trial Court from evidence drew a conclusion of fact which the learned Judge of the High Court Division reversed without positing any error of law. He merely drew a second conclusion of facts which section 25 of the Small Cause Act does not authorize him to do. The reversal of finding of fact by the learned Judge on this point cannot be sustained.

Abdus Sattar & ors. Vs. Suresh Chandra Das & ors.(1980) 32 DLR (AD) 170.

 

—When dealing with a case u/s. 25, Small Cause Court Act, the High Court is first of all to come to a finding that the decision of the court below suffers from an error of law, and then to sec whether the error of law has vitiated its decision and on so finding, the High Court can proceed to reverse the decision of the Trial Court. —Section has no application when the suit tried by Munsif was beyond his jurisdiction to try it as a Small Cause suit.

A suit for refund of earnest money (paid by way of advance for purchase of immovable property) is triable by Small Cause Courts.

The Munsif was empowered to try a suit of the nature of Small Cause Courts upto Rs.300/-. The suit was filed before him for refund of earnest money valued at Rs. over 500/- and the Munsif, in consequence, tried the suit in his ordinary evil jurisdiction. A first appeal was taken against the decision of the Munsif to the Subordinate Judge.

Relying on the principle enunciated in (1954) 6 DLR 277 it was contended that the present suit being a suit of the nature of Small Cause Courts even though it was tried under the ordinary civil jurisdiction, no appeal lay against the decision of the Munsif. Overruling this contention it was held— The Munsif in the present case who tried the Suit was not invested with powers to try a suit of the nature of Small Cause exceeding in value of Rs. 300/- and as such he had jurisdiction to try the suit in the ordinary civil jurisdiction and the order passed by him was appealable.

Ahmad Haldar Vs. Betu Mohwz Bhawal (1961) 13 DLR 140.

—When a small cause has been tried by a Court not invested with small cause court’s power, the decree passed by it is open to appeal.

Section 27 of the Provincial Small Cause Courts Act makes only a decree passed by a Court of Small Causes final. Where the Court of first instance was not such a Court the decree passed by it would not be final and would be appealable under section % of the Code of Civil Procedure.

S. 32(2)—Suit rightly instituted as a regular Suit must be tried as a regular suit, even though the Court trying it may at the time of its trial be invested with Small Cause Court powers.

Section 32(2) of the Provincial Small Cause Courts Act says that if a Suit is instituted in the ordinary Civil Court or proceedings arc commenced in such a Court the subsequent investment of such a Court with the powers of a Small Cause Court would not make the provision of sub-sec. (I) applicable. The words “suits instituted or proceedings commenced” in sub-sec.(2) mean when the plaint is presented or the application is put in. If at that time there is no Court of Small Cause in the locality and the suit or application is Filed in a Civil Court that sub-section would apply, even if the presiding judge of the Civil Court be later on invested with Small Cause Court powers.

A suit, if rightly instituted as a regular suit would continue to remain as a regular suit and must be tried as a regular suit, even though the officer trying it may at the time of its trial, but subsequent to the institution thereof, have been invested with the necessary Small Cause Court powers.

Md. Mozammal Huq Vs. Chatku Shaikh (1956) 8 DLR 196.

 

S. 33—Question is whether section 144 of the C.P. Code is available to a Court of Small Causes.

Section 33 of the Act says that Small Cause Courts and Civil Courts are distinct and should be always treated as different Courts.

Trial provisions of the Civil Procedure Code are available to a Court of Small Causes except to the extent the Small Cause Courts Act or Code of Civil Procedure excludes specifically the provisions of the Code from its application to a proceeding before a Court of Small Causes. It therefore follows that the trial procedure prescribed in the Code shall be followed in all suits cognizable by a Court of Small Causes except to the extent excluded. Provisions of section 144 of the Code which has not been excluded is available to the Court of Small Causes and the power is to be exercised when language of section is attracted to the facts of the case before it.

Sayeb Ali…Appellant Vs. Sree Gopal Chandra Das and others…Respondents. (1980) 32 DLR (AD) 212.

 

Art. 8. Suit under section 149(3) B.T. is recognizable by the Small Cause Court.

A suit under section 149(3) of the B.T. Act bears the character of a suit for a declaratory decree. Such a suit is recognizable by the Small Cause Court under Arts.8 and 19 of Sch. II of the Provincial Small Cause Courts Act.

M.H. Moharaja Sri B.B.M. Bahadur Vs. Abul Kamal (1949) 1 DLR 67.

Art.35 cl.(ii)—Conditions which must be satisfied in order to bring a suit within the exception.

In order to bring a suit within the exception provided by clause (ii) of Article 35 of the Provincial Small Cause Courts act and determine whether an act sued upon constitutes a crime or not, the nature of the allegations in the plaint are only relevant and the allegations must be such that, if those allegations are established, an offence under Chapter XVII of the Pakistan Penal Code would be made out. Where upon the facts as stated in the plaint, the case against the defendant is of an act merely wrongful or illegal but not necessarily penal so as to bring it within the provision of the Penal Code, the jurisdiction of the Small Cause Court is not at all excluded.

Majid Biswas & ors. Vs. Adiluddin Shaikh. (1960) 12 DLR 380.

 

Art.35 Item II of the Second Schedule.

Suit for compensation involving acts which constitute offence of theft and trespass cannot be entertained by Small Cause Courts and s such not entertainable as Small Cause Courts suit.

The plaint says: “The defendants had desired to cultivate the land in suit in borga under the plaintiff for a stipulated period of one year. As the plaintiff did not settle the land to them, the defendants out of that grudge, forcibly cut away the paddy grown by the plaintiffs.”

Held: The above mentioned allegation constitutes an offence of theft, and the charge of tress- pass in this context would be for the purpose of committing an offence, namely theft. That being so, the allegation, if proved, constitutes also an offence of criminal trespass as defined in section 441 of the Penal Code.

Since the allegation as noticed, Constitutes the offence of ‘penal nature’ the suit cannot, by reason of the provisions of the Article 35, item II of the second schedule be entertained by the Small Cause Courts. It has to be treated as ordinary money suit.

Abdul Hakim Vs. Obedus Samad (1964) 16 DLR 304.
