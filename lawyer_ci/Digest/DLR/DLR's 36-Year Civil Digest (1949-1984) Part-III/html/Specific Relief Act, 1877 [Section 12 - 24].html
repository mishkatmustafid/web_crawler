Specific Relief Act

(I of 1877)

 

S. 14—The Act contemplates in certain cases payment of money in compensation.

The Specific Relief ACL contemplates the possibility in certain circumstances, one of which is that the unperformed part of a contract in fact admits of compensation in money”, that a decree may be made awarding compensation in money for the deficiency while granting specific performance as to the rest. In equity jurisdiction, it has often been said that equity may treat land as money or money as land for the purpose of doing justice in the case.

Joydeb Agarwala Vs. Baitulmal Karkhana (1964)16 DLR (SC) 706.

 

S. 15—Applicability of the section—is confined to a case where the vendor purports to sell the whole property making no reference to any co sharer, when in fact a co-sharer exists.

On a proper reading of section 15 of the Specific Relief Act it becomes plain that this section would have application if the vendor had expressly purported to sell the whole property to the plaintiff, making no reference to any co-sharer. But where (as in the present case) the agreement expressly says that the vendor undertakes to get the property duly and legally conveyed in favour of the vendee by his co-sharer the provisions of sec. 15 of the Specific Relief Act have no application.

This circumstance makes it clear that the vendor (as in the present case) was not entering into an agreement by which he was obliging himself to convey the whole property to the plaintiff and consequently the provisions of sec Lion 15 cannot be called in aid (to the present case).

Anwara Chowdhury Vs. M. Majid (1964) 16 DLR (SC) 609.

 

—Applicability of the section when a part of a contract can be performed and a part cannot be performed.

Section 15 is applicable only where the defendant made a representation that the whole of the property belonged to him, which eventually turned out not to be correct. The section has no application where the defendant contracted on his behalf and on behalf of a minor and it is found that the contract is unenforceable against the minor. The object of enactment of section 15 is to provide for abatement of compensation when the title of the persons agreeing to sell or lease is defective.

The section applies only when “a party to the contract is unable to perform the whole of his part of it.” A defendant who holds himself out as the owner of the entire property and whose claim turns out not to be correct is the party to the contract who is unable to perform the whole of his part of it, because it was his duty to convey the whole property to the plaintiff. Section 15 applies to him. But when a defendant contracts on his behalf and on behalf of a minor and it is found that the contract is unenforceable against the minor then that defendant is not a party to the contract who is unable to perform the whole of his part of it.

Archana Prasad Nandi, Vs. Miss Chilia Randolph (1980) 32 DLR 118.

 

S.17—Specific performance cannot be enforced in regard to a part only.

It is the essence of specific performance that a part only of an agreement ought not to be performed except under special circumstances mentioned in section Ss.14, 15 and 16 of the Specific Relief Act.

In this case the entire land transferred by the document of 1914 is in existence but claim has been made for a part of the land transferred and, as such, such a suit is barred under section 17 of the Specific Relief Act.

Tazal Haque Vs. Md. Affan (1965) 17 DLR 613.

 

—When shares are definite, even though part of the contract is sought to be enforced, if does not offend section 17.

Plaintiffs case that late M.R. entered into a contract with the plaintiff to transfer his property to the plaintiff and in pursuance of the contract the plaintiff made some advances. Before the actual transfer of the property M.R. died leaving behind the plaintiff and the defendant in the case as his heirs and legal representatives and the share which the plaintiff got was 1/4th of the property and remaining 3/4th fell to the share of the defendant. After Mr.’s death plaintiffs filed the present suit against the defendant to transfer the 4th share of the property on the basis of the contract with M.R. Question arose whether the plaintiffs claim in the case amounts to enforcing a part of contract within the meaning of section 17 of the Specific Relief Act.

Held : In this case after the death of M.R. 1/ 4th share of the property has devolved on the plaintiff and the defendant has inherited the specific 3/4th share of M.R. and it cannot therefore be said thai defendant’s share is either undefined or indivisible.

The share of defendant is well defined and capable of being specifically enforced and if the contract is enforced against the defendant there will be performance of the whole contract and no part will remain unperformed.

In granting specific performance against the defendant the obligations under the whole contract arc satisfied and so it cannot be held that in allowing the suit there will be violation of the provisions of section 17 of the Specific Relief Act which prohibits enforcement of a part of contract and, therefore, it cannot be said that it will amount to allowing specific performance of part of the contract.

Nur Nessa Khatun Vs. Saleha Rahman (1974) 26 DLR 347.

 

—When specific performance of contract cannot be granted. The general rule of equity is that the Court will not compel specific performance of a contract unless it can enforce the whole contract. This rule is embodied in section 17 of the Specific Relief Act. Under section 17 a contract cannot be specifically enforced in part except under situations provided for under sections 14, 15 & 16 of the said Act.

Archana Prasad Nandi Vs. Miss Chilia Randolph (1980) 32 DLR 118.

 

Ss. 17 and 23—Contract for reconveyance of property, parties to the contract on each side more than one—One of the several promises may obtain specific performance of contract on payment of the entire consideration money and by impleading the other promises as co-defendants.

The questions which were before the Full Bench for decision were: ‘Whether any one or some of the joint promises under a contract of reconveyance can obtain specific performance of the contract in its entirety;

Whether specific performance of a part of a contract of reconveyance can be obtained in any case by anyone or some of the joint promises or by all of them jointly;

Whether the case of Niranian Das Vs. Charu Bala Das reported in 16 DLR 611 has been correctly decided.

Held: Both under the general law of contract and the Specific Relief Act, one or some of the joint premises may obtain specific performance of a contract of reconveyance in its entirety on payment of the whole of the stipulated price, provided the other promises are impleaded in the suit as co-defendants.

A suit for specific performance of a contract of sale or reconveyance at the instance of one or some of the joint premises appears to be maintainable under the provisions of section 23 of the Specific Relief Act.

According to clause (a) of section 23 specific performance of contract may be obtained by any party thereto. The expression “any party thereto” in clause (a) does not mean any one of the parties to the contract.

The plain meaning of clause (b) of section 23 appears to be that the representative-in-interest of any one of the several joint promises may claim specific performance of a contract. If that be so, there is no reason why any of the several joint promises should not be entitled to obtain specific performance of a contract under clause (a) of section 23.

Part performance of contract enforceable in relation to cases under sections 15, 16 and 17 of the Specific Relief Act.

In view of the rigid provision of section 17 of the Specific Relief Act, specific performance of a part of contract can be obtained only in a case covered by any of the sections 14, 15 and 16 of the Specific Relief Act and not otherwise.

All co-promises under a contract ought to be joined as plaintiffs in a suit where performance of the contract is claimed. Section 45 of the Contract Act appears to be the basis of this rule.

Kabil Mridha Vs. Amena Khatoon (1968) 20 DLR 1027.

 

S. 18—Section 18 of the Specific Relief Act recognizes as enforceable in law the right of a person contracting to purchase or to lake in lease property from person who at the Lime of the contract does not own it but who subsequently acquires it.

Ghulam Mohd. Shah Vs. Fateh Mohd. Shah. (1955) 7 DLR (FC) 70 (77) 1-h.col).

 

S. 20—Where a thing is agreed upon to be done though there is penalty annexed yet the very thing must be done.

Where in a stipulation in a contract for sale of immovable property a penalty is attached to secure the performance of that very thing the person concerned will not be relieved from the specific performance of the thing stipulated to be done.

In cases where there arc penal clauses such clause do not debar the party who has not defaulted from enforcing specific performance as they are not in the nature of alternative stipulations.

Dula Mia Vs. Haji Md. Ibrahim (1956) 8 DLR 616=8 PLR (Dac) 326.

‘If the vendor and the said panel of trustees fail to execute and register the lease in the manner on the (late and at the place aforesaid for any reason whatsoever, the Promises hereby agreed to be demised unto the lessee shall remain charged and shall be available for realisation of the amount paid together with all costs and damages.’

Held : There is nothing in this clause to indicate that the plaintiff had agreed to abandon his right to specific performance.

S. 21(a)(b)—Government servant—Dismissal from service of a Government servant or an employee of a statutory body—Civil Courts have jurisdiction to go into question whether services validly terminated or not—Suit for declaration that termination of service void, wrongful and inoperative—Cannot be termed as one for enforcing a contract for personal service and dismissed on that score.

Mst. Manni Vs. Karachi Cantonment Board. (1969) 21 PLD (Karachi), 304.

 

S. 21(b)—A contract of personal service with a private firm—Suit for reinstatement on dismissal does not lie— Suit for damage is the proper course.

A private firm must have a free choice in the matter of appointment and discharge of its employees. A court cannot foist upon an unwilling private firm an employee whom it has thought fit to get rid of, and to that end, rightly or wrongly whom it has discharged, or whom, for one reason or the other, the firm is unwilling to accept. Should an employee, however, feel aggrieved and consider that he has been wrongfully and illegally discharged, all that he can seek from a Court is damage and not his reinstatement, which the Court is not competent at all to order.

Although an employee may establish that he has been wrongly dismissed, still he is not entitled to the remedy of an injunction or of specific performance inasmuch as a contract involving his personal service cannot be specifically enforced in view of section 21(b) of the Specific Relief Act.

Azizar Rahman Vs. Burmah Oil Co. (1961) 13 DLR 458.

 

S.22—Decree for specific performance is discretionary

—Section 22 of the Act provides that the jurisdiction to decree for specific performance is discretionary, but the discretion of the Court is not arbitrary but must be guided by judicial principles and capable of correction by a court of appeal.

Jamaruddin Mandal Vs. Mahiruddin Mandal (1955)7 DLR 642.

 

—Specific performance of a contract may also be refused when its enforcement will involve the Mutawalli in litigation about his authority to transfer.

Administrator of Waqfs Vs. Mst. Sahera Begum (1976) 28 DLR 239.

 

—Discretionary jurisdiction in granting a decree for specific performance should not be arbitrary.

Section 22 of the Specific Relief Act does no doubt provide that the jurisdiction of the Court to pass a decree for specific performance of a contract is discretionary but this discretionary power is not arbitrary.

Administrator of Waqfs Vs. Mst. Sahera Begum (1976) 28 DLR 238.

—Specific performance of a contract may be refused where its enforcement will adversely affect third parties or lead to breach of contract or compel the defendant to do an act not lawful.

Administrator of Waqfs Vs. Mst. Sahera Begun (1976)28 DLR 238.

—Future eventuality envisaged in the deed of contract may be a ground for repudiation of contract.

Administrator of Waqfs Vs. Mst. Sahera Begun (1976)28 DLR 238.

 

Relief by grant of specific performance of contract discretionary—4llustrations under section 22 illustrative and not exhaustive.

The illustrations given in section 22 of the Specific Relief Act are not exhaustive but illustrative the said section clearly shows that the Court has discretion to refuse specific performance even it might be lawful.

To grant specific performance of contract is absolutely within the discretion of the Court and no rigid rule  can be laid down when and where this discretion can be exercised by the Court.

The sentence before sub-section (1) is not “The following are the cases’ but the sentence is “The following are cases” which shows that it is illustrative cases have not been definitely mentioned we do not find any substance in the contention that the said cases are the only cases where the court is to exercise discretion not to pass decree for specific performance.

Nowab Meah Chowdhury Vs. Ezazuddin Ahmed, (1961) 13 DLR 554.

 

Contracts more than one covered by same instrument—Each separately enforceable—When there is more than one contract although 4 by the same instrument, each contract may be separately and independently enforced.

Sahebuddin Mondal vs. Md. Aminuddin Mondal (1961) 16 DLR 105.

 

Time when the essence of contract—The stipulation of the agreement is as follows— “If after establishment of our good title you do not within the stipulated period pay the balance of consideration money and get the kabala executed by us, then only the earnest money paid today will be forfeited and the bainapatra will be null and void.’

As the time specified in the agreement for sale was subject to more than one condition, it cannot be said that the parties thereto intended the time mentioned therein to be the essence of the contract.

Eskandar Ali Vs. Musammat Alhamara Begum (1967) 9 DLR 43.

 

—If time is not made the essence of contract, subsequently it cannot be made so, barring Certain circumstances.

Eskandar Ali Vs. Musammat Alhamara Begum (1967) 9 DLR 43.

 

S.23—A settlement of land on receipt of a selami and also for consideration of reconveyance of demised land is a completed contract.

In the absence of any term prohibiting the transfer of the right or exercise of the right, to any other person, by the person in whose favour document was executed, the contract is not a personal contract. If there be consideration for the contract, it is a completed contract, and as such, it is assignable under the Specific Relief Act. 8 PLR (Dac) 1088.

 

—Pre-emption right available—Specific performance relief will not be granted.

Where specific performance is sought to be enforced against a subsequent purchaser who has got a right of pre-emption the court should, in the exercise of its discretion under section 23, Specific Relief Act, refuse to decree specific performance.

Nowab Meah Chowdhury Vs. Ezazuddin Ahmed (1961) 13 DLR 554.

 

—Section 23 of the Specific Releif Act clearly provides that the specific performance can be enforced by either parties to the contract or their legal representatives. The only bar that has been contemplated in section 23 is that if the contract is of such a personal nature which could not otherwise be enforced then it should not be specifically enforced. There is no other bar provided in the said section 23 of the Specific Relief Act.

Nur Nesa Khatun Vs. Saleha Rahman (1974) 26 DLR 347.

 

S. 23(b)—Contracts, benefit of— assignable, subject to contrary intention which may be either implied or expressed— Personal contracts are not assignable.

As a general rule, the benefits of a contract arc assignable subject to any contrary intention exhibited in the contract or the document itself.

A contrary intention may either be express or arise by necessary implication. One illustration of the cases in which such a contrary intention is implied is to be found in what arc commonly known as personal contracts or contracts depending upon the learning, skill, solvency or any personal qualification of the assignor or the party to the contract or from whom the benefits of the contract arc claimed under the particular agreement.

Lakshman Mandol Vs. Moslemuddin Sardar (1958)10 DLR 165.

 

—Judgment-debtor in a money decree entering into a contract with a third party whereby the latter undertakes to pay the decretal dues of the judgment- debtor to the decree-holder. In executing the decree, the decree-holder cannot proceed against the third party as he is not the legal representative of the judgment-debtor.

Sajedul Huq Vs. Sultan Hassan (1959) 11 DLR 293.

 

Benefit arising out of a contract of repurchase is assignable—The right that accrues to a vendor of immovable property on the basis of an agreement to retransfer by the vendee at the time of sale is assignable.

Ratish Chandra Mridha Vs. Hara Krishna Goldar (1963) 15 DLR 634.

[See under section 21(b) in case of Azizur Rahman Vs. Burmah Oil Co. in 13 DLR 458 above.]

 

—Right arising Out of a contract, when transferable.

A contractual right without limitation is assignable, transferable and heritable and thus, enforceable under section 23(b) of the Specific Relief Act, by the transferor or his representative-in-interest.

Aftabuddin Meah Vs. Kudrat Ali Miah (1982) 34 DLR 51.

 

Ss. 23 and 27—Suit must be against the sole contractor or entire body of contractors.

Reading sections 23 and 27 together, it seems that while a contract may be specifically enforced by any of one of the parties to a contract under clause (a) of section 23, a contract may be enforced against the sole contractor or the entire body of contractors and not against any one of them.

The case reported in 15 DLR 611, so far as it decided that only a part of contract of reconveyance cannot be specifically enforced, is not entirely a correct statement of the law.

Kabil Mridha V. Amena Khatoon (1968) 20 DLR.
