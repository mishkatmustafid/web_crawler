Specific Relief Act

(I of 1877)

 

S. 45—An application to the High Court for an order under section 45 of the Specific Relief Act does not lie where the act with reference to which relief is asked takes place outside the limits of the Ordinary Original Civil Jurisdiction of the High Court, even though the person against whom the relief is sought has office within those limits.

Sree Meenakshi Ltd Vs. Prov. Textile Commissioner (1951) 3 DLR 126.

 

Secs. 52-57—Injunction—Temporary and permanent.

The law relating to injunctions is contained in the Civil Procedure Code (Or.39, rr.I-5) and the Specific Relief Act (sections 52-57).

The law as to perpetual injunction is regulated by the Specific Relief Act, and as to temporary or interlocutory injunction it is regulated by the Code of Civil Procedure.

Although the Specific Relief Act deals with perpetual injunctions leaving temporary injunctions to be regulated by the Civil Procedure Code, the same principles in the Specific Relief Act must equally apply to granting of temporary injunctions and these are to be sought in the Specific Relief Act itself.

SN Gupta & Co. Vs. Sadananda Ghosh (1959) 11 DLR 470.

 

Injunction is a specific relief— Temporary injunction concerned with preventive relief’s—Principles governing issue of temporary injunction.

Issue of temporary or perpetual injunction is a form of specific relief. When a specific relief is granted by preventing a party from doing that which he is under an obligation not to do, it is called preventive relief. This relief is to continue until the specific time named in die order. It is merely provisional in its nature and does not conclude a right. Its sole object is to preserve the subject in controversy without determining any question of right and to prevent further perpetration of wrong.

In order to sustain an action for the preservation of a property or a right in issue in status quo, it is not necessary to decide in favour of the plaintiff upon the merits nor it is necessary that he should present such a case as will certainly entitle him to a decree upon the final hearing; since he may be entitled to a temporary injunction although his right to the relief prayed for may ultimately fail. In other words, it would be sufficient if it be shown that he has a fair question to rise as to the existence of the right alleged.

SN Gupta & Co. Vs Sadananda Ghosh (1959) 11 DLR 470.

 

—In the case of a temporary injunction, (a) an applicant must show a fair prima fade case in support of the right claimed: (b) an actual or threatened violation of that right; (c) productive of irreparable or a least, serious damage: (d) his conduct must be such as not to disentitle him to assistance but it should be fair and honest and in particular there must be no acquiescence or delay; (e) there must be greater convenience in granting than refusing the injunction; and (1) equally efficacious relief must not be obtainable by any other usual mode or proceeding.

S.N. Gupta & Co. Vs. Sadananda Ghosh (1959) 11 DLR 470.

 

—Specific performance and injunction—difference between them.

Though relieves by specific performance and by injunction belong to the same branch of law, yet there is a distinction between the remedies, in that specific performance is directed to compel performance of an active duty while an injunction is generally directed to prevent the violation of a negative duty.

SN Gupta & Co. Vs. Sadananda Ghosh (1959) 11 DLR 47O.

 

—Injunction will lie at the instance of a person out of possession, when.

It is well—established that an injunction will lie at the instance of a complainant out of possession when irreparable injury is proved.

SN Gupta & Co. Vs. Sadananda Ghosh (1959) 11 DLR 47O.

 

—A plaintiff is not entitled to relief by way of temporary injunction if there be acquiescence or delay. S.N.

SN Gupta & Co. Vs. Sadananda Ghosh (1959) 11 DLR 47O.

 

—Delay to ask for an injunction, when a ground and when not a ground for refusing.

It is well established that delay is not material so long as matters remain in status quo, and it does not mislead the defendants or amount to quiescence.

Where it would be unjust to give a remedy, either because the party has, by his conduct., done that which is equivalent to a waiver of it, or where by his conduct or neglect he has put the other party in a Situation in which it would not be reasonable to place him if the remedy were afterwards to be asserted; in either of these cases lapse of times and delay are most material.

SN Gupta & Co. Vs. Sadananda Ghosh (1959) 11 DLR 47O.

 

S. 53—Temporary or permanent injunction, when granted.

Plaintiff can have temporary injunction in a suit if he succeeds to make out a good prima facie case and be ‘good prima facie case’ has been explained to mean that a serious question is to be tried in the suit and that in the event of success, if the injunction be not ranted, the plaintiff would suffer irreparable injury.

Power to issue temporary injunction under Chapter IX of the Specific Relief Act is general and wider than that provided is section 54 under Chapter X of the Act which contains special power. Under Chapter IX of the Act it has been provided that preventive relief is granted at the discretion of the Court by injunction, temporary or perpetual, whereas granting of perpetual injunction has been specifically provided for in section 54 under Chapter X with the me-condition as to the existence of an obligation and a perpetual injunction may be granted to prevent the breach of an obligation existing in favour of the applicant, whether expressly or by implication.

Sk. Nur Mohammad Vs. The Province of East Pakistan, (1973)25 DLR 69.

—Circumstances when temporary injunction can be granted.

Injunctions are granted when the court is satisfied (I) that the applicant has a prima facie case to go to trial; (2) that protection is necessary from irreparable injury before his legal right can be established; (3) the mischief or inconvenience likely to arise from withholding injunction will be greater than which is likely to arise from granting it. It is well settled that there can be no injunction for actionable wrongs for which damages arc proper remedy.

Uttara Bank Vs. Macneill & Kilburn Ltd. (1981) 33 DLR (AD) 298.

 

—The object of the interlocutory injunction is to protect the plaintiff against injury by the violation of his right for which he could not be adequately compensated in damages recoverable in the action if the uncertainty were resolved in his favour at the trial; but the plaintiff’s need for such protection must be weighed against the corresponding need of the defendant to be protected against injury resulting from his having been prevented from exercising his own legal right for which he could not be adequately compensated under the plaintiffs undertaking in damages if the uncertainty were resolved in the defendant’s favour at the trial. The Court must weigh one’s need against another and determine where “the balance of convenience” lies.

Uttara Bank Vs. Macneill & Kilburn Ltd. (1981) 33 DLR (AD) 298.

 

—Temporary injunction is an equitable relief which is merely provisional in its sole objective to preserve the subject-matter in controversy without determining the rights of the parties and to prevent the doing of an act whereby the subject-matter in controversy may be endangered. In order to sustain an action for temporary injunction it would be sufficient if it can be shown that the plaintiff has a fair and arguable case as to the existence of his alleged right.

Mizanur Rahman Vs Serajuddin Bepari and ors (1981) 33 DLR 212.

 

—Rule 183 of the Government Estates Manual contemplates the establishment of a new hat where there was none.

In case of a hat (herein it is called Tarabo hat) which has been newly set up, rule 183 of the Government Estates Manual is not attracted.

Hat—right of a party to hold a hat—Mere proximity not a valid ground for restraining another hat to be held.

Mizanur Rahman Vs Serajuddin Bepari and ors (1981) 33 DLR 212.

 

—The legal position is that a private hat is non retainable. The right that the plaintiffs wants to assert in support of their claim must be a legal right and the injury that they allege must be legal injury.

The mere prospect or an apprehension of injury would not entitle the plaintiffs to an order of injunction.

Mizanur Rahman Vs Serajuddin Bepari and ors (1981) 33 DLR 212.

There is no doubt that in an exceptional circumstance, as for example in the case of an urgency or where the order of injunction is, on the face of it, without jurisdiction, an application under Order 39, rule 4 of the Code may be made for an ex parte order made under rule 3 of the order but such an application can be disposed of only on hearing the parties on merit, which is likely to be almost similar to the hearing of the parties in pursuance of a notice under rule 3.

S. 54—Grant of perpetual injunction to prevent breach of obligation.

Provisions of section 54 of the Specific Relief Act authorize grant of perpetual injunction to prevent the breach of an obligation existing in favour of the applicant, whether expressly or by implication. The Court may grant perpetual injunction also under the said provisions when the defendant invades or threatens to invade the plaintiffs right to or enjoyment of a property.

M/s. June Ferguson Vs. Ameenur Rasheed Chowdhury, (1973) 25 DLR 1.

 

—Circumstances when perpetual relief is granted. Perpetual injunction is granted as provided under section 54.

It lays down that an injunction may be granted to prevent the breach of an obligation in favour of the applicant, whether expressly or by implication, provided there exists no measure for ascertaining the actual damage or likely to be caused, by threat of the defendants and where such invasion or threat is such that pecuniary compensation would not afford adequate relief.

M/S. Hossain Ahmed Vs. HD Hossain, (1980)32 DLR (AD) 223.

 

—So far as permanent injunctions are concerned it will appear from section 54 of the Specific Relief Act that they are issued only to prevent breach of an obligation existing in favour of the plaintiff. 1954 PLR (Lab) 175.
