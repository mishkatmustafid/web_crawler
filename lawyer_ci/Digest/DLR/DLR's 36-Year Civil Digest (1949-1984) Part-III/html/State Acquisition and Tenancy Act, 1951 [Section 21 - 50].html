S. 42—Publication of notification under section 3 of the Act does not eliminate different classes of tenancies till publication of compensation assessment-rolls.

It is clear that the different classes of tenancies including under-raiyati tenancy do not cease to exist with publication of Notification of acquisition under section 3 of the East Bengal State Acquisition and Tenancy Act. The entire chain of interest of rent- receivers will be eliminated only after the publication of the compensation assessment-roll under section 42 of the Act.

Arfan Ali Sheikh Vs. Ead Ali Talukdar (1962) 14 DLR 791.

 

S. 43—Lands not included in compensation assessment-roll—Not vested in the Government.

A close reading of the provisions made in sections 42, 43 and 44 of the Act point to inevitable conclusions, firstly, that every interest which is liable to be acquired under the Act has to be paid for and secondly, that except in the cases of acquisition under Chapter II of the Act the interests which are acquirable vest in the Provincial Government only if assessment of compensation in regard to the same has been made and published as provided in the Act.

It having been found that the lands in question have not been included in the compensation assess merit roll it cannot be contended that the said lands hay: vested in the Government merely because they are acquirable and because there has been notification in respect of that area under section 43(2) of the Act.

Shah Mohd. Elias Shah Vs. Province of East Pakistan (1965) 17 DLR 277.

Ss. 42 and 43—play a very important part in the matter of determining the rate of the complete cessation of the right of rent-receiver—be they the holder of secular lands or endowed lands. When the compensation assessment-roll is finally published under section 42 and the certificate thereto is given under section 43(2) all chain of interest of the rent- receiver is eliminated.

Arfan Ali Sheikh Vs. Iad Ali Talukdar (1962) 14 DLR 791.

 

Ss. 43 and 44—Sections 42, 43 and 44 of the Act point to conclusions, firstly, that every interest which is liable to be acquired under the Act has to be paid for; and, secondly, that except in the cases of acquisition under Chapter II of the Act the interests which are acquirable vest in the Provincial Government only if assessment of compensation in regard to the same has been made and published as provided in the Act.

If the lands in question have not been included in the compensation assessment roll it cannot be contended that the said lands have vested in the Government merely because that they are acquirable and because there has been a notification in respect of that area under section 43(2) of the Act.

Shah Md. Elias Shah Vs. Province of E Pak. (1965) 17 DLR 277.

—Publication of Compensation Assessment - Roll—Tenant’s non-retainable land vests in the Government on publication of such notification.

In view of the provisions of sections 43 and 44 of the Act. a tenant’s non-retainable land like Bazar vests in the Government on the publication of a notification in the official Gazette under sub-section (2) of section 43. The notification referred to hereinbefore is a notification under sub-section (2) of section 43. This notification relates to a number of villages including Jarulia in which the disputed Bazar situates. Sub-section (2) of section 43 of the Act provides that notification under that sub-section shall be conclusive proof of its publication and its date thereof. Since the notification relates to a number of villages including the whole of the village Jarulia and as the disputed Bazar lies within that village, it should be taken that the compensation assessment- roll published in this case is also relatable to the disputed Jarulia Bazar. Thus the disputed Jarulia Bazar vested in the Provincial Government during the pendency of this appeal.

Province of East Pakistan Vs. Haji Abul Hussain (1970) 22 DLR 191.

 

Ss. 43(2) and 45—The provision embodied in section 45 has been made, not for the purpose of vesting any right in the Provincial Govt. but for the purpose of protecting the tenants from being misled into payments to somebody other than the Provincial Govt. who become entitled to rent on and from the first day of the next agricultural year on the publication of a notification under section 43(2). It is, therefore, a case of statutory transference of possession to the provincial Govt. and statutory creation of the relationship of landlord and tenant between the Provincial Govt. and the tenant of the ex-rent receivers as well as the rent-receivers themselves in respect of their retainable khas lands.

S. 44 and Chap.V—Tenant’s non-retainable khas lands—Classification of such lands is determined after the preparation of record-of-rights which gives the description of such land (whether land or fishery, etc.)—thereafter consequences under section 44 ensue—Government must inform the tenant to the satisfaction of the Court that these steps have been completed.

A tenant can be dispossessed of his non- retainable khas land only when the steps enumerated in various sections in Chapter V have been taken.

So far as a tenant is concerned the classification of his khas lands is determined after the preparation of record-of-rights and possession thereof is to be taken after completing the steps detailed in Chapter V of the Act. Preparation of the record-of-rights involves decision as to whether land is agricultural land, or a fishery or a hat or bazar and then the consequences set out in various clauses under section 44 would ensue. He is, therefore, entitled to claim to be informed that those steps have been taken.

In a Court of law, when challenged that those steps have not been taken, the Provincial Government which seeks to take possession of those non- retainable khas lands would be called upon to prove that the requirements of law have been complied with.

Province of East Pakistan Vs. Nakuldas Mridha (1968) 20 DLR 769.

S. 44(3) read with S. 20(2a)—The words “in excess” mean whatever is non-retainable—Hats and Bazars in view of the 1960 amendment are excluded from the retainable lands and are vested in the Government.

The High Court held the view that the words in excess” in sub-section (3) of section 44 necessarily refer only to retainable lands and can have no application at all to lands which are not retainable tinder section 20(2a).

Held: Whatever is non-retainable is in excess of that which is retainable. In view of the retrospective effect by Ordinance No. XII of 1960 in section 20 by the incorporation therein of sub-sec. (2a) which had the effect of excluding hats and bazars from the category of retainable lands from the very beginning, the hats and bazars in the khas possession of rent-receivers became vested in the Govt. from the date of the notification in 1956 under subsection (2) of section 3 of the Act. But so far as raiyats, agricultural tenants and other tenants were concerned, the vesting took place, after the preparation of the compensation assessment roll and its notification by virtue of sub-section 3 of Section 44.

Province of E. Pakistan Vs. Secretary, MAS  Madrasha (1964) 16 DLR (SC) 281.

 

S. 44(3)—The words “in excess” refer only to maximum retainable lands.

They do not refer to lands which are outside such classes of lands which the tenants are entitled to retain possession of under Chapter IV of the Act.

The above conclusion is irresistible for the following reasons; (a) Because such a result follows from the plain meaning of the words “in excess” which have been used in relation to lands which “tenants are entitled to retain under Chapter IV.” (b) Because, in accordance with the well-established canons of interpretation, a provision of a statute, which takes away the rights of citizens, has to be construed strictly against the Government and liberally in favour of the Citizen; (c) Because in the Act itself the Legislature has used the words “other than” in subsection (1) of section 44, in contradistinction to the words “in excess” in sub-section (3) of that section of the Act; (d) Because when the Legislature wanted to divest and disposes the rent-receiver of all his khas lands, other than the lands which he could claim to retain possession of under Sec. 20, they have specifically said so in sub-section (3) of section 46E of the Act; (e) Because the legislature has refrained from making provisions similar to Section 46E(3) with regard to tenants, as distinguished from rent-receivers.

Secretary, Muktagacha Abbasia Senior Madrasha Vs. Province of East Pak (1963) 15 DLR 37.
