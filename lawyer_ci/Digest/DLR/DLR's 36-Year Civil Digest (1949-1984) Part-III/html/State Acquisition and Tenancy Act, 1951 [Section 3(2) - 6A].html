State Acquisition and Tenancy Act, 1951

 

S.3(2)—Hat when cannot be acquired—Hats when owned and possessed by persons who are not rent-receivers, cannot be acquired.

Mohar Ali vs. Prov. of E. Pak. (1957) 9 DLR 569.

 

—It cannot be held that the lands of which a rent-receiver cannot be dispossessed had to be specified in the notification.

Jibendra K. Acharya Vs Prov. of E. Pak. (1957) 9 DLR (SC) 21 (46-47).

 

—Fresh notification for acquisition of Kutcheryghar not necessary.

It is not correct to say that if and when the Provincial Government decides to acquire the kutcheryghar it will be necessary for them to issue a fresh notification under section 3(2) of the Act. Action already taken, if supplemented by an order f the Provincial Government specifying the property in question as one which they have decided to acquire, will elliptically serve the purpose.

Secretary, Revenue Department Govt. of E Pak. Vs. Ibrahim Mondal. (1964) 16 DLR (SC) 125.

 

—Kutcheryghar does not become acquired upon the issue of notification under section 3(2)—ft must be acquired upon an express decision to that effect.

The right of a rent-receiver to retain his kutcheryghar against a vesting notification under section 3 of the Act is not lost unless the kutcheryghar is expressly acquired by the Provincial Government.

Unless a decision to acquire a kutcheryghar is made by the Provincial Government, the rent- receiver to whom it appertains has a statutory right to retain it as a tenant under the Provincial Government.

Secretary, Revenue Department Govt. of E Pak. Vs. Ibrahim Mondal (1964) 16 DLR (SC) 125.

 

—A kutchery would come within the category of retainable khas land, unless the Provincial Government has decided to acquire—The decision must be that of the Provincial Government—But the Provincial Government will have to acquire under a notification under section 3(2).

—After such a decision by the Provincial Government, the property iii question, if it is a kutchery, would not be acquired automatically upon decision; it would become acquired khas land inasmuch as it would be outside the category of khas land as defined in section 20(2)(a); but the Provincial Government will have to acquire under a notification under section 3(2) of the Act, which is the only manner prior to a final publication of the assessment-rolls by which such property can be acquired.

Md. Ibrahim Mondal Vs. Province of E Pak. (1963) 15 DLR 703.

 

—All khas lands’ till the retainable area is determined are held by the (formal) rent-receivers as tenants under the Government.

As the acquisition of the khas lands of the rent- receivers is subject to the provisions of subsections (2),(3),(4) and (5) of section 20, the rent- receivers whose interests have been acquired under sub-section (1) of section 3 can retain all their khas lands in excess of the retainable limit till the excess lands are determined and taken out in the manner prescribed in sub-sections (2), (3), (4) and (5) of section 20, but those rent-receivers shall till then hold alt their khas lands as tenants directly under the Provincial Government under clause () of sub-section (4) of section 3.

Al-Haj Kutubaddin Ahmed Vs. AJ Haridwan Uddin (1962) 14 DLR 228.

 

—Unless there is a notification under section 3 (2) on whole of the khas lands of a rent-receiver he remains in possession as a tenant under the Government.

Tofazzal Hossain Vs. Province of East Pakistan (1963) 15 DLR (SC) 139.

 

—Upon the publication of a notification under subsection (2) of section 3 all the lands in the khas possesion of the rent-receiver, excluding retainable lands under section 20(2)(a) and (b) shall vest in the said Government. Such khas lands which come within the meaning of section 20(2)(a) and (b) have not been acquired by the Provincial Government.

Md. Ibrahim Vs. Province of East Pakistan (1963) 15 DLR 703.

 

—Rent-receivers cannot retain Flat and Bazar after introduction of sub-section (2a) to sec. 20. Hat and Bazar of a rent-receiver will be affected by a notification issued under sub-section (2) of sec. 3 of the Act.

Province of East Pakistan Vs. Nakuldas Mirdha (1968) 20 DLR 769.

 

—Notified acquisition of khas lands under sec. 3 (2) can be taken possession of only after observance of the requirement of law.

As regards rent-receivers, there were notified acquisitions of non-retainable khas lands under subsection (2) of Section 3 of the Act and in pursuance of these notifications such land could be taken possession of only after observing the requirements of law as laid down by the Special Bench of this Court in the case of Jibendra Kishore Acharya Vs. Province of East Pakistan, (1956) 8 DLR 457.

Province of East Pakistan Vs. Nakuldas Mirdha (1968) 20 DLR 769.

 

By Government notification hats in khas possession of a landlord vested absolutely in the Government free from encumbrances.

Golam Hossain Khan Vs. Abdul Mannan Khan (1983)35 DLR 331.

—Customary right of compulsorily holding the hat on Tuesdays and Fridays is also an encumbrance and is wiped out on the vesting of the hat in the Government free from encumbrances. Ibid

 

S. 3(2a) lithe Government by a notification acquired the interests of all rent-receivers in a district, part of a district, or local area, by reference to areas wherein they had interest, it was not beyond the powers of the provincial Government given to it u/s 3 (1)3.

The notifications for the acquisitions of khas lands under section 3(2) are subject to the provisions of sub-clauses (a) and (b) of s. 20(2) and, therefore, it cannot be held that the lands of which a rent- receiver cannot be dispossessed had to be specified in the notification.

Jibendra Kishore Vs. Province of East Pakistan (1957) 9 DLR (SC) 21.

 

Failure to specify the interests acquired does not render the acquisition invalid. The specifications of the properties which are intended to be acquired mentioned in sub-sections (1) and (4) of section 3 of the Acquisition Act refer to rent-receivers and not to their interests. Sub-section (2a) of section 3 of the Act provides that in a notification issued under this section rent-receivers may be specified or described by name, or by reference to areas wherein they have interests, or in such other manner as the Provincial Government may determine. The notification dated the 2nd of April, 1956 was issued with reference to area. For the failure to mention the interest of the rent-receivers falling in the area, no illegality has been committed.

Haji Aftabuddin Kazi Vs. Prov. of E Pak. (1962) 14 DLR 420.

 

—If the Government by a notification acquired the interest of all rent-receivers in a district, part of a district, or local area, by reference to areas wherein they had interest—It is valid.

Jibendra Kishore Vs. Province of East Pakistan (1957) 9 DLR (SC) 21.

 

—Omission in the notification of the extent of share of the interest acquired, not fatal.

Nawab Sir Faruqui Vs. Prov. of E Bengal (1957) 9 DLR 174.

 

S. 3(4)—Right of way and casements are encumbrances and, as such, arc destroyed, with the acquisition of an estate to which they relate, by the Government.

Kali Charan Das. Vs. Tamiruddin (1958) 10 DLR 523.

 

S. 3(4)(a)—Persons who were holding over as tenants, after the expiry of the lease, became tenants directly under the Government.

With the acquisition of rent-receiving interests, all tenants holding lands under rent-receivers became tenants directly under the Government under section (3)(4)(c) of the East Bengal State Acquisition and Tenancy Act. Thus, the defendant who was a tenant holding over under the plaintiffs became a tenant directly under the Government on and from the 14th April, 1956, the date on which the rent-receiving interests were acquired by the Government.

Sm. Lalita Roy Vs. Rafiqueullah Khan (1966) 18 DLR 107.

 

S.3(4) clauses (a) & (1)—Under clause (I) a rent-receiver retains all lands which have not been acquired under sec. 3(2) as tenant under the Government.

By virtue of clause (a) sub-section (4) of section 4 of the Act on the publication of a notification by the Government the whole of the land in the khas possession of the rent-receiver vests in the government. By virtue of clause (I) of sub-section (4) of section 3, however, the rent-receivers are entitled to retain as tenants under the government such of their khas land as has not been acquired by a notification under sub-section (2) of section 3.

Tofazzal Hossain Vs. Prov. of East Pak. (1963) 15 DLR (SC) 39.

 

—The cumulative effect of clauses (a) and (I) of sub-section (4) of section 3 on khas lands of rent- receivers is that on and from the date specified in a notification under sub-section (1) all khas land hitherto held by them in their capacity as rent-receivers, that is, as proprietors, tenure-holder, etc. shall with effect from the date, be held by them in a different capacity, namely, as tenants directly under the provincial Government on payment of the rent determined for the khas lands under section 5.

Al-haj Kutubuddin Ahmed Vs. Haridwan (1962) 14 DLR 128.

 

S. 3(4)(e)—After acquisition of rent-receiving interest there will be only one class of tenants, namely, the raiyats, under the Government. There being no under-raiyats, they shall be up-graded as raiyats.

Rafiqul Islam Vs. Kazi Taibur Rahman (1966) 18 DLR 475.

—Also see Sm. Lalita Roy Vs. Rafiqullah Khan (1966) 18 DLR 197.

 

—Clause(e) of section 3(4) of East Bengal State Acquisition and Tenancy Act secures the actual tenant in possession of his land as a tenant directly under the government and the omission of the words “other than sub-tenancies” by Ordinance No. Ill of 1956 has not the effect of nullifying clause (e).

Ali Haider Khan Vs. Prov. of E. Pak. (1958) 10 DLR (SC) 80.

In the absence of attachment under a decree for arrears of rents, the rent-receiver cannot be thought to possess any special interest in the land merely through accrual of arrears of rent in his favour. Ibid.

 

S. 3(4)(e)(f)—Tenants’ interests were auction-sold to the plaintiff and encumbrances annulled and decree for khas possess ion was passed on 24.7.52—Execution proceeding for delivery of possession started on 24.7.53 which continued when on 1.5.54 Government acquired the rent-receiving interests—Tenants-judgment-debtors’ contention that with effect from 1.5.54 they became tenants under the Government is untenable—Plaintiff became the owner of the tenants’ interests right from 24.7.53. Execution proceeding cannot affect plaintiffs right— Government acquired rent-receiving interest but not the decrees which he obtained for khas possession before acquisition. Plaintiff became the tenant under the government from 1.5.54 in respect of lands decreed in his favour.

Tayabullah Sowdagar Vs. Shiddique Ahmed (1961)13 DLR 610.

 

Tenant under the Government—Effect of these clauses and of section 5.

There is no procedure laid down in the Act as to how possession of acquirable interests is to be taken.

The effect of provisions embodied in clauses (c) and (I) of section 3 (4) as well as of section 5 of the Act is that on and from the date mentioned in a notification under sub-section (1) of section 3 the Provincial Government became entitled to collect rent from the tenants of the rent-receivers in question and on and from the date mentioned in a notification under sub-section (2) the rent-receiver becomes tenant under the Provincial Government in respect of the retainable khas lands.

Jalil Ahmed Vs. Province of East Pakistan (1967) 19 DLR 106.

 

S. 3(4)(ff) read with sec. 19(3)

After final determination of rent u/s. 19(3) every tenant is liable to pay enhanced to (if any) under clause (ff) of s. 3(4a).

In this connection reference may be made to the provisions of clause (f) of sub-section (4) of section 3 of the State Acquisition and Tenancy Act which provides for the obligation of every owner of any land to pay rent to the Government at the enhanced rate, if any, after the final determination of the rent under sub-section (3) of section 19 of the State Acquisition & Tenancy Act and in such case the tenant in question shall be liable to pay the balance with retrospective effect from the notified date, that is, the date of final publication of the record of rights under sub-section (3) of section 19 of the State Acquisition and Tenancy Act.

Chandpur Tea Company Ltd. Vs. Joint Secretary & ors. (1980) 32 DLR 130.

 

—Balance dues finally determined and finally published tinder sec. 19(3), recoverable with retrospective effect.

Thus it will appear that once the rate of rent is finally determined and published in the final record- of-right under sub-section(3) of section 19 of the State Acquisition and Tenancy Act, any balance dues there under can be recoverable with retrospective effect from the date of such final publication of the record-of-right under sub-section(3) of section 19 of the State Acquisition and Tenancy Act. Ibid.

 

—For prospective realisation of rent at the final rate. limitation shall run from date of final publication of record-of-rights under sec. 19(3) which, in case of Sylhet district, and in view of section 3(4a) read with s. 142 shall be 5 years. Ibid.

 

S. 3(15)—Land with buildings on and let out is ‘khas land’ and therefore retainable as khas land.

The act of the Government in excluding these premises along with the other premises by the Notification of 11th August, 1952 clearly shows that these were treated as retainable khas land of the plaintiff. “Khas land” has been defined in sub-section (15) of section 2 of the State Acquisition Act and reads as follows:

“(15) “Khas land” or “land in khas possession”, in relation to any person, includes any land let out together with any building standing thereon and necessary adjuncts thereto, otherwise than in perpetuity.”

In this ease there is ample evidence to show that the land in question had the structures on it and the plaintiff was realizing rent and the lease was not a lease in perpetuity.

Sudhir Chandra Das Vs. Hatem Bepari (1968) 20 DLR 627.

 

Revenue authority’s decision on question of title to land—Not binding on a Civil Court.

It is true that there is a finding in favour of the defendant but then it remains a finding of a Settlement Officer and is subject to findings of a Civil Court. The point for determination before the Settlement Officer would be actually who is in possession. On the question as to who was the owner of the premises the decision of the Settlement Officer cannot be final and binding on a Civil Court.

Sudhir Chandra Das Vs. Hatem Bepari (1968) 20 DLR 627.

 

S.3A—Where complaint was made by ex.- proprietors of illegal acquisition of hats and jalkars by Government and taking possession of all connected papers and not producing them before authorities for determining correct compensation:

Held: Where the petitioner did not even submit a return when called upon to do so, nor asked for the inspection of the papers in possession of Government or for copies thereof the petitioner is not entitled to any relief in the absence of any evidence either oral or documentary. The burden of proving the claim to enhanced compensation lies entirely on him. (1957) 9 PLR (Dac) 766.

 

—In s. 3A reference has been made as to acquisition of khas lands in addition to rent-receiving interest before preparation of record of rights. This also refers to notice to be served on the rent-receivers before acquisition u/s 3A directing the rent-receivers to submit returns and particulars as may be required by the Revenue Officer.

Jadu Nath Roy Vs. Province of East Bengal (1961) 13 DLR 496.

S. 6A—Property can be saved from acquisition by creation of charitable and religious endowment.

 

S. 6A(1) — read with notification No. 7463 LR dated 9-5-59 lays down that if the grantor had anticipated that his properties might be eventually acquired it was within his right to give such properties to charity and to religious purposes instead of allowing the same to be acquired by the Government in future.

Kishti Dhar Roy Vs. Prov. of E Pak. (1962) 14 DLR 680.
