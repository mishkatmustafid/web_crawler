5.69—Decrees which cannot be executed and Court’s duty.

In view of the provisions of section 69 of the Act directing the Courts not to execute certain kinds of decrees, the amount of which under the Act is liable to be scaled down, the Courts are bound to stay their hands as soon as their attention is drawn to them. No estoppel can operate against the provisions of a statute. The failure of the judgment-debtor to raise the objection under section 69 of the Act in his petition of objection which he filed earlier cannot operate as a bar to the Court giving effect to these provisions when its attention is drawn subsequently.

Jinendra Ch. Mazumdar Vs. Dhirendra Ch. Saha Roy (1956) 8 DLR 170.

 

—Court must stop executing a decree against a rent- receiver as soon as it is brought to its notice that the judgment-debtor is a rent-receiver, irrespective of the plea that the judgment-debtor by his own conduct is estopped from raising this plea before the Court inasmuch as so far as the Courts are concerned, there cannot be any estoppel against a statute.

Dhirendra N Datta Roy Vs. Sudhindra Chandra Chakraborty (1959)11 DLR 151.

 

—Sale barred in public interest and that in the interest of some persons— Difference.

If a Statute is passed barring Certain property to be put to sale by execution in the interest of public policy and not merely to benefit a particular class or individual, any order for the sale would be void, and the executing Court should refrain from putting it into execution. If, on the other hand, the law is introduced simply for the benefit of a particular class or individuals, a sale would be voidable only, and it might be that in such circumstances the matter would be res judicata by reason of judgment of the Court which granted the decree.

Dhirendra N Datta Roy Vs. Sudhindra Chandra Chakraborty (1959) 11 DLR 151.

 

—Orders passed are appealable.

Orders passed under section 69 of the Act, either refusing or granting stay of execution proceeding, come under section 47, C.P.C., and are appealable.

Radhika Mohan Munshi Vs. Shama Kishore Munshi (1954) 6 DLR. 531.

 

—Even before the properties arc acquired or compensation money is paid, as soon as the East Bengal State Acquisition and Tenancy Act came into force it was open to the Civil Court to stay the execution of the decree under sec Lion 69 of the Act.

Birendra Ch. Das Vs. Naresh Ch. Bhattacharyya (1955) 7 DLR 339.

 

—The legislature has provided in section 69 of the Act that after the commencement of the Act no Court shall entertain any suit, etc. In suits instituted before the commencement of the Act the Court will look into the matter in a prospective sense, and decide whether it could still maintain the suit; the Court will, provided other requisites are fulfilled, stay its hand and stop the whole process, and suspend the progress of the suit, and may, after finding that it is not entertainable, return the plaint or keep it in the file during the interregnum period.

Monmotho N Poddar V.s. Tripura State Bank (1959) 11 DLR 204.

 

—Entertainment of the suit – A  question of procedure.

The question of entertainment of the Suit in certain contingencies is a question of procedure, and section 69 of the Act was enacted by the Legislature to regulate the procedure. It may be that the debtors might acquire some rights incidentally on account of the law enjoining to regulate that procedure but the main question is how and in what circumstances, the Court will entertain the suit and dispose of it. This is eventually a question of procedure, and in this view also the amendment to section 70 by the amending Act IV of 1956 must be taken in a prospective sense.

Monmotho N Poddar Vs. Tripura State Bank (1959) 11 DLR 204.

 

—Orders staying or refusing to stay execution proceedings—Appeal lies.

An order staying or refusing to stay execution proceedings under section 69 is an order which comes under section 47 of the Civil Procedure Code and as such appealable. 5 PLR(Dac.) 267.

—Section 69(1) of the Act prohibits the entertainment of any suit or execution of any decree or order against any property of any rent-receiver for the recovery of any debt which is liable to be scaled down under section 70. Such prohibition is to last until all the interests of the rent-receiver which are liable to be acquired have been so acquired and compensation for the acquisition paid.

Radhika M. Munshi Vs. Shama Kishore Munshi (1954) 6 DLR 531.

 

—The section lays down three requisites; firstly, the section would have application in suits brought after the commencement of the Act (16.5.5 1), secondly, it would apply in the case of a rent-receiver; and for debts which were liable to be scaled down under sec. 70 of the Act; and, thirdly, no Civil Court would entertain a suit or execute a decree or order if it was brought to the notice of that Court that the debtor was a rent-receiver, and his debts were liable to be scaled down.
