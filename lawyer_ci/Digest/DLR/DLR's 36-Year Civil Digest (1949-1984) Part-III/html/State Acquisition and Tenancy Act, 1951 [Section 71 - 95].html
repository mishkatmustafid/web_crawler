Surendra Nath Vs. Saryandra Nath Halder (1984) 36 DLR 163.

There is no inconsistency between the provision of section 75A of the State Acquisition and Tenancy Act and those of the Premises Rent Control Ordinance, 1963 which provides for letting out of premises on monthly basis.

Ibid.

—When not applicable—Letting Out of land along with building thereon on a temporary basis is outside the scope of sec. 75A—The land continues to be in the khas possession of the lessor and as such he is not a rent-receiver. Ibid.

 

—Kabuliyat (creating a tenancy) executed after coming into force of section 75A is hit by its provision.

Before the High Court question arose, whether the “Kabuliyat” executed after coming into force of section 75A of the Act was hit by its provision and, on the other hand, whether the Suit was barred by estoppel.

Held: The “Kabuliyat” is null and void being hit by the provision of section 75A of the Act. Section 75A being a statute there can be no estoppel against a statute under certain Circumstances as in the present case where the “Kabuliyat” is shown to be tainted with illegality.

Sree Sudhir Chandra Saha Vs Heirs of late Jan Mamud Sirker (1969) 21 DLR 429.

 

—Contract for lease exclude before section 75A was enacted—Contract void and unenforceable.

The plaintiff’s contract of lease having been made prior to the coming into operation of section 75A was lawful at the time when it was made. But the object of that contract i.e., the demise of the Suit properties being now prohibited under section 75A—in this  Section has rendered the object of the plaintiff’s contract of lease unlawful and thereby made the contract itself void.

Further, the doctrine of frustration coming within the purview of the second paragraph of section 56 of the Contract Act comes into play with the result that the plaintiff’s contract of lease has become void and unenforceable also under the provision of section 56, paragraph second of the Contract Act.

Al-Haj Kutubuddin Ahmed Vs. Abu Jafar Hridwan Uddin Ahmed (1962) 14 DLR 128.

 

—An executory contract does not create a vested interest—Due to the operation of section 75A, section 56 of the Contract Act renders it unenforceable.

The question to be considered is whether it is necessary that right acquired by the plaintiff under his con tract of lease may properly be called a vested right.

The contract of lease in question is a mere executory contract not a deed affecting an actual demise. Therefore the right acquired by the plaintiff under the contract is only a contractual right to have a lease of the suit properties on fulfillment of the terms mentioned therein but is not a completed and accomplished right so as to be termed as vested right.

When no vested right accrued to the plaintiff under the contract of lease that contract falls within the mischief of the aforesaid section 75A and is, therefore, hit by section 56 of the Contract Act. Ibid.

 

—Sub-lettings—not illegal

Sub—letting connotes the creation of a subordinate tenancy or under raiyati, under which the sub— lessee would but for a special contract be liable to pay rent for that land to a person other than the Provincial Government. Section 75A was intended to prevent this eventuality.

“Sub-letting” in this section should, therefore, be construed strictly and unless the transaction sought to be invalidated comes strictly within the ambit of sub-letting in this strict sense, it should not be brought within the mischief of this section.

Government of East Pak. Vs. Md. Hossain (1964) 16 DLR (SC) 667.

—Purpose of the section—The intention of this section appears to be that since after the acquisition of the interest of rent-receivers, rent- receivers were themselves to retain certain classes of retainable khas land as tenants directly under the Government, they should not be allowed to create further sub-tenancies under them.   Ibid.

 

—Does not invalidate leases which were effected before the notification under section 17(3) or 3 1(1).

Section 75A, though it came into force on the 30th August, 1954, was made operative only fromthe date of the notification under section 17(3) or section 31) and, as such, it ‘baa no retrospective operation and did not invalidate leases created before such date. The provisions of sections 9 to 9D would still continue to govern such cases.

All that happened after the publication of the notification under section 17(3) was that the authority prescribed under section 9 could no longer permit a transfer by lease.

The view that even in the case of a lease created after the issue of notification under section 17(3) which was effected on the 16th December. 1955, the provisions of section 9A to 9B should have been resorted for effecting a forfeiture of the leased land cannot be accepted as sound: This would render the provisions of sub—section (2) of section 75A wholly nugatory.

Government of East Pak. Vs. Md. Hossain (1964) 16 DLR (SC) 667.

—No conflict— There is no real conflict between the provisions of sec Lions 75A and 9 to 9D of the Act and the two can well stand together. Ibid.

Debaranjan Sen Vs. Md. Abdus Sabur & Ors. (1979) 31 DLR (AD) 109.

S. 81(2)(3)—Nadabipatra not a deed of transfer—No pre-emption.

A nadabipalra being merely a deed of disclaimer disclaiming any interest in the properties transferred by an earlier sale-deed is not itself a deed of transfer and, therefore, no right of pre-emotion can be claimed upon the registration of such a deed of nadabipatra.

Simply the fact that the document was registered and ad valorem stamp-fee was paid on the document—that by itself cannot turn a deed of disclaimer into a deed of transfer. (1959) 11 DLR 539.

—Position of tenants of non-agricultural lands under sec. 81A falls under two categories referred to in sub-sections (1) and (2) of sec. 81A. Their positions as referred to in sub-sections (1) and (2) explained.

According to the section 81A, such tenants have been divided into two categories, namely, (1) those who have become tenants under the Government because of the acquisition of the superior interest of their landlords by the Government under the Act and were governed by the provisions of the East Bengal Non-Agricultural Tenancy Act at the time of the acquisition of the superior rights over the said land, and (2) other non-agricultural tenants. S.86—Tenant’s right in his diluviated land subsists for 20 years—In case where this period is over he can still save his right by payment of rent.

Right, title and interest of a tenant in his diluviated land subsists during the period of diluvion not exceeding 20 years and he is entitled to immediate possession of the same on reappearance of such land within 20 years of diluvion of the lands irrespective of the question whether any abatement of rent has been taken for the lands or not. Under the provisions of the East Bengal State Acquisition Act, the tenant will be entitled to possession of the land, subject to the limitation regarding total area that can be possessed under section 20 or 90 of the State Acquisition Act. But if the land reappears beyond 20 years after diluvion then he cannot claim right to the land as a matter of course. It is, however, conceivable that a tenant may contrive to have subsisting interesting such land irrespective of the extent of the period of submergence of the land if he has continued to pay the entire rents and the same has been accepted or realised.

Abdur Rahman Gazi Vs. QM Rahman (1969) 21 DLR 192.

S. 87—The provisions of the section which occur in Part V and which deal with incidents of a holding are no: retrospective in effect.

With the acquisition of all rent-receiving interests under the State Acquisition Act on the 14th April, 1956, the plaintiffs in this case became raiyats under the Government but the incidents of their holding continued to be governed by the provisions of the Bengal Tenancy Act which Act in view of section 80 of the State Acquisition Act, stood repealed only with effect from the date of coming into operation of Part V of the latter Act, that is, with effect from 1.8.1963 on which date the said part was applied to the district of Chittagong.

Chapter XIII which includes section 87 of E.B. State Acquisition Act deals with incidents of holding of ralyats as defined in section 89(2). The Bengal Tenancy Act deals with the incidents of occupancy and other holdings. The provisions of these two Acts were not intended to be operative simultaneously. As the Bengal Tenancy Act was kept alive till before 1.8.1963, the clear implication is that Chapter XIII including section 87 were intended to be operative from the date of application of Part V of the State Acquisition Act by a notification under section 79 thereof and not from an earlier date. Thus, the said section 87 which is a substantive law is not retrospective in operation by implication.

Province of East Pakistan Vs. Imam Sharif (1966) 18 DLR 276.

S. 95—A ralyat can only enter into a complete usufructuary mortgage as defined in s. 2(6) of the Act.

Section 95 of the Act provides that a raiyat could not enter into any form of usufructuary mortgage other than the complete usufructuary mortgage as defined in section 2(6) of the Act and every such complete usufructuary mortgage is subject to the limitation of section 90. The State Acquisition and Tenancy Act placed embargo that “notwithstanding anything contained in any law for the time being in force a raiyat shall not enter into any form of usufructuary mortgage other than complete usufructuary mortgage”. The Transfer of Property Act so far as mortgage of a raiyat-holding is concerned is thus displaced.

The amendment was made by President’s Order No.88 of 1972 and section 95A was inserted by the same President’s Order. Section 95A provides that in any transaction when the transferor receives from the transferee any consideration, and transferee acquires the right to possess and enjoy the usufruct of such holding, etc. for a specified period in lieu of such consideration, then such transfer shall be deemed to be a complete usufructuary mortgage for not exceeding 7 years and the provisions of section 95(4) and (5) will apply for restoration of possession.

Bangladesh Vs. Haji Abdul Gani Biswas and others (1980) 32 DLR (AD) 233.

—The Supreme Court (Appellate-side) has, on the interpretation of section 95 and 95A of the State Acquisition and Tenancy Act, reversed the earlier decision of High Court Division on precisely the same point [see (1974) 26 DLR page 394]. Due to the importance of the point at issue the head-notes of that case is reproduced below. The head-notes run as follows:

Transfer of a holding or part thereof by a raiyat by way of out and out sale with an agreement of to reconvey shall be deemed a complete usufructuary mortgage for maximum 7 years. So also when a transferor received consideration from the transferee who, on the basis thereof, possesses and enjoys the usufruct of the land—that shall be treated a complete usufructuary mortgage. To both transactions section 95(4)(5) shall apply. Provisions of the Transfer of Property Act shall not apply to such kinds of transactions.

Transactions subsisting when P0 88/72 was promulgated are hit by s. 95A save and except those which were not alive when P0 88172 was promulgated, being transactions past and closed. Ibid.

S. 95A—Introduced by PO No. 24 of 1973 is discriminatory and as such unconstitutional.

Section 95A of E.B. Act XXVIII of 1951 affects only one class of persons, viz, agricultural tenants: the law providing for extinction of interest of such tenants acquired on the basis of a transaction of an out and out sale with agreement to reconvey, but without affecting other class of people must be considered discriminatory and violative of the fundamental right guaranteed under Article 27 of the Constitution when there appears to be no rational basis of this classification.

The law regulating the right of an agricultural tenant to mortgage his land may not be considered to be discriminatory inasmuch as indebted agricultural tenants who constitute poorer section of the people may be regarded as a class apart for which there appears to be a rational basis, having a just and reasonable relation to the object of the legislation. But the position of such a person with regard to the land which he has acquired on the basis of a deed of out and out sale with an agreement to reconvey, has no unique distinction providing a reasonable basis for classification for the purpose of expropriation.

Bangladesh Vs. Haji Abdul Gani Biswas (1980) 32 DLR (AD) 233.

—The present law regarding usufructuary mortgage was enacted to protect the helpless raiyat. Ibid.

—The legislature in section 95 provided that the raiyat shall not enter into any form of usufructuary mortgages other than the complete usufructuary mortgage. The legislature also prohibited a contract of Out and out sale with a clause of reconveyance and by a deeming clause had made such transaction as complete usufructuary mortgage. In other words, in Bangladesh a raiyat cannot enter into any other transaction of his raiyat holding except a complete usufructuary mortgage. Ibid.

—After an enjoyment of the usufruct for a maximum period of 7 years u/s.95A the raiyat has the right to get back the property as provided in s. 95. Ibid.

—Since 1928 recourse was taken of the deeming provision by the legislature and all sorts of mortgages, usufructuary mortgages, mortgages by conditional sale, anomalous mortgages have been displaced by providing that all such mortgages by a raiyat “shall be deemed to be a complete usufructuary mortgage” for which a definition was given in the Bengal Tenancy Act. Ibid.

—Transaction entered into with raiyat, by way of mortgage is not governed by the Transfer of Property Act but must be deemed a complete usufructuary mortgage as defined in the Bengal Tenancy Act.

The High Court Division has fallen into error by construing the expression “transfer” in the context of the Transfer of Property Act. Since 1928 the Legislature wanted to take out the transaction of the raiyat from the purview of Transfer of Property Act for which specific definition was supplied, namely, “complete usufructuary mortgage” in the Bengal Tenancy Act.

So far as the complete usufructuary mortgages are concerned with its meaning as given by the law there is no question of vested interest.

The High Court Division observed that the vested right of the property should not be extinguished “except in the manner as provided in the second part of Article 42(1) or 47(1)”. There is, however, no question of vested right because the complete usufructuary mortgage presupposes the restoration of land to the transferor either by redemption or by efflux of time whichever is earlier.

A transferee enjoyed the rights during these years before redemption or restoration and the transaction known in vernacular is “Khai Khalashi’ for which both the principal and interest are liquidated. Therefore, the question of vested right does not arise, nor can it be said that the raiyat gets it as a windfall because, after all, the property is his and he had to transfer it as complete usufructuary mortgage or “Khai Khalashi” in order to obtain loan. Ibid.
