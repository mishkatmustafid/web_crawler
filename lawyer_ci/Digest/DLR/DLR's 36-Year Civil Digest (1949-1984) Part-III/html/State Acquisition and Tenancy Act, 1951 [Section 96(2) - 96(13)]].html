State Acquisition and Tenancy Act, 1951

 

S. 96(2)—Parties to be impleaded in the application.

This sub-section in plain language provides that when an application for pre-emption under subsection(I) is made by one or more co-sharer tenants, all other co-sharer tenants of the holding shall be made parties but when the application is filed by a tenant claiming to be the owner of contiguous land to the land transferred, all co-sharer tenants plus all the tenants holding lands contiguous to the land sought to be pre-empted are to be made parties.

Akhtarun Nessa Vs. Habibullah (1979) 31 DLR (AD) 88.

 

—When lands consisting of several plots are transferred—Right of tenant or tenants holding land contiguous to the hind of particular plot or plots.

If lands consisting of several plots are transferred by a kabala, tenant or tenants holding land contiguous to the land of particular plot or plots are entitled to pre-empt only the land or land to which they have contiguous lands. In such a case the preemptor or pre-emptor are required to implead all the co-sharer tenants’ o1 the holding and all the tenants holding lands contiguous to the land transferred and the transferee.

Pre-emptor having been found to be a tenant holding land contiguous to only two plots out of three plots transferred, was entitled to pre-emptor only the land to which he holds land contiguous thereto.

Akhtarun Nessa Vs. Habibullah (1979) 31 DLR (AD) 88.

 

—The matter of impleading of parties in a partition suit is governed by Order I, rule 10 C P Coue while that of impleading of parties in a pre-emption case under SAT Act is governed by section 96(2) of this Act.—When impleading of parties in a preemption proceeding not necessary.

Md. Abdul Jalil Vs. Durjan Ali (1981) 33 DLR (AD) 269.

 

—A co-sharer in the holding cannot be allowed to purchase the entire land without impleading the other co-sharers and thereby depriving them from getting their share.

Abdus Samad Vs. Sohrab Ali (1981) 33 (AD) 114.

 

—Presence of all the persons mentioned in subsection (2) is a matter of absolute necessity. Their presence is necessary, so that the Court can adjudicate the claim of pre-emption more effectively and completely.

Abdus Samad Vs. Sohrab Ali (1981) 33 (AD) 114.

 

—Sub-section (2) has two parts,-Co-sharer applying for pre-emption need not implead contiguous land-holders as parties, but a contiguous land owner applying for pre-emption must implead other contiguous land holders as well.

—In a pre-emption application who are to be impleaded as parties.

The different provisions of section 96 clearly point out that a duty has been cast on the preemptor, if he is a co-sharer tenant, to make all the remaining co-sharers tenants; and if he is a tenant holding land contiguous to the land transferred, all the remaining tenants holding lands contiguous to the land transferred and the transferee, arc parties to the application. Object of sub-section (2) is to have all the co-sharer tenants or all the tenants holding lands contiguous to the land transferred, before the Court for proper and complete adjudication in accordance with law.

Abdus Samad Vs. Md. Sohrab Ali (1981) 33 DLR (AD) 113.

—As regard impleading of parties in a suit for partition as well as in an application for pre-emption u/s. 96 both are not governed by the same principles of law—In partition Suit all co-sharers are necessary parties, failure to implead any one of such co-sharers will render the suit in fructuous and apart from the question of leaving out a necessary party a court will not pass a decree in a suit (as in a partition suit) which will be rendered infructuous—General principle that objection as to impleading of parties at the proper time is also applicable in partition Suit.

—Impleading of all the co-sharer tenants and tenants holding land contiguous to the land transferred in the pre-emption case mandatory.

Under the law of pre-emption a person is allowed to take away the properties of another person although the latter acquired the said properties under a valid and legal transaction, all the provisions of the said law would be construed very strictly. On a proper and careful analysis it is clear that the provisions as laid down in sub-section (2) of section 96 that all the co-sharer tenants of the holding and all the tenants holding land contiguous to the land transferred shall be made parties are mandatory and not directory irrespective of the provision made in proviso to sub-section (I) of section 96. If we read this provision of sub-section (2) together with that made in sub-sections (4),(5)(b), (6)(b) and (7) of section 96 it appears that the intention of the Legislature to make it mandatory in this regard is very clear.

Suit for pre-emption not abates if unnecessary parties are not substituted.

After the filing of the application for preemption and lapse of statutory period of limitation to pray for rateable pre-emption the co-sharer tenants and other parties concerned become unnecessary parties and as such rule cannot be affected for not substituting the heirs.

Binodini Dasi Vs. Matilal Sikdar (1969) 21 DLR 262. [Reversed by 28 DLR (AD) 5]

S. 96(5)(a)—Clause (a) of sub-section (5) shows that law recognizes 3 classes of pre-emptor:

(i) a co-sharer tenant whose interest has accrued by inheritance;

(ii)   a co-sharer tenant whose interest has accrued by purchases; and

(iii) a tenant or tenants holding land contiguous to the land transferred.

The statute gives priority to the co-sharer tenant whose interest has accrued by inheritance. The first class excludes both second and third class, and the second class, excluding the third. Clause (a) provides necessary guidance for determining order of priority among the tenants claiming pre-emption on the ground of having lands transferred.

Akhtarun Nessa Vs. Habibullah (1979) 31 DLR (AD) 88.

 

S. 96(5)(b)—Pre-emption under 2nd part of s. 96(I)—Contiguity of land—According to subsection (5)(b) in an application for pre-emption only those tenants holding lands contiguous to the land sought to be pre-empted are to be impleaded; and tenants holding lands contiguous to other lands transferred under the kabala but not subject of preemption arc not required to be empleaded. Subsection (5)(b) removes any doubt that there might be.

Akhtarun Nessa Vs. Habibullah (1979) 31 DLR (AD) 88.

 

—Extent of contiguity of land—Test for determining order of priority—Right regarding contiguous plots u/s 96(5)(b).

“Extent of contiguity of land” is the only test for determining the order of priority as amongst tenants claiming pre-emption on the ground of holding land, contiguous to the land transferred. If land transferred consists of one plot, all the tenants having land contiguous to the land under pre-emption are to be impleaded. Their right is to be determined in the manner as provided in sub-section (5)(b). In a case where land transferred consists of more than one plot, a tenant or tenants having land contiguous to the plot or plots may apply for pre-emption but in such a case, a tenant having land contiguous only to one of the plots, cannot claim pre-emption of other lands. Ibid

 

S. 96(5)—Pre-emption not to be refused for non-joinder of  co-sharers.

A case of pre-emption under section 96 may not be defeated on the analogy of non-joinder of all co sharers in a suit for partition. This right of preemption is not absolute but subject to the right of other co-sharers and holders of land contiguous to the land transferred, as the case may be. Requirement of the statute of impleading those in whose favour right of pre-emption accrued, is for the sole purpose of enabling the Court to adjudicate the claim of all the parties as provided under sub-section (5) of section 96 in one proceeding and avoiding multiplicity of proceedings. So, for the sake of full and complete adjudication of the rights and granting relief, there should not be absence of any necessary party.

Objection as to omission of a necessary party, if not taken at the earlier opportunity, will be treated as waived. Ibid.

 

S. 96(5), Clause(a)—Co-sharer by inheritance shall have the highest priority followed by a co-sharer by purchase and thereafter a contiguous owner.

Co-sharer comes first in priority over a contiguous owner.

Dhirendra Narayan Das Vs. Gouranga Mohon Das (1982) 34 DLR 177.

 

—Co-sharer by inheritance has the highest priority to claim pre-emption in any holding.

Rafiqul Alam Vs. Rahimuddin Sarkar. (1982) 34 DLR 180.

 

S. 96(5)(b)—The words ‘contiguous’ and ‘contiguity in clause (b) of sub-s. (5) to he interpreted to mean touching or adjacent and not meaning neighboring.

Ahmed Hossain Vs. Basharat Ali (1980) 32 DLR (AD) 54.

—Clause (b) provides guidance regarding order of priority when more than one tenant holding contiguous land applies for pre-emption. Ibid.

—Priority of the applicants is to be determined having regard to (1) the total quantity of land in possession of each of the tenants; (ii) whether the contiguous laid of the tenant is homestead land or any other class of land; (iii) extent of contiguity; (iv) to what extent k is necessary for the applicant to have possession of the contiguous land; and (v) the right of easement, if any, of the applicants. Ibid.

—Clause (b) of sub-section (5) sufficiently indicates that pre-emptor must show that his land is contiguous to the land transferred, that is to say, it is touching with the land transferred. Only then he can maintain his claim, In the case of more than one tenant fulfilling the requirements to claim preernption, in determining the order of inter se priority one of the critena for consideration is “extent of contiguity.”   Ibid.

—When in between the land transferred and the piece of land whose owner seeks preemption on the ground of contiguity, a public pathway intervenes, the contiguity is broken and pre-emption can not be allowed. Ibid.

—Pre-emption case be allowed only with respect to that piece of land which is just contiguous to a transferred piece.

It is very often noticed that pre-emption under section 96 is allowed to a tenant having land contiguous to one of several plots transferred, and preemption is allowed even in cases where the lands are not in a compact block, which is contrary to the provision of law itself.

If more than one plot of lands are transferred by a sale deed, a tenant having land contiguous to one of the plots, cannot lay his claim of pre-emption beyond that plot, because, the contiguity cannot be stretched to other plot.   Ibid.

 

S. 96(7)—One of the several co-sharers is entitled to pre-emptor to the extent of his own share in a pre-emption case.

Eleven co-sharers claimed pre-emption in respect of the land transferred. The trial Court granted the prayer for pre-emption but the lower appellate Court dismissed the whole pre-emption case. As against this order of dismissal one out of eleven co sharers moved the High Court in revision. High Court upheld the order of the Munsif but held that the petitioner before the High Court is entitled to pre-empt only to the extent of his own share and nothing more.

Ruhul Amin Mestory Vs. Fazar Banu, (1969) 21 DLR 647.

 

—Nadabipatra not a deed of transfer—No preemption.

A nadabipatra being merely a deed of disclaimer disclaiming any interest in the properties transferred by an earlier sale deed is not itself a deed of transfer and, therefore, no right of pre-emption can be claimed upon the registration of such a deed of nadabipatra.

Md. Arabullah Vs. Durgaprasad Tribedi (1959) 11 DLR 539.

S.96(10)(a)—A co-sharer by gift is a co sharer whose interest has accrued ‘otherwise than by purchase’ and when such a co-sharer makes a subsequent purchase in respect of a portion of the same holding he is protected from pre-emption under section 26F(1)(a) of the BT Act.

Held: A distinction has been made under the provisions of section 26F of the Bengal Tenancy Act between a co-sharer by purchase and co-sharer whose existing interest has accrued otherwise than by purchase. In the latter category falls a co-sharer whose existing interest has accrued by gift. In view of the clear provision of sub-section (I) (a) of section 26F of the Bengal Tenancy Act, the petitioner is not entitled to get any relief because the opposite parties are co-sharers in the tenancy whose existing interest has accrued otherwise than by purchase, i.e., by gift in the instant case.

Mansuruddin Vs. Yeakub Ali (1969) 21 DLR 565.

—An order referred to in S. 96(13) which is made appealable does not fall within the definition of “decree”.

Under section 96(13) of the State Acquisition and Tenancy Act an appeal lies from any order under that section and as such in terms of the definition of decree, it shall not include an order under that section. Furthermore, an order under section 96 of the Act, although adjudicates a matter finally does not come within the meaning of ‘decree” as the definition of “order” makes it abundantly clear that a decision of the civil court, does not become a “decree” unless it falls within the definition of “decree”.
