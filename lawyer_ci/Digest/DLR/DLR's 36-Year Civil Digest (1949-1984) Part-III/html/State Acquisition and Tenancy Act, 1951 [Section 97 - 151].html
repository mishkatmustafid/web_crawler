Secs. 116 and 117— For amalgamation and consolidation of a holding steps may be taken on the application by co-sharer tenants or the RO may do so on his own initiative—But in case of subdivision of a joint tenancy it can only be done on application made by co-sharer tenants.

Md. Khairullah Bhuiyan. Vs. Amena Khatun & ors. (1981) 33 DLR 412. (Reversed by decision in 35 DLR (AD) 338).

—Words “separation” and “amalgamation” bear specific meaning and their implication explained.

Md. Khairullah Bhuiyan Vs. Haji Nurul Alam Chowdhury (1983) 35 DLR (AD) 338.

 

S. 116 and 117 (1)(a)(c)— Several plots (numbering 5) which were recorded in 4 khatians were amalgamated and consolidated in one khatian-This being a case u/s. I 17(1)(a) and not of sub-division of a joint tenancy as dealt with in section 1 17(1)(c)— Payment of rent at the old rate can not set at naught the amalgamation and consolidation—Civil court can not ignore that order to give effect to a collateral purpose.

Md. Khairullah Bhuiyan Vs. Haji Nurul Alam Chowdhury (1983) 35 DLR (AD) 338.

 

—Section 116 read with section 117(1)(a) of the Stale Acquisition and Tenancy Act provides that amalgamation and consolidation can be taken by the Revenue-Officer, either on his own motion or on an application made to him. Section 1 17(I)(c) contains the provision for sub-division of a joint tenancy for distribution of rent thereof.

High Court Division was in error in its view that it is a case of sub-division of a joint tenancy as prescribed u/s. 1 17(I)(a) for which an application was required to be filed and in the absence of such an application the Revenue Officer had no authority on his own motion to sub-divide the joint tenancy.

Md. Khairullah Bhuiyan Vs. Haji Nurul Alam Chowdhury. (1983) 35 DLR (AD) 338. (This decision reverses 33 DLR 412).

 

S. 117—In case of amalgamation, consolidation and sub-division of a tenancy Prior notice must be served on the parties concerned.

Khairullah Bhuiyan Vs. Amena Khatun (1981) 33 DLR 412.

 

—Order (effecting a sub-division of a holding) validly made by a competent authority and has been given effect to and further there has been no appeal against the order made u/s. 117—the order remains a validly legal order and a court of appeal while dealing with a matter u/s. 96 for pre-emption cannot ignore the order.

Md.. Jabed Ali Vs. Rakhal Chandra Mondal (1981)33 DLR (AD) 309.

 

—Sub-division of a holding effected after giving reasonable notice to the parties concerned to appear and be heard is complete. No pre-emption can be claimed after sub-division u/s. 117.

Md. Rais Ali Vs. Imam Hossain & ors. (1981) 33 DLR 318.

 

—In a mutation case for split up of a Jama notice of the mutation case not having been served on the co-sharers and mutation been effected behind their back; in law that is not valid splitting up of the Jaina and as such the co-sharers have not lost their right of pre-emption under sec. 24 of Act XXIII of 1949.

Md. Abdur Rouf & ors. Vs. Ahmuda Khalutz & ors. (1981) 33 DLR (AD) 323.

 

S. 117(I)(c)—”Sub-division” of a joint tenancy can only be effected on the application of co sharers and by the RO on his own motion—Notice on other co-sharers compulsory.

Khairulla Bhuiyan Vs. Nurul Alam (1983) 35 DLR (AD) 338.

 

—Co-sharers amicably splitting up their jama for facilitating payment of land revenue—A conclusion can be arrived at that the co—sharers have effected an amicable partition---In which case provision of s. 96 of SAT Act can not be invoked.

Haladhar Dutta Vs. Abdul Rob Chowdhury (1984) 36 DLR 193.

 

—“Separation” means sub-division.

The term ‘separation’ is not a legal term like “amalgamation” or “sub-division”; it is not used in section 116 or 117, but nevertheless it denotes, in general sense, separation of share from a joint- tenancy and falls under, and is governed by clause (c) of section 117.

Md. Khairullah Bhuiyan Vs. Haji Nurul Alam Chowdhury (1983) 35 DLR (AD) 338.

—With the separation from a joint holding other co-sharers cease to be Co-sharers.

Effect of separation of a share from the joint holding falls upon all co-sharers who, after separation, cease to be co-sharers of the original holding. A co-sharer after getting his share separated may get his portion constituted into a new tenancy or he may tag this share with his other land, if any, and if he does so, his case may be referred to as a case of separation and amalgamation for the sake of convenience, though the two terms are quite different. His former co-sharers are not interested in such amalgamation.  Ibid.

—For separation of a joint tenancy mandatory provisions as required under clause (c) of s. 117 (1) must be complied with—undivided share of a joint tenancy can not be amalgamated with another independent holding without first having it ‘separated’ under clause (c) of s. 117(I). Ibid.

—When a joint tenancy is split up by a RO, thereby taking away the right of the co-sharer to exercise his right of pre-emption—a court of law is fully competent in a pre-emption proceeding to see whether the splitting up order was made according to law as required by s. 117(I)(c). Ibid.

 

S. 117(a)(b)(c)—Provisions made for amalgamation, consolidation or sub-division of holding—Amalgamation or consolidation by revenue officer on his only motion or on application—Subdivision can be effected only on application by co sharer tenants.

Haladhar Dutta Vs. Abdul Rob Chowhdury (1984) 36 DLR 193.

S. 117(3)—Sub-division may be demarcated and shown on the cadastral survey map. Ibid.

 

S. 141—Right to apply for setting aside a sale under section 174 (3) Bengal Tenancy Act not affected after the introduction of Chapter V of the Acquisition Act which contains section 141.

The contention raised was that application under section 174(3) of the Bengal Tenancy Act for setting aside sale filed after the introduction of the Chapter V of the East Bengal Slate Acquisition and Tenancy Act is not maintainable and that an application for setting aside a sale after the introduction of Chapter V of the East Bengal State Acquisition and Tenancy Act can only be filed under the Public Demands Recovery Act as contemplated by section 141 of the Acquisition Act.

Held: The right of action given to a judgment- debtor to invoke the aid of section 174(3) has not been expressly repealed and cannot be also said to be repealed by necessary implication.

Sarat Chandra Somaddar Vs. Sumati Bala Haldar (1966) 18 DLR. 293.

—Section 141 has no application when the sale had taken place before the introduction of Chapter V of the Acquisition Act and when the demand for which the sale had taken place was not a public demand. Ibid.

 

Notice—Proceedings introducing Chapter V of the East Bengal State Acquisition Act is no notice to the Judgment-debtor whose land has been sold.

Proceedings taken as preliminary to the intro- due section of Chapter V of the Acquisition Act cannot be construed as notice to a Judgment-debtor in respect of a sale of a holding held for arrears of rent unless it is shown that the judgment-debtor was, as a matter of fact, aware of the proceedings. Ibid.

 

S. 142 read with section 3(4a) — Period of limitation for recovery of arrear rents is 5 years in Sylhet district.

Chandpur Tea Co. Ltd. Vs. Joint Secretary (1980) 32 DLR 130.

 

S. 143A—Scope of section 143A.

An application under section 143A of the State Acquisition and Tenancy Act is not a Suit for declaration of title but is a proceeding for rectification of mistakes in the record-of-right prepared on the basis of possession in the land in question. The scheme of the section shows the possession is the material question. This was a special provision and this section was inserted after section 143 by EP Ordinance VIII of 1965. It was, however, omitted after a few years by Ordinance No. LXIV of 1975.

Within the scope of section 143A, there is hardly any ground for the Custodian of the Enemy Property appellant to challenge the title of the plaintiff. The learned Judge found that the Custodian of Enemy Property had no document in his possession to claim the property as enemy property. Before the High Court Division no one appeared on behalf of the present Custodian. The learned Judge of the High Court Division having found the plaintiffs prima-facie title and clear possession of the case land, rightly reversed the decision of the lower appellate Court and restored that of the trial Court.

Assistant Custodian Vs. Bholanath Guha (1984) 36 DLR (AD) 79.

 

— The section confers a summary power on the Civil Court for the purpose of correcting mistakes.

Section 143A contemplates a summary power to be exercised by the Civil Court within the prescribed limits. The purpose of introducing this special provision is correcting mistakes.” The special provisions made in section 143A arc intended for a definite and limited purpose.

Abu Daud Vs. Ruhul Amin Sardar (1979)31 DLR 421.

—Addition of parties in a proceeding u/s. 143A as envisaged in Or. 1 r. 10(a), CP Code not allowable. Ibid.

 

S. 143A—A right of easement sought to be confirmed in a proceeding under section 143A is absolutely beyond the scope of investigation under this section.

Prayer in the form of declaratory relief not contemplated in a proceeding u/s. 143A.

Certain prayers have been made in declaratory form with regard to title of the petitioner. Section 143A does not contemplate such relief.

Abu Daud Vs. Ruhul Amin (1979) 31 DLR 421.

 

Section 143(a)—has authorized a person aggrieved by an order or omission of entry in the record-of-rights finally published under Chapter IV of the Act to file a suit for correction of such record- of-rights in the Civil Court. It is obvious, therefore, that an alternative right has now been provided for a detailed probe into the matter and for rectifying an erroneous entry in a record-of-rights.

Pijush Kanti Chowdhury Vs. Abdar Rashid (1971) 23 DLR 60.

 

Secs. 144 and 144A—Presumption u/s. 144A—When not possible.

No record-of-right which was finally published under sub-section (7) of section 144 of the EB State Acquisition and Tenancy Act having been adduced in evidence the question of presumption under section 144A of The East Bengal State Acquisition and Tenancy Act does not arise.

Akrab Ali Vs. Zahiruddin Kari (1978)30 DLR (SC) 81.

 

S. 149(4)—Cultivated or cultivable land which was previously Jalkar (or a part of it) but was not so when the whole-sale State Acquisition was introduced could not be treated as non-retainable khas land—Such raised land continues to form part of the old tenancy and should have been recorded in the name of the person with title and possession thereto.

Ijjat Ali Vs. Golapjan Khatun (1969) 21 DLR (Board of Revenue) 25.

 

S. 151(1)(2)—To a proceeding u/s. 96(T) pro- visions of section 14 of the Limitation Act are attracted in view of section 151 (1)(2) of the SAT Act.

Sub-s. (1) of s. 151, State Acquisition and Tenancy Act provides that the provisions of section 14 of the Limitation Act shall be applicable to all suits and applications referred to in Part V of the State Acquisition & Tenancy Act. In other words, as stated in sub-section (2) of section 151, subject to the provisions of the Limitation Act which are specifically made applicable by sub-s. (1) to all suits and applications dealt with in Part V of the Act all such suits and applications not instituted within time proscribed thereof in this part “shall be dismissed.”

Abdul Majid Vs. Sarina Begum (1982)34 DLR 150.
