S. 2(1)(2)—Trade mark for use in respect of a limited area—perfectly valid.

The words within the ‘Provinces and the Capital of the Federation” mean any place within the Provinces and the Capital of the Federation.

It is possible under the Act V of 1940 for a person to apply for the registration of a trade mark to be effective within a limited area if he is not interested in the trade in any other area.

M/S Crescent Pak Soap and Oil Mills Vs. Deputy Registrar of Trade Marks, Chittagong (1965)17 DLR (SC)163.

 

S. 2(1)(e)— Use of ‘Camel’ mark or design in one kind of product does not deprive another of its use as a mark or design unless it is proved that such will cause confusion in relation to a particular kind. of goods of long standing—By registration of a design of camel it cannot be said that none else can use it as a design.

Merely because the picture of a camel is incorporated as a part of a trade mark registered for other kinds of products, it cannot be said that thereby a person had acquired any proprietary right in the design of device of a camel even when used without any qualifying words or other embellishments.

Unless one can establish a proprietary right as to the mark or design of a camel by a sufficiently long user it cannot be said that it had become associated with the products manufactured by such a person and that if anyone else uses it with other qualifying features there would still be a chance of the customer or user being confused.

Simply because the design of a camel formed part of a mark registered earlier, it cannot be said that no one else can use that design or device as a part of its own trade mark coupled with other totally different words or designs in respect of goods of different nature.

M/S Crescent Pak Soap and Oil Mills Vs. Deputy Registrar of Trade Marks, Chittagong (1965 17 DLR (SC) 163.

 

S. 6—Proviso—Trade mark in continuous use for a long period—Seven years elapsed since original registration— Removal from Register cannot be allowed.

In the application for registration filed on 31.8. 1942 it is stated that the trade mark is in use since 1914 and on that representation the respondent was given the registration. Thus the defendant’s respondent (Sadhana Aushadhalaya) obtained registration of the trade mark claiming user of the same for a period which would entitle him to the protection of the provisions of the proviso to section 6 of the Trade Marks Act.

The defendant (Sadhana Aushadhalaya) cannot claim any exclusive right to the use of the word “Saribadi” nor can the defendant claim any right to the exclusive use of the word ‘Salsa’. The combination of the words “Saribadi Salsa” is a coining of the defendant company. The defendant company is entitled to the exclusive use of the coined words “Saribadi Salsa” also on the ground that it had been in continuous use by the defendant company since 1914, according to the provisions of the proviso to section 6. Further seven years have passed since the original registration of the mark and as such the defendant company is also entitled to the protection afforded by section 24 of the Trade Marks Act as no acceptable case has been made out of any fraud perpetrated by the defendant company in having the trade mark registered nor does the trade mark offend against the provisions of section 8 of the Trade Marks Act. (Abdullah J.)

Ss. 8 and 10(1)—Registrar’s discretion to. register trademarks with conditions imposed.

In a case where the Registrar finds himself unable to grant registration he may, in his discretion, permit registration of such a trade mark subject to such condition as he may consider. This is an enabling provision by which the Registrar in his discretion may grant registration by imposing condition. In doing so, he is to bear in mind the provisions of section 8 and section 10(1). He has also to remember as to whether the case in which such discretion is sought to be exercised in favour of the applicant it could claim honest concurrent use of the trade mark.

M/s. Zenith Laboratory Pak. Ltd. Vs. M/s. British Drug houses Ltd., England, (1968)20 DLR 1171.

 

Ss. 8 and 10—Words used in two different trademarks are different but their mode of representation similar—No bar to registration.

The appellant applied to the Deputy Registrar, Trade Marks for registration of his trade mark consisting of the words “P.M.S.’ printed in “a letter cross device within an encircling border.” The respondent claims that the appellant’s trademarks should not be registered as the respondent uses a trade mark consisting of the word “Bayer” in a world famous “letter-cross device” which had been registered earlier and although the two marks “P.M.S.” and “Bayer” are different but the mode of representing them is common. So the respondent’s contention was that this will confuse and deceive the buyers who may purchase the product of “PMS” as that of “Bayer”.

Held: In the absence of legal bar the Registrar is not entitled to impose a condition arbitrarily. He can impose such condition when it is necessary in view of the prohibitions imposed by the statute in respect of registration of trade mark. He can also refuse when reasons direct-him to do so and if an applicant finds that a condition has been illegally or unreasonably imposed on him, he is entitled to prefer an appeal to the High Court.

The trade mark “Bayer” consisting of live letters and that of “PMS” consisting of three letters are phonetically different and their visual resemblance is not such as would warrant imposition of a condition and there is no likelihood of the public being misled or confused. The two marks being clearly different, the appellant’s trade mark should be directed to be registered.

Pakistan Medical Supplies Vs. M/s. Farbenfabriken Bayer (1969) 21 DLR 945.

 

S. 8(a)— The words “Sagar” and “Singer” are two dissimilar words and will create no confusion if registered as trademarks.

Held: That the word “Singer” is an English word and word “Sagar” is a Bengali word and a Pakistani purchaser is not likely to confuse the Bengali word “Sagar” for the English word “Singer”. Regard being had to the nature of the customer, the article in question and the intelligence of the persons likely to purchase such machines, there can be no likelihood of confusion as between “Singer” and “Saga?’. Moreover, the ideas revealed by the two words are dissimilar. The English word “Singer” connotes one who sings and the Bengali word “Sagar” means “sea”.

The Singer Company Vs. Amjad Ali (1969) 21 DLR 415.

Where a Pakistan party had been using mark ‘Nabisco’ in Pakistan but objection was raised by a firm to the Registration of the trade mark on the ground that the foreign firm had that trade mark in the international market for a long time prior to the date from which the applicant had been using it though it had not marketed its goods in Pakistan.

Held: For the purposes of registration under the Act, the Tribunal should ordinarily consider the user in this country to which the Act applies. If the case of the appellant is considered on this view of the law, there is not a shred of evidence on the record to establish that the public or a common man in this country will in any way be deceived or confused while purchasing goods under the trade mark “Nabisco’ manufactured by the appellants. The respondents have no market of this trade mark in respect of any goods in this country. They have no reputation and as such there can possibly be no deception or confusion in the mind of the public f this country that the goods manufactured by the appellants belonged to the respondents. In these circumstances the respondents had not established a reputation for their mark upon which an opposition could be founded.

—Stay of suit—When may be granted on application under the sub-section.

It cannot be laid down as a general principle that whenever an application is made to the Registrar under section 10(2) of the Trade Marks Act, the Court must stay its hands to enable the party so applying to obtain an order from the Registrar. There may be cases in which an application for stay of the suit may be sought merely for the purpose of delaying the Suit by making an application to the Registrar under section 10(2). This application may be frivolous and devoid of any substance and may have been made after the institution of this suit. when the defendant may have found his position difficult and untenable. To stay the suit in this situation and to wait for the decision of the Registrar which may sometimes take years, would be denial of justice. It may also be noted that the decision by the Registrar in any case is not final and is subject to an appeld to the High Court. Then there may be cases which may involve the determination of other issues apart from those arising under section 10(2) which could only be tried by the Civil Court. The stay of the suit, therefore, cannot be granted as a matter of course merely upon the presentation of an application by the defendant before the Registrar particularly when that application is made after the institution of this suit. The question whether stay should be granted must be answered with reference to the facts of each case. It is a matter within the discretion of the Court and the discretion has to be exercised in the interest of justice. One of the considerations which may determine an exercise of that discretion is the time when the application for stay is made. Another factor to consider would be whether the application for stay is bona fide. Yet another consideration would be the nature of the case itself.

N. B. Philip Vs. Electric Lamp Manufactures of Pak. PLD 1962 Karachi 191 =PLR 1963(1) WP 510. (Faruqui, J).

 

—Trade name used for long time— Party acquires a right to make it its trade mark—Limitation placed on use of Trade Mark—When High Court may interfere with discretion of Registrar.

Shipton Tea Company applied for Registration of “Shipton Tea Company” as its Trade Mark, “Lipton Ltd.” objected. The Registrar holding that although the word “Shipton” had been coined with an ulterior motive yet since the Shipton Tea Co. had been using the name as its trading style for a number of years to the knowledge of Lipton without objection to it, accepted the application for the registration of the trade mark subject to conditions in the following terms.

The applicant’s mark to proceed to registration only after the words “Shipton Tea Company” within the circumference of the inner and outer circles have been deleted and the trading style given at the hooting of the various panels of the label is reduced so much in size as to make it lose its conspicuity by ceasing to catch the eye. Further that if and when these labels are printed, the trading style should not e shown in colour different from that of the rest of be descriptive matter.

Held: The exercise of the discretion by theRegistrar under sub-section 2 of section 10 of the Trade Marks Act, 1940 should not be interfered with without sufficient reasons for doing so. The reasons interference in this case were that the discretion I been exercised partly unreasonably and partly ‘ the effect of over reaching the limitations it had been created on the rights of Lipton Limby the acquisition of a right by Shipton Tea to freely use its trade name, The conditions imposed were unreasonable because they aimed snapping the connection between the goods and their manufacturer, or at least at creating an obscurity inconsistently with the object of a trade mark, which is to ensure easy identification of goods with reference to their origin. The limitations were over reached because, whereas Shipton Tea Co was entitled without registering its trade mark to freely use its trade name and to print it in any colour it liked on its goods, so long as its labels did not in any other respect infringe the trademarks of Lipton Ltd., after Its registration the name would be partly suppressed by its deletion from the most important place in the trade mark and partly obscured by the flattening effect of reduced size of panels of labels and turned down colour scheme.

Abdul Gani Vs. Registrar of Trade Marks PLD 1961 (WP) Karachi 158=1961 (1) KLR 29=PLR 1961 (2) WP 608. (Qadeeruddin I).

 

Ss. 12 and 13—Disclaimer—Trade mark ‘Saribadi Salsa’ is for the whole of it, and not for any part—No disclaimer as regards the use of ‘Saribadi’ is necessary

—The right to use it being open to all Sadhana Aushadhalaya (appellant) has a right of using the trade mark ‘Saiibadi Salsa’ to the exclusion of all others. The question is whether a disclaimer is necessary to declare that the Sadhana Aushadhalaya has no exclusive right to the use of the word ‘Saribadi’: The answer is Sadhana Aushadhalaya has no exclusive right to use the word ‘Saribadi.’ This is quite clear and Nutun Chandra Sinha (respondent) will not be prejudiced if such a disclaimer, as prayed for by him, is not added. A disclaimer is added to define a right arising out of registration in a doubtful case.

Even without such a disclaimer the respondent (Nutun Chandra Sinha) will be entitled to such rights as will be ensured by a disclaimer. On the other hand, its addition may give rise to an impression that a disclaimer is the remedy whenever someone seeks to make an extravagant claim. To create such a situation will be most undesirable commercially. (A. S. Chowdhury, J., Mahmud Hossain, J.)

Sadhana Aushadhalaya Vs. Nutun Chandra Sinha (1968) 20 DLR 92.

 

Ss. 13 and 24—Disclaimer can be entered into the register at any time—Does not affect the validity of the trade mark— Nor its right to remain on the register

The entry of a disclaimer on the Register does not necessarily affect the validity of the registration of the trade mark as contemplated by section 24 of the Trade Marks Act nor does it affect the right of the trade mark to remain on the register. Therefore, a disclaimer can always be entered on the register provided the circumstances in section 13 are fulfilled. (Abdullah, J.)

Ss. 14 and 15(1)— Application for registration of trade marks if on the face of it satisfies the authority—should not be refused without objection from any quarter.

The proviso to section 15 lays down that in a case of the present nature if it appears to him that it is expedient by reason of any exceptional circumstances, he may cause the application to be advertised before acceptance.

In the present case the application has been refused without any objection from any quarter. The power of refusal on an application before advertisement can be exercised only when the trade mark mark sought to be registered does not, on the face of it, satisfy any of the the conditions laid down in section 6 of the Act. The discretion vested under section 14 cannot be exercised arbitrarily.

Trade Marks, Chittagong Vs. M/s. Popular Polish House (1969) 21 DLR (SC) 114.

 

S. 15—Rules framed by the Government prescribing time limit, are enforceable.

Section 84 of the Trade Marks Act authorizes the Central Government to make rules for prescribing the times or periods required by the Act and section 15 merely says that the notice of objection must be filed within the time prescribed, which means prescribed by the Rules. Rules 30 to 35 prescribe the times within which the steps therein specified should be taken.

M/s. Crescent Pak Soap and Oil Mills Vs. Deputy Registrar of Trade Marks. Chittagong (1965) 17 DLR (SC) 163.

 

S. 16(1)—Objection as to the bar of limitation by lapse of time not being raised, the inference is that it is waived.

It was contended that since no objection was filed or evidence produced either within the time prescribed by the rules or in the prescribed manner the inference is there was no objection at all in the eye of the law to the application of the appellant and the same should, therefore, under section 16(1) have been accepted as a matter of course.

Held: This contention does not take into account the fact that the appellant not only did not protest before the Registrar of Trade Marks against the filing of the belated objection, but in fact waived this delay by filing his counter-statement. This amounted to submission to jurisdiction for, the provisions as to notice of opposition and filing of evidence are for the benefit of a party who may well choose to waive the irregularity and join issue on merits.

Ss. 46(4), 23—.--Trade mark duly registered—’Suo motu’ notice by Deputy Registrar under S. 46(4)—Burden of proof of similarity with another trade mark is on Deputy Registrar—Long use and initial registration are factors to be considered.

Under s. 23 the registration of a Trade Mark is prima-facie evidence that it was validly registered. The burden of proof for rebutting that presumption is on those who question its validity. In this case, the burden has been on the Deputy Registrar himself. He has discharged it by. pointing out phonetic similarity, while conceding that there was no obvious visual likeness. The fact that the similarity on which he has now relied was not initially noticed by him, is a proof against its obviousness. His view, therefore, that there is transparent phonetic similarity is not an adequate rebuttal of the statutory presumption. The weakness of the rebuttal becomes more clear when it is noticed that the trade mark has remained on the Register for about seven years and no consumer or trader thought of any deceptive similarity during this long period.

Bengal Oil Mills Ltd. Vs. Deputy Registrar of Trade Marks PLD 1963 (WP) Karachi 920. (Qadeeruddin Ahmad, J).
