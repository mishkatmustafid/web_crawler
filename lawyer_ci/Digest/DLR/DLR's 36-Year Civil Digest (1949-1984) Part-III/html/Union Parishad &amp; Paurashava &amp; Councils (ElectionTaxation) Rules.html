Union Councils and Town and Union Committees (Election of Chairman) Rules, 1965

 

Rules do not provide for obtaining consent of candidate nor provide procedure for withdrawal— Unwilling candidate cannot be considered to be a candidate—A man cannot be elected when he refuses to be so elected—Consent can be express or implied.

Mazharul Huq Vs. Additional Commissioner (1968)20 DLR 282.

 

Rule 3—Chairman’s election should be held without delay.

The entire scheme of the Rules with particular reference to rule 3 and rule 15 shows that the Legislature intended that the election of the Chairman 01 LISL he held at the earliest possible opportunity after the Union Council has been constituted under section 57 of the Electoral College Act and that when a Chairman has actually been elected, there should be no impediment in his way from taking over charge of the office of the Chairman from the outgoing.

Chairman, Md. Serajul Huq Vs. Sub Divisional Officer (1968)20 DLR 37

 

R. 5—Resignation of the Chairman accepted and thereafter another member elected as the Chairman. The fact that resignation was gazetted after the fresh election of the Chairman does not invalidate fresh election of the Chairman.

Shah Badrul Haque Vs. MR Chowdhury (1968) 20 DLR 556.

 

Rule 5 (I)—Where mark is clear for whom vote was cast, some irregularity makes no difference.

The form of voting paper which is prescribed under rule 5(l)is merely for the purpose of facilitating the voters to put their cross marks against the name of a particular candidate they are voting for and the rule does not make it obligatory that the cross mark must be put in column 2. Ii is sufficient compliance with the rule if the mark is clearly put against the name of candidate.

Taleb Ali Vs. Additional Commissioner Dacca (1967) 19 DLR 259.

 

Rr. 8 & 10—The Deputy Commissioner and Divisional Commissioner have jurisdiction to declare a candidate elected as chairman where there has been a failure of election due to contesting candidates getting equal number of votes. Ibid.

 

Rule 10—Validity of election of Chairman can be challenged before the Tribunal only.

Validity of election of Chairman can be challenged before the Tribunal only. Writ jurisdiction under Article 98 of the Constitution cannot be involved.

It is evident from the rules 10,11,12 and l3of the EP Union Councils and Town and Union Committees (Election of Chairman) Rules, 1965 that a particular forum has been established for the resolution of a dispute as to the validity of the election of the Chairman of the Union Council. IL is now a settled rule of law that if a statute or for the matter of that rules framed there under has created a special tribunal for the resolution of a particular dispute, ii is that Tribunal alone which has jurisdiction to decide the dispute. The petitioner having failed to approach the Collector under rule 10 of the Rules for a resolution of the dispute cannot maintain the present application under Article 98 of the Constitution.

Md. Serajul Haq Vs. Sub-Div. Officer (1968) 20 DLR 37.

 

Rule 11—Deputy Commissioner acts in quasi-judicial character—Should not take guidance from any quarter for disposing of an election petition—Decision should be in unambiguous terms.

We have no hesitation to say that the Deputy Commissioner who in the matter of disposal of an election petition was acting in a judicial capacity should not have under any circumstances taken any guidance from any communication made to him by way of clarification by the Government on the point. He should have acted independently according to his best judgment, knowledge, good conscience and the rules governing the matter. That the Deputy Commissioner acts at least in quasi-judicial character under rule 11 of the Rules is also admitted by the learned Advocate-General.

We would like to impress on the functionaries who are entrusted with election matters that decision should be given in clear and unambiguous terms touching upon each point involved in the case without leaving anything to be guessed or conjectured. This must be so as very important rights of citizens are involved in election matters.

Mazharul Huq Vs. Addi. Commr. (1963) 20 DLR 282.

Rule 15—(Sec under rule 3 in case of Md. Serajul Hoq Vs. Sub-divisional officer (1968) 20 DLR 37).

 

Union Councils (Taxation) Rules, 1960

 

R. 43A(2)—Union Council Lax—Companies and Corporations not entitled to the benefit of exemption provided in rule 43A(2).

The question was whether the word.” person” mentioned in the Government Order dated 16th July, 1966 passed in pursuance of sub-rule (2) of rule 43A of the East Pakistan Union Councils (Taxation) Rules, 1960, includes the petitioners (which arc Limited Companies) for extending benefit of the rule to them also to the effect that they, like an individual, are entitled to exemption of maximum payment of Rs. 500/ as union council tax.

Held: The order obviously means an individual though the word “person” was used and in the operative order the word “person” meant an individual, a natural person. The use of the word “person” in the said Govt. order may be an obvious slip or the word was inartistically used. The word “persons” has been used with the qualifying words “one” preceding it and therefore, in the Context it must be held that “person” in the order meant an individual or natural person and therefore the Companies and Corporations arc not entitled to the benefit of exemptions provided in rule 43A(2).

The two clauses in rule 43A refer to two different purposes and the use of different words “individual” and “person” must be deemed to have been employed to convey two different meanings. Interpreted in its proper context the exemption of maximum payment of Rs. 500/- in the former clause of the proviso to rule 43A seems to have been granted to any individual meaning natural person as opposed to anybody corporate or association of persons like the limited Company or Partnership.

Chittagong Jute Manufacturing Co. Ltd. Vs. Mohara Union Council (1970) 22 DLR 247.

 

Union Parishad (Election) Rules, 1976

Election Tribunals are given exclusive power to decide all matters relating to election disputes.

Md. Safar Ali Vs. SDO Sadar North (1977) 29 DLR 390.

 

Rule 16(3)—Proviso 11—When defect is not of substantial nature correction of the same allowed forthwith before the Returning Officer. The election can not be called in question for non- correction of such defect.

Mozammel Huq Vs. Bangladesh (1978) 30 DLR (SC) 306.

 

Rule 35—Ballot paper issued to voter unless it bears the official mark and the Polling Officer’s initial it shall be rejected, despite the contention that it was otherwise genuine.

Abdul Aziz Vs. Election Tribunal (1979) 31 DLR 140.

—It is true that a voter has no hand in taking the initial or the official seal on a ballot paper given to him but the Rules make a specific provision in order to be valid a ballot paper shall contain both the initial of the Polling Officer and the official mark. This procedure appears to have been prescribed to guard against any unfair means in ballot paper such as casting of a ballot paper which is not issued to a genuine voter. In view of this provision of the Rules, for the purpose of accepting a ballot paper as valid it must bear both the official mark and the initial of the Polling Officer and for the purpose of rejecting it as invalid absence of either the official mark or the initial of the Polling Officer will be sufficient. Ibid.

 

Rules 35 and 40(4)—Rule 35 speaks of the voting procedure to be observed by the Polling Officer, whereas rule 40 speaks of counting of votes. Sub-rule (4) of rule 40 is a direction to be followed by the Presiding Officer while counting votes.

Abdul Motin Vs. Election Tribunal (1979)31 DLR (AD) 331.

 

Rule 40(4)—It cannot be that the legislature requires a voter to be vigilant regarding the performance of duties by the Government functionaries.   Ibid.

—A ballot paper is not to be rejected as invalid if it contains either the official mark or Polling Officer’s initial—It is to be rejected when both these arc absent. Ibid.

—A ballot paper should be treated as valid if it contains either the official mark or the initial of the Polling Officer.   Ibid.

 

Rules 48 & 49— Election Tribunal, though happens to be a judicial officer and appointed by the Government does not become a civil court within the meaning of Civil Procedure Code and can not as such grant temporary injunction in an election case.

Md. Sharifullah Vs. Election Tribunal Munsif Court Rajshahi (1979) 31 DLR 119.

—To find out the Status of the Election Tribunal one is to examine the powers conferred under rules 48 and 49 of the Union Parishads (Election) Rules, 1976. Rule 48 corresponds to rule 61A of the Union Parishad and Paurashava (Election) Rules, 1973. Similarly, rule 49 corresponds to rule 61B of the said Rules. Rules 48 and 49 are identical to rules 61A and 61B. Ibid.

 

Rule 50—Election when can be declared void.

A declaration that the election of returned candidate or election as a whole is void depends on two conditions—(I) failure of any person to comply with or the contravention of the rules, and (2) the noncompliance or contravention must materially affect the result of the election.

Iman Hossain Bepari Vs. Election Tribunal (1979) 31 DLR 381.

 

R. 50(2)—An Election Tribunal acting under R. 50(2) may order recounting of votes.

Abul Basher Chowdhury Vs. Habibur Rahman Mia & ors. (1979) 31 DLR (AD) 296.

 

—Election Tribunal may consider and determine whether counting of votes by the Presiding Officer was correct or irregular.

Abul Basher Chawdhury Vs. Habibur Rahman Mia & ors. (1979) 31 DLR (AD) 296.

 

Union Parishad & Paurashava (Election) Rules, 1973

 

Rules 2(d) and 56(2)—’Election”— What it means.

Interpretation given by the Tribunal is not found to be in accordance with the definition of election as given in rule 2(d) of the Election Rules. Election means an election of a Member, Commissioner, Women Commissioner, Chairman, or Vice- Chairman, as the case may be. This shows that for a person contesting the Chairmanship ‘election’ means election of the Chairman only. It is, therefore, clear that when the question of election of the Chairman arises the election of Vice Chairman or of any member is irrelevant.

Siddique Ahmed Vs. Munsif 1st Court (1976)28 DLR 470.

 

Rule 3(2) (read with r. 4)—Rule 3(2) does not allow the SDO or any executive officer of the district or sub-division to order recounting of votes which will be illegal—Recording of votes is permitted under r. 40(5).

Azhar Mridha Vs. Election Tribunal (1975)27 DLR 307.

 

Rule 10(4)—Returning Officer Sound to authorize, in the absence of the Presiding Officer, the Assit. Presiding Officer or some polling officer to act in the absence of such Presiding Officer—Failure renders the election void.

Siddique Master Vs. Republic of Bangladesh (1975)27 DLR 240.

 

Rules 15(5) and 59(3)—Deposit not made in Treasury as required by Rules but in the Civil Court—Not a valid deposit according to law and, therefore, election petition not maintainable.

Abdul Malek Vs. Election Tribunal (1976)28 DLR 467.

 

Provision about deposit in the Treasury—mandatory.

This provision is mandatory and not directory. As such non-compliance of the said provision is notn a mere irregularity but is an illegality.

Abdul Malek Vs. Election Tribunal (1976) 28 DLR 467.

 

Rule 20A—The rule clearly negatives introduction of fresh candidates to those already in the field. The word candidates which is in plural has been used because of the possibility of more than once candidate left in the field.

It is true that the last word in rule 20A is ‘candidates’ which refers to persons in the plural but the expression ‘the poll shall be restricted to the remaining candidates’ is highly significant. This expression clearly excludes the possibility and negatives the idea of introduction of a new candidate or candidates in the field through fresh nominations.

Mazharul Huq Vs. Returning Officer (1975) 27 DLR (SC) 11.

 

Rule 21—When the number of candidates for election is equal to the number to be elected, no election is held and the remaining candidate or candidates are elected uncontested.

Mazharul Huq Vs. Returning Officer (1975) 27 DLR (SC)11.

 

Rule 22—Contested election—Poll when held. Rule 22 relates to contested election. It provides that if the number of the contesting candidates for election as member or commissioner in a ward or constituency, as the case may be, is more than the required number of members or commissioners or that of the contesting candidates for election as Chairman or Vice Chairman is more than one, a poll shall be held.

Mazharul Huq Vs. Returning Officer (1975) 27 DLR (SC)11.

 

Rule 27—Non-observance of the requirements of the rule—Election void. Nonobservance of the formalities regarding the giving of public notice as mentioned in rule 27 of the Election Rules renders an election null and void.

Abu Siddique Master Vs. Govt. of Bangladesh (1975)2 7 DLR 239.

 

Rules 27 and 40—”Poll” means casting of votes and does not mean counting of votes.

Ismail Uddin Vs. Election Commission (1977) 29 DLR 285.

 

Rule 30—If poll at a polling station could not be held, a fresh election shall be held only in that polling station and not in all the polling stations of a union.

Haji Munir Ahmed Vs. Chief Election Commr (1975) 27 DLR 308.

 

—Disturbance at a particular election poll necessitating fresh poll—Fresh poll must remain confined to the polling station where disturbance took place—All voters in cl. (3) means all voters of that particular polling station.

Mahiuddin Sardar Vs. Chairman, Election Commission (1974) 26 DLR 181.

 

Rr. 35-39, 42, 44, 46 & 81—For election purposes ballot papers are principal pieces of evidence—Non-production of some ballot papers does not render the ballots produced inadmissible.

Ayub Ali Howladar Vs. Election Tribunal (1976) 28 DLR (AD)1.

 

Rule 40—Votes must be counted immediately after conclusion of the polling—For unavoidable reason if that is not possible it must be done the next day.

Ismail Uddin Vs. Election Commission (1977) 29 DLR 285.

 

—The provisions of rule 40 do not empower the Presiding Officer to take away the ballot boxes to any place outside the centre and then count the votes. These provisions require that the counting should take place at the centre and as soon as the polling is over. There can be no change of time and place according to the will of the Presiding Officer.

If that is done, the sanctity and impartiality of the entire election is affected.

Md. Imanuddin Sarkar Vs. The Election Commission of Bangladesh (1975) 27 DLR 662.

R. 57—Any officer, not necessarily a judicial officer, may be appointed Presiding Officer.

Election Tribunal is appointed by the Election Commission which is a statutory body set up under the Rules and according to rule 57, any officer may be appointed as an Election Tribunal and he need not be a judicial officer, though, mostly judicial officers have been made the Presiding Officers of Election Tribunals.

Provisions of Order 39, CP Code have no application in terms of an election dispute tried by an Election Tribunal.

Rule 58—High Courts interference with the finding of the Election Tribunal when justice demands it.

It has been contended that the finding of facts arrived at by the Tribunal on re-examination of ballot papers in presence of the parties can hardly be interfered with under Article 102 of the Constitution.

Held: Firstly, it cannot be said that determination of validity of ballot papers is a question of facts; it is a mixed question of law and facts. As to the High Court’s jurisdiction under Article 102, it should be remembered that writ is a writ and not an appeal. Ordinarily, this Court does not interfere with the findings of a subordinate Court, if the findings are arrived at on consideration of relevant facts and correct appreciation of evidence being free from any unfairness of mind. If the findings are not so arrived at, this Court has got every power to interfere therewith.

Decision of the Appellate Division cannot be interpreted to hold that the ballot papers found valid by the Presiding Officers in presence of the parties can be arbitrarily rejected by the Tribunal.

Nurul Islam Vs. Munsif Chittagong (1976) 28 DLR 375.

 

—Circumstances which necessitate the interference by the High Court with material irregularities by the Tribunal.

In the instant case, the Tribunal acted with material irregularity when it proceeded to examine the ballot papers ignoring the broad fact that the gunny bags containing the election papers were tampered with having a number of stitched-up holes. Again, when, on discovery of apparently tampered ballot papers, so many in numbers, examination of the Presiding Officer was indispensably necessary in the interest of justice, but he arbitrarily refused to examine them and thereby failed to exercise jurisdiction vested in him. Findings arrived at in these circumstances cannot claim any sanctity nor can they be immune from interference.   Ibid.

NuruI Islam Vs. Munsif, Chittagong (1976)28 DLR 375.

 

—In an orderly election the reports of the Presiding Officer showing performance of duties must be accepted.

When the election was held in a peaceful atmosphere and scrutiny and Counting of ballot papers were made in an orderly mainer, without any objection from any quarter, reports of the Presiding Officers as to due performance of their duties must be accepted. Otherwise there will be no sanctity of a free election which is a must for running democratic institutions. Ibid.

 

—Election laws should be stringently applied to prevent corruption in elections.

The facts and circumstances of the case unerringly show that the questioned ballot papers of the petitioner were tampered with by some malicious hand after their dispatch from the local officers. Subsequent act of tampering with the ballot papers cannot be a ground for undoing the election. To do so would be to give premium to corrupt practices. To ensure free and fair election of people’s representatives Election Laws against corrupt practices should be made stringent. In this case the authorities may direct an enquiry as to how the ballot papers were tampered with. Ibid.

 

Rule 59(3)—Election petition must accompany a receipt showing deposit as required under rule 15(5). When election petition was filed at the first instance and thereafter deposit by a chalan was shown to have been made—This violates a mandatory provision of law and as such election petition cannot be entertained.

Abdul Malek Vs. The Election Tribunal (1977) 29 DLR 221.

 

R. 60 clauses (a) and (b)—What clauses (a) and (b) provides—’Election as whole’ explained.

Clause (a) of rule 60 provides that election of any returned candidate may be declared void and some other candidate might be declared elected in his place and clause (b) provides that election as a whole may be declared void.

Siddique Ahmed vs. Munsif 1st Court (1976)28 DLR 469.

 

—Under clause (a) election of returned candidate void and some other person elected Under clause (b) election as a whole void.

Abul Hayat Vs. Narul Amin (1977)29 DLR (SC) 125.

 

—R. 60, clause (b)—”Election as a whole void’ explained.

The expression ‘election as a whole’ may be declared void’ means that if the election of a returned candidate for a particular office is declared void but no other candidate is declared elected in his place. then, the election for that office will fall through and a fresh election will be necessary, but this does not and cannot mean that the election to all other offices will also be void.

Siddique Ahmed Vs. Muns 1st Court (1976) 28 DLR 470.

 

Rr. 60 & 62—In circumstances coming under r. 6(1) no necessity for fresh election and in cases falling under r. 60(2) fresh election necessary.

Syed Mofazzal Hossain Vs. Election Commissioner (1976)28 DLR (SC) 51.

—Election means to be the final act of selection of a person for a particular office and this right to office is ascertained on the basis of the highest aggregate number of votes which a candidate may have received at different stations set up for the said purpose.   Ibid.
