Words and Phrases

 

Quasi-judicial Tribunal

Quasi-judicial Tribunal—No personal knowledge to be imported into the proceedings.

Quasi-judicial Tribunals who deal with the rights and obligations of parties should not, under any circumstance, import their personal knowledge into a decision given by them.

Yar Ali Khan Chowdhury Vs. Administrator of Wakfs (1968) 20 DLR 535.

 

Quasi judicial—What the word ‘quasi’ indicates.

The word ‘quasi’ as prefixed to the word judicial may either indicate that the tribunal is not acting ‘purely administratively’ or that it is acting in a manner in which a judicial tribunal is expected to act.

Tariq Transport Co. Vs. Sargodha Bhera Bus Service (1959) 11 DLR (SC) 140.

 

Quasi judicial body

Order of such, is illegal if it affects a person adversely without giving a notice to him. Momin Motor Co. Vs. Regional Transport Authority (1958) 10 DL)? 284.

 

Question of fraud

Question of fraud and collusion is a question of fact. Salema Khatun (1978) 30 DLR (SC) 99.

 

Raiyat and tenant—Tenant of an agricultural holding is generally called ‘raiyat’ while the word ‘tenant’ is used in relation to lands, agricultural and nonagricultural.

M.H. Khondoker Vs. Bangladesh (1978) 30 DLR (SC) 1.

 

Reasonable and probable cause— of time. Malicious- prosecution.

It means a genuine belief based on reasonable grounds that the proceedings are justified. The defendant must show me reasonable care in determining whether there are sufficient grounds for the proceedings instituted by him, and any failure to exhibit such judgment or care will be imputed to him as a want of reasonable and probable cause.

Province of East Bengal Vs. S.M. Faruque (1958) 10 DL)? 601.

 

Reconstruction of record—Court’s duty

Where one party alleges that a record of a Court has been lost the onus is on that party to prove that there was a pending proceeding before the Court, but once it is established that a record did exist it is the duty of the Court to take all steps to secure or reconstruct the record.

When a Court is unable to reconstruct a record at one stage because of the non-availability of sufficient evidence and therefore refuses to proceed with the suit, it cannot be said that the Suit henceforth stands dismissed. All that has happened is that on account of a practical but insuperable impediment the suit cannot be tried. The suit still remains, pending .

Sk. Mahboob Alam Vs. Central Exchange Bank (1960) 12 DLR (WP) 96.

 

Reconstruction of the Court’s records.

A judicial proceeding—Reconstruction on the basis of First Appeal—Paper-Book.

it is in exercise of its inherent jurisdiction to do justice that steps are taken by a Court to reconstruct its own records on the basis of the available materials which may be lawfully taken notice of by it. Such a proceeding is a judicial proceeding and the proof of the contents of the documents which are to be reconstructed must be in conformity to the law of evidence.

The learned Judges of the High Court Division have directed the Court of the Subordinate Judge to reconstruct the required documents on the basis of the Paper Book of the First Appeal which was preferred to the High Court against the decree under execution as well as certified copies of the judgment and decree passed by the High Court in the said appeal.

Masud Ali Vs. Eskander Ali (1976) 28 DLR (SC) 99.

 

Reconveyance, agreement of counting

In the instant case the general expression with regard to time for payment of consideration for the reconveyance, “within 4 years,” appears to be in conflict with the clearly spelt out period of time, i.e., between the 1st. Aswin 1370 B.S. to 30th Kartick 1374 B.S. The apparent conflict gets easily resolved when the two expressions as to time are construed in proper perspective.

In ascertaining the period of time the general expression is to be ignored when the beginning and the end of the questioned period of time is clearly specified and dated.

Nazera Begum Vs. Motahar Ali Mia (1982) 34 DLR 49.

 

Record

The word ‘Record’ is wider in meaning than ‘decree’. “Record” is a wider term than “decree”.

Record” includes judgment, decree, pleading, evidence, and exhibits but “decree” means precisely that which is defined in section 2(2) CPC. Again, “error apparent on the face of the record” is a much wider term than the words “clerical or arithmetical mistake or error apparent on the face of the decree.” Whereas in Or. 47 rule 1 the application for review can be made “to the Court which passed the decree or made the order”; in Or. 47 rules 2, except on the grounds stated in the rule itself, the application for review” shall be made only to the judge who passed the decree or made the order sought to be reviewed.”

Alhaj Md. Ruhul Amin Vs. Dhaha Municipal Corporation (1984) 36 DLR 1.

 

Record-of-right—is evidence of present possession—Registered Kabala evidence of title.

Sultanuddin Chowdhury Vs. Govt. of Bangladesh (1980) 32 DLR 252.

 

Record of right not a document of title.

Record of right is not a document of title. It does not create or extinguish title to land. At best, along with other evidence, it may support an evidence of tide. By itself, it is not an evidence of title.

Unless the plaintiff can make out his title, he cannot recover possession since the defendant is on the land.

Lal Meah, Vs. Haji Md. Ibrahim Meah (1976) 28 DLR (SC) 61.

—Latter record-of-rights will prevail over the earlier one. Abdul Hamid Vs. Abdul Hossain Mir. (1983) 35 DLR. 293

 

—Entry in the record of rights—Its weight and effect.

In a case where the plaintiff relies upon the en- try in the record of rights for the basis of his title it is not necessary for him to show the basis of his title, or the entry in the record of rights.

Meherjan Bibi, Vs. Naimuddin Dhali (1981) 33 DLR 126.

 

—Reformation in situ: Is incidental to the proprietary right of the person to whom the land belonged and during the period of submergence of the land the ownership is not lost but merely suspended and on its reappearance in the old site the owner gets it back.

A. Mannan Vs. Kulada Ranjan (1979) 31 DLR (AD) 197.

 

—When a land reappears on its old site which originally belonged to one person, he does not lose his ownership of it only because the re-appeared land joins the land which belongs to another man and the latter can not claim it as an accession to his land. Ibid.

 

—When a navigable river swallows up hard and firm soil, capable of being cultivated in the usual manner, and subsequently yields up the same in more or less the same condition of soil, it can be claimed by the original owner as reformation in situ.

‘Registrar of Co-operative Society acting as a Chairman of the society—.Propriety of—

The Registrar of Co-operative Societies being invested with power to exercise control over the Society and to adjudicate disputes between the Society and others, it does not seem desirable that the Registrar should function also as Chairman of the Society. The holding of both the offices of Registrar and Chairman of the Society by the same person may give rise to an embarrassing situation.

Zeaul Haq Vs. East Pakistan Co-operative Society Ltd. (1967) 19 DLR 498.

 

Re-imbursement to co-sharer

A co-sharer is under no obligation to re-imburse the defaulting co-sharer-purchaser by payment of the expenses properly incurred in the purchase together with the interest thereon. The purchases made by such a co-sharer-purchaser should ensure to the benefit of all the other co-sharers and the property must be regarded as being held for the benefit of all the co sharers according to their respective shares at the date of the sale. In order to perfect the title of the other co-sharers, they are bound to reconvey the property purchased free of cost.

Jamila Khatun Vs. Yasin (1959) 11 DLR 199.

 

Relief must follow the right claimed

It is of the very essence of judicial proceedings that the relief to be granted should follow as a legal result from the right alleged and found. The error in the proceedings is fundamental where the relief granted is different from the one prayed and flows not from the right alleged but from a right which has not been pleaded and as to which the court has never heard the parties. This is true in all proceedings but more so in proceedings for prerogative writs where the court does not generally act ex debito justitiae but confines the petitioner to a specific right and a specific relief.

The petitioner had asked for a writ of mandamus requiring the Government of Pakistan to let the respondent continue in office as Joint Secretary, National Assembly, for a further period of two years, but the writ or direction actually issued was not in accordance with this prayer and it restrained (he Government from interfering with the rights of the respondent as an employee for the remaining period of his service. Thus, in place of a writ of mandamus, a writ of prohibition in substance was issued against

the Government, requiring it not to interfere with the rights of the respondent as an employee of the Government. Thus the High Court granted to the petitioner a relief for which there had been no argument before the High Court and accordingly the order of the High Court was set aside.

Pakistan through Secy. National Assembly Vs. Khondkar Ali Afzal (1960) 12 DLR (SC) 38.

 

“Remedial enactment”—defined

A “remedial enactment” is one which improves and facilitates remedy already existing for the enforcement of an existing right and for the redress of wrongs or injuries, as well as to correct defects, mistakes and omissions in a former law.

Osman Gani Vs. Mainuddin Ahmed (1975) 27 DLR (SC) 61.

 

‘Rent-receipts’ are evidence of possession, and may be used as collateral evidence of title.

Erfan Ali Vs. Joynal Abedin Mia (1983) 35 DLR (AD) 216.

 

Repeal—Law regarding repeal

Repeal of an earlier repealing Act—revival of the original Act if can be presumed.

The question to be answered is as to whether by such subsequent repeal of the earlier repealing Act the original Act XXXIII of 1951 stood revived or not.

Wazed Ali Vs. Mofizuddin Shah (1981) 33 DLR 418.

 

—Where an Act repealing, in whole or in part, a former Act is itself repealed, the last repeal dock not revive the Act or provisions before repealed, unless words be added reviving them.   Ibid.

‘Reserve’

The word ‘reserve’ in Rule 2 Sch. II of the Business Profits Act has been used in its plain ordinary meaning. It has been used in a much wider sense than in the Companies Act.

According to the plain and dictionary meaning of the word ‘reserve’ there must be some setting apart of the amount for some special or general purpose by a person or a body authorized under the Articles of Association of the Company to allocate the funds for particular purposes.

R. Sim & Co Vs. Commr. of Income Tax East Bengal (1956) 8 DLR 208.

 

Retrospective effect

—Retrospective effect of the statute, applicability of statues is presumed to be applicable to cases and facts coming into existence after their enactment unless there is clear intention to give them retrospective effect.

Nabi Ahmed Vs. Home Secretary, Government of West Pakistan (1970) 22 DLR (SC) 20.

 

—Retrospective effect to a statute may be given either by express words or the same may be inferred from the language employed.

—An act which is invalid when it is done cannot be legalized by legislature giving it retrospective effect.

‘Retrospective operation’—Construction of the term.

The term ‘retrospective operation’ is ambiguous because it is applied both to the fact that a particular enactment operates from before its date or so as to affect pre-existing rights and to the problem of construction which may be presented by an enactment as to whether it extends backwards or not.

When an enactment extends backwards, by its own clear language, it operates retrospectively. It is only when the intention of the Legislature does not lie on the surface that a question of interpretation arises and, in such a case, in deciding whether the enactment concerned is intended to operate retrospectively certain well-known principles are followed.

Commr. of Income Tax East Pak Vs. Redhashyam Agarwalla (1960) 12 DLR I (PC).

 

—Legislature cannot validate an invalid law where it lacks power to pass that law.

Constitution not retrospective—Fundamental rights—Validity of pre-Constitution law, when in clash with the fundamental rights under the Constitution laws, must remain unaffected unto the date the Constitution came into force.

Jibendra Kishore Acharyya Vs. Prov. of East Pakistan (1956) 8 DLR 457.

Revenue courts order holding the plaintiff to be a tenant can be challenged in civil Court in a subsequent Suit.

It is not permissible for the revenue courts to go behind the decision of civil courts and re-open matter already decided by competent judicial authority. In a suit for rent whether the claim was based on a civil court decree, it was altogether wrong to enter on enquiries whether that decree had been rightly given or not.

Review

—Of a decision—any order passed on such review is without jurisdiction when an appeal or revision against the decision itself was pending before a superior authority and the law prohibits reviewing when an appeal or revision is pending. Question of knowledge or lack of knowledge about pendency of appeal or revision irrelevant, the bar to review being complete.

Ghulam Mohi-ud-Din Vs. C.S. Commissioner, Lahore (1964) 16 DLR (SC) 655.

 

—Of an order—passed in revision by an authority to which the power of revision was delegated—if permissible under law.

Majority view (per S.A. Rahman and Hamoodur Rahman, JJ.) was that such authority has no power to review its own order. The power of review in this context cannot be regarded as ancillary to the revisional power conferred on the delegatee. A power of review is a creature of the statute and it cannot be said that a delegatee exercising revisional powers under sub-section (2 of section 20 of the Displaced Persons (Compensation & Rehabilitation) Act have inherent power to review orders passed in his capacity as a delegatee.

Mst. Zainab Khatoon Vs. Mian Ghulam Shabbir (1964) 16 DLR (SC) 712.

 

—Right of review can be exercised only in case of excusable failure on the part of a party to bring to the notice of court new and important matters of error.

Review and appeal—Scope of—The appellate jurisdiction is wider than its jurisdiction of review and that an erroneous view of evidence or mw is ordinarily not a good ground for review.

Mailer not raised in the original court, should not ordinarily be allowed to be raised in appeal.

Allahdino Vs. Fakir Mohammad (1969) 21 DLR (SC) 382.

 

Review and revision—Joint Registrar has no independent power of review—Whenever he exercises the power of review he acts as a Registrar exercising power u/s 135(2). Once the Joint Registrar exercises the power of review which is one u/s 135 (2), Registrar cannot review once again u/s 135(2).

Registrar of the Co-operative Societies took the view that application can be filed a second time under section 135(2) of the Bangladesh Co-operative Societies Act and that this section does not limit his power of review to only one occasion.

Section 135 was not amended to give this power to the Joint Registrar. This power of review and revision has been given to the Government and the Registrar. It is therefore clear that the Joint Registrar in disposing of the first application for review acted as the Registrar under section 135(2). Registrar’s power of review was exhausted thereafter and he became functions officio and was left with no power to review or revise his order already made in review or revision.

Progati Khudra Babashayee Samabaya Samity Ltd. Vs. Bangladesh (1979) 31 DLR 152.

 

Revision petition—A revision petition may be treated as a memorandum of appeal and vice-versa.

If a petition for revision has been filed when an appeal should have been filed or vice-versa, that is not sufficient for depriving a party of a remedy which has been sought under a wrong nomenclature. The object of the petitioner was to have the impugned order examined by the High Court and the circumstance that the petition was described as a petition for revision and not as a memorandum of appeal cannot, on any reasonable ground, be considered fatal for the case of the petitioner. It is open to the High Court to treat a petition for revision as a memo of appeal and a memo of appeal as a petition for revision.

“Salary”

The definition of the word “salary” will include a servant who is on leave without pay, for a period of time as also a servant who is under suspension for a contingent period, receiving lesser payment without doing any work. The aforesaid classes persons are both “in service” and in the habit of “salary” as an incident to their service.

A person, who is under suspension in respect a Government service, is not in a different position from a servant, who is on leave without pay.

The expression “salary” is co-related to term “service” or being in ‘service’ and postulates habit or a recurring procedure. The expression “in also connotes a continual or habitual process, notwithstanding casual interruptions.

This definition will, however, exclude a person receiving a “pension” as he is no longer “in service”.

Mukhlesur Rahman Vs. SDO, Narayanganj (1962) 14DLR 762.
