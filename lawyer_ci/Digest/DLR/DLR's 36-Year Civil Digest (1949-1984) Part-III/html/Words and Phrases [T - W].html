Words and Phrases

Tax and Company—’Lifting the Veil’ theory—When a Company in order to avoid tax payment changes its name—Courts to look close into the matter.

Govt. of E Pak. Vs. M/s. Lucky Biri Company (1978) 30 DLR 411.

‘Tax’ and ‘toll’.

A tax does not aim at conferring any direct benefit upon any particular individual and there is an element of quid pro quo between the tax payer and the public authority while, on the other hand, fee or a toll is a charge for a special service rendered to some individuals by the agency authorized to charge a toll or fee.

A tax toll or fee is imposed in public interest and in a toll or fee individuals receive some special benefit while in the case of a tax there is absence of such direct benefits or advantages and if there is one it is incidental to the action of the State.

Zaminur Rahman Vs. Bangladesh (1979) 31 DLR (AD) 171.

Technical objection—when fundamental

Where technical objection of a decision goes to the root of the matter, i.e., the decision challenged rests on an issue not raised in the action, it must be given effect to if justice is to be done.

Attorney- General, Fiji Vs. JP Baylay (1951)3 DLR 482 (PC).

“Temporary employment” Distinguished from non-temporary employment.

It was contended that the petitioner was a temporary employee and therefore was not entitled to constitutional protection and reliance was placed in the case of Muhammad Umar Vs. Malik Hakim Khan and another reported in PLD 1968 Lahore, 1093.

Held: If no definite period of employment is specified then employment is non-temporary employment as distinct from a temporary employment which must be for a definite shorter period.

Mir Alauddin Ahmed Vs. Province of E.P. (1975) 27 DLR 394.

—Appointed temporary—Various implications of the expression explained at length.

Saleh Ahmed Joarder Vs. People’s Republic of Bangladesh. (1984) 36 DLR (AD) 26.

—The interpretation of the expression ‘temporarily’ would, therefore, depend more upon the intention of the maker of the order and the subsequent conduct of the appointing authority. To put it differently, whether in making the appointment the authority was intending to make permanent appointment a temporary one or whether, though intending to make a temporary appointment, by its subsequent conduct it was treating the appointment made temporarily as a permanent one. Thus, the temporariness or permanence of an appointment would not depend so much upon how it has been qualified but would depend upon whether the appointment is intended to be permanent, though apparently made temporarily. Ibid.

—Temporary post alternating between Chief Engineer’s office and subordinate office—Service in Chief Engineer’s office over long period does not confer a lien or vested right—Reversion on promo- lion to temporary Assistant, to subordinate office does not involve a violation of natural justice or denial of right—No reduction in rank caused.

Tenant—Meaning of—The English term ‘tenant’ ordinarily means an occupier of immovable property having a kind of relationship respecting the said property with another person who is generally called the ‘landlord’. The relationship of landlord and tenant generally arises when the owner of the property confers by demise a right of exclusive possession of that property upon another person on condition of periodical payment of what is called rent.

M.H. Khandker Vs. Bangladesh (1978) 30 DLR (SC) 1.

Tenancy—Original purpose may be shown to have been changed,

—Tenancy under in BT Act.

Document, if written, must be registered

If any party resorted to any document creating a permanent tenancy, it must be registered but if the document is not registered, the tenant can prove his tenancy independently of the written lease, inasmuch as under the Bengal Tenancy Act no written lease is necessary for the creation of the tenancy.

Tenure Post—The expression “Tenure post” as defined by Fundamental Rules 9 is a permanent post which an individual Government servant may not hold for more than a limited period. A tenure post is one which cannot be held for more than the prescribed period without an order of extension; it is not to be understood to mean a post which is to be held for the period prescribed, once the appointment is made.

Pakistan Vs. Fazlur Rahman Khandkar, (1959) 11 DLR (SC) 253.

“Terminal tax”—Liability of goods to pay terminal tax

Though the terminal tax could not be levied in respect of goods imported by sea or air it could be validly levied on goods imported by rail or mad even after the coming into force of the late Constitution. Furthermore, the tax could not be levied on goods merely in transit. Where, however, the goods were unloaded at a rail head or terminal point within the municipal limits for transportation outside, the tax could be levied on such goods as there would then be a terminal tax limits. A terminal tax was by its very nature a tax chargeable at a terminal point or one which could be referable to some service, whether of caniage or otherwise, rendered or to be rendered at a terminal point.

Pakistan Tobacco Co. Ltd. Vs. Karachi Municipal Corporation (1968) 20 DLR (SC) 109.

—Termination of service.

Dismissal or discharge of an employee carries with it penal consequences—Compulsory retirement does not carry any such consequences in which case show cause notice can not be issued.

MA. Rashid, Assistant Engineer, Bangladesh Water Development Board Vs. Secretary, Ministry of Power, Water Resources and Flood Control. (1979) 31 DLR 339.

—Termination of tenure.

Termination of tenure dates from the institution of the suit for its annulment. Revenue sale as has been held terminated the proprietary interests of the plaintiffs in the touzi but it did not automatically wipe Out the under-tenures in touzi. The sale only rendered these under-tenures voidable at the option of the purchaser. He was to exercise his option by institution of a suit, by serving a notice upon the under-tenure holder, by collection of rents or by some other overt act clearly indicating his intention to annul.

Amulya Chandra Kundu Vs. Monendra Nah Biswas. (1979) 31 DLR 75.

“Territorial Waters’ and ‘Economic Zone’ are different things—Territorial water which is a 12-miles sea belt from the base line is same as Customs Water as defined in the Customs Act.

Thak and survey map.

If thak and survey maps show that the land formed bed of a river, it cannot be assumed that it was dry land at the time of the permanent settlement.

Province of Bengal Vs. Jamila Khatun Chowdhurani (1952) 4 DLR 222.

—Thak and survey map—Lands included in—No conclusion that they existed during the Permanent Settlement.

TI cannot be presumed as a matter of law that the state of things described in the thak and survey maps existed at the time of the Permanent Settlement.

The question what lands were included in the Permanent Settlement is a question of fact and not of law which may or may not be satisfactorily proved by subsequent survey maps. The onus of proving, that any particular lands were included in the Permanent Settlement of 1793 is clearly on those who affirm that such was the case, and the burden of proof is not necessarily shifted by the production of the thak and survey maps showing that specific lands are included in a particular estate.

Shamser Mandal Vs. Bhagyaban Biswas. (1960)12 DLR 510.

‘To sue’—What it means.

The words ‘to sue’ not only signify ‘prosecute’ but also ‘to defend’, or to do something which the law requires for the beuer prosecution or defense of the cause.

Bhaba Kanta Joddar Vs. Satish Chandra Mandal (1960) 12 DLR 649.

“Tort”—Liability of Government for tortious acts of its servants.

It was contended that no suit for tort could lie against Government in respect of acts done by its servants just as no action can in such a case lie against the Crown in England.

Held: The constitutional status of the Crown of England is wholly different from that of the Government of Pakistan.

Pakistan Vs. Muhammad Yaqoob Butt. (1963) 15 DLR (SC) 369.

—Damages for negligence—Joint and several liability—Decree can be executed against any.

Action being in tort instituted against a firm of which defendant 2 is a partner the several partners will be liable severally and jointly as joint tortfeasors and if any negligence could be proved as against the firm defendant No. 2, who is one of the partners of the said firm would be liable for the damages jointly as well as severally and the decree for the entire damages could be awarded against him.

Abdul Awal Vs. Mofasiluddin Ahmed (1975) 27 DLR 628.

 

—Action in tort—Liability to damages may arise out of general relationship—Duty to neighbor—Reasonable foreseeability is an important element of a neighbor’s duty of care—Immunity in certain cases.

Abdul Awal Vs. Mofasiluddin Ahmed (1975) 27 DLR 628.

—Is a defamatory statement.

The word ‘tout’ is an expression which is highly offensive, and hence its application to a person of normal reputation and standing would be prima fade defamatory of him.

‘Towns’ and ‘urban areas’

The words ‘towns’ or ‘urban areas’ have no statutory definitions or precise connotation at the time when the E.P. Urban Immovable Property Tax Act was promulgated and such terms mean in ordinary municipalities and other areas where the dominant pursuit of life is other than agriculture.

“Trespasser”—Persons already in peaceful possession of land in assertion of title are entitled to recover possession of the same from a trespasser who dispossessed him, although the title of the person possessing the land had not yet become perfect.

Although the defendants were not parties to the documents and the suits still the peaceful possession of the Dastidars and after that of the plaintiffs’ (in succession to the Dastidars) being earlier and peaceful such possession could not have been disturbed by the defendants who, are mere trespassers. Trial Court—Its finding.

An appellate Court would not ordinarily interfere with the findings of fact of a court of first instance but this principle is not without exception. Where the (I) facts are such that the appellate Court is in a similar position with the original court to sift and weigh the evidence; when; (2) the oral testimony has not received due consideration in the original court; and where (3) the disbelief of witness by the trial court is based on an alleged contradiction which is in reality a mere incapacity of the witness to remember or explain a certain thing—then in all such cases the appellate tribunal would be unfettered to examine the evidence afresh and arrive at an independent decision.

Md. Akbar Abdul Sattar Vs. W.L. Bogtstra (1956)8 DLR (PC) 19(23).

—Trial Court’s order sheet

Trial Court’s order-sheet shows that both the plaintiff and the defendant admitted certain facts. Neither the plaintiff nor the defendant was examined in the case nor any affidavit was filed admitting such facts—Trial Court decreed suit relying on such facts—Trial Court decreed suit relying on such admissions.

Held: Observations of the Trial Court regarding such admissions have a legal presumption of correctness and unless dislodged, should prevail.

Syed Modaris Ali Vs. Syed Md. Ilias Ali (1972) 24 DLR 191.

Tribunal

The question whether an act is judicial, quasi judicial or administrative is clouded by a confusion which it is extremely difficult to resolve.

The true question in which character a tribunal acts always is whether the act which is complained of is a judicial act and not, whether the procedure adopted by the tribunal is judicial or quasi judicial or whether the dominant or general aspect of the tribunal is that of a judicial, quasi judicial or administrative body. A Tribunal is not always furnished with the trappings of a Court, nor will such trappings make its action judicial.

Tariq Transport Co. Vs. Sargodha Bhera Bus Service (1959) 11 DLR (SC) 140.

—Words ‘Tribunal’ and ‘Courts in Art. 102(5) explained. The word ‘tribunal’ does not mean the same thing as ‘court’ but includes within its ambit adjudicating bodies provided they are constituted by the state and are invested with judicial as distinguished from purely administrative or executive functions. The expression ‘tribunal’ has been used along with ‘court’ in sub-article (5) of Article 102. The ‘court’ acts judicially and ‘tribunal also acts judicially or at least quasi-judicially.

Bangladesh Vs. AKM Jahangir Hossain (1982) 34 DLR (AD) 175.

“Turnover”

The expression ‘turnover’ means the aggregate amount for which during the year of taxation, the dealer bought or sold “goods’ of the kind enumerated in the definition of “goods” contained in the Pakistan General Sales Tax Act, 1948.

Undue influence—Allegation of undue influence—Burden of proof.

The Privy Council, in the case of Harmes Vs. Hinkson AIR 1946, PC. 156. held that “the burden of proving that the will is the outcome of undue influence exercised on the testator is on those who attack the will”.

Paresh Chandra Bhowmick Vs. Hiralal Nath (1984) 36 DLR (AD). 156.

—Illiterate village woman though not a pardanashin is entitled to protection extended to the latter when evidence shows that under the influence of the defendant she executed a sale-deed.

The object of the rule of law, which is applicable to pardanashin ladies, is to protect the weak and helpless and consequently such a rule should not be restricted to that class only but should apply to the case of a poor woman who is equally ignorant and illiterate though not a pardanashin woman in the strict sense of the term.

Sree Sree Gopal Jeo Bigraha Vs. Mahmuda Begum (1968) 20 DLR 192.

Umpire.

An umpire is a person appointed to take over the reference from the arbitrators who are unable to agree amongst themselves. In general, he is in the position as a sole arbitrator, must be appointed in the same way, and must possess the same qualifications or absence of disqualifications. An umpire has no power to act until the arbitrators disagree.

Umpire assumes jurisdiction only when there is a disagreement between the arbitrators. The mere fact that the umpire has signed the award in the absence of any disagreement between the arbitrators is superfluous.

Majibul Hoque Meah Vs. Ali Ahmed, (1973) 25 DLR 286.

Validation of law declared illegal, when can be done.

Ordinarily, a court holds a law to be invalid because the power to enact such law is wanting or it does not sufficiently create the jurisdiction. Validation of a law so declared illegal may be done only if the grounds of illegality or invalidity are capable of being removed and are in fact removed and the law made competent.

Mofizur Rahman Khan, Vs. Govt. of Bangladesh (1982) 34 DLR (AD) 322.

“Vested interest”

An interest is said to be vested when it is not subject to any condition precedent. A person takes a vested interest in property when he acquires the proprietary right in it, but the right of enjoyment is deferred till a future event happens which is certain to happen. An interest may be a vested one, though its enjoyment’ may be postponed.

Jalil Ahmed Vs. Province of East Pakistan (1967) 19 DLR 106.

Wakf created without making provision for charitable purposes of permanent nature is not a valid and legal wakf—Provision made by wakif for feeding persons who might be present at his death and burial is not a charitable object to make the wakf valid.

Wakf-al-aI-aulad

For application of section 4(b) of the Agricultural Income Tax Act to a valid wakf-alal-aulad created by a Muslim, it should be considered on the basis of the basic principle of the institution of wakf as contemplated under the Muslim Law.

Director of Taxation Vs. Mehdi Ali Khan (1980) 32 DLR (AD) 138.

 

—The legal meaning of wakf, according to the accepted rule of Hanafi School of Muslim Law, is the extinction of the proprietor’s ownership in the property dedicated and its detention in implied ownership in God in such a manner that the profits may revert to and be applied for the benefit of the mankind. Ibid.

 

—Shariat Law—Western conception of charity differs from shariat law.

What in the estimation of the Western Jurists could be pernicious perpetuity calculated to aggrandize the family of the founder, is according to the Shariat the best of charity. Ibid.

 

—A provision for one’s self, for one’s children, is as good and charitable an act as a dedication for the support of the general body of the poor.

Wakf providing for both religious and charitable purpose, makes the wakf wholly for religious and charitable purposes.

A valid wakf-al-al-aulad as provided under the statutes, is a religious institution. Ibid.

 

—Wakf-alal-aulad entitled to the benefit of tax exemption as provided in the first part of clause (b) of s.4. of Agricultural Income Tax Act.

In view of the position of private trusts, and wakf-ai-al-aulads being such trusts, and it being a religious institution, such private trusts have not been excluded from the benefit of exemption as provided in the first part of clause (b) of section 4 of the Act. The wakf in question being a mixed one, that is, providing maintenance of wakifs family, children and descendants and also public charity and religious object, it comes under the first part of clause (b). Ibid.

—Wakf-alal-aulad under the Muslirñ Law is not to be treated as a partial trust—Legislature exempted income applied to wakf-alal-aulad from Agricultural Income Tax Act.   Ibid.

In the Income-Tax Act private trust has been expressly excluded from the benefit of exemption, whereas in the Bengal Agricultural Income-Tax Act this exclusion from exemption, not only omitted, but also wakf-alal-aulad, which is a private, trust, has been expressly exempted by specific mention of it. This makes the difference between the two statutes. It is not safe to interpret one statute on the authority of decided cases under another statute containing different provision and of different purpose and connotation.

Whether on the facts and circumstances of the case, under section 4(b) of the Act the word ‘applied’ means the actual amount of income applied to for such purposes by the Mutwalli or the amount of income set apart by the Waqif as per wakf deed for such purposes.   Ibid.

Will

Presumption as regards execution of a will

Execution of a will by a testator ordinarily raises a presumption that the testator knew and approved of the contents of the will if nothing appears to the contrary; but where suspicious circumstances exist, strict proof to remove the doubt will be required.

Execution of the will in the present case whose probate has been sought to be obtained is surrounded with several suspicious circumstances such as:

1. The propounder of the will is the sole beneficiary under it.

2. He took leading part in the preparation of the will.

3. Scribe and attesting witnesses are persons. who are in the confidence of the propounder.

4. It has not been proved that the will was prepared according to the instructions of the testator or its contents were read out to him.

Held: In these circumstances the probate of the will cannot be granted.

Judhisthir Nath Vs. Haji Badsha Meah Sowdagar (1969)21 DLR 848.

 

Withdrawal of a case—as distinguished from composing an offence.

Withdrawal and composition are the distinct concepts. Withdrawal is the act of the complainant whereas the composition of an offence requires the cp-operation of both parties—Whether a petition is one for withdrawal or compromise, is to be judged from the facts of each case.

Witness

In an appeal from the decision of a trial judge on a question of fact based on his opinion of the trustworthiness of witnesses whom he has seen, the court of appeal, in order to reverse the decision, must not merely entertain doubts whether the decision below is right but be convinced that it is wrong.

Mukunda Baroi Vs. Nim Chand Sidhy. (1953) 5 DLR 206.

—Witness on Commission—Many advantages of having a witness’s testimony in open court not available when examined on commission.

One of the basic characteristics of judicial administration in the country is the holding of a trial in an open court and the rule requiring a witness who wants to depose in the case to go upon the witness box and to give a sworn testimony which may be tested by cross-examination lays down a salutary procedure. That such a testimony is to be given in an open Court presupposes a kind of moral pressure which, under normal conditions of civilized society, is expected to ensure some amount of truthfulness on the part of a witness.

The Judge of the trial Court who is to decide the case should be in a position to make an assessment of the testimony given by a particular witness on the basis of the words deposed, mode of deposition as well as his entire demeanor in the witness box. The right of cross-examination of a witness of an opposite party in presence of the Judge is a valuable right. The provisions of the Code should be interpreted in a manner which is consistent with this basic principle of the judicial procedure.

Dacca WASA Vs. Roushan Ara Begum (1973) 25 DLR 461.

‘Worker’

— A person does not cease to be a worker only because he is employed in a supervisory capacity. It depends on the nature of the job done to say whether he is a worker or not.

A person does not cease to be a ‘worker’ merely because he is employed in a supervisory capacity. To be able to say that he is not a worker it has to be established further that he exercises functions mainly of a managerial or administrative nature.

What is important in determining whether a person is a ‘worker’ or not is to see the main nature of the job done by him and not so much his designation.

The issue as to whether a person was a worker or not has to be resolved in each case with reference to the evidence on record.

Mujibur Rahman Sarkar Vs. Chairman, Labour Court, Khulna. (1979) 31 DLR 301.

 

—Doing over-time work gets over-time allowance—No over-time work no allowance—Employer alone competent to decide whether there is need or not for over-time work.

Chief Executive Vs. Chairman, Labour Court (1977) 29 DLR 83.

—Any allowance paid for work in the nature of over-time work, does not create a vested right in the worker to such allowance. Ibid.

—Does not include a person whose services have been terminated under section 19 of the Standing Orders Act, 1965.

—A ‘worker’ or ‘workman’ who falls in the first category of workers as defined in clause (xxviii) of s. 2 of Industrial Relations Ordinance can maintain an application under section 34, but the first category does not include a dismissed worker.

G.M., Hotel Inter-Con. Vs. 2nd Labour Court (1976) 28 DLR 161.

 

—Workers or workmen—Two categories of workers or workmen—distinction defined.

The definition of workers or workmen in clause (xxviii) of section 2 of the Ordinance has classified them into two distinct categories. The two categories fall in two parts of the clause and are constructionally joined together by the use of the conjunction ‘and’ which is preceded and followed by comma. A worker or workman who falls in the first category means any person who is not an employer as defined in the Ordinance but is employed for hire or reward under any express or implied term of employment. An apprentice would also fall in this category.

G.M., hotel Inter-Con. Vs. 2nd LabourCourt (1976) 28 DLR 161.

 

—“Workman”

‘Workman’ as defined includes casual worker. The word ‘and’ is to be read in a conjunctive sense. Mason engaged for plastering wall of the employer’s mill is a workman. Question whether employment casual or not is a question of fact.

Aftab Flour and Allied Industries Ltd. Vs. Mst. Nasim Akhtar. (1964) 16 DLR (WP) 199.

 

Works Programme Employees.

—Their relationship with the District Board is that of master and servant.

Administrator, Zilla Board Vs. Mukhlesur Rahman. (1984) 36 DLR 71.

 

Writ

Delay in seeking remedy under writ is a ground for refusing the prayer.

These related applications of the petitioners cannot be allowed on the ground that it will upset a condition prevailing for the last three years and create complications and chaos. If the petitioners thought that the notifications were made illegally, they should have promptly come before this Court seeking remedy.

M/s. Amin Jute Mills Ltd.Vs. The Chittagong Municipality (1968) 20 DLR 1041.

 

Writ jurisdiction—High Court’s writ jurisdiction when question on facts and title involved.

There is a long line of decisions in favor of the view that the High Court should not enter into disputed questions of fact nor decide any question as to title which requires investigation into facts and taking of elaborate evidence.

A.H. Sikder Vs. Bangladesh (1979) 31 DLR (AD) 303.

 

—Emergency proclaimed on December 28, 1974 was revoked on November 27, 1979.

Decision of the Martial Law Courts in Martial Law period not challengeable except where there is want of jurisdiction or coram non judice.

Nasiruddin Vs. Government of the People’s Republic of Bangladesh (1980) 32 DLR (AD) 216.

 

Writ petition

Writ petition found maintainable even though there was alternative remedy open. It was contended that there was alternative remedy against the impugned order by way of appeal which was not, however, availed of by the petitioner. But in view of the admitted position and expressive violation of statutory provision, the writ petition in this case is quite maintainable even if the alternative remedy was not availed of.

Kazi Ali Noor Vs. Secretary, Ministry of Agriculture. (1979) 31 DLR 428.

 

Writ of Certiorari

This being a Writ of certiorari, the superior Court is not competent to substitute its own finding reversing the finding of the Election Tribunal when the said finding is based on materials on record.

Imam Hossain Bepari Vs. Election Tribunal (1979) 31 DLR 381.

 

When it lies.

It is open to anyone to question, in a Writ of certiorari, an order passed in a quasi judicial proceeding. An application for a writ of certiorari can be maintained when it is shown that the tribunal has acted without or in excess of jurisdiction.

Application by the Municipal Committee complaining that the Controlling Authority did not properly appreciate the questions raised in respect of clauses 13 and 14 of the Model Tax Schedule was not maintainable under Article 98 of the Constitution of Pakistan—the decision arrived at by the Controlling Authority being either in excess or without jurisdiction.

American Life Insurance Vs. Dacca Paurashabha (1979) 31 DLR (AD) 277.

 

—Writ of certiorari lies against a statutory authority acting in quasi-judicial capacity—Such writ lays when Municipal Committee makes illegal or unauthorized assessment on land and building—Such a writ would lie when illegality of proceeding is apparent on the face of the record. Ibid.

 

Writ of mandamus—When will not be issued-There must be a demand of justice and refusal, before writ of mandamus will issue—Circumstances justifying a departure from the demand for justice and its denial.

Zamiruddin Ahmed Vs. Govt. of Bangladesh (1982) 34 DLR 34.

 

—Writ of mandamus in English Courts—when will not be to compel restoration of offices.

Cases in which the English Courts have invoked this extraordinary power for compelling restoration by writ of mandamus are all cases of offices which arc either corporate or elective offices or municipal positions. In England no court could possibly compel the restoration of office of any public servant in the service of the Crown, for all such servants of the Crown hold office during the pleasure of the Crown and no Court in England would presume to command the Crown. The authorities cited in Halsbury would also seem to indicate that even for other offices, the tenure of which is precarious or dependent on the will of another, a mandamus would not lie to compel restoration to such offices.

Munshi Abdul Jabbar Vs. Barisal Municipal Committee (1968) 20 DLR 1186.
