                            Imports and Exports (Control) Act  (XXXIX of 1950)

            

Ss. 3(3) and 2(c)—It was contended as much as the goods in respect of which a r lion under sub-section (I) of section 3 of this Act has been made shall be deemed to be the goods of which the export and import have been prohibited or restricted under section 19 of the Sea Customs Act, the prohibition or restriction cannot be extended to goods which have been brought by air since section 19 of the Sea Customs Act does not in terms refer to goods brought by air.

 

Held : In view of the definition of the word Import' as given in section 2 of the Imports and Ex­ports (Control) Act, section 19 of the Sea Customs Act and all its provisions would apply to goods brought by air also. Lt.Col.Syed Naiycr Reza Vs. Pak. (1958) 10 DLR(WP) 46.

 

—Provisions of the Act apply only to goods imported into or exported out of Pakistan—It does not apply to means of transport used for removal of such goods—Grant of option under section 3(3) dis­cretionary only in respect of contraband goods and not in respect of vehicles used as means of convey­ance for removal of such goods. Murad Ali Vs. Collector, Central Excise (1963) 15 DLR (WP) 3.

 

S. 6 : Cognizance of offence under section 167 (8)(81) on the report of E.P.R. personnel is without jurisdiction.

 

Section 6 of the Imports and Exports Act which lays down that no Court shall take cognizance of any offence punishable under section 5 of the said Act except upon complaint in writing by a Customs Controller or the Chief Controller or any officer authorized in that behalf and that no Court inferior to that of Magistrate of the first class shall try any such offence.

 

The offence in the present cases were punishable under section 167(8)(81) of the Sea Customs Act. That being so, the Magistrate had no jurisdiction to take cognizance of the cases on the complaint by the E.P.R. personnel except complaint made in writing by the Customs authorities. Tamjid Ali Vs. The State (1968) 20 DLR 546.

 

—Offence falling both under the Sea Customs

Act and Imports and Exports Control Act— Prosecution of accused only under the Sea Customs Act competent.

 

Accused for committing the offence of smug­gling bidi leaves was liable to be charged both under section 167(81) of the Sea Customs Act as well as under section 3 of the Imports and Exports (Control) Act. Prosecution under section 6 of the Imports and Exports Control Act for violation of the provisions under section 3 of the Imports and Exports Control Act could be upon complaint in writing of the rele­vant authorities. In the present case the accused was, however, charged under section 167(81) of the Sea Customs Act which was a cognizable offence and for the prosecution of which no complaint by any Cus­tom Officer is necessary. It was contended that the accused being liable for prosecution both under the Sea Customs as well as under the Import & Exports Control Acts, his prosecution and trial under section 167 (81) of the Sea Customs Act alone was illegal and the Magistrate taking cognizance of the case un­der section 167(81) of the Sea Customs Act (which requires no written complaint by Custom Officer) without his prosecution under the Imports and Ex­ports Control Act, which requires written complaint, was illegal and, therefore, the trial held was illegal and without jurisdiction.

 

Held : The prosecution having not chosen to prosecute the accused under the Imports and Exports Control Act there was nothing illegal and no want of jurisdiction if the accused is prosecuted and tried under the Sea Customs Act. Saleh Mohammad Vs.' The State. (1969) 21 DLR (SC) 321.
