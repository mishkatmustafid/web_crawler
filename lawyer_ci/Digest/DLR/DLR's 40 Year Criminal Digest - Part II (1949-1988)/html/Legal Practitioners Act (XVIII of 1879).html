                         Legal Practitioners Act (XVIII of 1879)

 

S. 13—Advocate—Professional misconduct— Advocate representing tenant, and a co-sharer land­lord as a proforma defendant, in ejeetment proceed­ings, tenant taking plea that he was attorned to the co-sharer landlord—Later Advocate filing ejeetment proceedings on behalf of the co-sharer landlord against tenant who confessed judgment—This does not amount to professional misconduct. Faizullah Khan Vs. Pir Mukammal Shah (1961) 13 DLR TO 62:1961 PLD (SC) 34.

 

—Counsel's duty in regard to Court—

 

The obligation is incumbent upon a counsel to do nothing consciously, by act or omission which might mislead the Court in some matter touching the exercise of its jurisdiction and powers in the case. Faizullah Khan Vs. Pir Mukammal Shah (1961) 13 DLR (SC) 62: 1961 PLD (SC) 34.

 

—Misconduct or 'conduct' unbecoming an Ad­vocate.

The misconduct which the Court found against Mr. Akhlaque Hossain was in respect of two charg­es: One of these charges related exclusively to his conduct as a Judge, during a period of duty as Chair­man of an Election Petition Tribunal. Such misconduct is not beyond the Court's purview when the .question arises whether an enrolled Advocate of the supreme Court has been guilty of conduct "upcoming an Advocate".

 

The other charge of misconduct upon which Mr. I Akhlaque Hossain was found guilty is directly rclat-ledlo his conduct as a member of the legal profession.

 

It has been found that Mr. Akhlaque Hossain as lawyer and as a Judge had been guilty of a great fraud in regard to his income-tax.

 

The order of the Court is that the name of Mr. Akhlaque Hossain shall be removed from the roll of the Senior Advocates of the Supreme Court, but he shall be permitted to apply for reinstatement upon the roll after the expiry of one year from the date on which his removal from the Bench of the High Court of West Pakistan took effect. State Vs. Akh­laque Hossain (1960) 12 DLR (SC) 285.

 

—Legal Practitioner—Misconduct of— Suspended not on evidence of the case, but upon ma­terial taken from record of departmental inquiry against a Magistrate—Suspension justified. Jabdul Huque Vs. Slate (1960) 12 DLR (SC) 168: 1960 PLD (SC) 323.

 

—All legal practitioners, be they even employ­ees of the Government of Pakistan or a Provincial Government are officers of the Court. State Vs. Ed­ward Snelon (1961) 13 DLR (WP) 14: (1961) DLR (Lah)52.

 

S. 36—Bar Association declaring a clerk to be a tout—Sufficient evidence of general repute under the section.

 

Section 36 which clearly indicates that a resolu­tion of a Bar Association declaring a person to be a tout, by itself is sufficient evidence of general repute within the meaning of section 36 and if a Court acts upon it such decision should not be interfered with by the High Court. Rajab Ali Bhuiyan Vs. State (1962) 14 DLR 660.

 

—The Legal Practitioners Act is a self-contained Act and its object is to provide for disciplinary or ad­ministrative action against the officers of the Court. MJbrahim Vs. Crown (1955) 7 DLR (FC) 65

.

—Proceedings under the Act need not follow conviction.

 

There is no hard and fast rule that proceedings under the Legal Practitioners Act must follow only when one is convicted in a criminal court. 54 CWN (2 DLR) 140.

 

—The words "any other reasonable case" in sec­tion 13c (0 of the Legal Practitioners Act are not confined to misconduct of which a practitioner is guilty in his professional capacity but embrace all cases which may afford reasonable grounds for his suspension or dismissal. 54 CWN (2 DLR) 140.

 

—Jurisdiction which is exercised under section 36 of the Act for declaring a person or persons as touts is a peculiar jurisdiction but it is a disciplinary or administrative jurisdiction.

 

Revisional Jurisdiction of the High Court under section 439 Cr.P.Code can not be invoked as against such orders passed under section 36 of the Legal Practitioners Act. Md. Shafiqul Islam Vs. State (1974) 26 DLR 175.

 

—Jurisdiction exercised by Courts u/s. 36 of the Legal Practitioners Act is neither civil nor crimi­nal jurisdiction but an administrative jurisdiction. Abul Khair Vs. Slate (1982) 34 DLR 332.

 

—An application under sections 435 and 439 of the Code of Criminal Procedure against an order passed by the District Judge u/s. 36 of the Legal Practitioners Act is not maintainable in law. Abul Khair Vs. State (1982) 34 DLR 332.

                                                      Limitation

 

Criminal proceedings—Law of limitation not applicable. Muhammad Raft Parvez Vs. State (1960) 12 DLR (WP) 73: I960 PLD (Lah) 986.

 

—Delay beyond statutory period in filing appeal against acquittal can only be condoned when excep­tional circumstances exist. State Vs. Ghulam Shah (1959) 11 DLR (WP) 60.
