Both the last Proclamation of Martial Law and Act 1 of 1979 saving Martial Law were issued on the same day, i.e., on 6th April, 1979. The specific period ascribed in the last Martial Law Proclamation for saving all Martial Law Proclamations, Regula­tions, Orders, acts and actions however extended be­yond the period from date and lime of the withdrawal of Marlial Law on 6th April upto 9th April, 1979.

The review authorities under clauses (c) and (f) of the last proclamation of Marlial Law were obliged to complclc the pending continuing proceedings of Martial Law Courts by making a final review order thereon within 9lh April, 1979 at the latest, since Ihcrcaftcr no act or action of any continuing surviv­ing authorities of Martial Law administration like the review authorities have been saved and ratified by the Parliament under Act I of 1979.

Any order made by the reviewing authorities un­der clauses (e) and (f) of the last proclamation of Martial Law beyond 9th April, 1979 was not man­dated by the Parliament under Act I of 1979 and con­sequently such act and action would under any cir­cumstances be acts and actions without any lawful authority with no legal effect having constitutional sanction.

In such circumstances, the review of the pro­ceeding taken before the Special Martial Law Court by the Government having been made on 17th April, 1979 and the review of the proceeding taken before the Summary Martial Law Court made by the Session Judge on 10th and 18th April 1979 were all having been done and made after 9lh April, 1979 and were taken and done without any lawful authority with no legal effect. Consequently the review of the proceedings of the Martial Law Courts being a con­tinuation of the proceeding to reach the finality thereof having been held to have been done without any lawful authority, the conviction and sentence imposed by the Martial Law Courts in the respective judgments and orders lose all validity for further en­forcement and becomes a nullity in law.

In the result the review made by the Govern­ment and the Session Judge with regard to the pro­ceedings taken against the petitioners before the Martial Law Courts is hereby declared to have been done without any lawful authority and consequently the conviction and sentence imposed by the Martial Law Courts against the petitioners in the respective proceeding having not been reviewed within the specified period in accordance with law, arc declared to have no legal effect and in the result the petition­ers are set at liberty. Rczaul Karim Vs. Govt, of Bangladesh (1981)33 DLR 358.

Held: Under the provisions of the last procla­mation of Martial Law while lifting the Martial Law, the respective authorities have been empowered to review the proceedings already terminable before Martial Law Courts even after the final withdrawal of Martial Law.

—The jurisdiction of the Government to review the judgments of Special Military Courts was given by the amendment of the Regulation No. 4 of Mar­tial Law Regulation 1 of 1975 by which the Chief Martial Law Administrator has been substituted by the Government. To consider whether that review of the petitioner's case by the Chief Martial Law Ad­ministrator is a vested right or not we have to con­sider what are the nature of these rights. Right to re­examine witnesses, right of appeal arc substantive rights and cannot be taken away by amendment of the procedure available to a parly. A review of judg­ment of Martial Law Court by any authority docs not provide any right of hearing or personal appear­ance by the party aggrieved and no action has to be taken by the party concerned. As such it cannot be said to be a substantive right which could not be al­tered of the judgment passed by the Martial Law Court.

Further, it is to be seen as to how the party's right has been affected or he has been prejudiced by the change of the authority competent to review the judgment of the Special Martial Law Court. In the absence of any right of personal appearance or any­thing to be done by the party, he has not in any way been prejudiced by the change of the forum of review from C.M.L.A. to the Government. The review has not been taken away but kept intact. It is only the [reviewing authority that has been changed and as such it has not prejudiced the petitioner. Kh Mos-taque  Ahmed Vs. Govt, of Bangladesh (1981) 33 DLR348.
