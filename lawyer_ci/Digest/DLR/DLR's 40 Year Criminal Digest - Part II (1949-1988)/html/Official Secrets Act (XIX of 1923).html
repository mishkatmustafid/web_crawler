   Official Secrets Act (XIX of 1923)

 

S.14—Magistrate ordering the hearing of a case in camera can only do so after being satisfied that it is necessary for the safety of the State.

It is obligatory that the discretion vested in the Magistrate under section 14 of the Official Secrets Act is exercised by him properly and judicially. A mere desire on the part of the Public Prosecutor who made the application to hold the proceeding of the Court in camera will not be enough to exclude pub­lic hearing and to justify an order for hearing in cam­era. It must be shown that the paramount object was safety of the State.

In understanding and applying section 14 two main features thereof have to be kept in mind. The initiative is that of the Public Prosecutor but Magis­trate has to determine the matter judicially, that is, must exercise a discretion. This would necessarily mean that he has to satisfy himself upon the materi­als before him that for safety of the Stale the trial should be held in camera and that it was not an at­tempt to interfere with the normal course of justice for illegitimate reasons or purposes.

 

In the present case, the allegation relates to the disclosure of a certain secret directive issued by the Government, and this directive has already been pub­lished in the "Pakistan Times". Hence, no question of secrecy arises so far as this document is con­cerned. Ch. Ghulam Muhammad Vs. The State, (1967) 19 DLR (SC) 128.
