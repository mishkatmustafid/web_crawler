Bangladesh Telecommunications Act, 2001 

Section 3(2)— Excludes the application of the law to grant licence for Radio and Television Broadcasting—

Unless there is any ambiguity in the language of an enactment, the court is not authorised to import any meaning different and inconsistent with the plain meaning thereto by interpretation. Section 3(2) of the Bangladesh Telecommunications Act, 2001 clearly excludes the application of the Act and the authority of the Bangladesh Telecommunication Regulatory Commission (BTRC) to grant any licence for Radio and Television Broadcasting. However the BTRC has the power to allocate frequency spectrum to those who are granted licence or no objection certificate by the Ministry of Information for Radio and Television Broadcasting. Chairman, Bangladesh Telecommunication Regulatory Commission and others Vs. Ekushey Television Ltd. and others 14 MLR (2009) (AD) 369.

Bangladesh Telecommunications Act, 2001—

Section 36— Grant of licence and allocation of frequency by the BTRC and their cancellation—

Bangladesh Telecommunications Regulatory Commission (BTRC) has the exclusive jurisdiction to grant licence for telecom system and services under section 36 of the Bangladesh telecommunications Act, 2001. But it can not grant licence for broadcasting and telecasting programmes through satellite channels. In the instant ease the Ministry of Information issued "No Objection Certificate"(NOC) which is not a licence in the true sense of the term. BTRC allocated the frequency and NOC for importing equipments in favour of the Jamuna Television which was subsequently cancelled without affording any opportunity of defence. The Appellate Division held the cancellation of allocation and NOC during the validity period without issuing any show cause notice illegal and without jurisdiction. Ministry of Information, represented by its Secretary, Government of Bangladesh and others Vs. Jamuna Television Limited and others 14 MLR (2009) (AD) 114.

Bangladesh Telecommunications Act, 2001 (Act No. 18 of 2001)—

Section 37(3)(g), 89 and 90— Monopoly in providing telecom Services by particular operator being opposed to the National policy is prohibited and the Bangladesh Telecommunication           Regulatory Commission is vested with the power to amend the licence condition.

Contract Act, 1872—

Section 23— Agreement opposed to public policy is void—

The Bangladesh Telecommunication Regulatory Commission (BTRC) is empowered under sections 89 and 90 of the Bangladesh Telecommunications Act, 2001 to amend any condition of licence or licence agreement which'are inconsistent with the provisions of the Act. The co-exclusivity agreement of the World Tel for 4 years in the Dhaka Multi-Exchange area in disregard of the huge unmet demand of the customers having been found opposed to public interest has been cancelled by the BTRC in exercise of its power under the law. The High Court Division upon scrutiny of the relevant provisions of the law held the impugned order of cancellation perfectly justified. The Appellate Division having found nothing wrong in the findings and decision upheld the judgment and order of the High Court Division and dismissed the civil petition for leave to appeal. World Tel Bangladesh Ltd. Vs. Bangladesh represented by the Secretary Ministry of Post and Telecommunications and others 11 MLR (2006) (AD) 65.

 

Bangladesh Telecommunications Act, 2001

Section 90— Power of the Bangladesh Telecommunication  Regulatory Commission (BTRC) to amend/cancel condition of licence—

The Bangladesh Telecommunication Regulatory Commission in exercise of its power under section 90 of the Bangladesh Telecommunications Act, 2001 cancelled the exclusivity clause of the licence of the World Tel prohibiting the entry of the other operators for 4 (four) years in the Dhaka Central Zone  as the clause being monopolistic was opposed to law and National policy. The High Court Division held the impugned order to be perfectly justified. The court further held there is no provision in the BT Act, 2001 which provides for any such monopoly to any particular operator and that the right accrued under the previous regulation can not be taken away by subsequent regulation. World Tel Bangladesh Ltd. Vs. Government of Bangladesh and others 11 MLR (2006) (HC) 201.
