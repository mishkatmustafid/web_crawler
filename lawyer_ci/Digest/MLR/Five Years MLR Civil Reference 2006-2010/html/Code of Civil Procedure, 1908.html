Code of Civil Procedure, 1908—

Section 21 and 99— Suit when decided on merit, cannot fail on account of misdescription of the plaintiff—

The nature of relief shall be determined in reference to the averments made in the plaint and not on the basis of allegations of the written statement. When does not affect the merit the suit does not fail on account of mis-description of the plaintiff. Divisional Forest Officer, Dhaka Vs. Md. Shahabuddin and others 12 MLR (2007) (AD) 287.

 

Code of Civil Procedure, 1908— Section    24— Transfer of suit from one district to another on sufficient ground—

Court's power to transfer a suit from one district to another is discretionary which has to be exercised judiciously. The grounds which are considered sufficient have by now been clearly , specified by series of pronouncements of the superior courts. That the opposite party is influential is not a sufficient ground for transfer of a suit from one district to another. The party seeking the transfer must prove the grounds to the satisfaction of the Court. Jamal Hossain (Md.) Vs. Md. Mazid 11 MLR (2006) (HO 409.

 

Section 24— Power to withdraw a suit or case and transfer it from one court to another is discretionary power of the court—

The allegation on the basis of which the transfer is sought must be proved by the party concerned to the satisfaction of the court. Power to transfer of a suit or a case from one court to another of competent jurisdiction either by the High Court Division or the District judge as the case may be, within their respective jurisdiction being discretionary shall be exercised judiciously. Shafiqul Alam (M) and others Vs. Qazi Rafiq Ahmed and others 14 MLR (2009) (EC) 476.

 

Section 56— Applies against arrest and   detention   of   woman   in   civil prison—

Section 115— Concurrent findings of fact arrived at by the trial court as well as the appellate court are binding upon the revisional court—

In the instant case the trial court decreed the suit and the appellate court below upheld the decision of the trial court on concurrent findings. The High Court Division also affirmed the decision of the courts below. The Appellate Division held that the concurrent findings of the trial court as well as the appellate court are binding upon the revisional court unless there is a case of misreading or non-consideration of material evidence on record. Mohar All Bhuiyan Vs. Michir All Bhuiyan and others 15 MLR (2010) (AD) 500.

 

Code of Civil Procedure, 1908— Section 115— Scope of interference with the findings and decision of lower appellate court— Setting aside exparte decree on ground of non-service of summons— Onus lies upon the plaintiff who obtained the exparte decree to prove the service of summons—

Suit for declaration of title to joint property in ejmali possession without seeking partition is not maintainable—

The trial court setaside the exparte decree with the finding that the summons upon the defendant-plaintiffs were not served. The onus to prove that the summons upon the defendants were duly served lies upon the plaintiff. When this onus was not discharged and it was evident from the record that there was no service of summons the High Court Division held the lower appellate court was wrong in setting aside the judgment of the trial court in respect of setting aside the exparte decree and accordingly setaside the same. The part of the judgment of the lower appellate court dismissing the suit for declaration of title to the joint property in ejmali possession as being one not maintainable without seeking partition is up held by the High Court Division. Amiruddin Mondal (Md.) and others Vs. Md. AshrafAli and others 11 MLR (2006) (HC) 49.

 

Section 115—Concurrent findings of fact of the trial court as well as the appellate court can not be interfered with by the revisional court unless there is a case of non-reading or non-consideration of material evidence on record or an error of law in the decision-making—

The consistent views of our apex court is that the concurrent findings of facts of the court of appeal as well as of the trial court can not be interfered with unless the said findings suffer from any illegality arising out of non-consideration or misreading of any material evidence on record or an error of law in making the decision. In a partition suit where the defendants did not lead any evidence or could not substantiate by any specific evidence during trial the objection as to non-joinder of parties and properties, subsequently the defendants cannot agitate the defect of the suit for non­joinder of parties or properties. Muslem Fakir and others Vs. Md. Moniruddin Khondaker and others 11 MLR (2006) (HC) 92.

Section 48— Transfer earlier in point of time takes precedence over the subsequent one—

Findings of facts of the appellate court when do not suffer from any legal infirmity do not call for interference by the revisional court. Section 115— Findings of fact arrived at by the appellate court on proper appreciation of evidence on record are binding upon the High Court Division—

Unless there is perversity in the findings due on non-consideration or misreading of material evidence occasioning failure of justice the findings of fact arrived at by the court of appeal on proper appreciation of evidence, cannot be interfered with by the High Court Division in exercise of its revisional jurisdiction. Salina Begum Vs. Mojibar Rahman and others 13 MLR (2008) (HO) 58.

 

Code of Civil Procedure, 1908— Section 115— Scope of interference with findings of fact of lower appellate court—

Suit for a mere declaratory decree is not maintainable without seeking consequential relief for recovery of possession when the plaintiff is out of possession of the suit land as required under section 42 of the Specific Relief Act, 1877. Appellate Court is required to advert to the findings of fact of the trial court with reference to the evidence on record while reversing those findings. The revisional court in exercise of jurisdiction under section 115 of the Code of Civil Procedure can not interfere with the findings of fact of the appellate court unless they suffer from non-consideration or misreading of evidence on record or perversity. Abdul Jabbar Bhuiyan and others Vs. Kulsum Banu Bibi and others 13 MLR (2008) (HC) 212.

Section 115(2)— Power and jurisdiction of the District Judge in entertaining revisional application is confined to the limit of pecuniary jurisdiction relating to appeal—

A Full Bench of the High Court Division decided that the District Judge's power to entertain revisional application shall be confined to the limit of his pecuniary jurisdiction relating to appeal. Statement of valuation of the subject-matter wherefrom the revision arises shall be given in the revisional application for the purpose of jurisdiction.  Section 148— Extension of time to deposit fee within specified time passed by conditional order with a default clause— not permissible—

When the High Court Division directed the commissioner's fee to be paid within certain date with a default clause, the trial court cannot extend such time beyond the limit so fixed. On the expiry of the specified time in case of default the consequence automatically takes effect and no further order is required to be passed. Government of Bangladesh represented by the Deputy Commissioner, Patuakhali Vs. Md. Nurul Haque and others 12 MLR (2007) (AD) 257.

 

Section 148 and 149— Power of the Adalat to enlarge the time for putting in deficit court fee—

Order 7 rule ll(c) Rejection of plaint for non-payment of deficit court fee within specified time—

To put in deficit court fee within 21 days is not the mandatory requirement  of law and as such the Adalat can enlarge the time under section 148 and 149 of the Code of Civil Procedure. When the deficit court fee is subsequently paid and the Adalat accepted the same there is no ground for rejection of the plaint. Apparels Limited and another Vs.  Order 6 rule 4— Allegations of fraud and forgery to be specifically mentioned in the pleadings—

Though the party making the allegations of fraud or forgery of certain documents is required under Order 6 rule 4 of the Code of Civil Procedure, to specifically mention the particulars thereof in his pleading, the court is not precluded from scrutinising such document to ascertain the genuineness or otherwise of the same even if the particulars of forgery are not stated in the pleadings. Order 6 rule 17— Provides for amendment of pleadings to rectify error, mistake or omission for effective adjudication of dispute and to avoid multiplicity of suits—

The purpose of rule 17 of Order 6 of the Code of Civil Procedure is to advance the cause of justice rather than to stifle it by allowing liberally amendment of pleadings for effective adjudication of disputes between the parties and to avoid multiplicity of suits. When the amendment sought for does not change the character of the suit the court should allow the same. The order refusing the amendment should contain reasons and must be speaking one in order to be sustainable in law. Afsaruddin Mina (Hazi) and others Vs. Alhaj Sk Sultan All being dead his heirs Selina Begum @ Rani and others 12 MLR (2007) (HC) 41.

 

Order 6 rule 17— Amendment of plaint when it does not change the nature and character of the suit, should be allowed for the purpose of effective adjudication of the dispute between the parties—

In a suit for declaration of title and recovery of possession of part of the suit land, the apex court held the amendment sought for incorporating the alternative prayer for partition as perfectly justified since the same does not change the nature and character of the suit, rather it would be necessary for effective adjudication of the dispute between the parties. The courts below misunderstood the suit as one under section 9 of the Specific Relief Act, 1877. Shafiqul Islam Chowdhury (Md.) and others Vs. Mustafizur Rahman and others 13 MLR (2008) (AD) 147.

 

Order 6 rule 17—Amendment of pleadings—Amendment of plaint may be allowed at any stage of the proceedings—

Law on amendment of plaint is well settled that amendment of plaint may be allowed at any stage of the proceeding including the stage of appeal for the purpose of effectively adjudicating the controversy between the parties provided the same does not change the nature and character of the suit and does not introduce new cause of action. Abdul Aziz Sheikh Vs. Kachuran Bibi and others 11 MLR (2006) (HC) 241.

Ershad All and others Vs. Md. Ruhul Amin and others 11 MLR (2006) (PIC) 325.

 

 Order VI rule 17— Amendment of plaint at the appellate stage—

Amendment of plaint can be made at the appellate stage provided the same is not barred by limitation.     The amendment of the plaint in the instant case has been rejected on the ground of limitation and vagueness. Abdul Wadud and others  Vs.  Abdul Wahed   and   others    14   MLR   (2009) (AD) 107.

 

 Order 7 rule 3— Plaintiff is required to clearly describe the suit land—

In order to succeed in a suit the plaintiff must describe the suit land specifically. No relief can be granted inrespect of any unspecified and vague land. Ershad AH Hoivlader being dead his heirs Naznil Islam (Babul) and others Vs. Santi Rani Dhupi and others 12 MLR (2007) (AD) 105.

 

Order 7 rule 3— Suit land must be specific and ascertained—

It is required as provided under Order 7 rule 3 of the Code of Civil Procedure that the suit land must be specific and ascertained and should not be vague and indefinite. The objection as to the identity of the suit land must be raised at the earliest opportunity. When no objection was taken by the defendant during trial, such objection cannot be raised for the first time in revision. Abdul Khaleque and others Vs. Akhteruzzaman Mia and others 13 MLR (2008) (HC) 193.

 

 Order 7 rule 10— Rejection of plaint is not permissible when it is properly constituted and filed in the court of competent jurisdiction—

Artha Rin Adalat Ain, 1990 is a special law which shall prevail over all other laws—

The Artha Rin Adalat Ain, 1990 has clearly defined the term (^H) loan which includes interests of all kinds by whatever name they are called. Accordingly the learned judges of the High Court Division found nothing wrong with the impugned order and discharged the rule. Shrimp and Fish Processing Plant Ltd, represented by its Managing Director Vs. National Bank Ltd and others 15 MLR (2010) (HC) 55.

 

 Order 7 rule 11— Rejection of plaint in suit barred by law—

It is well settled that no separate suit shall lie against exparte decree passed by Artha Rin Adalat as provided under section 6 of the Artha Rin Adalat Ain, 1990. The remedy against exparte decree is available under Order 9 Rule 13 of the Code of Civil Procedure upon deposit of 50% decretal dues. The rejection of plaint in the suit is held perfectly justified. M. Tariqullah Sikder Vs. Sonali Bank 12 MLR (2007) (HC) 73.

 

 Order 7 rule 11— Rejection of plaint on ground of maintainability of the suit is not permissible before filing of written statement—

Specific Relief Act, 1877—

Section 42 and 56F— Preliminary issue as to maintainability of suit can not be decided before filing of written statement—

Whether a suit is maintainable or not under section 42 read with section 56F of the Specific Relief Act, 1877 is a matter which can be decided upon consideration of the materials contained in the pleadings and as such neither the plaint of a suit can be rejected under Order 7 rule 11 of the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 nor the preliminary issue thereon can be decided before filing of written statement and some evidence are led in support thereof. The apex court directed the trial court in the facts and circumstances to decide all the issues after filing of the written statement and evidence taken. Ismat Zerin Khan Vs. The World Bank and others 11 MLR (2006) (AD) 58.

 

Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 — Order 7 rule 11— Rejection of plaint of a suit when barred by limitation —

The Plaintiff for the self same matter in a suit fought upto the Appellate Division and was unsuccessful. In a subsequent suit on the same matter the plaint was rejected as being barred by the principle of res-judicata. The apex court held the rejection of plaint perfectly justified. Serajud-Dowla (Alhaj Mohammad) and others Vs. The Secretary, Ministry of Works 11 MLR (2006) (AD) 169.

 

Order 7 rule 11— Rejection of plaint when the suit is barred by law—

Plaint of a suit is liable to be rejected when the suit is barred by law—

Public Demands Recovery Act, 1913— Section 37—Ousts the jurisdiction of civil court when the auction sale is challenged in suit by a stranger to the certificate proceedings.—

Section 37 of the Public Demands Recovery Act 1913 provides in clear terms that auction sale in a certificate proceedings can only be challenged either by the certificate holder or certificate debtor or by their representatives which shall be decided by the certificate officer. No stranger can challenge such auction sale except on ground of fraud. In the instant case the Revenue authority set aside the auction sale after 12 years on an application of a stranger which was challenged in the suit. The apex court setaside the impugned order of the High Court Division rejecting the plaint as being illegal and held the suit was quite maintainable that in view of the legal position the civil court was perfectly competent to see how far the Revenue authority was justified in setting aside the auction sale. Marfat Alt Miah (Md) being dead his legal heir Few] Ahammed Bhulu Vs. Sree jagadish Chandra Sheel and others 11 MLR (2006) (AD) 313.

 

 Order 7 rule 11— Rejection of plaint and the grounds therefor—

Plaint can not be rejected on the ground of maintainability of the suit or vagueness of the subject matter or non-production of documents as required under Order 7 rule 14 of the Code. Again plaint also cannot be rejected on the question of lack of personal interest of the plaintiff in the subject matter of the suit. Mohammad All Vs. It. Col. (Reid.) Habibullah Bahar and others 11 MLR (2006) (AD) 405.

 

Order 8 rule 1— Provision for filing written statement within 2(two) months are directory and not mandatory—

Since no consequence is provided for the non-compliance within the specified period, the provisions of Order 8 rule 1 of the Code of Civil Procedure to file written statement within 2(two) months is held by the apex court directory and not mandatory. The court has the discretion in appropriate case to grant extension of time to file written statement beyond the period of two months. Khan Md. Firoj Kabir Vs. Rupali Bank Ltd. and others 12 MLR (2007) (AD) 186.

 

Order 8 rule 6 and Order 20 rule 19— Set-off and counter claim arising out of same transaction and when such claim can be treated as plaint in cross suit—

When the claim raised by the defendant as set-off does not exceed the claim of the plaintiff and when such counter claim filed along with the written statement exceeds the claim of the plaintiff and requisite court fees are paid thereon, this can well be treated as plaint in a cross suit particularly when the plaintiff withdraws his suit. Loyal Shipping Ltd. Vs. Castrol U.K. Ltd and others 13 MLR (2008) (AD) 291.

 

Order   9   rule   9—  Applicable  to restoration of pre-emption  case dismissed for default— State  Acquisition  and  Tenancy Act, 1950—

Section 96(13)— Does not bar the applicability of Order 9 rule 9 C.P.C in respect of restoration of pre-emption case dismissed for default—

Law is well settled that section 151 of the Code of Civil Procedure is not applicable in the case for setting aside dismissal of suit for default of the plaintiff as there is specific provision under Order 9 Rule 9 of the Code. Order 41— Power of appellate court to reassess evidence on record—

It is well settled that while reversing the judgment and decree the appellate court must advert to the findings of the trial court and in doing so the appellate court shall have all the powers of the trial court to reassess the evidence on record. Service of summons by hanging on the outerfencing of the dwelling hut of the defendant when established is held by the apex court a good service. Order XX Rule 18— Partition of e/mali property by mets and bounds and allotment of Saham— Exclusive possession of cosharers need to be maintained—

Where the trial court in the preliminary decree has given certain direction for making partition of the joint property among the cosharers and allowed saham for their shares, the Advocate Commissioner can not go beyond the direction of the preliminary decree. Where the Advocate commissioner allotted saham by violating the term and direction of the preliminary decree and disturbed the possession of the respective cosharers, the learned judge of the High Court Division setaside the final decree and remanded the suit with direction to issue fresh writ to the advocate commissioner to effect partition and allot the saham as per the preliminary decree. Order XX rule 18(2)— Suit for partition and allotment of saham. Purchaser having purchased land in excess of share of his vendor is not entitled to get saham—

Defendant who purchased part of suit land in excess of the share of his vendor who had no subsisting salable interest and who did not contest the suit and challenge the rafanama/compromise by filing written statement having acquired no right, title and interest thereby is not entitled to get saham for his said purchased land. Order XLI rule 22—Respondent can file Cross objection— Time limit is not inflexible --

It is well settled that a respondent may file cross objection against the decree within one month of the service of notice. The time limit is not inflexible. Order 21 rule 99— When no objection as to bonafide possession is raised before the executing court— Order 39 rule 1— Temporary injunction against execution proceedings intended to delay or defeat the decree obtained 30 years back is refused—

The execution proceedings in respect of a decree obtained 30 years back were at the fag end. The petitioner did not raise objection or obstructed on the bonafide claim of possession before the executing court as provided under Order 21 rule 99 of the Code of Civil Procedure. The apex court held such belated move for temporary injunction at this stage is designed to delay or defeat the decree obtained 30 years back and accordingly refused the same. The High Court Division discharged the rule with the finding that application of a third person who is not a party to the decree is not maintainable. Order 21 rule 11—Execution of decree and the limitation thereof—

Limitation Act, 1908—

Article 182(3) and (5)— Applicability of article 182(3) to execution proceedings—

Limitation for execution of decree is three years. Execution case filed within the period of limitation can well be continued after disposal of appeal and the dismissal of review. Article 182(5) is applicable when the decree is altered or modified in revision. But when revision is dismissed article 182(3) of the Limitation Act, 1908 shall be applicable and as such the pending Execution case can well be continued. Kazi Shamsul Huq and others Vs. Suit filed against dead person is held not a nullity where there are other defendants alive and the right to sue survives. Order 22 rule 9 of the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 provides for substitution of legal heirs of deceased plaintiff/appellant or defendant/ respondent, as the case may be, where the right to sue survives after setting aside abatement. Section 5 of the Limitation Act, 1908 has been made applicable to such case. In certain exceptional circumstances the apex court held that necessity of filing separate application u/s 5 of the Limitation Act may be waived where the court is satisfied on the basis of the materials on record to secure the ends of justice and set aside the abatement and-allow substitution based on the principles that the right of a party cannot be defeated by mere technicalities. Since the plaintiff did not take steps at the earliest opportunity to amend the plaint nor sought for the withdrawal of the suit on grounds of formal defect with permission to sue afresh on the same cause of action, the learned judge of the High Court Division held the impugned order of the Subordinate judge rejecting the prayer for withdrawal perfectly justified. Sekander Alt and others Vs. Md. Seraj Mia and others 12 MLR (2007) (HC) 154.

Law is settled that an order of statusquo restraining the parties from changing the nature and character of the suit property is in fact an injunction of temporary nature which is an appealable order as contemplated under Order 43 rule l(r) C.P.C. against which revision does not lie. Doulat Ahmed Vs. Md. Shafiqul Islam i Chowdhury and others 11 MLR (2006)  (AD) 48.

 

Order   39   rules   1   and   2—   Not applicable to cases of payment under L.C relating to international trade—

A letter of credit when issued by a bank becomes an independent contract between the issuing bank and the negotiating bank apart from the contract for sale of goods between the seller and buyer. When documents of shipment are presented before the bank which issued the L.C it is bound to make the payment unless the documents so presented ex-facie appear to be forged and fraudulent. No prohibitory order or an injunction can stop such payment which will seriously affect international trade. Dutex Enterprise Co. Ltd. Vs. RMS Textile and Apparels Ltd and others 15 MLR (2010) (HC) 393.

 

 Order 40 rule  1— Appointment of receiver should be made very sparingly in exceptional circumstances—

In a partition suit receiver cannot be appointed without the consent of the other co-sharer defendants in possession of the suit land. Unless there is imminent danger of waste or destruction of the suit property receiver should not be appointed. Salahuddin Khan (Md.) Vs. Most. Halima Akhter Khatoon and others 13 MLR (2008) (AD) 77.

 

Order 39, rules 1 and 2— Necessary requirements  in  granting temporary injunction—

In order to get an order of temporary injunction the plaintiff must prove his primafacie title and possession in the suit land and also must show that the balance of conveniance and inconvenience is in his favour. When it is found that the plaintiff is not in possession of the suit land, no injunction can be granted

In an appropriate case the court of appeal is not precluded from allowing rehearing of the appeal by reason of dispensing with the necessity of serving notice upon the non-contesting defendants. The learned judge of the High Court Division found no illegality in the impugned order allowing rehearing of the appeal. Abdus Shahid @ Shahid and others Vs. Code of Civil Procedure, 1908— Order 41 rule 23— Remand cannot be granted as a matter of course to provide opportunity to fillup lacuna—

When a suit was decided on contest without any material issue undecided or left out, the suit cannot be remanded for providing opportunity to the plaintiff to fillup the lacuna. The apex court deprecated such a belated move and dismissed the appeal. Aslam Khan (Md.) Vs. Haji Abdur Rahim and others 12 MLR (2007) (AD) 149.

 Order    41    rule    27—    Additional evidence at the stage of appeal- when can be admitted—

Law governing the question of admission of additional evidence oral or documentary at the stage of appeal is now well settled. Only the evidence which was tendered but refused by the trial court and the document or witness could not be produced during trial inspite of due diligence which the appellate court considers necessary for adjudication of the dispute between the parties having direct bearing on the result of the suit may be admitted at the appellate stage of the proceedings upon recording the reason therefor, as envisaged under Order 41 rule 27 of the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908. Tahedul Islam (Md.) being dead his heirs Asma Bewa and others Vs. Md. Mojibar Rahman being dead his heirs Jabeda Bewa and others 11 MLR (2006) (AD) 385.

Appeal   against   final   decree   of a partition suit without challenging the preliminary decree is held defective. When the defendants' did not pray for saham before the trial court the High Court Division did not allow them to adduce additional evidence by producing certain documents at the belated stage of the proceedings of the appeal which was held defective as the appellant did not challenge the preliminary decree granting saham to the plaintiff. It is well settled that while reversing the judgment and decree the appellate court must advert to the findings of the trial court and in doing so the appellate court shall have all the powers of the trial court to reassess the evidence on record. Service of summons by hanging on the outer fencing of the dwelling Irmt of the defendant when established is held by the apex court a good service. Laches of advocate when taken as a ground of non-appearance must be established by evidence. Sitdhir Kumar Das and another Vs. Abdul Makk Mia and others 12 MLR (2007) (AD) 57.

 

Order XLI rule 21— Applicable to appeal but not to revision—

Lawyers of the parties are required to remain present in the court when judgments and orders are passed. When the judgments and orders are posted in the daily cause list, there is sufficient compliance with the requirement of informing the parties thereof. Court officers are not found to call the lawyers of the parties and inform them the judgment and orders of the court by taking their signature on the margin of the note sheet. Provisions of Order XLI rule 21 C.P.C are applicable to appeal but not to revision. Once a revision is disposed of on merit by a regular judgment, there is no further scope of rehearing the same. Manirunnessa Khanam and another Vs. Syed Madassir All and another 12 MLR (2007) (AD) 51.

 

Code of Civil Procedure 1908— Order 41 rule 23— Remand cannot be ordered   when   there   are   sufficient evidence on record to effectively decide the issue under controversy—

A court of appeal is empowered to send a suit or case on remand to the trial court where it omitted to frame a material issue and give its finding thereon or where the evidence on record are not sufficient for effective adjudication of the issue under dispute. But where the evidence on record are sufficient the appellate court cannot send,the suit or case on remand as a matter of course. The learned judge of the High Court Division set aside the impugned order of remand without issuing any rule and directed the appellate court below to decide the appeal on merit. Nilchuni Bibi being dead her heirs Ayub All and others Vs. Nedu Sheikh and others 15 MLR (2010) (HC) 174.

 

Order 41 rule 27— Admissibility of additional  evidence  at  the  appellate stage-Evidence Act, 1872— Section 73— Comparison of disputed handwriting by court—

In appropriate case additional evidence may be accepted at the stage of appeal in order to advance the cause of justice—

Though section 73 of the Evidence Act, 1872 empowers the court to compare by itself disputed handwriting and signature, yet the apex court discouraged such practice as unsatisfactory and insisted upon getting the disputed signature and handwriting examined by handwriting expert. The learned judges of the High Court Division also opined that in view of the continuing advancement of the science and technology of examination of handwritings, it is the safe course to obtain the opinion of the expert on the disputed signature and as such upheld the impugned order passed by the appellate court below allowing examination of the disputed documents by expert. Abdur Rashid Miah and another Vs. Md. Zami Shah Neiuaz and others 13 MLR (2008) (HC) 317.

 

Order 41 rule 27— Amendment of plaint and recalling P.W.I at the appellate stage to fill up lacuna— Cannot be allowed—

In the instant case the appellant sought for the amendment of the plaint after 7 years and prayed for recalling the P.W.I at the appellate stage to fill up the lacuna which the appellate court below rejected. The learned judge of the High Court Division held the impugned order perfectly justified and dismissed the revision. Abdul Khair Mollah (Moulavi) Vs. Golafernessa and others 15 MLR (2010) (HC) 203.

 

Section 44A(1)— Filing of certified copy of decree passed by a superior court of foreign country in the court of District Judge for execution—

The decree in question was passed by the Kolkata High Court which the decree-holder filed in the court of the District Judge, Dhaka for execution. The District Judge started an execution case and transferred the same to the court of joint District Judge for disposal. The judgment-debtor filed an application for dismissal of the execution case which the joint District Judge rejected. The judgment-debtor preferred revision which was also dismissed by the Additional District Judge. Against the said order the judgment-debtor preferred revision before the High Court Division. The learned judges of the High Court Division upon analysis of the provisions of the law relating therewith found nothing wrong with the impugned order and directed to dispose of the execution case within 4 months. Al-Arafah Islami Bank Ltd. Vs. M/s Nobel Enterprise 15 MLR (2010) (HO) 351.

 

 Order 47 rule 1— Grounds for review of judgment— Supreme Court Appellate Division Rules, 1988— Order 26— Procedure of review— State Acquisition and Tenancy Act, 1950— Section 3(2)— Hat and Bazar land and its periphery are not retainable—

A review of judgment may be made by the Appellate Division only on the grounds enumerated under Order 47 rule 1 of the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 and in the manner as required by Order 26 of the Supreme Court Rules, 1988. But review of its judgment where the grounds raised were all answered in the judgment in question is not permissible. Hat and Bazar land and the periphery thereof being non-retainable as contemplated under section 3(2) of the State Acquisition and Tenancy Act, 1950 vested in the Government free from all encumbrances after the notification dated 14.4.1956. Nurul Islam Bhuiyan and another Vs. Government of Bangladesh represented by the Deputy Commissioner, Netrokona and others 13 MLR (2008) (AD) 33.

 

Order 47 rule 1— Review of judgment on the grounds specified therein— Bangladesh Supreme Court (Appellate Division) Rules, 1988— Rule 26— Grounds for review of judgment—

Unless there is ground of error of law apparent on the face of the record occasioning failure of justice, Review Petition cannot be entertained. Despite the ouster clause of jurisdiction Civil Court can scrutinize the impugned order to see as to whether the same is passed in accordance with law. An issue which was never raised earlier cannot be raised for the first time at the stage of review before the Appellate Division. Sena Kah/an Sangstha and another Vs. Hazi Sufi Fazal Ahmed being dead his heirs Nazir Mohiuddin and others 13 MLR (2008) (AD) 296.

 

Order   47—   rule   1—   Review   of judgment   can   only   be   made   for correcting error apparent on the face of record-Supreme        Court        Appellate Division Rules, 1988— Order 26— Review of judgment of the Appellate Division can be made on similar grounds as under Order 47 rule 1 C.P.C—

Review of judgment or order of the Appellate Division can only be made on the grounds of correcting error apparent on the face of the record as provided under Order 26 of the Supreme Court Appellate Division Rules and Order 47 rule 1 of the Code of Civil Procedure. When the points raised were answered in the impugned judgment, no review thereof is permissible. Review can not be resorted to for rehearing of the appeal concluded by pronouncement of a regular judgment. Abdul Halim Miah (Md.) and others Vs. Bangladesh represented by the Secretary Ministry of Establishment and others 12 MLR (2007) (AD) 209.

 

Order    47    rule    1—    Review    of judgment   or   order   on   ground   of discovery of new facts or evidence—

When the fact of the alleged ground taken for the review of a decree or order was already within the knowledge of the defendant and the same was stated in the written statement, the provisions of Order 47 rule l.C.P.C are not attracted in the instant case. Matiar Rahman being dead his heirs Shahajada Hosstiin Mallik and others Vs. Assistant Custodian of Enemy Property Lands and Buildings, Bakerganj and others 12 MLR (2007) (AD) 305.

 

Order    47,    rule    1—    Review    of judgment can be made only on grounds enumerated thereunder—

Judgment can be reviewed on the grounds enumerated under Order 47 rule 1 of the C.P.C. Similar grounds for review are also contained in Order 26 of the Supreme Court Appellate Division Rules, 1988. The apex court is not required to answer any academic question however important the same may be. Bangladesh represented by the Secretary, Ministry of Home Affairs and another Vs. Mostafizur Rahman 12 MLR (2007) (AD) 316.

 

 Order 47 rule 1— Review of judgment can be made only on the specific limited grounds— Bangladesh Supreme Court (Appellate Division) Rules, 1988— Order 26 rule 4—Requirements of rule 4 must be fulfilled—

Law is well settled that review does not mean reopening the matter and rehearing of the appeal concluded by pronouncement of judgment. Appeal is a right while review is not. Unless the conditions of rule 1 of Order 47 of the Code of Civil Procedure and the requirements of rule 4 of Order 26 of the Bangladesh Supreme Court (Appellate Division) Rules 1988 are fulfilled Review Petition is not maintainable. The instant review petition having been found not satisfying the above mentioned conditions is dismissed. Azizullnh & Azizur Rahman Vs. Abu Taker Chowdhury and oliters 11 MLR (2006) (AD) 129.

 

 Order    47    rule    1—    Review    of judgment   can   not   be   allowed   for affording opportunity of rehearing— Supreme Court of Bangladesh (Appellate Division) Rules, 1988— Order XXVI (part IV) rule 4— Requirement   of   filing   the   Review petition—

Review is not intended to reopen and rehear the matter finally decided by a judgment. After a matter/dispute has been finally decided by pronouncement of judgment it can not be reopened for rehearing except on the ground enumerated under Order 47 rule 1 and upon fulfilling the requirement of rule 4 of the Supreme Covirt of Bangladesh (Appellate Division) Rules, 1988. Shafiqne Ahmed (Md.) Vs. Bangladesh represented by the Secretary, Ministry of Law, Justice and Parliamentary Affairs and others 11 MLR (2006) (AD) 174.

 

Code of Civil Procedure, 1908— Order 47 rule 1— Review of judgment on ground of non-application or wrong interpretation of law is not permissible—

Where remedies are available under specific law, review of its judgment by the same court on the ground of non-application or wrong interpretation of law is not entertainable. Executive Engineer, Roads and Highways, Noakhali Vs. Naderuzzaman and others 13 MLR (2008) (HC) 136.
