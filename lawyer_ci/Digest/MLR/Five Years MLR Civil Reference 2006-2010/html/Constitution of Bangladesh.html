Constitution of Bangladesh

 

Constitution  (Fourteenth Amendment) Act, 2004— Not violative of basic structure of the constitution—

The provisions of reserved seats for women in Parliament were contained in article 65(3) of the constitution from the very beginning. The constitution (Fourteenth Amendment) Act, 2004 did not introduce in the constitution any new concept and as such it did not change any basic structure of the constitution nor it is violative of fundamental rights. Act No.30 only provides for the procedure of election of women members in the Parliament to the reserved seats on the basis of proportional representation by a single vote and thus is not violative of fundamental rights nor it offends the basic structure of the constitution. Farida Akhter and others Vs. Bangladesh represented by the Secretary Cabinet Division and others 11 MLR (2006) (AD) 237.

 

Constitution of Bangladesh—

llth Amendment does not oust the jurisdiction of judicial review of actions ratified thereunder and as such the detention of former President H.M. Ershad was held illegal and passed without any lawful authority. The apex court declined to review the judgment after fifteen years. Bangladesh represented by the Secretary, Ministry of Home Affairs and another Vs. Mostafizur Rahman 12 MLR (2007) (AD) 316.

 

Constitution of Bangladesh-Preamble and article 7—

The preamble contains the lofty ideals of nationalism, socialism, secularism and democracy as basic principles for which our valiant freedom fighters fought for the independence and millions of them made their supreme sacrifice in laying down their lives by embracing martyrdom. Bangladesh came into being as a sovereign independent state through liberation war. Millions of our valiant freedom fighters were inspired by the high ideals and the framers of our constitution which is the embodiment of the will of the people engrained the said ideals in the constitution as the fundamental and basic features of the constitution. Change of the basic feature tantamouiits to violation of the constitution.

 

Article 7— Supremacy of the Constitution—

The constitution is the embodiment of the will of the people and is the supreme law of the land and all powers shall be exercised under the authority of the constitution and any other lav/ inconsistent therewith is void. Khondaker Dehuar Hossain and others Vs. Bangladesh Italian Marble Works Ltd and others 15 MLR (2010) (AD) Page 287, 296, 357.

 

Article 102— Power of the Supreme Court in matters of judicial review—

Supreme Court has the power of judicial review of all administrative actions and or the laws passed by the parliament so far the vires as to their constitutionality are concerned, ibid 15 MLR (2010) (AD) Page 296, 350, 364.

 

Article 80— Power of the legislature to make law and validate invalid law—

No doubt Parliament has the absolute power to make law in consonance with the constitution. But Parliament can not validate any law which it cannot make, ibid 15 MLR (2010) (AD) Page 299,349.

 

Martial law

Martial law is black law. It is unknown in our constitution. With the proclamation of martial law the supremacy of the constitution is lost and it is subordinated to the M.L.R, MLOS. This usurpation of power through extra constitutional means and the actions made thereunder are illegal, void and non-est which constitute offence of sedition of worst kind, ibid 15 MLR (2010) (AD) Page 303, 309, 316.

 

Fifth amendment of the constitution— Its validation—

Waiver and acquiescence by acceptance of the people—

Proclamations made in violation of the constitution are illegal, void and non-est which can not be validated on the basis of the principle of waiver and acquiescence, ibid 15 MLR (2010) (AD) Page 308, 309, 330.

 

Article 150— Means to protect acts done in transitional period—

Article 150 does not mean to protect the actions done by usurpers in violation of constitution, ibid 15 MLR (2010) (AD) Page 315, 316.

 

Article 142—

Parliament can add some provisions which are innocuous through the procedure under article 142. But it can not make any law chaning the basic feature of the constitution. Similarily it also can not legalise any such illegal actions, ibid 15 MLR (2010) (AD) Page 315, 349.

 

Fifth Amendment of the constitution-Basic concepts never intended the Republic to be a theocratic state by omitting the basic feature of secularism, ibid 15 MLR (2010) (AD) Page 315, 316.

 

Article 9—

Principle of nationalism as Bangladeshi and Bangalee are discussed and Bangladeshi nationalism, is retained, ibid 15 MLR (2010) (AD) Page 327, 332.

 

Secularism as existed in the constitution on 15.8.1975 will revive and the Orders which changed this basic principle is declared by the High Court Division as illegal void and non-est. ibid 15 MLR (2020) (AD) Page 329.

 

Doctrine of necessity and condonation—

Some acts which relate to welfare of the people are condoned on the principles of doctrine of necessity, ibid 15 MLR (2010) (AD) Page 351, 352, 358, 359.

 

Condonation allowed by the Appellate Division—

The Appellate Division approved the judgment and orders of the High Court Division with certain modifications enumerated in the last two paragraphs of the judgment, ibid 15 MLR (2010) (AD) Page 367, 368.

 

Articles 18 and 32 — Enshrines in the constitution the obligation of the state to ensure measures for sound health of its citizen and fundamental right to life- Value Added Tax Act, 1991—

The imposition of value added tax on the medical service in the medical institution, Dental care centre and pathological test in the laboratory being in conflict with constitutional provisions are declared illegal and void —

In view of the provisions contained in articles 18 and 32 of the Constitution the High Court Division declared the imposition of VAT on the medical service and pathological test to be paid by the citizen as illegal and unconstitutional. The Appellate Division affirmed the impugned judgment and order and dismissed the leave petition. Chairman, National Board of Revenue (NBR) Vs. Advocate Ziilhasuddin Ahmed and others 15 MLR (2010) (AD) 457.

Constitution of Bangladesh — Article   21 —  Right   to   life   means meaningful life in healthy environment —

Construction of multi-storied building on the space kept earmarked for open car parking by the Dhaka City Corporation in violation of the master plan and without approval of the plan by the RAJUK is held by the apex court illegal and as such directed demolision of the said construction as being opposed to the spirit of article 21 of the constitution. Sharif Nurul Ambia Vs. Dhaka City Corporation represented by the Mayor and others 11 MLR (2006) (AD) 288.

 

Constitution of Bangladesh — Article 22 and 102 — Separation of judiciary means functional separation — Writ petitioner    being a retired District Judge and a concious citizen can well invoke jurisdiction of the High Court Division to issue writ of co-warranto—

A judicial officer by reason of performing non-judicial functions on deputation does not cease to be member of the judicial service— Nitimala of 2001 held to have the force of law—

Bidhimala of 2002 clearly provides for filling up exclusively 75% posts of Deputy Secretary, Joint Secretary. Additional Secretary and Secretary of the Ministry of Law, Justice and Parliamentary Affairs by the members of the judicial service on deputation. The judicial officers who opted for the service in the Legislative Drafting Wing have already ceased to be members of the judicial service and they are not eligible for appointment and promotion to those reserved posts of the Ministry of Law, Justice and Parliamentary Affairs. Like-wise none of the members of the other cadre or non-cadre service is eligible for appointment or promotion to those reserved posts including the post of Secretary. In that view of the position of law the apex court declared the promotion of writ respondent No. 5 Kazi Habibul Awal as Secretary incharge and then as Secretary illegal, without jurisdiction  and  of no  legal effect. Government of Bangladesh and others Vs. Md. Aftabuddin, retired District and Sessions Judge and another 15 MLR (2010) (AD) 41.

 

Article 27 and 29— Right to equal treatment before law— Principle of legitimate expectation does not hold good when the right is waived—

Right to promotion is based on seniority cum merit. When duly qualified before the selection committee and a fitness list is prepared including the name of the petitioner he could legitimately expect to be promoted to the position of General Manager. But before it was exhausted the list was cancelled and the second list was prepared after selection test in which the petitioner appeared and was unsuccessful. By appearing in the subsequent test for preparing the second list and becoming unsuccessful the petitioner waived his right to be promoted on the basis of the 1st list since cancelled. Syed S.M. Hasan Vs. Bangladesh represented by the Secretary, Ministry of Finance and another 12 MLR (2007) (AD) 192.

 

Constitution of Bangladesh— Article 27 and 102— Equality before law is not always absolute— Classification is permissible-Import Policy of 1997-2000— Article 21— Empowers the authority to relax the conditions—

Import of reconditioned cars of more than 5 years old for personal use and for commercial purpose are two different cases which are distinguishable from the other. While the authority relaxed the conditions for personal use and refused the prayer of the writ petitioner for commercial purpose that does not offend article 27 of the Constitution. Chief Controller of Import and Exports and others Vs. Md. Farnk Ahmed 12 MLR (2007) (AD) 122.

 

Constitution of Bangladesh— Articles 27 and 102— Interference on ground of discrimination—

The writ petitioner was selected for appointment to the post of Principal of Abdul Malek Degree College alongwith others for similar position having similar qualification by the same committee. The petitioner was not allowed the portion of salary under the M.P.O although those others on similar footing were granted the said salary. Having found the impugned action discriminatory offending the fundamental right of equality under article 27, the High Court Division directed the respondents to pay to the writ petitioner the part of the salary under the M.P.O. The apex court upheld the impugned decision as being perfectly justified and accordingly dismissed the leave petition. Government of the People's Republic of Bangladesh, represented by the Secretary Ministry of Education and another Vs. Syed Md. Belayet Hossain 15 MLR (2010) (AD) 512.

 

Article 31 and 42— Right to be treated in accordance with law and the right to own and enjoy property are fundamental rights guaranteed under the Constitution—

The learned judges further held that while passing the impugned order of cancellation and direction of removal of the building the petitioner's fundamental rights guaranteed under article 31 and 42 of the Constitution were violated which eventually warranted intervention of the court. A Roitf Chowdhury and another Vs. Bangladesh represented by the Secretary, Ministry of Works and another 12 MLR (2007) (HC) 177.

 

Constitution of Bangladesh— Article  35(2)—  Prohibits   trial  and conviction  of  a  person  twice  for the same offence— General Clauses Act, 1897— Section 26— Contains similar provision against trial and conviction of a person more than once for the same offence—

From the above provisions it is abundantly clear that a person once tried and convicted by a court of competent jurisdiction for a particular offence shall not be tried and punished for the second time for the same offence. The appellant upon conviction by a Special Martial Law court though having no jurisdiction, had already served out the most part of the sentence and was then released on amnesty. The apex court held it inappropriate and against the interest of justice to reopen and restart the case afresh after lapse of long period and in that view set aside the order of the Sessions Judge. Mohammad Ullah Vs. Sessions Judge, Noakhali and others 12 MLR (2007) (AD) 351.

 

Constitution of Bangladesh-Article 48(3)— Privileged documents are immune from being disclosed or produced before court—

Evidence Act, 1872—

Sections 123 and 162 — Immunity of documents relating affairs of the state — When privilege as contemplated under article 48(3) of the Constitution with regard to certain documents are claimed, the court has to hold inquiry as to the validity of such claim. The apex court set aside the impugned order of calling for the documents as the same was done without holding any such inquiry. Government of Bangladesh, represented by the Secretary, Ministry of Law, justice and Parliamentary Affairs Vs. Md. Shamsul Hilda and others 14 MLR (2009) (AD) 224.

Constitution of Bangladesh — Article 102— High Court Division in a writ petition can not grant substantive relief while making an ex-parte interim order at the initial stage —

The apex court deprecated the practice of granting substantive relief at the initial stage of the proceeding while making interim order. In the instant case the High Court Division by an interim order pending the disposal of the writ petition granted all the substantive reliefs which rendered the proceedings redundant with nothing left for adjudication to the great detriment of the right of defence of the respondent. With strong disapproval to such practice the apex court setaside the impugned interim order. Chairman, Bangladesh Agricultural Development Corporation Vs. S.M. A.H. Imam and others 11 MLR (2006) (AD) 83.

 

Constitution of Bangladesh — Article 102- Adinterim order granting substantive   relief   in   writ   petition cannot be passed

Law is well settled that the High Court Division cannot grant entire relief sought in a writ petition while passing adinterim order. Agrani Bank and another Vs. Essential Garments ltd. and others 11 MLR (2006) (AD) 201.

 

Article 102— Writ petition does not lie against judgment and decree of Artha Rin Adalat—

As there is specific provision of appeal under section 41 of the Artha Rin Adalat Ain, 2003 Writ Petition under article 102 of the Constitution does not lie against judgment and decree of Artha Rin Adalat. Anisur Rahman @ K.M. Ziaitl Haque Vs. Government of Bangladesh and others 12 MLR (2007) (HC) 75.

 

Article  102—  Writ  Petition  against judgment  and   decree   of  Artha   Rin Adalat is not maintainable—

Alternative efficacious remedy is available against the judgment and decree of the Artha Rin Adalat by way of appeal as provided under section 7 of the Artha Rin Adalat Ain 1990 and as such Writ Petition under article 102 of the constitution does not lie against such judgment and decree. Therefore the law on this point is well settled. Bangladesh Agricultural Development Corporation (BADC) represented by Chairman Vs. Artha Rin Adalat, Court No. 3 Dhaka and others 11 MLR (2006) (AD) 308.

 

Article 102— Writ Petition does not lie  against  judgment  and  decree  of Artha Rin Adalat—

Section 7 of the Artha Rin Adalat Ain 1990 provides for appeal against the judgment and decree of Artha Rin Adalat upon depositing 50% of the decretal amount. When the writ petitioner contested the suit by filing written statement and was aware of the suit but did not prefer any appeal and thereafter instituted Writ Petition which in the facts and circumstances is held by the apex court not maintainable. M/S Noor Crokaries and another Vs. Islam! Bank Bangladesh Ltd. and others 11 MLR (2006) (AD) 273.

 

Article 102— Article 117(2)— High Court Division's writ jurisdiction is ousted in respect of matters falling within the jurisdiction of Administrative Tribunals—

As provided under section 4 of the Administrative Tribunals Act, 1980 the Administrative Tribunals have been vested with exclusive jurisdiction to decide all disputes relating to the terms and conditions of a person in the service of the Republic or of the Statutory Authority. The jurisdiction of the High Court Division in such matters is ousted as contemplated under article 117(2) of the Constitution. However the High Court Division can exercise limited jurisdiction only when such matters involve question of determination of vires or constitutionality of any law or rules connected therewith. Merely because the Administrative Tribunal has no power to grant injunction or adinterim order, that does not confer jurisdiction upon the High Court Division to entertain writ petition in such matters. Khalilur Rahman ASP Vs. Md. Kamrul Ahsan and others 11 MLR (2006) (AD) 5.

 

Constitution of Bangladesh— Article 102— Writ Petition does not lie  against  the  decree  of  Artha  Rin Adalat—

Against the decree of the Artha Rin Adalat the petitioner judgment debtor did not prefer appeal but filed the writ petition which having not been maintainable has been summarilv rejected by the High Court Division. The apex court upheld the decision of the High Court Division as one perfectly justified. Saifur Rahman Eman (Md.) Vs. Government of Bangladesh represented by the Secretary, Ministry of Local Govt. and Rural Development and others 11 MLR (2006) (AD) 190.

 

Constitution of Bangladesh— Article  102—  Writ  Petition  is  not maintainable where there is alternative remedy by way of appeal—

Where the executive committee of the Voluntary Organisation was suspended/cancelled by the Registering Authority after proper inquiry as provided under section 9 of the Ordinance, 1961, the petitioner could seek the remedy thereagainst by way of appeal. Since alternative efficacious remedy is available the application under article 102 of the Constitution is held by the learned judges of the High Court Division not maintainable. Shuddhananda Mahathero Vs. Bangladesh represented by the Secretary, Ministry of Social Welfare and others 12 MLR (2007) (HO) 1.

 

Constitution of Bangladesh— Article 102— Cancellation of bid on the  ground  of being  non-responsive not based on material  facts declared illegal—

The respondent No. 1 having fulfilled all the necessary requirements, such as technical, financial and professional was factually responsive. The cancellation of the bid of the respondent No 1 by the Review Panel terming the same as non-responsive without any material ground, has been declared by the High Court Division as illegal which the apex court held perfectly justified. St. Electronics (Infor Soft Ware System) Pvt, Ltd. Vs. Patimas International Sdn. Berhad and others 12 MLR (2007) (AD) 325.

 

Constitution of Bangladesh— Article 102— Writ jurisdiction of the High Court Division cannot be invoked in respect of non-statutory contract—

Contracts which are not made in exercise of the sovereign functions of the Government are non-statutory contracts, the remedy for the breach thereof lies in a suit in the civil court but not in the writ jurisdiction of the High Court Division under article 102 of the Constitution. Managing Director WASA Vs. Md. AH and others 12 MLR (2007) (AD) 361.

 

Constitution of Bangladesh— Article 102— Action of the Government in leasing out the Balu Mahal situated within Chundeecherra Tea Estate to a third party during the continuance of the lease is declared illegal—

The appellant took the lease of the Chundeecherra Tea Estate for a term of 35 years within which the disputed Balu Mahal is situated. The sands of the Balu Mahal which are used for construction purposes are held not minerals and as such the apex court declared the disputed lease of the said Balu Mahal illegal and of no legal effect. National Tea Company Limited Vs. Deputy Commissioner, Habiganj and others 13 MLR (2008) (AD) 189.

 

Constitution of Bangladesh— Article  102-— Writ  petition  against judgment and decree passed in suit by Artha Rin Adalat is not maintainable—

Since there is specific provision of appeal to be preferred against judgment and decree of the Artha Rin Adalat within 30 (thirty) days of the passing thereof upon depositing 50% decretal dues, the writ petition thereagainst is held by the High Court Division as well as the Appellate Division, not maintainable. A.QM Shah Alam Chowdhury Vs. Government of Bangladesh represented by the Ministry of Law, Justice and Parliamentary Affairs and others 13 MLR (2008) (AD) 258.

 

Constitution of Bangladesh— Article 102— Power of the Election Tribunal to pass order for recounting ballot papers—

It is well within the competence of the Election Tribunal to pass order for recounting of ballot papers for the purpose of proper adjudication of the dispute. S.M Fazlul Haque Manik Vs. Md. Habibulkh Baher and others 14 MLR (2009) (AD) 79.

 

Constitution of Bangladesh— Article 102— High Court Division has no power to negotiate price for the auctioned materials—

Evidence Act, 1872— Section 115— Estoppel— When it operates—

The High Court Division can not negotiate price of auction materials which is the exclusive domain of the Government. The writ petitioner upon accepting the auction price of some of the articles has taken delivery of those materials. Thereafter he can not raise objection against fresh auction of the remaining materials and as such he is barred by the principle of estoppel. M/S Ashrafand others Vs. Md. Zahangir Alam and others 14 MLR (2009) (AD) 87.

 

Constitution of Bangladesh—­Article 102— Maintainability of writ petition—

The apex court held the writ petition perfectly maintainable when the dispute involves interpretation of certain law, no disputed fact needs to be decided and   the   alternative   remedy   is   not efficacious. Commissioner of Customs, Customs House Benapole, ]essore Vs. Cab Express (BD) Limited and others 14 MLR (2009) (AD) 294.

 

Constitution of Bangladesh— Article 102— Writ petition does not lie against the dismissal order of the Principal of recognised Non-Government Madrassa as the same is neither a statutory body nor a local authority—

In the instant case the dismissal order of the Principal of recognised Non-Government Madrassa was passed by the Upazilla Nirbahi Officer as the Chairman of the Governing body. The Madrassa is neither a statutory body nor a local authority and as such their lordships of the judges of the Appellate Division held writ petition does not lie against the impugned order of dismissal. Upazilla Nirbahi Officer Vs. Abu Saleh Md. Harunur Rashid and others 14 MLR (2009) (AD) 309.

 

Constitution of Bangladesh— Article 102— When no condition is mentioned in the lease order, the writ petitioner is not required to obtain clearance certificate from the Department of Environment—

As there was no terms and conditions in the lease order the High Court Division held the writ petitioner was not required to obtain clearance certificate from the Department of Environment. Appellate Division affirmed the decision of the High Court Division as perfectly justified. Bangladesh and others Vs. Serajuddin Khan 14 MLR (2009) (AD) 312.

 

Article 102— The power of the High Court Division under writ jurisdiction is limited. It can not exercise the power of appellate court in writ jurisdiction— The apex court firmly held that a fugitive in law and justice is not entitled to get the protection of law unless he surrenders in the proper court—

The writ petitioner was convicted and sentenced in a trial held in his absence. Instead of surrendering before the trial court he instituted the writ petition through his authorised agent which the High Court Division entertained and made conflicting orders from time to time with regard to his bail, stay of the operation of sentence, and the manner of surrendering before the proper court by violating the long established principles of law, norms and practice which the apex court deprecated and disapproved in strong words and directed the convict-writ petitioner to surrender before the trial court. Anti-Corruption Commission Vs. Dr. H.B.M Iqbal Alamgir and others 15 MLR (2010) (AD) 103.

 

Constitution of Bangladesh— Article 102— Writ petition to protect the possession of the disputed flat is held maintainable—

' In the instant case the respondent No.5 having found unable to decide the title informed the writ petitioner that the title of the disputed flat can be decided by a civil court of competent jurisdiction. Thereafter the respondent No. 6 issued the impugned letter to the petitioner directing her to vacate the possession of the said flat. The High Court Division as well as the Appellate Division held the impugned letter passed without any lawful authority. Shaid Ha mid and another Vs. Nilufar Moml-nz and others 15 MLR (2010) (AD) 130.

 

Article 102— Disputed question of facts as to title cannot be decided in writ jurisdiction—

In the instant case the identity of the disputed land is challenged which can not be ascertained without relayment by a survey knowing advocate commissioner  and  as  such  the writ petition is held not maintainable. Sum jit Bose (Raju) Vs. Bangladesh and others 15 MLR (2010) (AD) 206.

 

Article 102— Writ petition is held not maintainable against stay of all actions for registration of two buses purchased by the loan of the bank—

The impugned action of BRTA croped up out of the proceedings relating to suit for recovery of loan. The High Court Division held the writ petition not maintainable. The Appellate" Division also concurred with the impugned decision of the High Court Division. Monir Hossain Vs. The Artha Rin Adalat No. 4, Dhaka and others 15 MLR (2010) (AD) 236.

 

Constitution of Bangladesh— Article 102— Writ petition for giving direction to pay an unascertained amount of money is not maintainable-Writ petition for giving direction to pay an ascertained amount of money is maintainable. But the writ petition is not maintainable when the amount is unascertained. In the instant case the High Court Division directed to settle the pending bills. The Appellate Division found nothing wrong with the impugned order and as such dismissed the leave petition. Bangladesh Water Development Board (BWDB), represented by its Director General, WAPDA Bhaban and another Vs. Golam Rabbani Khan and others 15 MLR (2010) (AD) 402.

 

Article   102—   Right   of   legitimate expectation—

When the Chief of the Administration and the Ministries concerned j recommended for transfer of 160 posts of the project under the revenue budget I but the Ministry of Finance transferred 1 only 118 posts to the revenue budget. As I the remaining 42  posts stand  on  the same footings the legitimate expectation of the petitioners has become lawful right to be treated in the same manner. Government of Bangladesh and others Vs. Md. Shahinoor Bari and others 15 MLR (2010) (AD) 424.

 

Article 102— Writ petition against private institution is not maintainable—

Law is clear that writ jurisdiction can not   be   invoked   against   a   private institution not established by any law. In the   instant   case   the   writ   petition I instituted against the Governing Body of  the Bangladesh Rifles School and College being a non-government institution is held not maintainable. The apex court disposed of the appeal with the observation that the decision of the Education Board is binding upon the appellant school and college and accordingly directed, to reinstate the respondent to his job. Chairman, Governing Body, Bangladesh Rifles School and College and others Vs. Md. Golam Kibria and others 15 MLR (2010) (AD) 497.

 

Constitution of Bangladesh— Article  102— Writ petition seeking direction to the appellants to execute and register the lease deed—

The respondent Samabaya Samity was granted lease of the disputed land for construction of Hawkers Market by a letter granting lease for 30 years.
