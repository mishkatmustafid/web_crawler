Customs Act, 1969

 

Section 25— Assessment of customs duty and VAT on the basis of the S.R.O operative at the time of opening the letter of credit—

In the instant case the High Court Division held the writ petitioners are entitled to assessment of custom duty and VAT for the imported good of secondary quality as per the S.R.O in force at the time of opening the letter of credits. The Appellate Division up held the impugned decision and dismissed the appeals. Mainmunal Islam and others Vs. The Collector of Customs and others 15 MLR (2010) (AD) 227.

 

Section 25 and 30— Importer does not acquire any vested right under S.R.O prevalent at the time of opening the letter of credit—

The apex court held the importer did not acquire vested right under the S.R.O in force at the time of opening the letter of credit as to assessment of the customs duty on imported goods. It is further held that the customs authority was perfectly justified in assessing the duty on the tariff value as per the subsequent S.R.O prevalent at the time the bill of entry was presented. Collector of Customs, Customs House Chittagong and others Vs. M/S Sumi Enterprise 14 MLR (2009) (AD) 290.

 

Section 25B— Assessment of customs duty and other charges on the yellow book value of the imported reconditioned vehicles—

Consequent upon exclusion of pre-shipment requirement, the apex court held in a number of cases consistently that the assessment of customs duty of the imported reconditioned vehicle shall be made on the basis of the yellow book value. Monzurul Islam (Md.) Vs. National Board of Revenue and others 15 MLR (2010) (AD) 211.

 

Section  30(b)—  Assessment of custom duties on the goods imported under letter of credit-Law is well settled that the custom duties on the goods imported under letter of credit shall be assessed on the basis of the rate prevalent on the date the bill of entry is presented before the customs authority—

In the instant case the custom duty was assessed at the rate of 5% although on the date the bill of entry was presented the duty on the goods in question was 0%. The learned judges of the High Court Division having regard to the settled principle of law declared the impugned assessment illegal and of no legal effect.] Shnbnnni Vegetable Oil Industries Ltd, Chittagong Vs. Commissioner of Custom (Import), Chittagong and others 15 MLR (2010) (HC) 437.

 

Section 193— not a bar to invoke writ jurisdiction—

Constitution of Bangladesh— Article 102— Maintainability of writ petition—

The apex court held the writ petition perfectly maintainable when the dispute involves interpretation of certain law, no disputed fact needs to be decided and the alternative remedy is not efficacious. Commissioner of Customs, Customs House Benapolc, ]e.ssore Vs. Cab Express (ED) Limited and others 14 MLR (2009) (AD) 294.
