Section 32— Dying declaration as to the cause of death of the deceased may form the basis of conviction— The quality but not the quantity of evidence which matters—

Circumstantial evidence unerringly pointing at the guilt of the accused can safely form the basis of conviction. Similarly as in the case of husband of a wife killing case, the wife has also equally the same liability to explain as to how her husband met his death when they were residing in the same house at the time of the occurrence.

 

Section 32— Dying declaration voluntary and true has great importance and evidentiary value which can alone form the sole basis of conviction—

A dying declaration has special sanctity in view of the fact that a dying person under the pangs of death can not tell lie. When a dying declaration is found by the court to be voluntary and true this can form the sole basis of conviction without any corroboration. Touhid and others Vs. The State 12 MLR (2007) (HC) 158.

 

Section 35— Presumption of public documents as to their correctness—

Voter list and Kabinnama duly registered under the Muslim (Marriages and Divorce) Registration Act, 1974 are public documents and have presumption of their correctness so long such presumption is not rebutted, these are admissible in evidence. From these public documents it is found that the age of the defendant was about 22 years at the time of the disputed marriage and as such the defendant can not repudiate his marriage with the plaintiff on ground of minority and accordingly the learned judge of the High Court Division upheld the impugned judgment of the appellate court below which decreed the suit by reversing the judgment and decree of the trial court. Azizur Rahman (Md.) Vs. Mosamnwt Nazmun Nahar 13 MLR (2008) (HC) 2460.

Section 73— Though the court can compare disputed handwriting with the admitted one but such comparison has been discouraged by the apex court—

As the science and technology has advanced and the disputed handwritings can best be examined and compared by expert by use of scientific methods, the apex court in such circumstances discouraged the comparison of disputed handwritings by the court as contemplated under section 73 of the Evidence Act, 1872. Status quo order has no binding effect on the Ad-hoc Managing Committee which is not a party to the proceedings and as such the appointments of the teachers made by the Ad-hoc committee are held by the Appellate Division legal and valid. Sarder Md. Hashim Zaman and others Vs. Thana Nirbahi .Officer, Mithapukur, Rangpur and others 13 MLR (2008) (AD) 172.

 

Section 73— Comparison of disputed handwriting by court—-

In appropriate case additional evidence may be accepted at the stage of appeal in order to advance the cause of justice—

Though section 73 of the Evidence Act, 1872 empowers the court to compare by itself disputed handwriting and signature, yet the apex , court discouraged such practice as unsatisfactory and insisted upon getting the disputed signature and handwriting examined by handwriting expert. The learned judges of the High Court Division also opined that in view of the continuing advancement of the science and technology of examination of handwritings, it is the safe course to obtain the opinion of the expert on the disputed signature and as such upheld the impugned order passed by the appellate court below allowing examination of the disputed documents by expert. Abdur Rashid Miah and another Vs. Md. Zami Shah Newaz and others 13 MLR (2008) (HC) 317.

Section  115—  Estoppel—  When  it operates—

The High Court Division can not negotiate price of auction materials which is the exclusive domain of the Government. The writ petitioner upon accepting the auction price of some of the articles has taken delivery of those materials. Thereafter he can not raise objection against fresh auction of the remaining materials and as such he is barred by the' principle of estoppel. M/S Ashraf and others Vs. Md. Zahangir Alam and others 14 MLR (2009) (AD) 87.

 

Section 115— Estoppel by waiver and acquiescence—

When by act, conduct or consent, express or implied a person allows another person to proceed in doing certain act or transactions with bonafide belief, such conduct or consent constitute waiver and acquiescence and the person so allowing is estopped from claiming any right in respect therewith subsequently against the person who acted under such assurance. Major (Retd.) Quazi Hasna Hena Begum Vs. Lt. Col. Kazi Monsurul Islam and others 15 MLR (2010) (AD) 65.

 

Section  115—  Promissory  estoppel and equity—

When a person makes promise to another person who acts thereunder, subsequently the person so making the promise cannot be allowed to fall back from his promise. In the instant case though the Chittagong Port Authority (CPA) did not make any promise to the writ petitioner to grant lease of .075 acre land, but the CPA persuaded the petitioner to make payment of Tk 7,68,722.47 on behalf of the Amin Agency which the CPA accepted under the expectation of getting lease of the land in question. Moreover the writ petitioner filled up the low lying area and the ditch within the knowledge of the CPA. The apex court held in the facts and circumstances and in equity-the writ petitioner is entitled to get lease of the .075 acre land from the CPA. Abul Hashem Vs. International Oil Mills Limited and others 15 MLR (2010) (AD) 117.                    

 

Sections 123 and 162— Immunity of documents relating affairs of the state—

When privilege as contemplated under article 48(3) of the Constitution with regard to certain documents are claimed, the court has to hold inquiry as to the validity of such claim.
