Constitution of Bangladesh 1972

 

Article 35(4)— Notice given by DACO is not violative of article 35(4)—

Anti-Corruption Act, 1957— Section 3— Notice during preliminary enquiry—

A District Anti-Corruption officer is deemed to be an officer-in-charge of a police-station under section 3 of the Anti-Corruption Act, 1957 and as such he can very well exercise his powers under section 94 and 160 of the Code of Criminal Procedure. Giving notice by District Anti-Corruption officer to a person to give statement in preliminary enquiry which is of fact-finding nature at the stage when there is no criminal proceedings started against him, is not violative of the provision of article 35(4) of the Constitution.

 

Abdul Hafiz (Md) and others Vs. Director General, Bureau of Anti-Corruption and others— 3, MLR (1998) (HC) 130.

 

Article 35—

Anti-Corruption Act, 1957— Section 4(1)— Notice calling for Statement of property— during preliminary enquiry—Not violative of fundamental rights-There is nothing wrong in the order passed in pursuance of the satisfaction of the Government directing to submit statement of property under section 4(1) of the Anti-Corruption Act and as such this does not offend fundamental right guaranteed under article 35(4) of the Constitution as the petitioners are not accused in any criminal case following FIR or complaint.

 

Mustafizur Rahman and others Vs. Director General, Bureau of Anti-Corruption— 4, MLR (1999) (HC) 138.

 

Article 104— Inordinate Delay not condoned on plea of doing complete justice—

When the accused inspite of knowledge remained silent for 5 years after conviction, such inordinate delay cannot be condoned on the alleged ground of sickness and on the plea of doing complete justice.

 

Md, Abdw Rahman Vs. The State— 1, MLR (1996) (AD) 447.

 

Article 102—Direction for release of person wrongly put behind the bar—

When an innocent wrong person is apprehended and put into jail to undergo imprisonment for life in place of the actual accused tried and convicted in absentia for murder charge, the High Court Division can rightly interfere and direct his release. The court expressed serious concern against the irresponsible and careless police officer connected therewith and directed payment of exemplary compensation to the innocent person so detained.

 

Shahnewaz (Md) Vs. Government of Bangladesh represented by the Secretary,  Ministry  of Home Affairs and others—3, MLR(1998) (HC) 54.

 

 

Article 102- Public interest litigation in respect of safe custody / judicial custody of minor—

Penal Code- Section 364-Abduction of minor girl- custody of the victim girl—

Disputed question of facts does not come within the writ jurisdiction of High Court Division under article 102 of the Constitution. As the question of safe custody does not involve community interest, so a member of the Public who is not a person aggrieved has no locus standi and cannot invoke writ jurisdiction. Keeping a victim minor girl in 'safe custody/judicial custody has no express provision of law in support thereof but there are series of judicial decisions of our apex court which lay the strong legal basis of judicial custody of minor victim as a matter of long practice in our legal system.

 

Rokeya Kabir (Mrs.) Vs. Government of Bangladesh & others- 5.MLR (2000) (HC) 58.

 

Article 35— Preventive detention— Non-disclosure of grounds— Plea of public interest-Privileged document— court's power to look into— Principles of natural justice—

Freedom of movement of a citizen is a fundamental right guaranteed under the Constitution. Such right of a citizen can be curtailed only on sufficient grounds for preventing prejudicial acts. State has privilege not to disclose fact of any document in the interest of peace or public interest. But when liberty of a citizen is at stake' and when constitutional protection is sought for the constitutional court i.e. the High Court Division of the Supreme Court alone can look into such privileged document in order to be satisfied as to the propriety of the claim of the detaining authority. The detenu can not claim disclosure of such fact pertaining to his detention as a matter of right. Unless there is any such situation warranting privilege of non­disclosure of any such document 'in public interest withholding of such materials from being disclosed to the detenu will tantamount to violation of principle of natural justice and thereby rendering the detention denude of any legal authority.

 

Habiba Mahmud Vs.  Bangladesh-45 DLR (AD) 89.

 

Article 102—

Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898— Section 439A— Revisional jurisdiction— Once exercised—second Revision or writ does not lie-Where procedural remedy is expressly provided for in any particular matter a litigant has no scope to invoke inherent power of the court. On the disposal of a revision under section 439A of the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898 by the Sessions Judge the matter ends there. Thereafter neither second revision nor writ lies on the self same matter.

 

Haji Golam Hossain Vs. Abdar Rahman Munshi- 40 DLR (AD) 196.

 

Article 102— Interference in exercise of writ jurisdiction with conviction—

Permissible in case of trial held without jurisdiction—

High Court Division in exercise of writ jurisdiction can declare conviction of an accused in a trial held without jurisdiction as illegal.

 

State Vs. Zahir and others. 45 DIM. (AD) 163.

 

Article 102— Interference with detention in exercise of writ jurisdiction— on ground of malafide and collateral purpose—

Bangladesh judicial view is different from that of Indian jurisdiction in approach with regard to subjective satisfaction in cases of preventive detention. To follow principles of Indian decisions in cases of preventive detention will be dangerous slip from the well established judicial view of Bangladesh jurisdiction. The validity of the order of detention, has to be tested on the basis of materials before the detaining authority which gave a rational probative value to the order and not based on extraneous purpose beyond the scope of the Act. When this test is satisfied the order is immune from challenge. But when the order is passed for collateral purpose and is malafide one it is illegal and is liable to be struck down.

 

Sajeda Parvin Vs. Government of Bangladesh. 40 DLR (AD) 178.

 

Article 103— Scope of interference by Appellate Division with findings of fact on ground of serious defect—

Appellate Division can well come to its own finding upon re-assessment of evidence on record by reversing the finding of fact of the High Court Division on ground of serious defect.

 

State Vs. Abdus Sattar -  43 DLR (AD) 44.

 

Article 102—Detention for prejudicial activities—Malicious detention— Compensatory cost may be awarded—

Since detention involves curtailment of fundamental rights of freedom of movement and speech guaranteed under the Constitution, the order thereof must be based on specific genuine grounds relating to prejudicial activities. Malicious detention has to be viewed with serious concern and in appropriate cases apart from declaring such order as illegal exemplary compensatory cost may be awarded to be paid by the State as a deterrent measure so that the State instrumentalities do not indulge in reckless and malafide detention of its citizen curtailing the valuable fundamental human rights.

 

Bilkis Akter Hossain Vs. Bangladesh represented by the Secretary Ministry of Home Affairs & others. 2, MLR(1997) (HC) 113.

 

Article 142— Indemnity (Repeal) Act 1996—

Procedure for amendment of the provision of the Constitution— Indemnity Ordinance, 1975— Whether became part of the Constitution by reason of its inclusion in the paragraph 3A and 18 of the Fourth Schedule requiring its amendment through the procedure under article 142 of the Constitution. Indemnity (Repeal) Act 1996— Whether a valid piece of legislation—

Since the Constitution remained suspended during the Martial Law Regime the inclusion of all the Martial Law Regulations, Orders, Ordinance etc. in the Fourth Schedule of the Constitution after their ratification by the Parliament on the lifting of the Martial Law was for the purpose of maintaining continuity of the constitutional process. By reason of such inclusion the Indemnity Ordinance 1975 was never made part of the Constitution and as such this Ordinance could well be amended by the Parliament by simple majority just like ordinary legislation in which case article 142 of the Constitution is not applicable. Thus the Indemnity (Repeal) Act, 1996 is perfectly a valid law.

 

Shohriar Rashid Khan and another Vs. Bangladesh and others. 3,MLR (1998) (AD) 233.

 

Article 142—

Indemnity Repeal Act, 1996 (Act No.21 of 1996)—

Whether ultra vires the Constitution— Indemnity Ordinance, 1975— Paragraph 3A and 18 of the 4th Schedule of the Constitution— Inclusion in the schedule does not form part of the Constitution—­An Ordinance though protected by way of inclusion in the schedule of the Constitution does not form part of the Constitution. The Indemnity Ordinance, 1975 (Ord 50 of 1975) as such not being part of the Constitution does not require two third majority members of the Parliament for its repeal as contemplated by article 142 of the Constitution. When Constitution provides equality of every citizen before law, the Indemnity Ordinance 1975 was violative of the basic principle and fundamental rights guaranteed under the Constitution and as such the Indemnity Ordinance, 1975 being a simple legislation has been rightly and validly repealed by the Indemnity (Repeal) Act 1996 (Act No 21 of 1996) by simple majority members of the Parliament. The Indemnity (Repeal) Act, 1996 being a valid legislation is not ultra vires the Constitution and as such is not liable to be struck down.

 

Shahriar Rashid Khan and Mrs. Mahmuda Raliman Vs. Bangladesh represented by the Secretary, Ministry of Law and others- 2, MLR (1997) (HC) 25.

 

Article 102— The Special Powers Act,  1974—

Section 30- Provisions for appeal-Where the  alternative  remedy  is available  under  section   30  of the Special Powers Act, 1974 normally the writ jurisdiction under article 102 of the Constitution can not be invoked. But when in view of unusual circumstance the convict-appellant was deprived of his legal right of defence and the trial held beyond his knowledge and due to lack of knowledge he could not avail of the 'remedy under section 30 of the Special Powers Act, the extra-ordinary jurisdiction of the High Court Division under article 102 of the Constitution can well be invoked with a view to preventing the miscarriage of justice.

 

Mobarak All (Md) alias Mobarak Ali Mondal Vs. Bangladesh represented by Secretary Ministry of Home Affairs and others. 4, MLR (1999) (HC) 13.

 

Natural Justice—

Natural justice means no person should be condemned unheard and without giving opportunity of defence— 4, MLR (1999) (AD) 10.

 

Show cause notice—

Show cause notice is not a mere technicality or idle ceremony, Show cause notice must not be vague. When it is vague, it is not a show cause notice in the eye of law—4, MLR (1999) (AD) 9.
