Confessional statement of accused is not a substantive piece of evidence against the co-accused and such evidence alone without substantive corroborative evidence cannot form the basis of conviction of a co-accused in a joint trial involving charge of murder under section 302 of the Penal Code.

The Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898— Section 164 and 364— Recording of confessional statement— Requirements of law—

A Magistrate while recording confessional statement of an accused under section 164 must comply with the requirements of section 364 of the Code of Criminal Procedure. Non-compliance with such requirements renders the confessional statement inadmissible in evidence. Similarly omission on thp part of investigating officer to bring all the material witnesses to the scenario of the trial casts doubt on the prosecution case leading to acquittal of accused in heinous crime of murder.

 

The State Vs. Md. Shahjahan— 2, MLR(1997) (HC) 60.

 

The Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898— Section 164 — Confessional Statement of accused — Recorded on oath-illegal—

Confessional statement which is not voluntary and recorded on oath or putting question by the Magistrate being vitiated by illegality can not form the basis of conviction.

 

The State Vs. Haris and others— 2, MLR (1997) (HC) 373.

 

The Code of Criminal Procedure 1898— Section 164— Confessional Statement— Retracted confession- legal effect—

There is no hard and fast rule that a retracted confessional statement should always be rejected. Confessional statement though retracted if found true and voluntary and is recorded on compliance with the requirements of section 364 Cr. PC. and is so testified by the recording Magistrate can well be relied upon and safely form the basis of conviction.

 

The State Vs. Tota Mia and others— 3,MLR(1998) (HC) 27.

 

The Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898— Section 164— Confessional statement when not to form the basis of conviction— Circumstances explained—

When the Magistrate fails to remove the fear of torture by police while recording confessional statement of the accused produced from police custody and the accused is again sent back to the police custody after recording confessional statement, such confessional statement can neither be used against the maker nor against the coaccused so as .to form the basis of conviction because of legal infirmity.

 

The State Vs. Abul Hashem— 3,MLR(1998) (HC) 30.

 

The Code of Criminal Procedure 1898— Section 164— Confessional statement— Partly true—cannot be rejected—

Confessional statement partly true can be accepted and the other part which is not true can be rejected. The accused may also be convicted on the basis of the true part of the confessional statement if found justified warranting such conviction. The court is required to give due weight to both parts of the confessional statement.

 

The State  Vs.  Afazuddin  Sikder— 3, MLR (1998) (HC) 57.

 

The Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898— Section 164- Statements of witnesses recorded under section 164 Cr.P.C.— Not substantive evidence—

Evedince Act, 1872— Section 155—

With regard to the use of previous statements of witness recorded under section 164 Cr.PC the law is well settled. The statements of witnesses recorded under section 164 CrPc are not substantive evidence and as such such statement alone can not form the basis of any conviction. Such statements can be put to impeach the witness at the trial under section 155(3) of the Evidence Act.

The Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898— Section 164— Confessional Statement— Exculpatory statement—Alone does not form basis of conviction-—

Unless true, voluntary and inculpatory in nature confessional statement without independent corroboration cannot form the basis of conviction. Exculpatory confessional statement when not put to the accused drawing his attention during his examination under section 342 CrPC causes prejudice vitiating the trial which is not curable under section 537 CrPC.

 

Abu Jamal and two others Vs. The State— 3, MLR(1998) (HC) 292.

 

Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898— Section 164(3)— Recording of Confessional Statement of accused— In plain paper- Not inadmissible— Penal Code 1860— Section 304— Husband is to explain circumstances under which his wife died—

When the confessional statement of an accused is recorded by a competent Magistrate after due cauton to the accused about the consequence and compliance with the requirements of section 164(3) Cr. P.C. such confessional statement can not be inadmissible in evidence merely by reason of its being recorded in plain paper and one of its sheet not signed by the accused. The husband is under the legal obligation to explain the circumstances under which his wife died while they were living together in the same house.

Code of Criminal Procedure 1898— Section 164—

Confessional statement -admissibility— When no allegation of police torture—

Confessional statement recorded by competent Magistrate after complying with all the legal requirements can well form the basis of conviction. When there is no complain of Police torture, such confessional statement cannot be rejected merely on the ground of the failure of the Magistrate to tell the accused that he will not be handed over to the Police custody whether he confesses or not.

Section 30— Confessional Statement— Crebidility of— Number of witness to be examined—

The credibility and voluntariness of confessional statement becomes doubtful when it is made after a prolonged police custody. Retraction from Confessional Statement must be made at the earliest possible opportunity. Belated retraction from confession on the allegation of police torture and inducement, at the time of examination of the accused under section 342 of the Code of Criminal Procedure is of no avail. Confessional statement made voluntarily and recorded by Magistrate after observing all legal formalities and is not retracted at the earliest opportunity is a valuable peice of evidence which together with other corroborative evidence can form the basis of conviction against the maker and the other coaccused. Prosecution is not bound to examine each and every witness in order to establish the charge against the accused. Non-examination of material witness is fatal to the prosecution. But conviction can be based on the evidence of a solitary witness if he appears to be trustworthy. Long delay in disposal of Death Reference and appeal of condemned prisoner renders ground for commuting the death sentence into one of imprisonment for life.

Section  32- Dying  declaration— When conviction can be based on—

A dying declaration as envisaged under section 32 of the Evidence Act, 1872 is a piece of evidence like any other evidence and must satisfy the court that what is stated therein is true so that the conviction thereon can be sustained.

 

Syed Ahmed and six others Vs. The State— 1, MLR (1996) (HC) 207.

 

Section 32 (1)— Dying declaration— Reliability of—

Dying declaration of a deceased as to the cause of his death is relevant under section 32(1) of the Act. But it is a piece of evidence like other evidence and is equally subject to scrutiny by the court. Unless the dying declaration compared to other evidence appears to be true, it can not alone form the basis of conviction of the accused.

 

The Ltate Vs. Tota Mia— 3,MLR(1998) (HC) 27.

 

Section 32— Dying declaration— Evidentiary value of—­Dying declaration recorded at belated stage sensing the death of the victim is equally acceptable as the one recorded immediately after the occurence. Dying declaration cannot be disbelieved merely on the ground that it is not corroborated by other witness.

 

Humayun Matubbar Vs. The State— 4, MLR (1999) (HC) 176.

 

Section 33— Admissibility of statement when the maker is dead—

Statements recorded under section 164 Cr.P.C by Magistrate in criminal proceedings is admissible under section 33 of the 'Evidence Act when the person making the statement is dead or cannot be produced without inordinate delay or expenses.

 

Haroon Barker and others Vs. The State— 1. MLR (1996) (HC) 81.

 

Section   57—  Judicial  notice of facts—

Court can take judicial notice of facts and matters of Public history. Reference to books of unknown origin and not recognised by courts is discouraged.

Section 73— Power of the court to compare by itself disputed signature or hand-writing—

Opinion of handwriting expert is not binding upon court, nor it is conclusive evidence. It helps the court to arrive at a satisfactory conclusion. Section 73 of the Evidence Act, 1872, empowers the court to compare itself the disputed signature or handwriting as one of the modes of proving certain document. There is nothing wrong when such comparison by Court is fairly and reasonably done. Although conviction under section 467 of the Penal Code is maintained as having been based on consistent legal evidence, the sentence is reduced to the extent already served out by the convict-appellants in view of the long pendency of the appeal and the convict-appellants suffering the pangs of sentence so long.

 

Azahar Alt and others Vs. The State— 5, MLR(2000) (HC) 112.

 

Section 73— Comparing disputed handwritings by the judges themselves—When permissible—­In a case of forgery where the opinion of the Handwriting Expert is not clear and specific as to the disputed handwritings, the judges are entitled under section 73 of the Evidence Act, to compare the handwritings themselves and on such comparison together with other relevant circumstantial evidence conviction of the accused can well be secured.

Section 118— Competent witness— Child— witness- when competent—

A child is a competent witness to testify if it can understand the question put to it and give rational answers thereto.

 

Abdus Salam and others Vs. The State— 1. MLR (1996) (HC) 134.

 

Section 118— Competency of witness—

A Child witness who is capable to understand the question and give rational answers thereto is a competent witness. A judge is not required to hold a . preleminary enquiry to test the competency of a child witness before he proceeds to record his evidence. An endorsement as to the satisfaction of the judge either in the ordersheet, or in the deposition or in the judgment is a sufficient compliance with, the requirement of law.

 

Md. Simj Mia Vs. The State— 2, MLR(1997) (HC) 66.

 

Section 118— Competent witness—Prosecutrix—

When in a rape case the possibility of a prosecutrix's being a collaborator cannot always be ruled out, the safe course shall be that the evidence of the prosecutrix should be corroborated at least by circumstantial or a medical evidence.

 

Hasan (Md) Vs. The State— 4, MLR (1999) (AD) 415.

 

Section 154— Hostile witness—

Simply or the reason that a witness has been declared hostile and has been cross-examined by the party calling him, the evidence of such witness can not be thrown away out of the consideration. The settled principle of law is that the evidence of a hostile witness must be considered and assessed like other evidences whether they are favourable or unfavourable to any party.

 

Amir Hossain Dhali Vs. The State— 2, MLR(1997) (HC) 100.

 

Section 154— As to hostile witness—Evidentiary value of—

There is no provision in the Evidence Act for declaring a witness hostile who does not support the case in favour of the person who called him as witness. Section 154 of the Evidence Act provides that the court may permit the party calling such witness to put questions which could be put to him in cross-examination by the adverse party.

 

Babul (Md) and others Vs. The State— 3, MLR (1998) (HC) 243.

 

Section 137— Right of Cross-examination—

Accused can not be deprived of their right to cross-examine the prosecution witness for the laches of his lawyer. Accused declining to cross-examine the witness in- the absence of his lawyer cannot be the ground for refusal to recall the witness already examined. When the facts arid circumstances of a particular case demand so in the interest of justice, the accused may be allowed an opportunity to cross examine the prosecution witness by recalling.

 

Shamsul Hoque Leda and another Vs. The State— 5. MLR(2000)(HC) 237.

 

Circumstantial evidence—

Circumstantial evidence when well kint by unbroken chain of consistent events leading to irresistible conclusion pointing the finger at the guilt of the accused to the exclusion of any other hypothesis, can alone well form the basis of conviction even in the absence of any ocular evidence.

 

Noted Chandra Mondal (Sree) Vs. The State— 5 MLR(2000)(HC) 338.

 

Section 30- Confessional statement by a co-accused- Use of it in joint trial against other co-accused.

A confessional statement of a co-accused affecting himself and others may be taken into consideration in a joint trial. Such a confessional statement may lend assurance to other evidence on record.

 

Babar Alt Molla and others Vs. The State- 44 BLR (AD) 10.

 

Section 30-Extra-judicial confession made in presence of police— Not admissible—

Extra judicial confession in presence of police personnels after arrest is not admissible in evidence.

 

Mofazzal Hcssain Mollah and others Vs. The State - 45 DLR (AD) 175.

 

Section 32— Dying declaration— Evidentiary value of—

Dying declaration has special sanctity in view of the fact that a dying man is not supposed to tell lie against innocent person. In that consideration a dying declaration is substantive evidence under section 32(1) of the Evidence Act and when found reliable can form the sole basis of conviction of an accused even without corroboration. There is no requirement of law that like confessional statement of an accused as required under section 164(3) of the Code of Criminal Procedure, dying declaration should be recorded by a magistrate. Dying declaration may be recorded by any person available at the moment. Dying declaration in order to be admissible as substantive evidence must be a statement of the person under the pangs of imminent death as to cause of his death or circumstance leading to his death. Dying declaration may be written or it may be verbal or it may even be indicated by signs and gestures in answer to questions.

 

Nurjahan Begum Vs.The State 42 DLR (AD) 130.

 

Section 118— Child witness-When competent—

A child witness who is capable of understanding intelligently the truth and false hood as to certain fact is a competent witness and such a child witness can well be relied upon.

 

Abdul Qaddus Vs. The State - 43 DLR (AD) 234.

 

Section 134 - Conviction may be based on evidence of a single witness—

There is no hard and fast rule as to number of witness to be examined by the prosecution inorder to prove its case. If found reliable evidence of a solitary witness can form the basis of conviction.

 

Abdul Hai Sikder Vs. The State -43 DLR (AD) 95.

 

Section 32— Dying declaration— Evidentiary value of—

A dying declaration is relevant and material evidence in the prosecution of the assailants and a truthful and reliable dying declaration may form the sole basis of conviction , even though it is not corroborated. But the Court must be satisfied that the declaration is truthful. The reliability of a dying declaration should be subjected to a close scrutiny, considering that it was made in the absence of the accused who had no opportunity to test its veracity by cross-examination. If the Court finds that the declaration is not wholly reliable and a material and integral portion of the deceased's version of the entire occurrence is untrue, the court may, in all the circumstances of the case consider it unsafe to convict the accused on the basis of the declaration alone without further corroboration.

 

Thurukannai Pompaiah Vs. State of Myore AIR 1965 (SC) 939.

 

Circumstantial evidence— when can form the sole basis of conviction— In a case resting on circumstantial evidence, the circumstances put forward must be satisfactorily proved and those circumstances should be consistent only with the hypothesis.of guilt of the accused. Again those circumstances should be of a conclusive nature and tendency and they should be such as to exclude every hypothesis but the one proposed to be proved. In other words, there must be a chain of evidence so far complete as not to leave any reasonable ground for a conclusion consistent with the innocence of the accused and it must be such as to show that within all human probability the act must have been done by the accused.

 

Bakshish Singh Vs. State of Punjab, AIR 1971 (SC) 2016.

 

Circumstantial  evidence forming sole basis of conviction-It  is  well  settled  that where  the inference of guilt of an accused person is to be drawn from circumstantial evidence  only,   those  circumstances must, in the first place, be cogently established. Further, those circumstances should be of a definite tendency pointing towards the guilt of the accused, and in their totality, must unerringly lead to the conclusion that within all human probability, the offence was committed by the accused and none else.

Rama Nand Vs. State of Himachal Pradesh. 1981 Cr LJ. 198.
