The Special Powers Act, 1974— Section 3(2)- Grounds of detention—

It is obligatory on the part of the detaining authority to show by affidavit-in opposition that the detenu is not held in custody without lawful authority or in unlawful manner except when the cause or manner is adequately explained in the writ petition in which case the respondent can reply on the materials on record to justify the detention or continued detention otherwise the order of dentention will fall for lack of cause shown. Magistrate has the jurisdiction discharge an accused on the basis of police report exclusively triable by Special Tribunal. Under Section 27 Special Tribunal has been given discretion to take cognizance on the basis of police report against an accussed discharged by the Magistrate.

Special Powers Act. 1974 (Act XIV of 1974)

Section 5(3)- detention-extension of—

The order of detention passed by the District Magistrate/Additional District Magistrate under section 3(2) for more than 30 days is not valid unless in the meantime it; has been approved by the Government u/s 3(3) of the Act.  Government has no power to extend the detention ordered by the District Magistrate / Additional District Magistrate. The Government has power u/s 3(1) to order detention for unlimited period which is subject to sections 10, 11 and provisio to section 12 of the Act.

 

Yeasmin Akhtar Vs. the State— 1. MLR (1996) (HC) 14.

 

Section 3(l)(a) and 3(2)— Government can not extend detention given by the Magistrate—

District Magistrate/Additional District Magistrate is authorised under section 3(2) to issue an order of detention of a detenu for a period not exceeding 30 days which need no approval of the Government. On the other hand the Government under section 3(1)(a) can direct detention independently but it cannot extend the detention granted by the District/Additional District Magistrate under section 3(2).

 

S.M. Feroz Vs. Government of Bangladesh represented by the Secretary Ministry of Home Affairs and others —2, MLR(1997) (HC) 334.

 

Section 3(2)—Detention on grounds of prejudicial acts—

Detention cannot be sustainable in law unless the grounds therefor strictly conform to the ingredients of prejudicial acts within the meaning of section 3(2) of the Special Powers Act, 1974. Freedom of expression and movement, the valuable fundamental rights cannot be throatled and curtailed by colourable exercise of the authority under the Special Powers Act in a democratic polity. When specific case is started under relevant law the sword of damocles i.e. the detention cannot be resorted to.

 

Halima Farzana Vs. Government of the Peoples Republic of Bangladesh and others - 4 MLR (1999) (HC) 255.

 

Section 2(f)- defines prejudicial acts—Section 3(l)(a)- Order of preventive detention can only be made when any of the grounds as defined under section 2(f) exists and not otherwise—

Liberty of movement is an important fundamental right of a citizen guaranteed under the Constitution which can not be curtailed except in accordance with law. When there is no ground that a person is about to engage himself in prejudicial acts which may endanger public order and public safety the Government can not make preventive order to be kept him in detention. No person can be detained on the ground that some other persons are likely to endanger public safety and maintenance of public order. Satisfaction of the Government as to the grounds must be based on genuine materials and must not be subjective satisfaction. Executive action in relation to preventive detention is not immune from judicial review by the Supreme Court. When the order of detention does not conform strictly to the requirements of law it is liable to be struck down.

 

Mustafizur Rahman vs. Bangladesh represented by the Secretary, Ministry of Home Affairs-3, MLR (1998). (AD) 169,

 

Constitution of Bangladesh— Article 35—Preventive detention— Non-disclosure of grounds— Plea of public interest-Privileged document— court's power to look into— Principles of natural justice—

Freedom of movement of a citizen is a fundamental right guaranteed under the Constitution. Such right of a citizen can be curtailed only on sufficient grounds for preventing prejudicial acts. State has privilege not to disclose fact of any document in the interest of peace or public interest. But when liberty of a citizen is at stake and when constitutional protection is sought for the constitutional court i.e. the High Court Division of the Supreme Court alone can look into such privileged document in order to be satisfied as to the propriety of the claim of the detaining authority. The detenu can not claim disclosure of such fact pertaining to his detention as a matter of right. Unless there is any such situation warranting privilege of non­disclosure of any such document in public interest withholding of such materials from being disclosed to the detenu will tantamount to violation of principle of natural justice and thereby rendering the detention denude of any legal authority.

 

Habiba Mahmud Vs. Bangladesh-45. DLR (AD) 89.

 

Constitution of Bangladesh— Article   102—  Interference   with detention    in    exercise    of   writ jurisdiction— on ground of malafide and collateral purpose—

Bangladesh judicial view is different from that of Indian jurisdiction in approach with regard to subjective satisfaction in cases of preventive detention. To follow principles of Indian decisions in cases of preventive detention will be dangerous slip from the well established judicial view of Bangladesh jurisdiction.

 

The validity of the order of detention has to be tested on the basis of materials before the detaining authority which gave a rational probative Value to the order and not based on extraneous purpose beyond the scope of the Act- When this test is satisfied the order' is immune from challenge. But when the order is passed f6r collateral purpose and is malafide one it is illegal and is liable to be struck down.

 

Sajeda Parvin  Vs.   Government of Bangladesh. 40 DLR (AD) 178.

Section 30— Provision of appeal—Alteration of sentence into one of non-schedule offence- not permissible—

Section 30 of the Special Powers Act, 1974 provides for appeal against the judgment and .order of the Special Tribunal. In an appeal under section 30, the High Court Division can not alter the conviction and sentence passed by Special Tribunal under section 376 into one under section 342 of the Penal Code which is not triable by the Special Tribunal under the Special Powers Act.

 

Abdur Rahman and others. The State—4. MLR (1999) (AD)25.

An absconding accused may be tried in his absence after publication of proclamation in two daily news papers. Section 3(l)(a)—  Detention on ground of serious allegations— when Criminal case is pending

If the detaining authority is satisfied that the detenu might indulge in prejudicial activities and there are serious allegations against him, order of his detention may be made in Order to prevent him from indulging in prejudicial activities even when there is criminal case pending against him;

 

Jabitia Mahmud Vs. 'Bangladesh - 45 DLR (AD) 89.

 

Article 102— Detection for prejudicial activities-Malicious detention Compensatory cost may be awarded

Since detention involves curtailment of fundamental rights of freedom of movement and speech guaranteed under the Constitution, the order thereof must be based on specific genuine grounds relating to prejudicial activities. Malicious detention has to be viewed with serious concern and in appropriate cases apart from declaring such order as illegal exemplary compensatory cost may be awarded to be paid by the State as a deterrent measure so that the State instrumentalities do not indulge in reckless and malafide detention of its citizen curtailing the valuable fundamental human rights.

 

Bilkis Akter Hossain Vs. Bangladesh represented by the Secretary Ministry of Home Affairs & others. 2, MLR(1997) (HC) 113.

 

Section 30— Provisions for appeal-Where the alternative remedy is available under section 30 of the Special Powers Act, 1974 normally the writ jurisdiction under article 102 of the Constitution can not be invoked. But when in view of unusual circumstance the convict-appellant was deprived of his legal right of defence and the trial held beyond his knowledge and due to lack of knowledge he could not avail of the remedy under section 30 of the Special Powers Act, the extra-ordinary jurisdiction of the High Court Division under article 102 of the Constitution can well be invoked with a view to preventing the miscarriage of justice.

 

Mobarak All (Md) alias Mobarak Ali Mondal Vs. Bangladesh represented by Secretary Ministry of Home Affairs and others. 4, MLR (1999) (HC) 13.
