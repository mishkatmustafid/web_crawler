                                        Criminal Law Amendment Act, 1958

 

Section 6(5)— Sanction of Government for prosecution—

Penal Code— Section 21— officers and   employees of Cooperative Societies are held to be public servants—

In view of the provisions of section 2 of the Criminal Law Amendment Act, 1958 read with section 21 of the Penal Code the officers and employees of a Cooperative Society registered under the Cooperative Societies Act, are held to be public servants for whose prosecution sanction of the government under section 197 of the Code of Criminal Procedure is a mandatory pre-requisite without which trial of such person is a nullity and in that view of the matter the case is remanded to the trial court for retrial after obtaining sanction.Iman Ali Mallik (Md.) Vs. The State, 6 MLR (2002) (HC) 162.

Criminal Law Amendment Act, 1958—

Section 6(5)— Sanction for prosecution of a public servant—

Sanction of the Government for prosecution of a public servant as required under section 197 Cr.P.C. and section 6(5) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act, 1958 is a mandatory prerequisite for prosecution of a public servant. Sanctioning authority must take into consideration the relevant materials constituting the offence and should not act mechanically as a matter of course. Sanction order which is exfacie too mechanical and vitiated by non-application of mind is no sanction in the eye of law and as such the trial held thereon is without jurisdiction consequently rendering the conviction and sentence illegal and not sustainable in law.Syed Mustafizur Rahman Vs. The State, 6 MLR (2001) (HC) 213.

Criminal Law Amendment Act, 1958—

Section 10— Limitation for appeal against conviction and sentence passed in a case tried in the absence of accused as held by the High Court Division starts from the date of the knowledge about the judgment.

Where in a case the process were not served upon the accused and the case was tried in absentia keeping the accused completely in the dark while staying abroad ending in conviction. The convict-appellant returned home after about 12 years and then filed the appeal within 30 days of knowing about the judgment and order of conviction. The High Court Division considering the peculiar facts and circumstance of the case in relation to the relevant provisions of law admitted the appeal as being one filed within time. Eklimur Reza Khan Vs. The State, 10 MLR (2005) (HC) 323.

Criminal Law Amendment Act, 1958—

Section 10(4)—Withdrawal of prosecution—Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898— Section 494— Withdrawal of Prosecution  with consent of the Court—

Power of the Court where consent of the court is the legal necessity for withdrawal from prosecution has to be exercised Judiciously and not mechanically as contemplated by section 10(4) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act, 1958 read with section 494 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898.Loskar Mohammad Mostain Billah Vs. The State, 7 MLR (2002) (HC) 265.
