                                                              Evidence Act, 1872

Section 3 and 30— Evidentiary value of confessional statement—

Penal Code, 1860—

Section 302— Charge of murder-Legal requirement as to proof of—

The well settled principle of law is that the confessional statement of one accused cannot be used against the other co-accused. Moreover the confessional statement recorded under section 30 is not an evidence within the meaning of section 3 of the Evidence Act, 1872. When none of the eyewitnesses of the occurrence said a single word against the convict-appellant, this is factually a case of no evidence. Therefore the impugned order of conviction and sentence which is not based on any legal evidence is set aside with a serious stricture against the learned trial Court judge.Abu Syed Vs. The State, 6 MLR (2001) (HC) 237.

Evidence Act, 1872 —

Section 3 and 30- Confessional Statement of an accused is not evidence against the other co-accused—

Confessional statement of one accused cannot be used against the other co-accused without independent corroboration. Circumstantial evidence when unerringly points at the guilt of the accused can form basis of conviction.

When material witnesses are withheld by the prosecution without satisfactory explanation and the evidence brought on record are contradictory, the prosecution case becomes doubtful leading to the acquittal of the accused. Khokon Vs. The State, 8 MLR (2003) (HC) 409.

Evidence Act, 1872—

Section 3 and 30— Evidentiary value of confessional statement of co-accused— Need for corroboration—

Section 133 and 114— Accomplice is competent witness—

Confessional statement of co-accused is not substantive piece of evidence. Again though accomplice is competent witness the rule of prudence insists upon corroboration by other relevant evidence in order to ensure sustainability of conviction based thereon. Ershad AH Sikdar and others Vs. The State, 9 MLR (2004) (HC) 192.

Evidence Act, 1872—

Section 3 and 114(g)— Adverse presumption against non-examination of material witness— Section 30— Confessional Statement of co-accused—

Absconsion by itself is no ground for conviction. Confessional statement of an accused can not be used against other co accused without independent corroboration. Non-examination of material witness without satisfactory explanation raises adverse presump­tion against prosecution. Emdadul Haque (Md.) @ Raza Mia and others Vs. The State, 8 MLR(2003)(HC) 1376.

Evidence Act, 1872— Section 8 & 9 (c)— Aboscondance of accused- No ground for presumption | of guilt—Though abscondance of accused is I no ground for presumption as to the guilt of the accused, long abscondance lends support to the guilt of the accused. The State Vs. Ershad AH Sikdar and Uthers, 8 MLR (2003) (HC) 137.

Evidence Act, 1872— Section 11— Alibi— Alibi taken during trial— Proof thereof—

Section 342 Cr.P.C. provides for an opportunity to the accused to explain his position in relation to the evidence brought on record against him. He may file documents which form part of his statement. He is not required to prove those document. Such statement of accused is not evidence within the meaning of section 3 of the Evidence Act, 1872.

Neither the prosecution nor the court can use such statement against the accused except for his benefit. Alibi taken by the accused during trial must be proved by him to the satisfaction of the Court.Minhaz and another Vs. The State, 6 MLR (2001) (AD) 166.

Section 11— Alibi—

Alibi taken as defence when not proved lends support to the prosecution —

Charges may well be established by circumstantial evidence.

Inquest report is not substantive evidence.

In case of conflict the post mortem report prevails over inquest report—State Vs. Md. Ainul Haque, 9 MLR (2004) (HC) 393..

Evidence Act, 1872— Section 11— Acquittal on proof of Alibi-When satisfactorily established by evidence such as attendance Register that the accused was on duty in his office at the relevant time of the occurrence, the accused may be acquitted on such plea of alibi. Nurul Islam Vs. Abdul Malek and another, 8 MLR (2003) (AD) 37.

Evidence Act, 1872—

Section 27—Statement made by an accused in police custody when admissible in evidence—

General rule of evidence is that statement made by an accused in police custody is not admissible in evidence. Section 27 of the Evidence Act is an exception to this rule. Statement of an accused made while in police custody leading to recovery of incriminating material is admissible in evidence. But where there are public eye witnesses of the occurrence who do not support the prosecution case no conviction can be based on the evidence of police personnel only.Ziaur Rahman @ Zillur Vs. The State, 6 MLR (2001) (HC) 99.

Evidence Act 1872— Section 30— Evidentiary value of confession when retracted—

Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898— Section 164 and 364— Confessional statement    of  accused— Legal requirements for recording—

Confessional statement when found true and voluntary may well form the basis of conviction. Confessional statement partly inculpatory and partly exculpatory may be considered together with other corroborative evidence. Un-explained Police Custody indicates police torture and coercion. Suspicion is no evidence. When there is direct evidence motive of murder is immaterial. In case of retracted confession corroborative evidence will be necessary to sustain conviction.Abdul Qaium Vs. The State, 6 MLR (2001) (HC) 358.

Evidence Act, 1872—

Section 30— Confessional statement of accused forming basis of conviction Circumstantial evidence can also form basis of conviction— 

Law is well settled that crime need not be always proved by direct ocular evidence. Circumstantial evidence may well form the basis of conviction. Accused can well be convicted on the basis of his confessional statement when found true and voluntary. Confessional statement of an accused cannot be used against other co-accused unless such confessional statement is corroborated by other independent evidence. Circumstantial evidence may also be considered as corroborative evidence. State Vs. Mir Hossain and others, 10 MLR (2005) (HC) 91.

Evidence Act, 1872—

Section 32- Dying declaration- Its evidentiary value-

Dying declaration has special sanctity as valuable piece of evidence. When a dying declaration as to the cause of death of the victim is accepted by Court as true it can form the sole basis of conviction of the assailant.The State Vs. Abdul Barek and others, 7 MLR (2002) (AD) 17.

Evidence Act, 1872—

Section 32- Dying declaration-Written or oral- when proved- can form the sole basis of conviction—

The law on dying declaration has by now been well-settled. Dying declaration written or oral when proved and is accepted unhesitatingly by court as true account of the cause of death of the victim can well form the basis of conviction, Sttlifn (Md.) Vs. The State, 7 MLR (2002) (HC) 20.

Evidence Act, 1872—

Section 32- Dying declaration even oral when established by consistent evidence can form the sole basis of conviction—

Dying declaration oral or documentary has special sanctity. When established as true by consistent evidence oral dying declaration can very well form the sole basis of conviction.The State Vs. Abdul Hatem, 9 MLR (2004) (HC) 228. 

Evidence Act, 1872— Section 32- Dying declaration-Evidentiary value for conviction—

Dying declaration has a special sanctity in view of the fact that a dying man as expected cannot tell lie and as such a dying declaration when considered true can alone form the basis of conviction.

Order of acquittal which is perverse and is based on findings on improper appreciation of evidence need be set aside. The High Court Division in such a case is required to interfere with the order of acquittal which is shockingly unconscionable. The State Vs. Rashid Ahmed and others, 7 MLR (2002) (HC) 147.

Evidence Act, 1872—

Section 114(g)— Adverse presump­tion when can be drawn- Non-examination of charge sheeted witness—

There is no hard and fast rule that each and every charge sheeted witness must be examined by the prosecution. What is required is that the prosecution must prove the charge with cogent, consistent and reliable evidence on record beyond doubt. Non-examination of any or some charge sheeted witness not vital by itself is no ground for drawing adverse presumption against the prosecution. Rakhal Chandra Dey alias Rakhal Chandra Dev Vs. The State, 8 MLR (2003) (AD) 61.

Evidence Act, 1872— Section 114(g)— Presumption of non-examination of vital witness—

Non-examination of vital witness raises presumption adverse against the prosecution resulting in acquittal of the accused. Rahima Khatun and another Vs. The State, 9 MLR (2004) (HC) 123.

Evidence Act, 1872— Section 118— Evidence of child witness is admissible—

The cardinal principle of criminal jurisprudence is that the prosecution must prove the charge against the accused with consistent and reliable evidence beyond all reasonable doubt. When material evidence are withheld and the evidence brought on record are inconsistent and the charge does not stand proved to the hilt beyond all reasonable  doubt  the  accused is entitled to be acquitted on benefit of doubt. Such doubt must not be imaginary or fanciful. State Vs. Abul Kashem. 10 MLR (2005) (HC) 164.

Section 133 and 114(b)-Accomplice is competent witness-corroboration by independent evidence when can be dispensed with—

Section 133 of the Evidence Act, 1872 provides that an accomplice is a competent witness and conviction may be based on the sole evidence of accomplice when found trustworthy. Section 114(b) provides for precaution insisting upon corroboration by independent evidence. But in appropriate case the court in its discretion may dispense with the requirement of corroboration. The State Vs. Ershad Ali Sikdar and others, 8 MLR (2003) (HC) 132. 

Evidence of Partisan witness—

Evidence of a witness can not be rejected on the ground of his being partisan. Evidence of partisan witness when found reliable can well form the basis of conviction. The State Vs. Ershad Ali Sikdar and others, 8 MLR (2003) (HC) 134.

Evidence Act, 1872—

Section 134- No particular number of witness is necessary to prove the charge—

Law does not prescribe for any particular number of witness to prove the charge. Conviction may be awarded on the basis of evidence of solitary witness if found reliable. The State Vs. Ershad AH Sikdar and others, 8 MLR (2003) (HC) 134.

Minor discrepancies in evidence may be ignored—

Acquittal on ground of m tor discrepancies in evidence of witness is not permissible which should be ignored. The State Vs. Ershad AH Sikdar and others, 8 MLR (2003) (HC) 135. 

Benefit of doubt- as ground for acquittal—

To be a ground for acquittal benefit of doubt must be reasonable and should not be imaginary and fanciful. The State Vs. Ershad Ali Sikdar and others, 8 MLR (2003) (HC) 135.

Part of the evidence disbelieved-Remaining part may be accepted—

There is no legal bar to accept part of the evidence where the other part is disbelieved. The State Vs. Ershad AH Sikdar and others, 8 MLR (2003) (HC) 135.

Cruelty to Women (Deterrent Punishment) Ordinance, 1983—

Section 4(c)— Punishment for offence of rape—

Penal Code, I860—

Section 376- Punishment for offence of rape— Alteration of conviction and sentence from section 4(c) of the Ordinance 1983 to section 376 of the Penal Code—

In a case which was tried by the Special Tribunal and the accused was convicted and sentenced for offence of rape and when subsequently it was found that the offence should have been tried by the Session Judge where the same person presiding over both the Session Court and Tribunal, the conviction and sentence has been altered from section 4(c) of the Ordinance, 1983 into one under section 376 of the Penal Code having regard to the legislative intent and purpose instead allowing the ends of justice frustrated by technicalities.

It is further held by the High Court Division that conviction and sentence can well be based on the solitary evidence of the prosecutrix if it inspires confidence of the Court.

Section 134 of the Evidence Act, 1872 does not provide for any particular number of witness to prove any particular charge, the golden rule of evidence being that evidence has to be weighed but not counted. Shibu Pada Acharjee Vs. The State, 8 MLR (2003) (HQ 275.

Evidence Act, 1872—

Section 134— Evidence of Solitary witness can form basis of conviction—

Long Absconsion of accused immediate after occurence is important circumstantial evidence—

Golden rule of evidence is that conviction may be based on evidence of solitary witness when found trustworthy. Wasim Mia (Md.) and another Vs. The State, 9 MLR (2004) (HC) 162

Evidence Act, 1872—

Section 134— Conviction may be awarded on the basis of evidence of a solitary witness if found trustworthy—

It is well within the competence of the trial court to convict an accused for the offence proved during trial although he was not specifically charged with under the relevant section of the law as contemplated under section 236 and 237 of the Code of Criminal Procedure. Confessional statement-judicial or extra-judicial when found true and voluntary can well form basis of conviction. Prosecution is not bound to produce each and every witness mentioned in the charge sheet. Abettor is also liable to the same sentence as the principal offender. State Vs. Md. Abdns Samad Azad and another, 10 MLR (2005) (HC) 46.

Evidence Act, 1872— Circumstantial evidence—

Generally in criminal trial the accused has no liability to prove his innocence. Prosecution has to prove the charge against him beyond shadow of all reasonable doubt in order to secure conviction. But in a wife killing case when both the husband and the deceased wife were living in the same house at the time of the occurrence the husband has heavy liability to explain the circumstance under which his wife met with her death. When the explanation offered is found false, the circumstantial evidence strong and inconsistent with the innocence of the accused husband unerringly pointing at his guilt can safely form the basis of his conviction.

Since the Sessions Judge is also the Judge of the Nari-O-Shishu Nirjaton Daman Adalat the sentence passed under section 11 (Ka) of the Ain of 2000 can be altered into one under section 302 of the Penal Code when the commission of the murder is proved to have been caused as one unrelated with demand for dowry.State Vs. Kamrnzzaman alias Mantii, 10 MLR (2005) (HC) 346.
