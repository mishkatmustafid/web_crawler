Section 5 and 14— Cancellation of bail on ground of abuse of the privilege of bail—

Bail in a non-bailable case is a concession to the accused in trust. Such bail can be cancelled if the accused abuses the privilege of bail. Tayazuddin and another Vs. the State, 9 MLR (2004) (HC) 20.

Nari-O-Shishu Nirjatan (Bishesh Bidhan) Ain, 1995— Section 6(1)— offence of rape—

Children Act, 1974—Trial of Juvenile offender—

When the offender is a child below 16 years of age he has to be tried under the Children Act, 1974. Court is under a duty in such situation to undertake enquiry to ascertain the age of the accused, otherwise the trial of a child under the common law will be without jurisdiction rendering the conviction and sentence a nullity. Monir Hossain (Md.) Vs. The State, 6 MLR (2001) (HC) 275.

Section 6(1)- Minimum punishment for offence of rape can not be reduced in appeal —

Accused can well be convicted for the offence of rape on the basis of solitary evidence of the victim when her evidence appears in all probabilities to be reliable and trustworthy. There is no imperative of the law that the court shall always look for independent corroboration in rape case. Since the minimum punishment for rape is imprisonment for life as provided under section 6(1) of the Nari-O-Shishu Nirjatan Daman Ain 1995, there is no scope to reduce the sentence in appeal under section 423 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898.Manirid Islam (Md.) Vs. The State, 8 MLR (2003) (HC) 27.

Section 6(2)— Offence of rape and murder and the proper sentence—

When the charge of rape and murder is established by circumstantial evidence supported by consistent witness the accused can safely be convicted and the proper sentence is the death sentence. Prosecution is not required to produce each and every witness during trial. Reasonable doubt depends on degree of doubt.State Vs. Awal Fakir (Md.). 9 MLR (2004) (HC) 297.

Section 6(3)— Offence of rape and punishment—

Confessional statement recorded under section 164, Cr.P.C. when found true and voluntary can form the sole basis of conviction against the confessing accused. It is not binding upon other co-accused but such confessional statement when corroborated by independent evidence can also form the basis of conviction of other non-confessing accused.Nuruddin alias Shahabuddin and another Vs. The State, 10 MLR (2005) (HC) 379.

Section 6(4)— For the offence of rape and murder, the proper sentence is death—

In case of custodial death the custodian has to explain the circumstances under which the victim died—

When it is established that the victim was taken to the custody of the accused in the dead hours of the night and in the following morning her dead body was found with injuries on throat and her private parts with sign of rape and the explanation offered being not satisfactory the sentence of death has been held by the apex court as perfectly justified. Mainiri Haque and another Vs. The State., 9 MLR (2004) (AD) 321.

Section 9(c)— offence of abduction for committing rape—

Delay in lodging F.I.R when satisfactorily explained is not fatal. Even when the charge of rape is not proved, conviction can well be based on evidence of the victim girl when found reliable and inspires confidence for the offence of abduction. Misti and others Vs. Section 9(ka)- Offence of immoral act punishable under section 9(G)—

Taking nude photographs of victims by force for the purpose of black mailing is highly immoral act.—

When major charge of rape fails accused may be punished for minor offence of immoral act. No observation on extraneous matter not relevant to the merit of the case should be made in the Judgment against outsider.

In a case charge of rape when fails, the accused who took nude photographs of the victim by force may well be punished under section 9(G) of the 1995 Ain when such a highly immoral act Is proved beyond doubt though no charge was framed therefore. Since the accused were fully aware of the details of the occurrence and cross examined the prosecution witnesses on this point, such conviction is perfectly maintainable as no prejudice was caused to the accused.Rajib Kamrul Hassan and three others Vs. The State, 6 MLR (2001) (AD) 70.   

Nari-O-Shishu Nirjatan (Bishesh Bidhan) Ain, 1995—

Section 10(1) and 14— Causing death of wife for dowry— Offence triable by Nari-O-Shishu Nirjatan Daman Adalat. When death of wife caused not for dowry offence is triable by the Sessions Judge—

News paper report not admissible in evidence—

When the death of a wife is caused for dowry, the Nari-O-Shishu Nirjatan Daman Adalat is vested with jurisdiction to try such offence. Otherwise the offence of murder is triable by the Session Judge.

News paper report is not admissible in evidence without the reporter being examined on oath. 

When evidence on record materially contradict with regard to the manner of occurence and cause of death of the victim, conviction and sentence passed thereon cannot be sustainable. Osena Begum and another Vs. The State, 9 MLR (2004) (HC) 57.

Section 10(1)— Offence of causing death of wife for dowry— Conviction and sentence can be based on circumstantial evidence— 

Circumstantial evidence can form the basis of conviction when the same unerringly points at the guilt of the accused. The State Vs. Nasiruddin (Nasiruddin Vs. State), 9 MLR (2004) (HC) 82.

Nari-O-Shishu Nirjatan (Bishesh Bidhan) Ain 1995—

Section 10(1)— Punishment for causing death of wife for dowry—

Charge can well be proved by circumstantial evidence—

There is no imperative of the law that every commission of offence should be proved by direct ocular evidence. When the husband fails to offer any satisfactory explanation as to how his wife was murdered, when they were living in the same house at the time of the occurence, the husband accused can well be convicted on the basis of circumstantial evidence.State Vs. Md. Kaloo, 10 MLR (2005) (HC) 101

Section 12— Offence of child trafficking and the penalty—

Evidence Act, 1872—

Section 118— Evidence of child witness is admissible—

The cardinal principle of criminal jurisprudence is that the prosecution must prove the charge against the accused with consistent and reliable evidence beyond all reasonable doubt. When material evidence are withheld and the evidence brought on record are inconsistent and the charge does not stand proved to the hilt beyond all reasonable doubt the accused is entitled to be acquitted on benefit of doubt. Such doubt must not be imaginary or fanciful. State Vs. Abul Kashem, 10 MLR (2005) (HC) 164.
