                                                  Special Powers Act, 1974

Section 30(I)(a)— Detention  ordered by the Government— Grounds to be served upon the detenu—

Government has the power under section 3(1)(a) of the Special Powers Act, 1974 to issue detention order for 90 days more but in so doing it shall have to supply the grounds to the detenu as contemplated under section 8 of the Act. Hedayet Ali on behalf of Md. Abbas Ali Vs. Government of Bangladesh represented by Secretary Ministry of Home Affairs, 7 MLR (2002) (HC) 427.

Special Powers Act, 1974— Section 3(l)(a) and 3(2)— Power of the Government and District Magistrate to order detention.

Government has power tinder section 3(1)(a) of the Special Powers Act, 1974 to pass independently order of detention in order to prevent any person from doing any prejudicial act. But at the time of issuing the detention order the detenu must be supplied with the grounds of detention.

When grounds of detention are not supplied as required by section 8 of the Act the detention order becomes illegal and denude of any legal effect warranting release of the detenu. When criminal case is started for certain offence, the accused cannot be detained under the Special Powers Act for the same. Nazrul Islam Vs. The State and others, 8 MLR (2003) (HC) 222.

The Special Powers Act, 1974

(Act XIV of 1974)—

Section 3(2)— Detention should be on reasonable ground— Compensatory cost awarded in case of capricious detention.

Power of the District Magistrate to order detention of a person for prejudicial act under section 3(2) of the Special Powers Act, 1974 is extra­ordinary which should be exercised judiciously and not whimsically and capriciously. Korban (Md.) Vs. Government of Bangladesh and others, 8 MLR (2003) (HC) 179

Special Powers Act, 1974—

Section 3(2)— Detention-validity-vagueness of grounds—

G.D. Entry on vague allegations alone can not be the basis of detention-Detention in curtailment of fundamental rights on vague and unspecified allegations based on a G.D. entry in denial of opportunity of proper representation and its continuance for further period when the detenu has already been granted bail in a regular case started in pursuance of the said G.D. entry are held to be illegal. Alamgir alias Alam Vs. The State, 6 MLR (2001) (HC) 133.

Special Powers Act, 1974 —

Section 3(2)—Detention for prejudicial acts—Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898— Section   491— Writ of Habeas corpus— Constitution of Bangladesh— Article 102 (2) (b) (i)— Infringement of fundamental rights—

Under section 491 of the Code either the detenu himself or a third person on his behalf can invoke the jurisdiction of the High Court Division challenging the legality of detention. But under article 102(2)(b)(i) of the Constitution only the aggrieved person may apply.

District Magistrate or Additional District Magistrate has no jurisdiction to order detention of a person in the Metropolitan Area where the Chief Metropolitan Magistrate can pass such order. The authority while ordering detention must act judicially and not arbitrarily. Anwar Hossain (Md.) and others Vs. The State and others, 8 MLR (2003) (HC) 385. 

Special Powers Act, 1974—

Section 3 and 8(2)— Detention— Grounds for detention to be supplied within 15 days— Effect of order of detention—

As soon as the order of detention is signed by the District Magistrate it takes effect. Grounds for detention shall have to be supplied to the detenu within 15 days of making the order as mandated under sub-section (2) of Section 8 of the Special Powers Act, 1974 faihire of which renders the order of detention illegal. Habibur Rahman Vs. The State, 6 MLR (2001) (HC) 311.

Special Powers Act, 1974—

Schedule- Offence—Joint trial of Schedule and non-schedule offence—

Offences included in the Schedule of the Special Powers Act, 1974 are exclusively triable by Special Tribunal. Joint trial  of  schedule  and non-schedule offence is not permissible Batnl Rabidas and another Vs. The State, 6 MLR (2001) (HC) 349.

Special Powers Act, 1974—

Section 25A(b)— Offence of counterfeiting currency note—

Knowledge about the currency as counterfeit and means rea and attempt to use the same are the necessary ingredients which constitute the offence punishable under section 25A(b) of the Special Powers Act, 1974. When these essential ingredients are not established by the prosecution, the convict-appellant as village house wife is acquitted of the charge despite the fact of recovery of counterfeit currency from her possession. Omela Bibi Vs. The State, 7 MLR (2002) (HC) 84.

Special Powers Act, 1974—

Section 25(b}(2)— Offence of Smuggling— Proof of contrabands—

Chemical examination of phensydil—

When it is not shown that the seized articles were contraband goods and when the prosecution evidence are sharply contradictory on material points, conviction of the convict-appellant can not be sustained in the eye of law. Non-appealing convict may also get the benefit of acquittal when the whole prosecution case is found to have been not proved. Rajn Ahmed and others Vs. The State, 7 MLR (2002) (HC) 112. 

Special Powers Act, 1974—

Section 27— Special Tribunal to take cognizance upon police report —

Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898—

Section 200 and 190— Power of Magistrate to direct investigation by police—

It is not correct to say that the Magistrate who has power to take cognizance can only direct the police to investigate into offence. But in a case where the magistrate has no power to try the offence he' can send the complaint to the police officer for investigation without examining the complainant under section 200 Cr.P.C. Where the Magistrate examines the complainant Linder section 200 Cr.P.C. and then sends the complaint to the police for investigation, this is an irregularity curable under section 537 Cr.P.C. Where upon receipt of police report in writing the Special Tribunal took cognizance of the offence complained of as in the instant case, the proceedings do not suffer from any legal infirmity warranting quashment thereof. Golam Rahman (Md.) Vs. Bazlur Rahman and another, 6 MLR (2001) (HC) 232.

Special Powers Act, 1974—

Section 27(6)— Trial of accused in absentia— Mandatory requirements of law as to publication of proclamation in news papers—

Cruelty to Women (Deterrent Punishment) Ordinance 1983—

Section 4 and 9 — Offence are triable by Special Tribunal—

As provided under sub-section (6) of Section 27 of the Special Powers Act, 1974, the Special Tribunal shall publish in at least two daily news papers proclamation directing appearance of absconding accused before the tribunal on a date fixed. Without such publication the trial will be illegal and without jurisdiction. Special Tribunal can not act on the notification published by Magistrate under section 339B of the Cr.P.C. In every such case service of P & A under section 87 and 88 of the Code of Criminal Procedure shall be equally necessary for providing the reasons for the satisfaction of the Tribunal For holding trial of the accused in absentia. Samsul Alam Vs. The State, 6 MLR (2001) (HC) 297.

Special Powers Act, 1974—

Section 27(6)— Trial in absentia—Legal requirements— Accused in custody cannot be tried in absentia—

Special Tribunal himself has to direct by specific order publications in two daily newspapers requiring the appearance of the accused on certain date. But the accused who is in custody in connection with other case cannot be tried in absentia treating him as absconder. Such a trial being illegal and without jurisdiction the conviction and sentence are set aside with direction for retrial in accordance with law. Monir and Shamim Vs. The State, 6 MLR (2001) (HC) 301.

Special Powers Act, 1974— Section 29 and 34B— Exclude the application of other law—

The learned judges of the High Court Division upon interpretation of the provision of section 29 and 34B together with the preamble of the Special Powers Act, 1974 and having regard to the legislative intent and purpose held the concessionary provision of deducting the period of custody from the sentence as contained in section 35A of the Code of Criminal Procedure 1898 not applicable to the case of offence triable under the Special Powers Act, 1974. It is further held that the conviction can well be based on the unimpeachable evidence of police personnel without corroboration by independent public witness. Hint Miah Vs The State, 10 MLR (2005) (HC) 388.

Special Powers Act, 1974— Section 30—Appeal against judgment and order of conviction and sentence—

Order of conviction and sentence which is not based on evidence on record is no judgment and order in the eye of law and the Tribunal has no jurisdiction to pass any such order of conviction and sentence. In a case where the Special Tribunal passed judgment and order of conviction and sentence without any legal evidence on record, such order of conviction and sentence can be declared illegal and passed without jurisdiction in writ petition although the petitioner could not prefer appeal against the same under section 30 of the Special Powers Act, 1974 in the circumstance beyond control. Alam Hossain @ Ibrahim Vs. Government of Bangladesh and others, 8 MLR (2003) (HC) 13.

Special Powers Act, 1974—

Trial Procedure—Section 35A Cr.P.C. not applicable to accused tried by the Special Tribunal—

Special Powers Act is a special law providing special procedure for speedy trial of certain offences and for deterrent punishment. The scheme of the Special Powers Act being as such the provision of section 35A of the Code of Criminal Procedure shall not be applicable to sentence of imprisonment passed under the Act, 1974 for adjustment with the period of custody before trial. Hazrat AH alias Dulal Vs. The State, 10 MLR (2005) (HC) 134.
