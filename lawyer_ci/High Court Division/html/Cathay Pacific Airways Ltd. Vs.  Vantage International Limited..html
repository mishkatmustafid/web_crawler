Company Act (XVIII of 1994)

Sections 255, 256 and 264

Other than the “Official Receiver”, this Court may appoint any person/s competent to assist this Court in dealing with the actions/steps required to be taken after passing the Order of winding up of a company towards its dissolution. Usually, this Court opts to appoint either an Advocate or a Chartered Accountant or any director/employee of the company-in-liquidation as the “Official Liquidator” of the company. It is for this Court to decide as to who shall be appointed as the “Official Liquidator” (whether Official Receiver or any other person), how many (one or more) OL should be appointed and how much remuneration they will be paid. Section 264 of the Companies Act provides that any Official Liquidator (be it Official Receiver or any other person) may appoint an Advocate for assisting him in appearing before this Court, subject to the sanction of this Court. When the “Official Receiver” (OR) is appointed as the “Official Liquidator” (OL), there would be only one OL; no Assistant Official Liquidator (AOL) or Additional Liquidator (Additional OL) would be appointed by this Court. However, the Official Receiver (OR) may seek approval of this Court to appoint an Advocate on his behalf so that the said Advocate can represent the ‘OR-turned-OL’ before this Court. An Advocate is granted permission by the Court to perform the functions of an Advocate before this Court. In other words, an Advocate is allowed to appear before this Court only for carrying out the Court-related works, for example, making submissions, filing affidavits, applications, reports etc. But s/he is not permitted to deal with the Bank accounts or any other administrative functions. The Bank account must be opened and operated by the ‘OR-turned-OL’ himself; not by the appointed Advocate. It follows that when the ‘OR’ is appointed as the ‘OL’, there is no provision for appointing more than one ‘OL’ except taking assistance of an Advocate to send him in this Court. When an Advocate or Chartered Accountant or any Director/employee of the liquidated-company is appointed as the ‘OL’, this Court may consider for appointment of more than one ‘OL’. If more than one ‘OL’ are appointed, they all would be regarded as the ‘OLs’ as there is no provision in the Companies Act to name them Assistant/Additional ‘OL’.                                              .....(11, 13, 14 and 15)

JUDGMENT

 

M. Khurshid Alam Sarkar, J. This application is filed by the legal representative of the “Official Receiver” (hereinafter referred to as the OR). The OR has been appointed in this case by this Court as the ‘Official Liquidator’ (OL). The legal representative of the ‘OR-turned-OL’ is portraying himself before this Court as the Assistant Official Liquidator (hereinafter referred to as ‘AOL’). The legal representative of the OR has filed this application in an expectation to get a sanction from this Court of an amount of Tk. 27,29,037/- (twenty seven lac twenty nine thousand and thirty seven) towards payment of his own salaries & entertainment allowance and that of his two office peons for the period of October, 2016 to February, 2018, from the fund of any company which has been wound up by the Order of this Court and its Bank account is being operated by the OR.

 

2.             After taking up the application for disposal, a query as to under what authority this Court is empowered to pass an order for paying the salaries, allowance, incidental expenses etc of the Official Receiver’s office was raised by this Court to the legal representative of the OR, Mr. Moksadul Islam, Bar-at-law. In reply thereto, Mr. M. Moksadul Islam submits that these payments are being made by this Court for years and, in support of his aforesaid contention, Mr. Islam submits a number of Orders passed by this Court in the past. However, when this Court asked Mr. Islam to show the legal authority of this Court to pass such types of Orders, he could not show any provisions of law from any Act of Parliament, Rules, Regulations or any Government Office Order. Under the circumstances, this Court decided to settle the issue once and for all upon examining the relevant provisions of the law of the land.

 

3.             In carrying out the said exercise, this Court, at first, ventured to look for the expression “Official Receiver” in the Official Receivers Act, 1938 (hereinafter referred to as the OR Act) and, upon perusal of the entire OR Act, I find Sections 3 & 4 of the OR Act to be relevant for the on-going exercise.

Appointment, status, rights, liabilities etc of Official Receiver and Deputy Official Receiver;

3. (1)       There shall be an Official Receiver and there may be one or more Deputy Official Receivers.

(2)              The offices of Official Receiver and of Deputy Official Receivers (if any) shall be civil posts under the Government.

(3)              Subject to the provisions of sub-section (2) of Section 6, all rights, privileges, titles and liabilities of the Official Receiver subsisting immediately prior to the commencement of this Act shall accrue to and vest in the Official Receiver appointed under this Act.

Appointment of Official Receiver as receiver and his duties, rights and liabilities as such;

4.   Subject to, and in accordance with, the provisions of this Act and of Rules made thereunder but notwithstanding the provision of sub-section (2) of Section 3, the Official Receiver may be appointed a Receiver by the Court in respect of any property, and save as provided in this Act, he shall have the same powers, duties and liabilities, shall be entitled to the same rights and privileges and shall be subject to the same control by the Court as any other receiver so appointed.

 

4.             From a plain perusal of the above two provisions, it appears to this Court that there should be an office for the ‘Official Receiver’ with one ‘Official Receiver’ and one or more ‘Deputy Official Receiver’ who are to be regarded as the civil post-holders under the Government. The Court may appoint the ‘Official Receiver’ or the ‘Deputy Official Receiver’ as the receiver of any property.

 

5.             Then, I endeavoured to find out the term “Official Receiver” in the statute book of the Companies Act, 1994 (shortly, Companies Act) with an aim to know its meaning and purport. In definition clause of the Companies Act, the aforesaid terminology has not been placed. The words have been employed in a number of occasions under Part V of the Companies Act which begins with Section 234.   According to Section 234 of the Companies Act, winding up of a company may be either (i) by the Court or (ii) voluntary or (iii) subject to the supervision of the Court. Section 241 of the Companies Act stated circumstances in which a company may be wound up by the Court and Section 247 of the Companies Act incorporates the effect of winding up Order. In Section 249(3) of the Companies Act, it is provided that where the Court makes an order for the winding up of a company, it shall, except where a liquidator is appointed simultaneously, forthwith cause intimation thereof to be sent to the ‘Official Receiver’. Now, a question arises, as to who is the Official Receiver? In Section 251 of the Companies Act, the term “Official Receiver” has been defined and this Section also provides the provisions for appointment, duty and power of the “Official Receiver”. The provisions of Section 251 of the Companies Act are reproduced below:

Section 251: Vacancy in the office of liquidator:- (1) For the purpose of this Act, so far as it relates to the winding up of companies by the Court, the term “official receiver” means the official receiver attached to the Court, or, if there is no such official receiver, then such person as the Government may, by notification in the official Gazette, appoint for the purpose. (2) On the making of a winding up order the official receiver shall become the official liquidator of the company and shall continue to act as such until his further continuance is terminated by an order of the Court. (3) The official receiver shall, as the official liquidator, forthwith take into his custody and control all the books, documents and the assets of the company. (4) The official receiver shall be entitled to such remuneration as the Court shall fix.”

 

6.             From a plain reading of the above provisions, it appears to this Court that an ‘Official Receiver’ means a person, who has been attached to the Company Bench by the Government and s/he shall act as the ‘Official Liquidator’ whenever the Company Bench appoints her/him in any liquidated-company. An ‘Official Receiver’ is required to be always attached to the Company Bench and, in absence of such “attached Official Receiver”, the Government may appoint a person as the “Official Receiver” for the purpose of attaching the said person to this Court and the said “attached Official Receiver” having been appointed as the “Official Liquidator” shall assist this Court in dealing with the winding-up matters.

 

7.             When this Court asked Mr. Moksadul Islam to explain as to under what capacity he is appearing before this Court, in other words, whether he is appearing as the legal representative of the “Official Receiver” or as the “Official Receiver”/“Deputy Official Receiver”, he informs this Court that he has been appointed as the “Assistant Official Liquidator” (AOL) by an office order dated 27.06.2010 issued by the Ministry of Law & Justice and, in a bid to substantiate his above contention, he produces the aforesaid appointment letter before this Court and further informs this Court that there is a practice of this Court to confirm such an appointment.

 

8.             Mr. M. Islam then refers to an order dated 05.07.2010 passed by this Court, pursuant to an application filed by Mr. Islam for approval of his appointment as the ‘AOL’ by the Ministry on 27.06.2010, by which Mr. Islam’s appointment as the AOL was approved by this Court. Mr. M. Islam in this connection submits that it has become an established practice of the Government to appoint an Advocate of this Court to perform the duty of AOL by way of being engaged by the Ministry of Law and, thereafter, to receive salaries, remuneration and allowance against the duties performed by an AOL. He forcefully argues that the above practice has received the recognition of law inasmuch as this Court has consistently been passing Orders from time to time to approve the appointment of such AOL and this Court is also passing necessary orders from time to time to pay salaries, remunerations and allowances.

 

9.             After hearing Mr. M. Moksadul Islam on the question of appointment of an “Assistant Official Liquidator”, it has now become imperative for me to examine the relevant provisions regarding appointment of Official Liquidator as provided in Section 255 of the Companies Act, which are reproduced below:

255. Appointment of official liquidator.- (1) For the purpose of conducting proceedings in winding up a company and performing such duties in acting thereto as the Court may impose, the Court may appoint a person or persons, other than the official receiver, to be called an official liquidator or official liquidators.

(2) The Court may make such an appointment provisionally at any time after the presentation of a petition and before the making of an order for winding up, but shall, before making any such appointment, give notice to the company unless for reasons to be recorded it thinks fit to dispense with such notice.

(3) If more persons than one are appointed to the office of official liquidator, the Court shall declare whether any act, by this Act required or authorized, to be done by the official liquidator is to be done by all or any one or more of such persons.

(4) The Court may determine whether any and what security is to be given by any official liquidator on his appointment.

(5) The acts of an official liquidator shall be valid notwithstanding any defect that may afterwards be discovered in his appointment.

Provided that nothing in this sub-section shall be deemed to give validity to acts done by an official liquidator after his appointment has been shown to be invalid.

(6) A receiver shall not be appointed for the assets in the hands of an official liquidator.

 

10.         From a plain reading of Section 255(1) of the Companies Act, it appears to me that other than the “Official Receiver”, this Court may appoint any person/s competent to assist this Court in dealing with the actions/steps required to be taken after passing the Order of winding up of a company towards its dissolution. Usually, this Court opts to appoint either an Advocate or a Chartered Accountant or any director/employee of the company-in-liquidation as the “Official Liquidator” of the company.

 

11.         Then, comes the provisions regarding salary of the Official Liquidator, which is provided in Section 256 of the Companies Act, which run as follows:

256. Resignations, removals, filling up vacancies and compensation.-(1) Any official liquidator may resign, or may be removed by the Court on due cause shown.

(2) Any vacancy in the office of an official liquidator appointed by the Court shall be filled up by the Court and until the vacancy is so filled up the official receiver shall be and act as the official liquidator.

(3) There shall be paid to the official liquidator such salary or remuneration by way of percentage or otherwise, as the Court may direct and if more liquidators than one are appointed, such remuneration shall be distributed amongst them in such proportions as the Court directs.

 

12.         From a combined reading of Sections 255 & 256 of the Companies Act, it is vividly clear that it is for this Court to decide as to who shall be appointed as the “Official Liquidator” (whether Official Receiver or any other person), how many (one or more) OL should be appointed and how much remuneration they will be paid.

13.         At this stage, I consider the provisions of Section 264 of the Companies Act to be relevant for an effective disposal of this application.

264. Provision for legal assistance to official Liquidator.- The official liquidator may, with the sanction of the Court, appoint an advocate or attorney entitled to appear before the Court to assist him in the performance of his duties;

Provided that, where the official liquidator is an advocate or attorney, he shall not appoint his partner, unless the latter consents to act without remuneration.

 

14.         Section 264 of the Companies Act provides that any Official Liquidator (be it Official Receiver or any other person) may appoint an Advocate for assisting him in appearing before this Court, subject to the sanction of this Court.

 

15.         Thus, it is not difficult to understand for any one with ordinary prudence that when the “Official Receiver” (OR) is appointed as the “Official Liquidator” (OL), there would be only one OL; no Assistant Official Liquidator (AOL) or Additional Liquidator (Additional OL) would be appointed by this Court. However, the Official Receiver (OR) may seek approval of this Court to appoint an Advocate on his behalf so that the said Advocate can represent the ‘OR-turned-OL’ before this Court. An Advocate is granted permission by the Court to perform the functions of an Advocate before this Court. In other words, an Advocate is allowed to appear before this Court only for carrying out the Court-related works, for example, making submissions, filing affidavits, applications, reports etc. But s/he is not permitted to deal with the Bank accounts or any other administrative functions. The Bank account must be opened and operated by the ‘OR-turned-OL’ himself; not by the appointed Advocate. It follows that when the ‘OR’ is appointed as the ‘OL’, there is no provision for appointing more than one ‘OL’ except taking assistance of an Advocate to send him in this Court.

 

16.         When an Advocate or Chartered Accountant or any Director/employee of the liquidated-company is appointed as the ‘OL’, this Court may consider for appointment of more than one ‘OL’. If more than one ‘OL’ are appointed, they all would be regarded as the ‘OLs’ as there is no provision in the Companies Act to name them Assistant/Additional ‘OL’.

 

17.         Likewise, the provisions regarding remunerations are also very much clear as provided in Section 256(3) of the Companies Act and Rule 131 of the Companies Rules, 2009. The remuneration may be a fixed-amount or a percentage of the amount of assets left over/saved after payments of all dues & debts. When the ‘OL’ is the ‘OR’, the question of payment of salary does not arise at all, for, s/he is a Government Officer and the Government would pay the salary as per her/his scale. If an Advocate is appointed to assist the ‘OR-turned-OL’, her/his fees may be paid either from the Government Exchequer or from the Bank account opened and maintained by the ‘OR-turned-OL’ for the liquidated-company. If the fees of the Advocate (legal representative of the ‘OR-turned-OL’) is paid from the account of the liquidated-company, then the amount paid as the fees of the appointed-Advocate, if any, and as the salaries of the employees, if recruited on contract basis for the purpose of taking care and looking after the properties of the liquidated company, the same shall be deducted from the Government commission to be paid into the Government Exchequer at the time of dissolution of the company. The Government would be entitled to get the commission only in the event when there will be money available in the Bank account of the OL after payment of all debts, bills and other dues. The maximum commission the Government may get from the residue is its 5%.

 

18.         From the examination of the relevant laws, my view is that the appointment of Mr. Moksadul Islam has been given by the ‘OR-turned-OL’ as his legal representative, not as the AOL. The sanction of this Court for appointment of any legal representative should be sought for by the ‘OR’ separately for each case in which this Court would appoint the ‘OR’ as the ‘OL’. Under the provisions of Section 264 of the Companies Act, the ‘OR-turned-OL’ is statutorily required to obtain approval of this Court to engage an Advocate on his behalf and it is, then, for the ‘OR-turned-OL’ to pay the legal fees either from the Bank Account opened and run for the company-in-liquidation, for whom the Advocate’s service was sanctioned by this Court, or to pay from the National Exchequer upon arranging funds from the Ministry of Finance.

 

19.         Following the above examination of the law, I consider it useful to make some observations and also to pass some directions upon the concerned authority to ease their respective tasks and duties.

(i)                  This Court has been informed that no ‘OR’ has been appointed under the Official Receivers Act, 1938. Under the circumstances, the Government, as per Section 251(1) of the Companies Act, is obliged to appoint, by publication in the Official Gazette, a person who shall remain attached to this Court. The Government may appoint any Judicial Officer or Advocate or a Chartered Accountant to perform the duty of the ‘OR’ who shall remain attached only to this Court and have an office room in the present office building of the ‘Official Receiver’. This Court is told that the Secretary of the Ministry of Law & Justice has been performing the functions of the ‘OR’ for last many years. This Court takes the above information to be incorrect, for, ‘OR’ under the OR Act is a full-time Government civil post and there is no provision in the OR Act to depute a Government Officer in the post of ‘OR’ as in-charge. The scenario might be that the Government, by publication of Gazette Notification under Section 251(1) of the Companies Act, has appointed the Secretary, Ministry of law as the “attached person to the Company Bench”. Although no such Gazette has been produced by Mr. Moksadul Islam before this Court, I assume that the Secretary, Ministry of law, having been appointed as the “attached person to the Company Bench” through Gazette Notification, has been engaging Mr. Moksadul Islam as his legal representative. Since the Law Secretary remains overwhelmingly overburdened with the nationally important duties and assignments, it is very logical for him to appoint an Advocate on his behalf.

(ii)          However, it would be a better course of action for the Government to appoint, by publication in the Gazette notification, a meritorious Judicial Officer from the District Judge/Additional District Judge level as ‘OR’ who will have the opportunity to prepare her/himself as the future Company-Judge. S/he shall attend this Court regularly so that this Court is effectively assisted towards expeditious disposal of the winding-up matters. S/he will be competent under the Companies Act, 1994 to open and operate accounts for the companies wound-up by the Order of this Court and, also, shall be entitled to file necessary application/s before this Court as well as to take all other steps until a wound-up company is finally dissolved. The Government may also choose to appoint Mr. Moksadul Islam, Bar-at-Law, as “the attached person to the Company Bench” to ease its tasks given that he has, by now, gained sufficient experience in handling the winding up matters. 

(iii)        It is further told that although nearly 20 (twenty) employees and officials of different pay-scales are working in the office of the ‘OR’ situated at Baily Road, Dhaka, but there is hardly any function to be performed by them for not assigning the ‘OR’ as the ‘OL’ in sufficient cases by this Court or as the “Receiver of the property” under other statutes, namely, Bankruptcy Act, 1997, Civil Procedure Code, 1908 etc. It is further informed that pursuant to the recommendation made by the “Enam Commission” in the mid-eighties, three offices of this Republic, namely, the office of the ‘OR’ under the Official Receivers Act, 1938, the office of the “Official Trustee” under the Official Trustees Act, 1938 and the office of the “Official Administrator” under the Official Administrators Act, 1912 have been amalgamated into one office of “Administrator General and Official Trustee” (AGOT). It should be noted here that there is no legal basis for existence of such office given that “Enam Commission”; having been formed under a Martial Law Proclamation, has lost its legality and validity by the verdict of the Appellate Division passed in the Fifth Amendment Case. The net result is that currently there is neither any ‘Official Receiver’ nor any ‘Official Administrator’ nor any Official Trustee in the country. I am not interested in making any observation on whether the Government should take necessary steps to appoint an ‘Official Receiver’ and one or more ‘Deputy Official Receiver’ as per the mandate of Official Receivers Act, 1938. All that I wish to record here is that since the work-force of a more or less 20 (twenty) persons of this office (AGOT) is almost sitting idle, appointment of an ‘Attached Person in the Company Bench’ as provided in Section 251(1) of the Companies Act may make the officials/employees busy with the works, if this Court allocates sufficient cases to the said “Attached Person”. Presently, this Court is allocating most of the cases in favour of individual Advocates appointing them as the ‘OL’ on the prayer of the petitioners, for, the petitioners are aware of the present non-functional status of this office. When an “Attached Person to the Company Bench” would be appointed from the categories of the people illustrated hereinbefore in para (i), this Court unhesitatingly would appoint “The Attached Person” in all the winding up cases as the ‘OL’ and, consequently, the aforesaid work-force of 20 (twenty) persons would find themselves busy with works.

(iv)        If the Secretary, Ministry of law is desirous to continue to perform the duty of ‘Attached Person of the Company Bench’ (Official Receiver ‘OR’-for the purpose of Company Bench) under the provisions of Section 251(1) of the Companies Act, he is at liberty to do so and, in that event, either he himself would remain present in this Court regularly, at least once a day for an hour, or he may appoint any Advocate on his behalf. But the Bank account of the wound-up companies must be opened and operated by the Secretary, Ministry of Law as the ‘OL’ and, further, he is required to obtain sanction from this Court to appoint an Advocate on behalf of the ‘OL’ for each case separately, as per the provisions of Section 264 of the Companies Act.

(v)          Given the fact that this Company Matter was filed in the year 2007 and the ‘Official Receiver’ (OR) was appointed as the ‘Official Liquidator’ (OL) on 17.12.2009 and due to not being assisted properly by the OL in this case, the petitioner who is a foreign company, is getting frustrated, thus, till the time the ‘Official Receiver’ is appointed as per the mandate of the Official Receivers Act, 1938, the Secretary, Ministry of Law & Justice is directed either to appoint a meritorious Judicial Officer of the rank of District Judge or Additional District Judge or Mr. Moksadul Islam, Bar-at-Law through Official Gazette as an ‘Attached Person to the Company Bench’ (as the substitute of the ‘Official Receiver’) for the purpose of carrying out the functions imposed upon the said ‘attached person’ by the provisions of Section 251(1) of the Companies Act at the Company Bench or, if the Secretary, Ministry of Law & Justice wants to carry out the duties of  the aforesaid “Attached Person”, he is directed (a) to open the Bank Account of this company with his signature and (b) to do the required legal works in  this Court either by himself or through appointment of an Advocate upon obtaining prior sanction from this Court. The Secretary, Ministry of Law & Justice is directed to take any of the above two steps, as per his convenience, within the shortest possible time, preferably within 1 (one) month from the date of receipt of this Order.

(vi)        The Register General of the Supreme Court of Bangladesh is directed to place a copy of this Order before the General Administration (GA) Committee of the Supreme Court of Bangladesh, so that the GA Committee may share this issue with the Honourable Law Minister. After consulting with the Hon’ble Law Minister about the reasons behind appointment of a meritorious Judicial Officer or Mr. Moksadul Islam, Bar-at-Law, as the “Attached Person to the Company Bench”, the GA Committee of the Supreme Court would be in a position to properly guide the Secretary, Ministry of Law and Justice to do the needful for publication of the Official Gazette for the purpose of the aforesaid appointment.

 

20.               Before parting with this Judgment and Order, I find it pertinent to note here that this Court is usually reluctant to set out any guidelines for the Executives unless this Court finds that it would create a deadlock in the performance of the Judiciary if necessary guidelines are not laid down for a particular Division or Department of the Government. Given the fact that the Ministry of Law & Justice is manned predominately by the Judicial Officers, this Court initially, after filing of this application on 13.05.2018, asked Md. Moksadul Islam to sit with the concerned officials of the aforesaid Ministry to resolve the issue. Probably, in October, 2018, Mr. Islam informed this Court that the Deputy Secretary (Admin) of the Ministry of Law & Justice has opined that if this Court passes appropriate Order to pay off Mr. Islam’s salary, then, the Ministry will take necessary steps for payment of his salary. In the light of the fact that this Court cannot direct the Government to pay off Mr. Islam’s salary, this Court made some observation with regard to the remuneration of Mr. Islam as per the desire of the Law Ministry and Mr. Islam, in course of passing a Judicial Order in a different Company Matter. In the meanwhile, whenever Mr. Islam was appearing before this Court in other matters (which is quite significant in number) as the legal representative of the ‘OR-turned-OL’, Mr. Islam kept informing this Court that since he is not getting remuneration, it has become difficult for him to assist this Court and used to implore to find a permanent solution of this impasse. Again, on 08.04.2019, when Mr. Islam was asked by this Court to appear and assist in the Company Matter No. 105 of 2000, he informed this Court that he is present in the Court as per the Court’s verbal direction, but not to represent the Law Secretary. He goes on to state that he has met the Secretary, Ministry of Law and Justice and had a long discussion for two hours but without any result. At this juncture, I told him that after consulting the issue with my brother-Judges, who previously served as the Law Secretary, an appropriate Order shall be passed on 23.05.2019 and directed Mr. Islam to keep trying to resolve the matter by placing the interpretation of the relevant legal provisions before the Hon’ble Law Minister and Law Secretary. Today, when the matter is taken up, I asked Mr. Islam as to whether he has shared the legal issues with the Hon’ble Law Minister and the Law Secretary with regard to regularization of his appointment in each of the Company cases as the ‘Legal Representative’ of the ‘Official Receiver’ upon appointing the ‘Official Receiver’ or, as the Legal Representative of the ‘Attached Person to the Company Bench’ if any one (the Law Secretary or any officer) has been so appointed by publication of the Gazette Notification. Mr. Islam, in reply thereto, beseeched this Court to pass a detailed Judgment on this issue upon framing some guidelines for the Ministry of law and Justice, so that all the concerned persons and authorities may be benefitted. It is under this circumstance that this Court carried out the above exercise.

 

21.         This Court, therefore, makes it clear that the aim of this exercise was to ease the works of the Law Ministry as well as the duty of this Court and I earnestly hope that the Ministry of Law and Justice would help this Court to resolve the dilemma it has been facing for decades. Also, Mr. M. Moksadul Islam, Bar-at-law, an Advocate of this Court, would no longer have to face the embarrassment which he has been enduring for long by this Court in answering the questions as to how and under what provisions of law his salary from this Court may be paid, as asked by this Court, whenever any new Judge is given the jurisdiction of the Company Bench.

 

22.         The Secretary, Ministry of Law and Justice is directed to file an affidavit-of-compliance on or before 27th August, 2019 and the Registrar General of the Supreme Court of Bangladesh is directed to send this Order to the Secretary, Ministry of Law and Justice so as to assist this Court towards expeditious resolution of the matter.

 

23.         Let the matter be posted in the daily cause list on 27.08.2019 under the column “For Order”.
