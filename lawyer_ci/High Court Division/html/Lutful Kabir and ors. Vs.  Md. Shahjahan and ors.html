Section 233 (3)


The Legislature intended to  resolve all types of disputes or grievances arising out of the Companies Act expeditiously through a higher rank of Court i. e. either the District Court or the High Court Division, in a summary manner so as to help smoothly flourish the business and commerce without experiencing protracted, time-consuming and complex procedures which are required to be followed/observed/applied by the Civil Courts and, therefore, most of the Company Matters relating to/arising out of/in connection with the grievance of any members of the company or debenture holder of the company are tried/adjudicated upon without taking any oral evidence. However, it does not necessarily mean that the Company Court is not competent to dispose of a company case by taking oral evidance, because there is no provision within the four corners of the Companies Act prohibiting the Company Court to take oral evidence. Rather, the expressions employed in Section 233(3) of the Companies Act, “If after hearing the parties present on the date……”, implies that there may be petitioner witness/es (PW/s) or respondent witness/es (RW/s) or the Court witnesses/es (CW/s) if the facts and circumstances so warrant, inasmuch as this Court requires to form an opinion “If the Court is of the opinion”, (the wordings of Section 233 (3) of the Companies Act) for passing necessary Order and/or Direction.                                                              ...(32)

Companies Act (XVIII of 1994)

Sections 90 to 100

A Director is a person who is elected by the majority members entrusting upon him/her day-to-day business activities and finances and, also, to ensure that all statutory filing obligations are met. Thus, the Directors of the company in true sense are the ‘trustees’ of the company.        ...(37)

 

Companies Act (XVIII of 1994)

Section 233

When the Court is of the opinion that the interest of the petitioner of a Section-233 application is being prejudicially affected, it may pass any Order it deems fit, as mandated by the Legislature in Section 233(3) of the Companies Act. Accordingly, in exercising the power vested in this Court under Section 233(3) of the Companies Act, this Court is well empowered to pass an Order decreeing that a Director is hereby declared to be ‘disqualified’ or the offices of a Director is hereby declared to have been ‘vacated’.           ...(40)

 

Companies Act (XVIII of 1994)

Section 233 and 233(3)

The powers invested in the Court under Section 233(3) of the Companies Act is wider and superior than the power given to the Court by any other provisions of the Companies Act. By virtue of the power under Section 233(3) of the Companies Act, the Court is competent to order the company to conduct its audit, to appoint an Independent Auditor and to fix the remuneration of the Auditor, to direct the company for calling and holding any meeting (which may be Board meeting, EGM or AGM) and to make any necessary Order/Directions ‘to regulate the conduct of the company’s affairs’ (the expression engraved in Section 233(3)(b) of the Companies Act). However, since the power invested in this Court under Section 233(3) of the Companies Act is discretionary in nature, it should be exercised by this Court judiciously in the rarest of rare cases only upon carrying out meticulous examination of the facts and circumstance of an application under Section 233 of the Companies Act.          ...(41)

 

Companies Act (XVIII of 1994)

Section 94(1) and 108(2)

An incorporated company is competent to insert in its Articles of Association any provisions of disqualification in the matter of becoming a Director by virtue of the company’s power given in Section 94(2) of the Companies Act, in addition to the provisions relating to disqualifications for the Directors set out in Section 94(1) of the Companies Act. Also, under Section 108(2) of the Companies Act, a company is empowered to incorporate other provisions as the grounds for vacating the office of the Directors provided in Section 108(1) of the Companies Act.           ...(44)

 

Companies Act (XVIII of 1994)

Sections 85(3), 94(1) and 108(2)

The Company Court acts as the ‘Ombudsman’ of the companies; meaning that normally the Company Court will not interfere with the internal affairs of the company, but if the situation demands, then the Court shall do the needful. While only in a few circumstances, this Court is competent to suo motu interfere with the management of the company, however, in most of the events, this Court is allowed to look into the affairs of the company upon receiving an application. This is the scheme of enactment of Company Laws in most of the countries around the world. Any action taken or omissions occured either by the member/s of the Board (such as, Director, Managing Director and Chairman) or by the agent/s of the company (such as, Company Secretary, General Manager, Accounts Officer and other Officers) in running/managing the affairs of the company, may be examined by this Court either suo motu or on receiving application. For example, the Legislature has vested the power in this Court to suo motu direct the company to call, hold and conduct any meeting (Board Meeting, Extraordinary General Meeting, Annual General Meeting) when the situation described in Section 85(3) so warrants and, in dealing with the above situation, the Company Court is also empowered to pass any ancilliary, consequential and supplementary Order and Directions, including passing Order for appointment of auditor, fixing its remuneration, etc.                                                       ...(48)

JUDGMENT

M. Khurshid Alam Sarkar, J. Since, in both the Company Matters, common questions of law and facts are involved and the parties of these two matters are the same set of persons and, more so, the lis is centring around the common property, they are taken up together and disposed of by a single Judgment.

Company Matter No. 151 of 2018

2.             In Company Matter No. 151 of 2018, the petitioners (who are the respondent nos. 8 & 9 of the Company Matter No. 295 of 2018) challenges the resolution dated 20.04.2018 taken by the respondent nos. 2 to 15 in the Extraordinary General Meeting (EGM) of Neeshorgo Hotel & Resort Limited (hereinafter referred to either as the respondent no. 1 or as the company) and, also, the conduct/activities of the respondents in running the affairs of the company in a manner prejudicial to the interests of the company.

3.             The facts of the Company Matter No. 151 of 2018, briefly, are that the petitioners are the shareholder-Directors of the company which was incorporated as a private company limited by shares on 27.01.2016 under the Companies Act, 1994 (hereinafter referred to as the Companies Act). The company is engaged in running the business of hotel and resort, currently at only one spot, at Plot 492, Kolatoli, Marine Drive Road, Cox's Bazar, where a multistoried building had already been constructed with more than 100 (one hundred) studio apartments with facilities of a hotel and resort by Khettra Architects Limited, a Developer-company, in which the petitioner nos. 1 and 2 are the Managing Director and the Chairman respectively. The name of this iconic building was given ‘Neeshorgo’. While the landowner was owning 60% of the spaces/flats of the said iconic building and the Developer-company was owning 40% of its spaces/flats, they sold the bulk of their respective proportions to the present flat owners, and till now the landowner and the Developer-company own a considerable part of the ‘Neeshorgo’. The flat owners, which include the Managing Director and the Chairman of the Developer-company, of ‘Neeshorgo’ and members of their families purchased the shares of the company and as such all the shareholders of the company are either flat owners of the ‘Neeshorgo’ or their family-members. The company was incorporated with an authorized share capital of Taka 10,0000000/- (ten crore) divided into 1,000000/- (one crore) ordinary shares of Taka 10/- (ten) each and  27 shareholders were issued 30,00,000 (thirty lacs) shares of Taka 10 (ten) each and, out of the above shares, in the name of petitioner nos. 1 & 2, 4,00,000/- (four lacs) shares of Taka 10 (ten) each were issued. As per Article 21 of the Articles of Association (AoA) of the company, the company has 21 Directors. The petitioner no. 1 was nominated as the first Chairman and the petitioner no. 2 was appointed as the first Managing Director of the company for a period of 5 years and the respondent nos. 2 to 15 were nominated shareholder-Directors of the company.

4.             It is stated that the company could not hold its first Annual General Meeting (AGM) since its incorporation due to not being able to complete its financial accounts properly, for which the company formed a committee which gave its report on 04.07.2017. The Bank accounts of the company were being operated under the joint signatures of the petitioner nos. 1 and 2 along with another Director Mr. Mijanur Rahman. But due to the temporary absence of the said Mijanur Rahman, the cheques of the company were being signed by the petitioners and when objection was raised regarding withdrawal of money without the signature of Mr. Mijanur Rahman, in the 10th Board Meeting of the company held on 29.09.2017, it was unanimously decided that another Director, Dr. Md. Tajul Islam, should also be included as a co-signatory, whose signature shall be mandatory. Thereafter, the respondent nos. 2 to 15 served a Legal Notice dated 26.11.2017 upon the petitioners seeking explanations on certain matters and, eventually, also issued a requisition notice dated 31.01.2018 upon the Board of Directors of the company to hold an EGM of the company with the agenda of appointment of a new Managing Director and Chairman of the company and the matters relating to the present state of affairs and management of the company. Thereafter, the respondent nos. 2 to 15 held an EGM of the company on 20.04.2018 wherein resolutions were taken to remove the petitioners from the position of the Chairman and the Managing Director, and the respondent nos. 12 and 8 were appointed as the new Chairman and the Managing Director of the company. Hence, this petition.

5.             The respondent nos. 2-5, 7-8 & 11-15 (who are the petitioners of the Company Matter No. 295 of 2018) contest the case by filing an affidavit-in-opposition contending, inter alia, that the petitioner nos. 1 and 2 as the Chairman and the Managing Director of the company were managing the day-to-day affairs of the company from the very beginning in the most illegitimate, illegal and prejudicial manner in connivance with M Ershad Hossain, Md. Jashim Uddin Bhuiyan, Mohammad Jalaluddin, Md. Fazle Rabbi Khan and Supti Chowdhury (respondent nos. 2-6 of the Company Matter No. 295 of 2018), who are also the shareholders and Directors of the company. The petitioner nos. 1 and 2 submitted a letter dated 06.03.2016 to the Social Islami Bank Limited (the Bank), in which the company maintains a Bank account, requesting to change the operating instructions of the Bank account so that cheques are honoured with the signatures of the petitioner nos. 1 and 2 only, altering the instruction pursuant to the 1st Board resolution dated 05.02.2016, which required another Director’s (Mr. ATM Mijanur Rahman's) signature mandatorily for signing of the cheques with signature of either the petitioner no. 1 or 2. In the said letter, the petitioner nos. 1 and 2 mentioned that a Board meeting was held on 05.03.2016 and included an extract of the minutes of the said Board meeting quoting that it was resolved in the said Board meeting that a decision of agenda no. 7 of the meeting held on 05.02.2016 has been reviewed and discussed elaborately and for smooth functioning of the company, it has been decided that the account will henceforth be operated by any two of the three Directors namely, petitioner nos. 1 and 2 and ATM Mijanur Rahman. It is alleged that no such Board meeting was ever held and as such the contents of the letter dated 06.03.2016 are utterly false and the extracts of the Board meeting incorporated are wholly fictitious. The petitioner nos. 1 and 2 admitted the aforesaid allegation in the 10th Board meeting and also in the reply to the Legal Notice dated 05.12.2017. Since, as the Managing Director and the Chairman of the company, the petitioner nos. 1 and 2 have committed the above illegalities, since they have failed to conduct the 1st AGM so far in violation of Article 19 of the AoA and Section 81 of the Companies Act, since they have indefinitely postponed the date of AGM fixed by the Board upon cancelling the appointment of an audit firm by the petitioner no. 2 vide letter dated 23.01.2019, and since they are diverting the income of the company by collecting the rent payable by guests through the Credit Card Machine of Marine Drive Restaurant which belongs to M Ershad Hossain (the respondent no. 2 in the Company Matter No. 295 2018), the respondents having been aggrieved by the conduct of the petitioner nos. 1 and 2 called an EGM on 20.04.2018 and, vide an extraordinary resolution passed in the EGM, removed the petitioner nos. 1 and 2 from their positions of the Chairman and the Managing Director of the company.

6.             Mr. SM Atiqur Rahman, the learned Advocate appearing for the petitioners, took me through the averments made in the petition and, side by side, the Memorandum of Association (MoA) and AoA of the company, and contends that the company is the brainchild of these two petitioners and, accordingly, they were appointed the Chairman and the Managing Director of the company. He contends that in the course of running the company, when the Chairman and the Managing Director were experiencing difficulties to deal with the day-to-day expenses for not getting the mandatory signature from Mr. Mijanur Rahman, they sent a letter to the Bank in good faith for relaxing the condition for encashing the cheques. By admitting the respondents’ allegations, he contends that the same may, at best, be termed as an irregularity, but not as an illegality of any sort. Mr. S.M. Atiqur Rahman, then, took me through Articles 27 and 29 of the AoA of the company and submits that before expiry of the petitioners’ tenure of 5 (five) years, removal of the petitioners from the posts of the Chairman and the Managing Director was not a fair decision by the respondents for a mere irregularity.

7.             Per contra, Mr. Asif Bin Anwar, the learned Advocate for the respondents, takes me through the requisition notice of the EGM, enquiry notice, notice of the EGM and the resolution of the EGM, and submits that they have conducted the Extraordinary General Meeting in strict compliance with the provisions of the Companies Act and particularly in compliance with the provisions of Sections 84 and 87 of the Companies Act in that the requisition notice of the EGM was sent on 31.01.2018, thereafter, having no response, an enquiry notice was sent on 11.03.2018, soon after which the notice of the EGM was sent on 27.03.2018 and all of the members who attended the EGM voted in favour of the resolution passed.

8.             The learned Advocate for the respondents alleges that after knowing about the resolution impugned herein this case, the petitioner nos. 1 and 2 prompted M Ershad Hossain, Md. Jashim Uddin Bhuiyan, Mohammad Jalaluddin, Md. Fazle Rabbi Khan and Supti Chowdhury, respondent nos. 2-6 of the Company Matter No. 295 of 2018, to form a Partnership Firm imitating the company’s name, under the signboard of Neeshorgo Management Enterprise (hereinafter referred to either as ‘the Enterprise’ or as ‘the respondent no. 7’ of the Company Matter No. 295 of 2018). He further alleges that, thereafter, they, upon forming the aforesaid Enterprise, started to divert the business of the company to the said Enterprise by prejudicing the interest of the company and its shareholders. He submits that since the petitioners committed gross misconduct and acted against the interest of the company, as such all of them are liable to be disqualified from the position of the Directors of the company.

9.             In this Company Matter No. 151 of 2018, the learned Advocate for the petitioners and the learned Advocate for the respondents were making their respective contentions and counter contentions for days together, towards receving a full-fledged Judgment and, eventually, before the date of pronouncement of this Judgment, the learned Advocate for the petitioners, Mr. SM Atikur Rahman, made a humble prayer before this Court that the petitioners shall not proceed with this matter further and will not challenge the legality and propriety of the extraordinary resolution dated 20.04.2018, if the contesting respondents, namely, respondent nos. 7-8 & 11-15 do not raise any objection to keep these petitioners in the Board of the company as mere Directors of the company, for, the petitioners as the founding Chairman and the founding Managing Director of the company wish to see that the company is progressing at a gallop by overcoming all the hurdles. He contends that they shall cooperate with the respondents to remove any mismanagement in the company and misunderstanding among the shareholders of the company.

10.         At this juncture, this Court asked the learned Advocate for the above-mentioned respondents as to whether the respondents would agree to keep the petitioners as the Directors of the company and, in reply thereto, the learned Advocate contended that, in fact, these respondents want to run the company not only with the cooperation of these 2 (two) petitioners, but also with six other Directors who have set up a separate business, as a Partnership Firm, under the name and style of ‘Neeshorgo Management Enterprise’ in the same plaza. He contends that in the said EGM dated 20.04.2018, no decision has been taken to remove these two petitioners as the Directors of the company. He contends that the decision in the said EGM was taken to remove them only from the posts of the Managing Director and the Chairman.

11.         In view of the above agreement among the parties of this matter, that the decision taken in the EGM dated 20.04.2018 would remain valid and these two petitioners shall serve as the Directors of the company, the Company Matter No. 151 of 2018 does not require framing of any issue for adjudication, rather the same deserves to be ordered by this Court to have been disposed of on compromise. Accordingly, appropriate Orders will be passed for the Company Matter No. 151 of 2018 in the operative part of this Judgment, after dealing with the factual and legal issues of the Company Matter No. 295 of 2018.

12.         Company Matter No. 295 of 2018:

         The background against which the Company Matter No. 295 of 2018 has been filed by the majority respondents of the Company Matter No. 151 of 2018, succinctly, is that the petitioners are the Directors of the company and collectively own 9,00,000 (nine lacs) shares out of  30,00,000 (thirty lacs) issued shares of the company, and they have filed this petition invoking Section 233 of the Companies Act seeking the following remedies; (a) removing the respondent nos. 8 and 9 (who are the petitioners of Company Matter No. 151 of 2018) from their positions of the Managing Director and the Chairman of the company respectively, (b) removing the respondent nos. 8 and 9 from their positions as the Directors of the company and (c) removing the respondent nos. 2-6 from their positions as the Directors of the company on the ground that the respondent nos. 8 and 9 in connivance with the respondent nos. 2-6, have formed a partnership business with almost a similar name “Neeshorgo Management Enterprise”, establishing completely a parallel business of the company with a motive to exterminate the company by runing the same business at the same spot upon diverting the business of the company to the said partnership business out of greed to earn more benefit, by prejudicing the interest of the company and its shareholders.

13.         By filling an affidavit-in-opposition, the respondent nos. 2-6 contend that they are the shareholders and Directors of the company and, also, the partners of the newly-formed partnership business. The respondent nos. 2, 3 and 4 purchased portions of the 1st floor in 2017 and from November, 2017, they launched the restaurant business in the name of ‘Marine Drive Restaurant’ as per the decision taken in the 11th Board meeting. Being agitated with the activities of the mandatory signatory in payment of rent against their respective flats as well as the bills owed to the company (Neeshorgo Hotel and Resort Limited) against the services provided by the Marine Drive Restaurant, the respondent nos. 2-6 formed a Partnership Firm, namely, ‘Neeshorgo Management Enterprise’ to manage their flats. Thereafter, other flat-owners approached the respondent nos. 2-6 to rent out their flats through the Enterprise. Till date, the said agreements are being continued without any hindrance and the respondent nos. 2-6 have been paying rent to the flat-owners on a regular basis without any default.

14.         By filing a separate affidavit-in-opposition, the respondent nos. 11-48 state that the respondent nos. 11-48 are the flat owners of the building named ‘Neeshorgo’ at Plot no. 492, Kolatali Marine Drive Road, Cox’s Bazar. They have purchased their respective flats either from the landowner or from the Developer-company. They initially rented out their flats to the company, but the said company was very much irregular in payment of rents and in spite of repeated requests, the company was not paying monthly rents, resulting in financial loss of the respondent nos. 11-48. Under these circumstances, in order to minimize their loss, the flat-owners decided not to proceed any further in renting out the flats to the company and they decided to terminate their agreements to the company by issuing notice of termination. In the meantime, they came to know that the respondent nos. 2-6 have incorporated a Partnership Firm (the Enterprise) in the same building to manage their flats. On getting the above information, the respondent nos. 11-48 inquired about their business and came to know that the respondent nos. 2-6 are doing business with proper management, and being encouraged by, and on looking into, their business prospect and business activities, the respondent nos. 11-48 approached the respondent nos. 2-6 and they rented out their flats to the Enterprise vide separate rent agreements. After the respondents entered into new agreements with the Enterprise, they have been receiving their rents on a regular basis without any default.

15.         The learned Advocate for the petitioners, Mr. Asif Bin Anwar submits that the respondent nos. 2-6 have admittedly put themselves in a position of conflict with the company inasmuch as the respondent nos. 2-6 being the shareholders and Directors of the company engaged themselves as the partners of the Enterprise to run a similar business in the same building, that is to say, in conducting hotel & resort business at the same spot being Plot no. 492, Kolatoli, Marine Drive Road, Cox’s Bazar by damaging the business of the company. By placing the provisions of Article 23 of the AoA of the company, he submits that the respondent nos. 2-6  have committed a gross misconduct and have fallen within the scope of disqualification of Directors within the meaning of the said Article 23 of the AoA. He argues that since the respondent nos. 2-6 have put themselves in a position of conflict with the company, they have acted in contravention of their fiduciary duties. By placing the annexure-K series, the learned Advocate for the petitioners submits that the respondent nos. 2-6 have been in contravention of the fiduciay duty with the company by not only allowing the parallel business under the Partnership Firm (the Enterprise), but also by receiving the bills of the company, through the Credit Card Machine of the company’s rival business firm.

16.         In support of his above count of submissions, the learned Advocate for the petitioners refers to the case of Super-Max Offshore Holdings v Rakesh Malhotra, Actis Consumer Grooming Products Limited ([2017] EWHC 3246 (Comm) and the case of Adesokan v Sainsbury's Supermarkets Ltd, ([2017] EWCA Civ 22).

17.         Mr. Asif Bin Anwar, the learned Advocate for the petitioners, then, draws the attention of this Court to the fact that since the petitioners and the respondent nos. 8 & 9 have ensured that they shall not get involved in the business of the  Partnership Firm and they shall not do anything which may cause loss to the business of the company and, since both of them have agreed to accept the resolution dated 20.04.2018 taken in the EGM and since it has been amicably settled that they shall be performing the duty of the Directors towards helping the company, the petitioners are not seeking any relief against the respondent nos. 8 & 9 and, accordingly, the learned Advocate for the petitioners makes a humble prayer that the prayer made against the respondent nos. 8 & 9 in this petition as to removing them from the position of Directors of the company should be ignored. He contends that if the respondent nos. 2-6 refrain from damaging the business of the company by closing down the Partnership Firm in the same building, the petitioners shall not pray for their removal too. He further contends that since all the efforts of the petitioners are directed at rescuing the company from its death/annihilation, the petitioners are even ready to make any one out of the respondent nos. 2-6 either as the Chairman or as the Managing Director of the company, subject to the condition that they must be transparent to the other Directors in operating the Bank account and maintaining the accounts of the company and they shall also hold the AGM of the company regulary upon preparation of the Financial Reports of the company.

18.         Mr. A.M. Aminuddin, the learned Senior Advocate, appearing for the respondent nos. 2-6 and also for the respondent nos. 11-48, contends that the deadlock in the company was created at the behest of a Director of the company, who is a retired Additional Chief Engineer against whom ACC has filed a criminal case for allegation of corruption during his service time in the Government offices. He further contends that from the date of the incorporation of the company, there is no activity of the company, nor does this company possess any property. He contends that, in fact, the company currently exists only in the papers, as most of the flat owners have disassociated themselves from the company. He, then, sought permission to allow him to leave the Court with a prayer to permit his juniors Mr. Sakib Rejwan Kabir and Md. Minhaduzzaman to make submissions on behalf of the respondent nos. 2-6 & 11-48.

19.         Mr. Sakib Rejwan Kabir, appearing on behalf of the respondent nos. 2-6, submits that since the allegations raised by the petitioners are not in relation to any action of majority against the interest of the company and since there is no precise allegation that the action of the majority as a whole is prejudicial to the interest of the minority shareholders, the dispute does not come within the ambit of Section 233 of the Companies Act. He next submits that since the petitioners have raised disputed questions of fact, their adjudication requires taking oral evidence in a properly framed suit, and not in the present proceedings under Section 233 of the Companies Act and, therefore, the present application under Section 233 of the Companies Act is not maintainable. On this issue, he further submits that the cancellation of contracts with the company and execution of fresh agreements of the flats with the Enterprise by the flat owners in respect of management of the apartments are all matters of Civil Court to adjudicate upon. He next submits that the respondent nos. 2-6 did not violate Article 23 of the AoA of the company as they have not done anything which goes against the interest of the company, because the rent agreements of the flat owners have been terminated much earlier than the date when respondent nos. 2-6 entered into agreements with the flat owners and had the respondent nos. 2-6 not executed the rent agreements, the flat owners would have rented out their flats to third parties and as such the petitioners could not be removed from their position as Directors of the company. The learned Advocate for the respondent nos. 2-6 submits that the allegation brought against these respondents do not fall within the definition of gross misconduct and, therefore, they should not be debarred from performing the duty as the Directors of the company. He lastly submits that since all the flat owners of the ‘Neeshorgo’ have pulled themselves out of the company, the company is no more in existence and, on the other hand, since they have joined the Enterprise, it should be declared as a valid business.

20.         Mr. Md. Minhaduzzaman, the learned Advocate appearing on behalf of the respondent nos. 11-48, contends that these respondents have already cut off their contractual relationship with the company, as they were not getting rents regularly. He further contends that these respondents have entered into contracts with the newly formed Partnership Firm under the name and style of Neeshorgo Management Enterprise and they are happy to have their agreement continued with this newly created Enterprise.

21.         The learned Advocate for the respondent nos. 8 & 9 (who are the petitioners of the Company Matter No. 151 of 2018) contends that they are not involved in any manner with the formation of the separate Partnership Firm and they shall be the happiest persons to see that the company is running smoothly and making profit. He submits that since the prayer of these respondents as to amicably settling the dispute between these two respondents and the petitioners has been allowed by this Court, these respondents are not contesting this matter.

22.         Upon perusing the petitions of both the Company Matters, affidavits-in-opposition and the annexed papers as well as after hearing the learned Advocates for all the parties to these two Company Matters, it appears to me that on the Plot No. 492, Kalatoli, Marine Drive Road, Cox’s Bazar, a 10-storied mega building containing about 100 flats and 1(one) open space at the first floor have been constructed by Khetra Architects and Engineers Ltd (the Developer-company). This iconic plaza has been named ‘Neeshorgo’ by the Developer-company. The petitioners and all the respondents are the owners of the flats of the ‘Neeshorgo’. They have purchased their flats either from the land owner’s portion of 60% or from the Developer-company’s portion of 40% and, still now, the land owner and the Developer-company own some open spaces/flats, including the open space at first floor which is now being used as the restaurant (Marine Drive Restaurant), even after selling out the significant number of flats from their respective portions. The Managing Director and the Chairman of the Developer-company, eventually in the year 2016, came out with an idea that the flats and the restaurant may be rented out to a company with a view to getting the rents of the flats and restaurant without any hassle. Although it was possible to rent the flats to one company/individual, and the restaurant to a separate company/individual, they decided to manage all these assets by one company and, for the aforesaid purpose, most of the flat owners and owners of the restaurant-space at first floor (who are the Managing Director and Chairman of the Developer-company) formed a company having it incorporated as the ‘Neeshorgo Hotel and Resort Ltd’ on 27.01.2016. Thus, it appears to me that the company is not the owner of any flat or open space of this plaza named ‘Neeshorgo’. The company has simply taken lease/rent of the spaces/flats of this building. From the Memorandum of Association and Articles of Assocaition of the company, it transpires that the aim and object of the company is to run hotel and resort business, either by purchasing the flats, open space or by taking rent from the flat owners and building owners not only of this single premise (the Neeshorgo), but by purchasing and taking rent of other properties from all over Bangladesh and, simultaneously, to expand the business outside Bangladesh. In other words, the object of the company is not to keep its activities limited only within the periphery of a single spot at the Plot No. 492, Kolatoli, Marine Drive Road, Cox’s Bazar, but also to spread its business in some other suitable places of Bangladesh and abroad. 

23.         Apparently, it was a noble initiative by the Managing Director and the Chairman of the Developer-company to show the flat owners a dream of making them the owners/shareholders of an incorporated company, in addition to getting rent of their respective flats and space and also to enjoy dividends, if the company flourishes. In other words, the flat owners who would become the shareholders of the company would hold two statuses, and financially they would be benefitted in two ways. Their first status is that they are the flat/space owners of the plaza named ‘Neeshorgo’ and their second status is that they are the owners of a company engaged in hotel and resort business named ‘Neeshorgo Hotel and Resort Ltd’. And, from the perspective of financial benefit, while on the one hand, they shall get rent of their respective flats/spaces from the company, on the other hand, if the company can make profit, they would get dividends. Thus, the flat owners of this edifice ‘Neeshorgo’ hold separate and distinct entity from having their entity as the shareholders of the company (Neeshogro Hotel and Resort Ltd). While the flat owners who have not become the shareholders of the company are free to rent out their flats either to the company or to any person or they themselves may live therein, similarly, the flat owners who have become the shareholders of the company are also at liberty to rent out their respective flats either to their company or to other person/company or they themselves may stay therein and if the shareholder-cum-flat owners rent out the flats to the company, they will get the rents for the flats like the other general flat owners.

24.         So, whether the flat owners would rent out their flats to the company or to the newly formed Partnership Firm or they themselves would manage their flats/space is a matter, out of the touch and reach of this Company Matter No. 295 of 2018. Therefore, the flat owners who are not the shareholders of the company, such as the respondent nos. 11-48, have nothing to do in these cases, and the petitioners rightly had not impleaded them as the respondents of this Company Matter. But, for the reason best known to them, they filed an application for addition of party in this Company Matter No. 295 of 2018. Although this Court was reluctant to allow their application for addition of party, however, on the persistent insistence of the learned Advocates for the respondent nos. 2-6 & 11-48, they were added as the proper party; not as the necessary party. However, had the application for addition of party been strictly considered by this Court, their status would have been found to be one, which does not even come within the ambit of the proper party. But, since there was a direction by the Hon’ble Appellate Division upon this Court to give final verdicts on these two Company Matters (Company Matter No. 151 of 2019 and Company Matter No. 295 of 2019) on or before 09.05.2019, this Court considered it prudent not to create an opportunity for the respondents of the Company Matter No. 295 of 2019 to take the matter again before the Appellate Division against the Order of rejection of their application for addition of party towards squandering further time in disposing of two substantive Company Matters.

25.         In the light of the position of the added respondent nos. 11-48 that, as the flat owners of this mega building, they are always at liberty either to live in their respective flats or to rent out to the company or to rent out to the newly formed Partneship Firm or to any other individual or proprietorship businesses or partnership business or company and, further, given the nature of their legal relationship with the petitioners, the issue as to whether they are still with the company or they have terminated their contracts with the company, or whether they are currently with the newly formed Partnership Firm, is not the subject matter of the present application under Section 233 of the Companies Act. Therefore, I do not find any issue to be framed and adjudicated upon by this Court for the respondent nos. 11-48. More importantly, since the petitioners have neither sought any relief against the respondents nor have the petitioners brought any allegation against them in the petition, there is no issue to be framed for adjudicaton, so far as the respondent nos. 11-48 are concerned.

26.         And, since the respondent nos. 8 & 9 (who are the Chairman and the Managing Director of the company and the petitioners of the Company Matter No. 151 of 2018) have already settled their dispute amicably with the petitioners, there is no issue to be framed for adjudication in this case.

27.         Therefore, the only contesting respondents remain in this case are the respondent nos. 2-6. With this scenario, this Court is now obligated to frame the issues as to whether the attachment of the respondent nos. 2-6 in the company as the Directors of this company is beneficial or pre-judicial to the interest of this company and, secondly, whether this Court should make any declaration as to the legality of forming the Partnership Firm or as to the existence of the company. However, since the learned Advocates for the respondent nos. 2-6 have raised the issue of maintainability of this petition, let me embark upon the said issue at first. For examining the maintainability issue, it would be profitable to quote the provisions of Section 233 of the Companies Act, which run as follows:

233. Power of Court to give direction for protecting interest of the minority:- (1) Subject to the fulfilment of the conditions of the required minimum as specified in Section 195(a) and 195(b) any member or debenture holder of a company may either individually or jointly bring to the notice of the court by application that-

(a) the affairs of the company are being conducted or the powers of the directors are being exercised in a manner prejudicial to one or more of its members or debenture holders or in disregard of his or their interest, or

(b)  the company is acting or is likely to act in a manner which discriminated or is likely to discriminate the interest of any member or debenture holder;

(c)  a resolution of the members, debenture holders or any class of them has been passed or is likely to be passed which discriminates or is likely to discriminate the interest of one or more of the members or likely to debenture holder;

and pray for such order, as in his or their opinion, would be necessary for safeguarding his or their interest and also the interest of any other member or debenture holder.

(2)     The Court shall, on receipt of an application under sub-section (1) send a copy thereof to the Board and fix a date for hearing the application.

(3)     If after hearing the parties present on the date so fixed, the Court is of opinion that the interest of the applicant or applicants has been or is being or is likely to be prejudicially affected for reasons specified in the application, it may make such order as prayed for or such other order as it deems fit including a direction-

(a) to cancel or modify any resolution or transaction;

(b) to regulate the conduct of the company's affairs in future in such manner as is specified therein.

(c) to amend any provision of the memorandum and articles of the company (underlined by me).

28.         From a plain perusal of the above provisions of law, it appears to me that while sub-Section (1) of Section 233 of the Companies Act stipulates the qualifications required for a person in order to become an applicant of a Section-233 application, clauses (a) to (c) of the said sub-Section (1) provides the issues/subjects/grievances which may be brought to the notice of the Company Court, and at the end of sub-Section (1) of Section 233 of the Companies Act, the Legislature endows the applicant with wide options of making prayers to the Company Court. Then, sub-Section (3) of Section-233 of the Companies Act speaks about the role and power of the Company Court in disposing of a Section-233 application.

29.         About the petitioners’ qualification/ competency to stand as the applicant of a Section-233 application, since no question has been raised by the respondents, this Court is not going to embark upon examination of the aforesaid aspect. However, it may be briefly stated here that since the petitioners collectively hold 33.33% of the total issued shares, they are well competent to stand as the petitioners of a Section-233 application. Apparently, the respondent nos. 2-6 have raised the maintainability issue on the grounds that (i) no specific allegation has been brought against them, (ii) whatever the allegations they have brought, those can not be resolved in the Company Bench, for, oral evidence would require adjudication of the allegations and (iii) the issue as to whether the contracts of the respondent nos. 11 to 48 with the company have been terminated or not, can not be adjudicated upon in a Company Matter.

30.         Let me see whether there is substance in the aforesaid arguments made by the learned Advocates for the respondent nos. 2-6 on the ground of maintainability of the Company Matter No. 295 of 2018. The first ground is that no specific allegation has been brought by the petitioners against the respondents (the majority shareholders). In the light of the admitted fact that since the respondent nos. 2-6 & 8-9 collectively are the majority shareholders, and since the respondent nos. 8 & 9 as the Managing Director & the Chairman and the respondent nos. 2-6 as the Directors of the company were not taking necessary steps for conducting the audit of the company, nor were they holding the AGM of the company, and since all of the respondent nos. 2-6 directly and the respondent nos. 8 & 9 as abettors were blamed to have formed a parallel rival business, there is no scope for the respondents to say that no specific allegation has been brought against them. As per clause (a) of Section 233 (1) of the Companies Act, the petitioners are simply required to bring to the notice of this Court that the affairs of the company are being conducted or the powers of the directors are being exercised in a manner prejudicial to one or more of its members or debenture holders or in disregard of his or their interest. From the above admitted facts as to not conducting the audit of the company and non-holding of the AGMs of the company, it is vividly evident that ‘the affairs of the company are being conducted ……….. in a manner prejudicial to one on more of its members’ and, thus, I find that the petitioners having flawlessly brought to the notice of this Court the said admitted affairs of the company in a specific manner, there is no scope for the respondents to question the maintainability of this petition on the ground that no specific allegation has been brought against the respondents. In other words, while the petitioners have perfectly satisfied this Court about the requirement of Section 233(1)(a) of the Companies Act, the submissions of the learned Advocate for the respondent nos. 2-6 have no legs to stand upon and, accordingly, the same are repelled by the Court.

31.         Although there are some other allegations of not running the affairs of the company in a beneficial manner (a) by not operating the Bank account with the signatures of the authorized Directors, (b) by running a parallel rivalry business at the same site, (c) by not signing the further agreements with the flat owners, (d) by not paying the rents to the flat owners regularly and so on, however, I would not go for examination of each of the aforesaid allegations, for, when the allegations of not conducting the audit and not holding the AGM are the admitted facts by all the respondents, and the allegation of commencing a new parallel business is also admitted by the respondent nos. 2-6, which sufficientty attracts the provisions of clause (a) of Section 233 (1) of the Companies Act, there is no need to deal with other factual issues by this Court.

32.         The second ground of maintainability is that this Court is legally not empowered to adjudicate upon the factual issues involved in this case. There are two types of factual issues in any case, namely, admitted facts and disputed facts. When the facts contended by the plaintiff/petitioner are admitted by the defendants/respondents, question of taking any oral or documentary evidence becomes redundant. And, if any fact stated by the petitioner is disputed, not only by raising mere objection but also by attempting to substantiate the objection through credible written statements/affidavit and upon adducing the relevant document/paper, if any, in that scenario, the Courts are required to take oral evidence in an effort to discover and assess the truth. Now, the pertinent question that crops up for consideration is whether this Court is empowered to take oral evidence, if needed, for finding out the truth to arrive at the appropriate conclusion. In reply thereto, my humble understanding of the scheme of the Companies Act is that the Legislature intended to  resolve all types of disputes or grievances arising out of the Companies Act expeditiously through a higher rank of Court i. e. either the District Court or the High Court Division, in a summary manner so as to help smoothly flourish the business and commerce without experiencing protracted, time-consuming and complex procedures which are required to be followed/observed/applied by the Civil Courts and, therefore, most of the Company Matters relating to/arising out of/in connection with the grievance of any members of the company or debenture holder of the company are tried/adjudicated upon without taking any oral evidence. However, it does not necessarily mean that the Company Court is not competent to dispose of a company case by taking oral evidance, because there is no provision within the four corners of the Companies Act prohibiting the Company Court to take oral evidence. Rather, the expressions employed in Section 233(3) of the Companies Act, “If after hearing the parties present on the date……”, implies that there may be petitioner witness/es (PW/s) or respondent witness/es (RW/s) or the Court witnesses/es (CW/s) if the facts and circumstances so warrant, inasmuch as this Court requires to form an opinion “If the Court is of the opinion”, (the wordings of Section 233 (3) of the Companies Act) for passing necessary Order and/or Direction. Thus, I hold that since the Company Court is competent to frame any issue for trial, any complicated question of facts can be adjudicated upon by it through taking documentary and oral evidence towards passing an appropriate Order and Direction. Therefore, the ground taken by the learned Advocates for the respondents gets repelled. Even for the sake of argument if it is held, without being prejudiced, that the Company Court does not have the power to adjudicate upon the cases by taking oral evidence, in that event also, the ground taken by the learned Advocate for the respondents will not stand, for, the allegations of (i) not conducting the audit of the company, (ii) not holding the AGM of the company and (iii) running a rival business in the same building being admitted facts, there is no need of taking any oral evidence on these factual issues.

33.         The last ground is that, since the issue as to whether valid contracts exist between the company and the flat owners can not be adjudicated upon here in this case, the petition under Section 233 of the Companies Act is not maintainable. This Court is fully in agreement with the above proposition. This Court’s clear opinion is that whether the respondent nos. 2-6 or 11-48 have got any contractual relationship with the company as the tenant and landlord, is a matter to be adjudicated upon by the Civil Court. Given the fact that the petitioners have not sought for adjudication of an issue of this sort here in this Company Matter, therefore, I find it to be a completely irrelevant ground for attacking this case, on the issue of maintainability of this case.

34.         The above examen on the issue of maintainability of this application under Section 233 of the Companies Act leads me to hold that there is no substance in the arguments advanced by the learned Advocate for the respondent nos. 2-6 for rejection of this application on the ground of maintainability of this application under Section 233 of the Companies Act.

35.         Now, let me take up the substantive issue as to whether the respondent nos. 2-6 deserve to remain as the Directors of the company. To delve deep into this issue, it would be useful if I may know the statutory and dictionary meanings of a Director of a company. Section 2(1)(f) of Companies Act defines the word ‘Director’ in the following manner;

Section 2(1)(f): “Director” includes any person occupying the position of director by whatever name called;

36.         The above provision of law does not provide the definitive meaning of the word “Director” and, therefore, I need to take recourse to a dictionary to know the proper meaning of the word ‘Director’. Black’s Law Dictionary inscribes the following narration about the word ‘Director’ which is quoted below:

“A person appointed or elected to sit on a Board that manages the affairs of a corporation or other organization by electing and exercising control over its officers. Also, trustee.”

37.         Sections 90 to 110 of the Companies Act set out the provisions on the different aspects of the Directors of an incorporated company, including appointment of Directors (Section 91), disqualifications of Directors (Section 94), qualification of a Director (Section 97), removal of Directors (Section 106) and vacation of office of Directors (Section 108). From a minute perusal of the entire provisions relating to Directors in the Companies Act, as enshrined in Sections 90 to 110, my humble understanding about the terminology “Director” is that a Director is a person who is elected by the majority members entrusting upon him/her day-to-day business activities and finances and, also, to ensure that all statutory filing obligations are met. Thus, the Directors of the company in true sense are the ‘trustees’ of the company as defined in the Black’s Law Dictionary. The shareholders /members of the company keep a trust upon the Directors that they shall do everything for the welfare and benefit of the company and its members and, therefore, there exists a fiduciary relationship between the Directors and others members of the company. Once this fiduciary relationship is broken, a Director must no longer hold the position of a Director of the company.

38.         Having laid down the above dictum on the term “Director”, I need to see now whether the respondent nos. 2-6 have lost the trust of the shareholders (members) of the company resulting in breaking up the fiduciary relationship between them.        

39.         It is an admitted fact that the respondent nos. 2-6 have created a Partnership Firm under the name and style of ‘Neeshorgo Management Enterprise’ which has been impleaded in this petition as respondent no.7. It is also an admitted position that the respondent nos. 2-6 while performing functions as the Directors of this company did not take any consent from the company to form another parallel business during the existence of this company. If the respondent nos. 2-6 were eager to run a separate business of the same nature at the same spot under the same roof, it was the duty of these Directors of this company to, at least, discuss the issue in the Board meeting, let alone taking approval from the Board of the company and, thereafter, they could have opted to undertake a separate business under the same roof. Anyone with ordinary prudence would be of the view that if a parallel business is run under the same roof by the Directors of the existing company, the interest of the company as well as its shareholders would inevitably be jeopardised. And, particularly when it is admitted by these respondents that due to providing a better service by their newly formed Enterprise, the flat owners of the plaza ‘Neeshorgo’ are becoming interested to sever their ties with the company, it is crystal clear to this Court that the activities of the respondent nos. 2-6 are crippling the business of the company and, hence, there is no reason for the members of the company to trust them any longer as the trustees (Directors) of the company. Therefore, this Court is of the view that the respondent nos. 2-6, having acted against the interest of the company as well as the interest of the shareholders of the company, are not competent to perform their duty as the Directors of the company. In augmenting my above conclusion, I find it pertinent to state that when the majority members of a cricket team lose faith in some players because of the latter’s involvment in “match fixing” to help win the opponent team, like the respondent nos. 2-6, they should not be in the team any longer. Likewise, when an ocean-going ship is drifting in the middle of the sea and, eventually upon finding another boat/ship, a few of crew members selfishly escape by boarding therein, they lose their right to remain as the crew member of the ship.

40.         Now, a pertinent question may crop up as to under what provisions of law this Court is going to declare these Directors incompetent and expel them from the Board of Directors of the company. The answer thereto is that when the Court is of the opinion that the interest of the petitioner of a Section-233 application is being prejudicially affected, it may pass any Order it deems fit, as mandated by the Legislature in Section 233(3) of the Companies Act. Accordingly, in exercising the power vested in this Court under Section 233(3) of the Companies Act, this Court is well empowered to pass an Order decreeing that the respondent nos. 2-6 are hereby declared to be ‘disqualified’ or the offices of the respondent nos. 2-6 are hereby declared to have been ‘vacated’, but, for the reasons to be known hereinafter, I prefer not to use the legal terminologies ‘disqualified’ or ‘vacated’ in declaring them incompetent to be the members of the Board of the company.

41.           However, because of not using the above-mentioned terminologies, there should not be any confusion that this Court can not declare any Director of the company to be ‘disqualified’ until a Director’s case falls within the ambit of Section 94 of the Companies Act or the office of a Director shall not be ‘vacated’ until a Director’s circumstance attracts the provisions of Section 108 of the Companies Act or a Director cannot be ‘removed’ without passing an extraordinary resolution under Section 106 of the Companies Act, inasmuch as the powers invested in the Court under Section 233(3) of the Companies Act is wider and superior than the power given to the Court by any other provisions of the Companies Act. By virtue of the power under Section 233(3) of the Companies Act, the Court is competent to order the company to conduct its audit, to appoint an Independent Auditor and to fix the remuneration of the Auditor, to direct the company for calling and holding any meeting (which may be Board meeting, EGM or AGM) and to make any necessary Order/Directions ‘to regulate the conduct of the company’s affairs’ (the expression engraved in Section 233(3)(b) of the Companies Act). However, since the power invested in this Court under Section 233(3) of the Companies Act is discretionary in nature, it should be exercised by this Court judiciously in the rarest of rare cases only upon carrying out meticulous examination of the facts and circumstance of an application under Section 233 of the Companies Act.

42.         I find this case to be a fit one to exercise the power under Section 233(3) of the Companies Act and, accordingly, I am inclined to declare the respondent nos. 2-6 of the Company Matter No. 295 of 2018 to be incompetent to be or to remain as the Directors of the company and, accordingly, the respondent nos. 2-6 are hereby declared to be incompetent to remain as the Directors of the company. In order to let the company run smoothly and more beneficially, this Court should expel the respondent nos. 2-6 from the Board of the company. Now, let me consider as to whether the respondent nos. 2-6 should be expelled straightaway from the Board of Directors by this Judgment now, or should they be given an opportunity to correct themselves.

43.         After declaring the respondent nos. 2-6 to be incompetent to be in the Board of the company, as a logical sequence, this Court should make the consequential Order of expelling the respondents from the Board. However, in my view, it would be a prudent exercise if this Court gives these respondents an opportunity to think over their actions so far taken by them in running a rivalry business and consider as to whether they can unitedly run the company, like the respondent nos. 8 & 9, in a dream of making the company a reputed chain Hotel and Resort Company. For this to happen, the declaration made by this Court hereinbefore as to becoming or remaining incompetent as the Directors of the company is temporarily kept in abeyance till the date of holding the first Board meeting of the company, in an expectation by this Court that the respondent nos. 2-6 upon attending the Board meeting of the company will inform the Board about the closure of their Enterprise. If they do not turn up in the aforesaid Board meeting or if they do not agree to shut down the Enterprise, the company shall be competent to remove them from their directorship under Section 106 of the Companies Act. A company is competent to remove any of its shareholder-Directors under Section 106 of the Companies Act showing him as disqualified under Section 94 of the Companies Act or declaring the Director’s seat to have fallen vacant under Section 108 of the Companies Act. From a mere perusal of the provisions of Article 23(f) of the AoA of the company that “The office of the Directors shall be vacated…. for any work against the interest of the company”, it appears to me that the aforesaid provision in an unambiguous term empowers the company to declare vocation of the seats of the respondent nos. 2-6 as the Directors of the company, as they have been found by this Court to have worked against the interest of the company.

44.         In addition to the above provisions, to put the company on a safer side for endowing the company with a more specific power for removing these respondents, if needed, under Section 108 of the Companies Act, this Court is inclined to exercise its power under Section 233(3)(1) of the Companies Act towards directing the company to amend its Articles of Association to incorporate a provision as Article No. 23(g), which shall be added at the end of Article No. 23 and be placed as sub-Article (g) after the sub-Article (f) of Article No. 23. The following wordings shall be incorporated“runs any Hotel and Restaurant business anywhere in the world without the approval of the company” as a disqualification for appointment of a Director. An incorporated company is competent to insert in its Articles of Association any provisions of disqualification in the matter of becoming a Director by virtue of the company’s power given in Section 94(2) of the Companies Act, in addition to the provisions relating to disqualifications for the Directors set out in Section 94(1) of the Companies Act. Also, under Section 108(2) of the Companies Act, a company is empowered to incorporate other provisions as the grounds for vacating the office of the Directors provided in Section 108(1) of the Companies Act. Once the company amends its Article 23 in the manner as prescribed above, it may apply the said provision for vacation of the office of the respondent nos. 2-6, if they are not volitional to leave off their Enterprise.

45.         Now, I need to take up the second substantive issue as to whether this Court should make any declaration as to the legality of forming the Partnership Firm or as to the existence of the company. Mr. Sakib, the learned Advocate for the respondent nos. 2-6, has submitted that since all the flat owners of the ‘Neeshorgo’ have pulled themselves out of the company, the company is no more in existence and, on the other hand, since they have joined the Enterprise, it should be declared as a valid business. From a careful reading of all the prayers made in this case, I did not find any prayer seeking a declaration that formation of the Partnership Firm named ‘Neeshorgo Management Enterprise’ was illegal. From a plain perusal of the entire petition in tandem with its prayers, I find that the petitioners’ case is not at all directed at obtaining such a declaration.

46.         Mr. AM Aminuddin, the learned Senior Counsel, has submitted that since the company does not run practically, there is no point of allowing the petitioners to operate the business of the company. On this issue, this Court’s view is that until and unless the company is dissolved through the process of winding up, there is no impediment for the company to carry out its business in full swing. The incident of opening up a new business by the influential Directors of the company or the whimsical decision of some landlords as to deserting the company or the financial stutus of the company as to not possessing any fund - can not lead this Court to go for a declaration that the company does no more exist, in this application under Section 233 of the Companies Act. So, this Court is neither going to declare the ‘Neeshorgo Management Enterprise’ as an illegal business nor going to declare that the company has lost its substratum necessitating its dissolution.

47.         The above proposition, now, leads me to embark upon the issue as to whether this Court should make appopropriate Observations, Orders and Directions for this company, so that it may unhesitatingly go for its next course of actions, particularly, in the backdrop of keeping the existence of both these two business entities intact by this Court.

48.         It is to be known, if it is already not known, that the Company Court acts as the ‘Ombudsman’ of the companies; meaning that normally the Company Court will not interfere with the internal affairs of the company, but if the situation demands, then the Court shall do the needful. While only in a few circumstances, this Court is competent to suo motu interfere with the management of the company, however, in most of the events, this Court is allowed to look into the affairs of the company upon receiving an application. This is the scheme of enactment of Company Laws in most of the countries around the world. In other words, any action taken or omissions occured either by the member/s of the Board (such as, Director, Managing Director and Chairman) or by the agent/s of the company (such as, Company Secretary, General Manager, Accounts Officer and other Officers) in running/managing the affairs of the company, may be examined by this Court either suo motu or on receiving application. For example, the Legislature has vested the power in this Court to suo motu direct the company to call, hold and conduct any meeting (Board Meeting, Extraordinary General Meeting, Annual General Meeting) when the situation described in Section 85(3) so warrants and, in dealing with the above situation, the Company Court is also empowered to pass any ancilliary, consequential and supplementary Order and Directions, including passing Order for appointment of auditor, fixing its remuneration, etc. It was held in the case of Md. M. Rahman Vs Joint Stock Companies 2019(1) 15 ALR 96 that this Court is empowered to suo motu direct the company under Section 85(3) of the Companies Act to hold AGM/s of the company and to pass any other Order and Direction which may be considered by the Court to be necessary.

49.         Similarly, in an application under Section 233 of the Companies Act this Court is empowered to pass any type of Order/Direction which the Court considers to be necessary for the betterment of the company. Although there are differences of opinion as to the jurisdiction of this Court that the jurisdiction of this Court is limited within the certain provisions of the Companies Act, where the said provisions prescribe the petitioner/aggrieved person for approaching the Company Court (such as Sections 12, 13, 14, 41, 43, 59, 71, 81, 82, 83, 85, 115, 151, 171, 175, 176, 191, 193, 228, 229, 233, 241, 245, 248, 249, 253, 255, 258, 259, 261, 262, 263, 264, 265, 267-286, 294, 296, 299, 300, 301, 302, 303, 305, 309, 311, 312, 314, 316, 326, 328, 331, 333, 338, 339, 340, 342, 346, 349, 395, 396),  however, upon minute perusal of the Preamble and the entire provisions of the Companies Act, my view is that in the absence of any prohibitory provision in any Section of the Companies Act, in particular in Section 3 of the Companies Act which seeks to state about the jurisdiction of this Court, this Court is competent to deal with any issues/grievance relating to or arising out of  or in connection with any provisions of the Companies Act.

50.         In any event, this Court being well-empowered to pass necessary Orders and/or Directions and Observations in an application under Section 233 of the Companies Act, I consider it to be useful to make the following Observations, Orders and Directions for the company;

Orders, Directions and Observations for Company Matter No. 151 of 2018;

The present Chairman and Managing Director of the company shall be appointed as per the resolution taken in the EGM held on 20.04.2018, subject to the outcome of the first Board meeting to be held under the neutral Chairman to be appointed by this Court hereinafter.

(2)    The Order of stay granted earlier at the time of admission of this matter staying the operation of the resolution dated 20.04.2018, is hereby recalled and vacated. The company and its Chairman, Managing Director and other Directors are directed to operate the Bank accounts of the company under the signatures of the newly appointed Chairman and Managing Director, subject to the decision taken in the first Board meeting to be held under the leadership of the Court-appointed Chairman. However, the company shall be at liberty to make another signatory from the other Directors in consonance with the scheme of resolution taken in the 10th Board Meeting of the company. In any event, they shall always keep the founding Chairman and the founding Managing Director of the company updated with the status of accounts of the company.

(3)    With the above Observations, Directions and Orders, the Company Matter No. 151 of 2018 is disposed of, however, without any order as to costs.

Orders, Directions and Observations for Company Matter No. 295 of 2018;
