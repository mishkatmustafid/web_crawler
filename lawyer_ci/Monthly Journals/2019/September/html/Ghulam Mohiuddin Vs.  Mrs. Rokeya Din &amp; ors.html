Arbitration Act (I of 2001)

Sections 13 and 14

A party to an arbitration agreement may challenge any arbitrator’s competency and impartiality by making a written represe-ntation to the arbitral tribunal within 30 (thirty) days of knowing about the incompetency or partiality of the concerned arbitrator. . . .(20)

Arbitration Act (I of 2001)

Sections 13 and 14

The Arbitration Act permits a party to an arbitration agreement to challenge the competency and impartiality of a single arbitrator or all the arbitrators by filing a written representation to the arbitral tribunal, so that the arbitral tribunal itself can give a decision on the aforesaid application for challenge. Also, if a party to the arbitration agreement raises question that the arbitral tribunal is not properly constituted, it is a mandatory duty of the arbitral tribunal to pass an Order on the issue. . . . (23)

Arbitration Act (I of 2001)

Section 53

Once a party to an arbitration agreement approaches any Court, all the parties to the said arbitration agreement shall have to go to that Court for filing any additional or consequential or ancillary or any independent application, until the arbitral proceedings finally comes to an end. Whenever any party to an arbitration agreement will take recourse to the Court of District Judge of any particular District, the said District Judge Court shall have the exclusive jurisdiction over the said arbitration matter up to the period of final termination of the arbitral proceedings, and not any other District Judge Court. . . . (25 and 29)

Arbitration Act (I of 2001)

Section 53

There are two vital aspects of the provisions of Section 53 of the Arbitration Act. The first aspect is that through its non-obstante provisions, it seeks to herald that irrespective of any statements, stipulations and provisions to the contrary made in the Arbitration Act, or any other law, the provisions of Section 53 of the Arbitration Act shall stand. The second aspect is - if any party to the arbitration agreement registers its name in a Court for any purpose, that Court shall assume jurisdiction over the arbitration agreement and arbitral agreement in whose connection a party have moved before the Court. While as a normal course of action, the Legislature does not go for enactment of a law by which the superior Court’s jurisdiction and power will be scrapped or curtailed in order to enrich and empower the sub-ordinate Courts with the said jurisdiction and power, yet if the Legislature in their wisdom considers it necessary, they are competent to enact such law. However, this sort of enactment is normally made by the Legislature in clearer and specific expressions and, furthermore, in passing such laws the Parliament usually tables some explanations before the House.

. . . (27 and 28)

Arbitration Act (I of 2001)

Section 53

The specific jurisdictions conferred upon the High Court Division, thus, are not meant to have been taken away by the provisions of Section 53 of the Arbitration Act.            . . . (29)

Interpretation of statue

The golden rule of statutory interpretation is that when any ambiguity appears in a provision of a statute, the first option for the Court is to find out its literal meaning. And, in the event that it becomes a complex task for the Court to go with the above rule, only then, the Court would endeavour to discover its meaning with the help of the preamble and other provisions of the concerned statute without making any of its provisions nugatory.       . . . (29)

Arbitration Act (I of 2001)

Section 20

A petitioner, who has invoked Section 20 of the Arbitration Act, is under a mandatory obligation to  satisfy the High Court Division that all the conditions stipulated in clauses (a) to (c) of Section 20(2) of the Arbitration Act are satisfied before entertaining or registering an application. The power invested in the High Court Division for entertaining an application under Section 20 of the Arbitration Act is not an independent power, for, it can be exercised by the High Court Division only upon being satisfied that (a) the determination of the question is likely to produce substantial savings in costs, (b) the application was submitted without any delay and (c) there is good reason why the matter should be decided by the Court. Section 20(2) of the Arbitration Act requires that the petitioner must simultaneously satisfy all the three conditions laid down in clauses (a) to (c) thereof, for, none of the clauses (a) to (c) ends up with the word ‘or’. ...(33 and 34)

Arbitration Act (I of 2001)

Section 6

When a party before the arbitral tribunal does not raise any objection with regard to non-compliance of any provision of the Arbitration Act or with regard to non-fulfillment of any requirement set out in the arbitration agreement within the time prescribed in the Arbitration Act and/or within the time-limit stipulated in the arbitration agreement or, in the absence of any stipulation as to the time in the law or in the arbitration agreement, within a reasonable time without making any delay, for dealing with that scenario, the Legislature mandated the arbitral tribunal and the Courts to take a presumption that the aforesaid party has waived his right to so object. The crucial aspect of the provisions of Section 6 is, thus, the time to raise such objection, which is required to be prescribed either in the law or to be stipulated in the arbitration agreement. . . . (36 and 37)

JUDGMENT

Muhammad Khurshid Alam Sarkar: J. By filing an application under Section 20 of the Arbitration Act, 2001 (hereinafter referred to as the Arbitration Act), the petitioner challenges the legality and propriety of (i) formation of the arbitral tribunal comprising the respondent nos. 1, 2 & 3, (ii) the performance of the respondent nos. 1, 2 & 3 as the arbitrators and (iii) the interim Order passed by the aforesaid arbitral tribunal on 02.08.2018.

2.               In the application, it is averred that the petitioner is the partner of a partnership concern, named, Metro Super Centre (hereinafter referred to as the MSC) which is comprised of 2(two) partners, namely, the petitioner and the respondent no. 4. The MSC was created through a Deed of Partnership dated 01.09.2012, which was registered with the Registrar of Joint Stock Companies and Firms (RJSC) on 02.10.2012. It is stated that in the course of running the aforesaid partnership business, when disagreements among the partners surfaced to a great extent, the petitioner and the respondent no. 4 decided to amicably settle the dispute through mediation and, for that purpose, they appointed respondent nos. 1, 2 & 3 as the mediators, who happen to be close relatives of both the parties. On 12.03.2018 the mediators laid down certain conditions to be observed by the partners, including a direction upon the partners to conduct an audit to be carried out by a Chartered Accountant Firm (CA firm) named, Hoda Vasi Chowdhury & Company. However, without any reason, the mediators started to act as arbitrators and, eventually, on an application filed by the respondent no. 4, the respondent nos. 2 and 3 passed an interim Order on 02.08.2018 authorizing the respondent no.5 (CEO and the Manager of the MSC) to jointly operate the bank account of the MSC. Thereafter, the respondent no. 4 approached the Court of the learned District Judge, Dhaka for execution of the aforesaid interim Order dated 02.08.2018 by filing the Arbitration Miscellaneous Case No. 501 of 2018 under Section 21(4) of the Arbitration Act. After admission of the above Arbitration Miscellaneous Case on 12.08.2018, the learned District Judge, Dhaka directed to run the business of the MSC as per the arbitral tribunal’s interim Order dated 02.08.2018 till 23.10.2018. Thereafter, the petitioner having appeared before the Court on 19.08.2018 filed an application under Section 7A(5) of the Arbitration Act for rejection of the aforesaid Arbitration Miscellaneous Case. The learned District Judge fixed the matter on 05.09.2018 for hearing the petitioner, but the petitioner opted to approach this Court and filed the instant arbitration application.

3.               By filing an affidavit-in-opposition, the respondent no. 4 states that the petitioner deliberately distorted the true accounts of the facts of this case, for, the petitioner unequivocally agreed through e-Mails dated 07.02.2018 and 08.02.2018 to appoint the respondent nos. 1, 2 & 3 as the arbitrators for settlement of the dispute between them and, also, gave his undertaking to accept and abide by the decisions of the arbitrators. The respondent nos. 1, 2 & 3 were never nominated as the mediators, rather the arbitrators first tried for an amicable settlement otherwise than pursuing arbitration proceedings and pursuant to the failure of the settlement between the parties, the arbitrators started arbitration process based on certain terms and conditions as stipulated and agreed upon by the parties vide arbitration agreement dated 12.03.2018. The arbitrators ordered for audit of the MSC to be carried out by an independent auditor, named, Hoda Vasi Chowdhury & Co. It is claimed that the CA Firm was though selected by the arbitrators, but was appointed upon obtaining approval from the petitioner vide e-Mail 14.07.2018. In clause 9 of the arbitration agreement executed under the heading/title ‘The Decisions of the Arbitrators/Mediators’ (weÁ ga¨¯’ZvKvix‡`i wm×vš—), it was clearly stipulated that based on the audit report, the arbitrators would give their decisions which would be binding on both the parties. It is alleged that the petitioner was in the management and control of the MSC and did not cooperate with the auditor which has been reflected in the audit report. It is stated that the petitioner, subsequent to receipt of the audit report, although has acknowledged the audit report from the auditors and the ‘arbitrators’, however, after perusing the said audit report, which accounted for his misdeeds in the business, challenged the role of the arbitrators by creating a false claim that the arbitrators were appointed as mediators, not as arbitrators.

4.               It is stated that pursuant to filing the present application, the High Court Division on 03.09.2018 passed Stay Order upon the interim Order dated 02.08.2018 passed by the arbitrators and, thereafter, the petitioner filed a criminal case, being Tejgaon Thana Case no. 20 dated 05.09.2018 under Sections 381/114/109 of the Penal Code, 1860, against the respondent nos. 2, 3, 4 & 5.  The background fact of filing the above-mentioned criminal case and its subsequent events are stated to be as follows: that the petitioner while running the management of the MSC opened an L/C for importing goods in the name of the MSC and the said imported goods were lying at the Chittagong Port having been marked for auction, and since the petitioner was removed and replaced by Mr. Navid Chowdhury (respondent no. 5) as the CEO vide the interim Order passed by the arbitral tribunal, Mr Navid Chowdhury got the imported goods released from the Chittagong Port and on 29.08.2018 brought the goods to Dhaka and stored in the godown of the MSC. It is stated that at the time of admission of the instant arbitration application, this Court directed the petitioner not to use 66 (sixty six) signed-cheques given by the respondent no. 4 including other signed cheque, if any, held by the petitioner and, also, directed to return the same to the respondent no. 4 within 7 (seven) days from the date of receipt of the Order and to file an affidavit-in-compliance about the return of these aforesaid cheques within 2 (two) weeks thereafter; that the respondent no. 4 after obtaining the bail from the Court of competent jurisdiction filed the Criminal Miscellaneous Case No. 50198 of 2018 before this Court for quashing the above-mentioned criminal case (G.R. No.392/18 arising out of Tejgaon Thana Case no. 20 dated 05.09.2018 under Sections 381/114/109 of the Penal Code, pending in the Court of Chief Metropolitan Magistrate, Dhaka; that a Division Bench of this Court after hearing the parties issued a Rule and stayed all further proceedings of the above-mentioned criminal case for a period of 3(three) months on 31.10.2018 so far as it relates to respondent no. 4; that the said Rule is now pending for hearing before this Court.

5.               Mr. Tanvir Parvez, the learned Advocate appearing for the petitioner, submits that the respondent nos. 1, 2 & 3, upon misconception of law, have considered themselves as the arbitrators and have most illegally exercised the powers which are meant exclusively for the arbitrators in the Arbitration Act and, as such, the impugned interim Order should be declared to be without lawful authority. He submits that the opposite parties have failed to consider that they have not been appointed as the arbitrators under clause 20 of the Partnership Deed and, therefore, they have no power to pass interim Order. In an endeavour to place the above count of submissions in greater details, he submits that the respondent nos. 1, 2 & 3 have failed to consider that there is a fundamental difference between mediation and arbitration inasmuch as, while the provisions of the Arbitration Act applies in case of arbitration, the same have no application in a case of mediation. He argues that in view of the above position of law, their action as arbitrators and passing the interim Order is outside their jurisdiction and as such it should be declared without lawful authority. He takes this Court through the provisions of Section 12 of the Arbitration Act, and submits that the law sets out specific procedures for appointing arbitrators, but it is evident in this case that the said procedures were not followed in appointing the respondent nos. 1, 2 & 3 which conclusively indicate that it was never the intention of the parties to appoint the respondent nos. 1, 2 & 3 as the arbitrators, rather they were appointed as the mediators and, as such, there being no existence of arbitral tribunal, the impugned interim Order should be declared without any lawful authority.

6.               By making the above submissions, the learned Advocate for the petitioner prays to declare that the actions of the respondent nos. 1, 2 & 3 as the arbitrators are without lawful authority and the Order dated 02.08.2018 passed by the aforesaid persons purportedly claiming themselves to be the members of the arbitral tribunal is illegal.

7.               Ms. Faria Huq, the learned Advocate appearing for the respondent no. 4, takes me through clause 20 of the Deed of Partnership and submits that it is clear as the day-light that the respondent no. 4 has invoked the aforesaid arbitration clause. In elaborating her above count of submissions, she submits that the provisions of clause 20 of the Deed of Partnership and the e-Mail correspondences between the petitioner and his partner related to the nomination and formation of arbitration team were all written in English where the term ‘arbitrator’ has been used at all the times and the term “mediator” was never used for a single time whatsoever in any form. Then Ms. Huq places the provisions of Section 6 of the Arbitration Act in tandem with referring the case of Joinal Abedin Jamal Vs Noor Afzal 63 DLR 432, and submits that, without prejudice to the other submissions, if it is presumed that the arbitration clause has not been invoked by the respondent no. 4, the petitioner, vide Section 6 of the Arbitration Act, has waived his right to object as he has proceeded with the arbitration without stating his objection to such noncompliance of invoking the arbitration clause without undue delay. Ms. Huq next places before this Court the arbitration agreement dated 12.03.2018 executed by the petitioner and his partner, and submits that it was prepared in consultation with all the parties with mutually agreed terms and conditions and was properly executed on a non-judicial stamp and, by its clause 9, the arbitrators were authorized to award decisions binding upon both the parties. By taking this Court through Section 17(a) & (b) of the Arbitration Act, she submits that since the arbitral tribunal comprising the respondent nos. 1, 2 and 3 is legally empowered to rule on its own jurisdiction on whether there is existence of a valid arbitration agreement or not and, also, whether the arbitral tribunal was properly constituted or not, the petitioner ought to have approached the arbitral tribunal, at first. She argues that the petitioner has come to the wrong forum to challenge the validity of the agreement dated 12.03.2018 as it falls under the purview of the decision of the arbitral tribunal. By placing the provisions of Section 22 of the Arbitration Act, Ms. Huq submits that the petitioner is deliberately trying to confuse this Court with regard to the terms ‘mediators’ and ‘arbitrators’ inasmuch as the law mandates the arbitrators to play the role of mediators for an amicable settlement and, accordingly, the arbitrators initially approached as the mediators to settle the dispute amicably. She continues to submit that there is no fundamental distinction between the terminologies ‘mediator’ and ‘arbitrator’ if the same are translated into Bengali, because as per the Dictionary published by the Bangla Academy, the meaning of both the words ‘mediation’ and ‘arbitration’ is “ga¨¯’Zv”. Ms. Huq agitates that the agreement dated 12.03.2018 under the title “The Decision of the Arbitrators/Mediators” (weÁ ga¨¯’ZvKvix‡`i wm×všÍ) is being simply misinterpreted by the petitioner to mislead the Court with malafide intention to cause obstacles in the arbitration proceedings and to cause financial loss to the respondent no. 4. By placing the provisions of Section 20(2)(a) of the Arbitration Act, Ms. Faria Huq submits that in view of the fact that the learned District Judge had to adjourn the hearing of the Arb. Mis. Case No. 501/18 resulting in substantial loss in cost, instead of saving the expenses of the parties, the petitioner’s approach before this Court, thus, is contrary to the provision of Section 20(2)(a) of the Arbitration Act.

8.               By putting forward the above submissions, the learned Advocate for the respondents prays for discharging the Rule with exemplary costs.

9.               Given the peculiar facts and circumstances of this case, this Court considered it useful to call the parties to the arbitral proceedings as well as all the three arbitrators before this Court with an aim to have first-hand information about the background and all other events from them, so that this case can be disposed of more effectively and fairly. Accordingly, they all appeared before this Court in person and narrated the facts in their own style.

10.           After hearing all of them, it revealed that the partners of the MSC (the petitioner and the respondent no. 4) are full brothers and they are the sons of late Obaidul Huq Chowdhury who is the founding editor of Dhaka Law Reports (DLR). Also, the members of the arbitral tribunal are their close relatives (the Chairman of the arbitral tribunal is a cousin, one member, namely, Mrs. Ayesha Huq is a full sister, and another member, namely, Mrs. Rokeya Din is a niece of the partners of the MSC). This Court eventually told all of them in the open-Court that since it has come to the knowledge of this Court that the partners of the MSC are the sons of such a late lamented  person to whom the judiciary of Bangladesh is indebted for his contribution in law reporting of this country, which he undertook immediately after establishment of the Dhaka High Court, by which the Judges and the lawyers of this country are being greatly benefited, this Court, therefore, instead of letting this matter to be dragged on for long, wishes to resolve the dispute between the parties amicably and, accordingly, asked them to sit together and put in their best efforts to find out a solution. It was opined by this Court that the criminal case filed by the petitioner in connection with this matter appears to this Court to be very unusual inasmuch as the petitioner and the respondent no. 4 being the partners of the MSC are the joint owners of the goods, which are alleged to have been stolen by a partner, namely, respondent no. 4, and there is no scope to record a criminal case against a person on the allegation of stealing of the goods which belonged to him. It was also commented by this Court that in the case MH Khondker Vs AW Qazilbash 20 DLR 945, this Court has laid down a ratio that during pendency of any suit/case, if any party seeks to create pressure upon the other side, it amounts to interference with the course of justice. This Court made the observation that since the dispute among them is with regard to the business owned jointly by them and the District Judge’s Court of Dhaka and this Court were in seisin of this matter, filing of criminal case alleging removal/stealing goods of the said business amounts to committing contempt of Court. This Court has taken judicial notice of the fact that the criminal case was filed immediately after obtaining the Order of stay from this Court on the operation of the Order passed by the learned District Judge enforcing the arbitral tribunal’s interim Order dated 02.08.2018. When this Court asked the petitioner of the instant application, Mr. Ghulam Mohiuddin (Bhutto), about the filing of the criminal case, in reply thereto, he told that in fact he did not have any intention to file the criminal case; he only wanted to lodge a General Diary (GD). All the three arbitrators contended that as the senior family members, all that they want to see that these petitioners’ business is saved and, with this aim, they were trying their best to resolve the dispute without causing further damage not only to the business, but also to their personal and family relationship. In reply to a query made by this Court as to whether it would be possible for them to continue with their duties as the arbitrators, they informed this Court that they would be happy to resign for the sake of an acceptable resolution of the dispute. Finally, while the arbitrators opted to leave the matter upon this Court for finding a better solution even at the cost of reconstituting the tribunal either replacing them all or keeping anyone whom the Court considers to be useful, serviceable and acceptable, the learned Advocates of the petitioner and the respondent no. 4 insisted upon this Court that they expect a verdict from this Court as to the legality of the formation of the arbitral tribunal and the validity of the impugned interim Order. Under the circumstances, this Court decided to proceed with the hearing which culminated into delivering of the instant full-fledged Judgment.

11.           Upon hearing the learned Advocates for both the sides, on perusal of the petitioner’s application as well as the affidavits filed by the respondents together with their annexures and having read the relevant statutory laws and case-laws cited from the various Law Journals, it appears to this Court that the following issues are to be adjudicated upon by this Court; (i) whether there is existence of any arbitration agreement between the partners of the MSC i.e. between the present petitioner and the respondent no. 4, (ii) whether the respondent nos. 1, 2 & 3 were appointed as the arbitrators or as the mediators, and whether the arbitral tribunal was formed as per the provisions of law, (iii) whether the arbitral tribunal was competent to pass the impugned interim Order, whether the interim Order was a proper and legal one, and whether the interim Order passed by the arbitral tribunal can be challenged before the High Court Division, (iv) whether the law permits the petitioner to challenge the legality and propriety of formation of the arbitration tribunal comprising the respondent nos. 1, 2 & 3 and, thereby, to question the performance of the respondent nos. 1, 2 & 3 as the arbitrators, (v) whether the petitioner is competent to file the instant application during pendency of the Arbitration Miscellaneous Case before the Court of District Judge, (vi) whether this Court is competent to entertain and adjudicate upon the question relating to jurisdiction of the arbitration tribunal, (vii) whether the petitioner has waived his right to challenge the appointment of the arbitrators and to object to the impugned interim Order, (viii) whether the present Members and the Chairman of arbitral tribunal should continue to perform their functions, (ix) whether this Court should constitute/create a new arbitral tribunal and (x) whether the Arbitration Miscellaneous Case No. 501/18 pending before the Court of District Judge, Dhaka should be kept in abeyance, so that the new arbitrators may review the impugned interim Order passed by the arbitration tribunal on 02.08.2018.

12.           Let me take up the first issue, namely, whether there is existence of any arbitration agreement between the partners of the MSC i.e. between the present petitioner and the respondent no. 4. Upon skimming through the Deed of Partnership dated 01.09.2012 executed by the petitioner and the respondent no. 4 on 01.09.2012, it transpires that clause 20 of the Partnership Deed contains a provision of arbitration in the following terms:

“That in case of a difference of opinion among the partners with regard to the constitution, meaning and effect of this Deed of Partnership in respect of the accounts or dissolution or any other matter relating to running the partnership firm shall be decided upon by two (2) arbitrators nominated by the partners and legal representatives of the partners or and in case of difference of any opinion among them the matter will be decided upon by an umpire selected by the arbitrators”.

13.           Thus, it is an admitted position that in the event of any disagreement between the partners, they may seek to resolve the dispute through arbitration. Now, the question that crops up for consideration as to whether the arbitral tribunal was constituted as per the terms of the clause 20 of the Partnership Deed and, also, in accordance with the provisions of the Arbitration Act.

14.           Section 11(1) of the Arbitration Act states that the parties shall be at liberty to determine the number of arbitrators and, as per the provisions of Section 11(3) of the Arbitration Act, if the parties appoint an even number of arbitrators, a Chairman shall be appointed by the arbitrators. From a careful reading of clause 20 of the Partnership deed, it appears to this Court that the petitioner and the respondent no. 4 have agreed to form a three-member arbitral tribunal through appointing one arbitrator by each of the party and, then, the two arbitrators shall appoint the Chairman.

15.           In this case, from the e-Mail sent by the respondent no. 4 to the petitioner on 07.02.2018 at 5.52pm, as evident from annexure X-1, it transpires that the respondent no. 4 initially proposed the name of Mr. Muzammel Huq Babu as his arbitrator and, in replying to the above e-Mail, the petitioner wrote to the respondent no. 4 on 08.02.2018 at 4.21p.m that his arbitrator will be Mr. Debhorta Laha, as evident from annexure X-2. From the e-Mail sent by the petitioner on 28.02.2018 at 8.45pm to Mr. Afzal Husain Choudhury (respondent no. 3), it appears that the petitioner undertook to accept the verdict of the arbitration tribunal. From annexure X-6, it transpires that the respondent no. 4 appointed Mrs. Rokeya Din (respondent no. 1) in place of his initially nominated arbitrator Mr. Muzammel Huq Babu about whom the petitioner raised objection. It further transpires that on receipt of the audit report by the petitioner, he sent an e-Mail on 14.07.2018 at 2.46pm to Mr. Afzal Choudhury (respondent no. 3) in the following terms “thank you for providing a copy of reports from auditors and arbitrators.  In reference to the Arbitration Agreement dated March 12, 2018 the auditor report is an absolute requirement……….I  still hope that you the arbitrators will find a fair resolution”, as evident from annexure-X5. Thus, from the above-discussed e-mail correspondences, of which the latest one is dated 14.07.2018 being  a couple of weeks before the impugned interim Order dated 02.08.2018, it surfaces that the petitioner, after appointing the respondent nos. 1, 2 & 3, has been addressing them as the arbitrators all along, that is to say, from the commencement of the arbitration in the early part of February, 2018 till passing the interim Order by the arbitrators on 02.08.2018. Beside the above-mentioned correspondences, I have read all the e-Mail correspondences made by the petitioner with Mr. Afzal Husain Choudhury, wherefrom it transpires that the respondent nos. 1, 2, & 3 were nominated and appointed as the arbitrators as per the terms of clause no. 20 of the Partnership Deed dated 01.09.2012 and all the correspondences mentioned them as the arbitrators from the beginning of their assignment.

16.           Now, let me see on what basis the petitioner seeks to make out a case that the respondent nos. 1, 2 & 3 have not been appointed as the arbitrators; they have been appointed as the mediators. Upon hearing the learned Advocate and examining the annexures appended to the instant petition, it transpires that the petitioner’s basis of such claim is the written agreement dated 12.03.2018 executed between the partners of the MSC (the petitioner & the respondent no. 4) and all the three arbitrators, which was witnessed by two witnesses, namely, the wife of the petitioner and another person, who happens to be the common brother of the petitioner and the respondent no. 4. And, the reason for terming the arbitrators as the mediators is that the document (agreement dated 12.03.2018) incorporates the Bengali word ‘ga¨¯’ZvKvix’. However, it is evident from the correspondences that the arbitration proceedings commenced in the first week of February, 2018 and, in course of the arbitration proceedings, the arbitrators took a step to mediate the dispute in the month of March 2018 in an effort to resolve the dispute expeditiously and more effectively. Since the arbitrators are the close relatives of the petitioner as well as the respondent no. 4, the arbitrators’ above step appears to this Court to be bonafide and is also very much within the provisions of law, inasmuch as Section 22 of the Arbitration Act allows the arbitrators to opt for such a path. Section 22 of the Arbitration Act states that “it shall not be incompatible with an arbitration agreement for an arbitral tribunal to encourage settlement of the dispute otherwise than by arbitration and, with the agreement of all the parties, the arbitral tribunal may use mediation, conciliation or any other procedures at any time during the arbitral proceedings to encourage settlement.”

17.           More importantly, the agreement dated 12.03.2018 is written in Bengali and if the word ‘ga¨¯’ZvKvix’ is translated from the Bengali to English, the Dictionary published by the Bangla Academy provides the meaning of the word ‘ga¨¯’ZvKvix’ as both ‘arbitrator’ or ‘mediator’. That is to say that if the entire document is translated into English, the word in question would have to be read as ‘arbitrator’. Moreover, the fundamental difference between the mediators and the arbitrators are on the question of authority with powers related to giving a binding decision or not. And, since in clause 9 of the aforesaid arbitration agreement dated 12.03.2018 the arbitrators were authorized to award decisions binding upon both the parties, the agreement has the binding force upon the parties and, therefore, the agreement dated 12.03.2018 should be seen as a piece of document related to arbitration. Thus, this Court finds that the respondent nos. 1, 2 and 3 were never appointed as the mediators and they, having been appointed as the arbitrators, have constituted a lawful arbitral tribunal.

18.           Let me now take up the issue no. 3, namely, whether the arbitral tribunal was competent to pass the impugned interim Order, whether the interim Order was a proper and legal one and whether the impugned Order is appealable/challengeable in this Court. In view of the findings of this Court that the arbitral tribunal was formed as per the terms of the Partnership Deed dated 01.09.2012 and, also, in accordance with the provisions of the Arbitration Act, this Court has no option but to hold that the arbitral tribunal was well competent to pass any interim Order as mandated by the provisions of Section 21 of the Arbitration Act. Since the Proviso to sub-Section (3) of Section 21 of the Arbitration Act empowers the arbitral tribunal to pass an ex-parte interim Order in an appropriate case to meet the exigency, I find the impugned interim Order to be proper and legal. Although the interim Order in question has been signed by two arbitrators namely, Mrs Ayesha Huq and Mr. Afzal Husain Chowdhury, but it cannot be termed as an irregular Order, let alone to be an illegal Order. Because, after constitution of an arbitral tribunal, it may proceed to function with only two arbitrators, unless the third arbitrator raises any objection to any step taken or Order passed by the two arbitrators. Since, the third arbitrator, Mrs Rokeya Din, has not raised any objection to the interim Order in question, the same is to be taken as a valid and lawful Order of a properly constituted arbitral tribunal. And, in view of the clear stipulations made in Section 21(1) of the Arbitration Act that no appeal shall lie against any interim Order passed by the arbitral tribunal, an aggrieved party neither can approach this Court directly, nor the propriety and legality of an interim Order passed by the arbitral tribunal can be challenged in the form of seeking a declaration from a civil Court of first instance because of the prohibition imposed by the Legislature in Section 7 of the Arbitration Act.  

19.           I would now take up the fourth issue, namely, whether the law permits the petitioner to challenge the legality and propriety of formation of the arbitration tribunal comprising the respondent nos. 1, 2 & 3 and, thereby, to question the performance of the respondent nos. 1, 2 & 3 as the arbitrators. For adjudication upon the above issue, it appears to me that since the petitioner has questioned the appointment and performance of the arbitrators, quotation of the relevant portions of Sections 13 & 14 of the Arbitration Act would be useful.

(3)        Unless the arbitrator challenged under sub-section (2), withdraws from his office or the other party agrees to the challenge, the arbitral tribunal shall decide on the challenge within thirty days from the date of filing the written statement referred to in sub-section 2.

20.           From the above provisions of law, it appears that a party to an arbitration agreement may challenge any arbitrator’s competency and impartiality by making a written representation to the arbitral tribunal within 30 (thirty) days of knowing about the incompetency or partiality of the concerned arbitrator.

21.           In this case, the impugned interim Order was passed on 02.08.2018, the respondent no. 4 approached the Court of District Judge on 12.08.2018 for enforcement of the said Order and the petitioner entered his appearance on 19.08.2018. So, for the sake of argument, if it is presumed that the petitioner was not aware of appointment of arbitrators and formation of the tribunal and he came to know only after receiving notice from the Court of District Judge, Dhaka pursuant to filing the Arbitration Miscellaneous Case No. 501 of 2018 on 12.08.2018, it transpires that the petitioner has still time to approach the arbitral tribunal inasmuch as his thirty days would have expired on 13.09.2018. Since before expiry of thirty days, he approached this Court on 30.08.2018 wrongly, the period spent in this wrong forum shall not be counted and, accordingly, he is competent to approach the tribunal within fourteen days of receipt of this Judgment to challenge the appointment of the arbitrators. Thus, this Court holds that the petitioner is at liberty to challenge the appointment and performance/actions of the arbitrators before the arbitral tribunal within the remaining time-limit, and if the arbitrators do not step down from their respective positions, the petitioner shall have the right to prefer an appeal before this Court.

22.           And, if the petitioner’s intention is to obtain a ruling on the jurisdiction of the tribunal, the petitioner ought to have approached the tribunal. In this regard, this Court finds it useful to look at the provisions of Section 17 of the Arbitration Act, which run as follows:

17. Competence of arbitral tribunal to rule on its own jurisdiction-Unless otherwise agreed by the parties, the arbitral tribunal may rule on its own jurisdiction on any questions including the following issues, namely-

(a)        whether there is existence of a valid arbitration agreement;

(b)       whether the arbitral tribunal is properly constituted;

(c)        whether the arbitration agreement is against the public policy.

(d)       whether the arbitration agreement is incapable of being performed, and,

(e)        what matters have been submitted to arbitration in accordance with the arbitration agreement.

23.           From a plain reading of the above provisions, it can be noticed that the Legislature has endowed the arbitral tribunal with the power to adjudicate upon the issue as to its own competency. Therefore, it was incumbent upon the petitioner to ask the tribunal to decide on the question as to whether the tribunal is properly constituted or not. It means that the Arbitration Act permits a party to an arbitration agreement to challenge the competency and impartiality of a single arbitrator or all the arbitrators by filing a written representation to the arbitral tribunal, so that the arbitral tribunal itself can give a decision on the aforesaid application for challenge. Also, if a party to the arbitration agreement raises question that the arbitral tribunal is not properly constituted, it is a mandatory duty of the arbitral tribunal to pass an Order on the issue. 

24.           Let me now adjudicate upon the fifth issue, namely, whether the petitioner is competent to file the instant application during pendency of the Miscellaneous Arbitration Case before the Court of the District Judge. The above issue has been structured to answer the argument of the learned Advocate for the respondent no.4 that by Section 53 of the Arbitration Act, the Legislature intended that whenever any party to an arbitration agreement would approach a Court regarding any issue arising out of the said arbitration agreement, all the subsequent applications have to be filed in the said Court. Therefore, let me be familiar with the provisions of Section 53 of the Arbitration Act, which is quoted below:

53. Jurisdiction-Notwithstanding anything contained elsewhere in this Act or in another law for the time being in force, where with respect to an arbitration agreement any application under this Act has been made in a Court-           

(a)        that Court alone shall have jurisdiction over the arbitral proceedings, and

(b)       all subsequent applications arising out of that agreement and the arbitral proceedings shall be made in that Court and in no other Court.

25.           The literal meaning of the above provisions is that once a party to an arbitration agreement approaches any Court, all the parties to the said arbitration agreement shall have to go to that Court for filing any additional or consequential or ancillary or any independent application, until the arbitral proceedings finally comes to an end.

26.           However, from a minute reading of the provisions of Section 53 of the Arbitration Act, it appears to this Court that it requires an extensive and in-depth statutory interpretation taking into consideration the following scenario; (a) if a party to the arbitration, at first, approaches the District Judge Court for obtaining interim Order before formation of the arbitral tribunal and, thereafter, if the said party challenges an arbitrator’s competency or impartiality to the arbitral tribunal unsuccessfully, in that event, the said aggrieved party requires to prefer an appeal under Section 14(4)  of the Arbitration Act and the pertinent question then would arise as to whether Section 53 of the Arbitration Act is capable of taking away the specific appellate jurisdiction of the High Court Division for the purpose of conferring the said jurisdiction on its subordinate Court, (b) if a party to the arbitration agreement, initially, approaches the District Judge Court for appointment of an arbitrator and, later on, his challenge about the competency and impartiality of an arbitrator before the arbitral tribunal fails, will he approach the District Judge Court ignoring the specific appellate jurisdiction of the High Court Division conferred upon it by Section 14(4) of the Arbitration Act, (c) when a foreign party of a civil suit pending in the District Judge Court makes an application for staying the proceedings under Section 10 of the Arbitration Act, will the foreign party file application for interim Order in the District Judge Court, in the backdrop of existence of the provisions of Section 7A of the Arbitration Act, which requires that the High Court Division shall hear such application, (d) when a party to the arbitration, at first, approaches the District Judge Court either for obtaining interim Order under Section 7A of the Arbitration Act or makes application under Section 10 of the Arbitration Act in the District Judge Court for staying proceedings of the civil suit pending therein or asks the District Judge Court to appoint the arbitrator under Section 12 of the Arbitration Act and, then, during pendency of the arbitral proceeding, if he wants to challenge the jurisdiction of the arbitral tribunal in a Court, not in the arbitral tribunal under Section 17 of the Arbitration Act, will the provisions of Section 20 of the Arbitration Act be of any use for him to approach the Court of District Judge, in other words, will the jurisdiction and power of the High Court Division under Section 20 of the Arbitration Act be ipso facto conferred upon and exercised by the District Judge Court and (e) when a party  to the arbitral proceedings approaches the District Judge Court invoking Section 21(4) of the Arbitration Act for enforcement of the interim Order, whether any party to the said arbitration is barred from approaching the High Court Division by the provisions of Section 53 of the Arbitration Act for making applications under Section 7A, 10, 12 & 20 of the Arbitration Act.

27.           There are two vital aspects of the provisions of Section 53 of the Arbitration Act. The first aspect is that through its non-obstante provisions, it seeks to herald that irrespective of any statements, stipulations and provisions to the contrary made in the Arbitration Act, or any other law, the provisions of Section 53 of the Arbitration Act shall stand. The second aspect is - if any party to the arbitration agreement registers its name in a Court for any purpose, that Court shall assume jurisdiction over the arbitration agreement and arbitral agreement in whose connection a party have moved before the Court.

28.           Now, the question comes up for consideration as to whether the jurisdiction conferred specifically upon, and the power vested exclusively in, the High Court Division vide Sections 7A, 10, 12, 14(4) & 20 of the Arbitration Act can be discarded by a non-obstante provision, and that too for the purpose of endowing a sub-ordinate Court with the said jurisdiction and power of the High Court Division. While as a normal course of action, the Legislature does not go for enactment of a law by which the superior Court’s jurisdiction and power will be scrapped or curtailed in order to enrich and empower the sub-ordinate Courts with the said jurisdiction and power, yet if the Legislature in their wisdom considers it necessary, they are competent to enact such law. However, this sort of enactment is normally made by the Legislature in clearer and specific expressions and, furthermore, in passing such laws the Parliament usually tables some explanations before the House.

29.           Given the fact that in Section 53 of the Arbitration Act, the Legislature has incorporated the word “†Kv‡bv Av`vj‡Z” (in a Court), and Section 2(b) of the Arbitration Act defines “Court” means “District Judge’s Court and includes Additional District Judge’s Court appointed by the Government for discharging the functions of District Judge’s Court under this Act through Gazette notification”, it is difficult for anyone to comprehend the literal meaning. Because, though in ordinary sense the High Court Division is also a Court, but the Legislature consciously has not included the High Court Division as the ‘Court’ in Section 2(b), rather the Parliament has provided a separate definition of the ‘High Court Division’ in Section 2(q) in the following terms; “High Court Division means High Court Division of the Supreme Court of Bangladesh”. Therefore, this Court is led to hold that ‘Court’ means District Judge Court and, thus, the literal meaning should be that whenever any party to an arbitration agreement will take recourse to the Court of District Judge of any particular District, the said District Judge Court shall have the exclusive jurisdiction over the said arbitration matter up to the period of final termination of the arbitral proceedings, and not any other District Judge Court. The specific jurisdictions conferred upon the High Court Division, thus, are not meant to have been taken away by the provisions of Section 53 of the Arbitration Act. The golden rule of statutory interpretation is that when any ambiguity appears in a provision of a statute, the first option for the Court is to find out its literal meaning. And, in the event that it becomes a complex task for the Court to go with the above rule, only then, the Court would endeavour to discover its meaning with the help of the preamble and other provisions of the concerned statute without making any of its provisions nugatory. In this case, if this Court interprets the word “Court” to be the all types of Court, then, while on the one hand, Sections 2(b) & 2(q) shall turn to be superfluous, on the other hand, the specific jurisdiction conferred upon the High Court Division will be abridged abruptly.

30.           Having held as above, I find that since it is beyond the jurisdiction of the Court of District Judge, Dhaka to decide on the issue of the competency, impartiality of the arbitrators and to rule on the jurisdiction of the arbitral tribunal, the petitioner’s apparent fora for that purpose are (i) the arbitral tribunal itself and (ii) the High Court Division.

31.           Given the fact that the respondent no. 4 has approached the Court of the District Judge for enforcement of the interim Order passed by the arbitral tribunal under Section 21(4) of the Arbitration Act and the said Court being incapable of assuming jurisdiction under Section 20 of the Arbitration Act, the petitioner has nothing to agitate before the aforesaid Court.

32.           The sixth issue is whether this Court is competent to entertain and adjudicate upon the question relating to jurisdiction of arbitration tribunal. Since the petitioner’s apparent fora for addressing his grievances are either the arbitral tribunal to invoke the provisions of Sections 14(2) and 17(b) of the Arbitration Act or the High Court Division to invoke Section 20 of the Arbitration Act and, seemingly, the petitioner availed himself of the forum of the High Court Division, thus, at this stage, it would be profitable if the provisions of Section 20 of the Arbitration Act are examined by the Court. Section 20 of the Arbitration Act runs as follows:

20. Powers of the High Court Division in deciding jurisdiction-(1) The High Court Division may, on the application of any of the parties to the arbitration agreement, after serving notice upon all other parties, determine any question as to the jurisdiction of the arbitral tribunal.

(2)    No application under this section shall be taken into account, unless the High Court Division is satisfied that-

(a)    The determination of the question is likely to produce substantial savings in costs.

(b)   the application was submitted without any delay; and

(c)    there is good reason why the matter should be decided by the Court.

(3)    The application shall state-

The reasons on which the matter should be decided by the High Court Division.

(4)    Unless otherwise agreed by the parties, where any application is pending before the High Court Division under this section, the arbitral tribunal shall continue arbitration proceedings and make an arbitral award.

33.           From a minute reading of the entire provisions of Section 20 of the Arbitration Act, my understanding is that sub-Section (1) of Section 20 of the Arbitration Act is the provision which enables and empowers the High Court Division to entertain an application from any party to the arbitration agreement regarding jurisdiction of the arbitral tribunal, subject to fulfilling the conditions set out in sub-Section (2) of Section 20 of the Arbitration Act. Thus, a petitioner, who has invoked Section 20 of the Arbitration Act, is under a mandatory obligation to  satisfy the High Court Division that all the conditions stipulated in clauses (a) to (c) of Section 20(2) of the Arbitration Act are satisfied before entertaining or registering an application. In other words, the power invested in the High Court Division for entertaining an application under Section 20 of the Arbitration Act is not an independent power, for, it can be exercised by the High Court Division only upon being satisfied that (a) the determination of the question is likely to produce substantial savings in costs, (b) the application was submitted without any delay and (c) there is good reason why the matter should be decided by the Court. Therefore, the High Court Division’s power having been circumscribed by Section 20(2) of the Arbitration Act, an application under Section 20 of the Arbitration Act should be taken into account by the Court only after being satisfied as to fulfilling the conditions set out in clauses (a) to (c) of Section 20(2) of the Arbitration Act. Additionally, the petitioner is also required to state the reasons/arguments/ grounds, based on which the High Court Division would dispose of the application.

34.           From a meticulous scrutiny of the averments made in this application and careful examination of the annexed papers, I did not find that filing of the instant application before the High Court Division helped the petitioner or the respondents to save costs, nor did I find that the application was filed without squandering time inasmuch as the interim Order was passed by the arbitral tribunal on 02.08.2018, whereas the present application has been filed on 30.08.2018, save and except finding that the petitioner has placed before this Court some reasons as to why the question of jurisdiction should be adjudicated upon by this Court. However, Section 20(2) of the Arbitration Act requires that the petitioner must simultaneously satisfy all the three conditions laid down in clauses (a) to (c) thereof, for, none of the clauses (a) to (c) ends up with the word ‘or’. Since the petitioner has failed to satisfy this Court about how he is saving costs by invoking the provisions of Section 20 of the Arbitration Act and, also, since the petitioner approached this Court after nearly one month of passing the interim Order of the arbitral tribunal, I find that the petitioner ought to have challenged the jurisdiction of the tribunal before the arbitration tribunal. Therefore, this Court is led to hold that the instant application cannot be entertained for adjudication upon the question of jurisdiction of the tribunal and the interim Order passed by this Court at the time of admission of this matter on 03.09.2018, is liable to be vacated. Furthermore, in view of the stipulations made in sub-Section (4) of Section 20 of the Arbitration Act by the Legislature that “unless otherwise agreed by the parties, where any application is pending before the High Court Division under this Section, the arbitral tribunal shall continue arbitration proceedings and make an arbitral award”, the arbitral tribunal is under an obligation to continue with the arbitration proceedings towards disposing of the arbitration pending before it.

35.           The seventh issue is whether the petitioner has waived his right to challenge the appointment of the arbitrators and, also, whether he has waived his right to object to the impugned interim Order. The above issue has to be framed by this Court in order to address the submissions of the learned Advocate for the respondent no. 4 with reference to the provisions of Section 6 of the Arbitration Act and the case of Joinal Abedin Jamal Vs Noor Afzal 63 DLR 432. Let me, therefore, see the provisions of Section 6 of the Arbitration Act, which run as follows:

6. Waiver of right to object-A party who knows that-

(a)    any provision of this Act from which the parties may derogate, or

(b)    any requirement under the arbitration agreement, has not been complied with and yet proceeds with the arbitration without stating his objection to such non-compliance without undue delay or, if a time limit is provided thereof within such period of time, shall be deemed to have waived his right to so object.

36.           From a careful reading of the provisions of Section 6 of the Arbitration Act, it appears to this Court that when a party before the arbitral tribunal does not raise any objection with regard to non-compliance of any provision of the Arbitration Act or with regard to non-fulfillment of any requirement set out in the arbitration agreement within the time prescribed in the Arbitration Act and/or within the time-limit stipulated in the arbitration agreement or, in the absence of any stipulation as to the time in the law or in the arbitration agreement, within a reasonable time without making any delay, for dealing with that scenario, the Legislature mandated the arbitral tribunal and the Courts to take a presumption that the aforesaid party has waived his right to so object.

37.           The crucial aspect of the provisions of Section 6 is, thus, the time to raise such objection, which is required to be prescribed either in the law or to be stipulated in the arbitration agreement. Since in this case, the petitioner is raising question about appointment of arbitrators towards the formation of the arbitral tribunal in question, the respondents are taking a plea that the petitioner, by not raising the objection immediately after knowing about the appointment of the arbitrators or soon after passing the impugned interim order dated 02.08.2018, has waived his right.

38.           Upon skimming through the provisions (a) relating to appointment of arbitrators, (b) regarding raising challenge as to holding the position of the arbitrator and (c) about raising question as to the jurisdiction of the arbitral tribunal, it appears to this Court that no time-frame is prescribed in the law for raising an objection as to appointment of the members of the arbitral tribunal, nor is there any such stipulation in the arbitration agreement executed by the partners of the MSC (the petitioner and the respondent no. 4) and, therefore, the only tool to assess the time limit is whether the petitioner has made undue delay in raising objection as to the appointment of arbitrators and formation of the arbitral tribunal. This Court’s view is that since the parties to the ongoing arbitration proceedings are yet to file their respective written pleadings before the arbitral tribunal, there has not been any such delay in raising the objection as to appointment of the arbitrators or as to the jurisdiction of the arbitral tribunal which can be termed as undue or unreasonable. In the cited case of Joinal Abedin Jamal Vs Noor Afza 63 DLR 432, the aggrieved party raised objection as to appointment of the arbitrator after passing the award by the arbitral tribunal, and that too before the Execution Court, which led this Court to hold that by not raising the objection as to the appointment of the arbitrator before the arbitral tribunal within a reasonable time, the Court deems that the party has waived his right to raise such objection as to the appointment of arbitrators or jurisdiction of the arbitral tribunal. 

39.           I would finally take up the last three issues, being issue nos. viii, ix & x, together for the sake of their better resolution. The issues are whether the present Members and the Chairman of arbitral tribunal should continue to perform their functions, whether this Court should constitute/create a new arbitral tribunal and whether the Arbitration Miscellaneous Case No. 501/18 pending before the Court of District Judge, Dhaka should be kept in abeyance, so that the new arbitrators may review the impugned interim Order passed by the arbitration tribunal on 02.08.2018. The above issues have been framed by this Court to consider the suggestions proposed by the arbitrators before this Court.

40.           In order to deal with these three issues, I need to sum up the findings made by this Court hereinabove. The findings of this Court on the 7 (seven) issues so far dealt with by this Court are: (a) there is existence of an arbitration agreement between the partners of the MSC (i.e. between the petitioner and the respondent no. 4) and they are bound to resolve their dispute through the arbitration, (b) the arbitral tribunal is a valid one and the tribunal lawfully passed the impugned interim Order, (c) for resolution of the petitioner’s grievance, the apparent fora are either the arbitral tribunal for invocation of the provisions of Section 14(2) of the Arbitration Act and Section 17 of the Arbitration Act or the High Court Division for seeking a ruling on the jurisdiction of the arbitral tribunal under Section 20 of the Arbitration Act, (d) the petitioner’s instant application under Section 20 of the Arbitration Act cannot be entertained by this Court, (e) the petitioner has not waived his right to object to the appointment of arbitrator nor has he waived his right to raise objection as to the jurisdiction of the arbitral tribunal and (f) the Court of District Judge is not competent to hear and adjudicate upon the issues of appointment of arbitrators or jurisdiction of the arbitral tribunal. Now, the only option remains for the petitioner to go back to the arbitral tribunal and file application before it either under Section 14(2) of the Arbitration Act or under Section 17 of the Arbitration Act.

41.           After delivering of the Judgment with regard to the core issues of this case, namely, issue nos. (i) to (vii), this Court expressed its opinion in the open-Court addressing the parties of this case that although this Court has held hereinbefore that the tribunal has been formed lawfully and the interim Order passed by the said tribunal is also valid, however, this Court is going to afford this petitioner Mr. Ghulam Mohiuddin (Bhutto) an opportunity to approach the tribunal with an application for reviewing the tribunal’s Order dated 02.08.2018. At this juncture, this Court reminded both the parties that as per the prayers of both the parties this Court has adjudicated upon the core issues. Now, it is up to the parties to resolve the dispute either through the arbitral tribunal constituted by the present arbitrators or through reconstituting the arbitral tribunal as per the suggestions put forward by the arbitrators when they appeared before this Court. This Court opined that it would be fair for all the parties if the tribunal is reconstituted with one nominated person from each of the side and the Chairman is appointed by this Court. If they wish to resolve the dispute through a reconstituted arbitral tribunal by appointing any one from the concerned arbitrators, they must first take consent from the present arbitrator/s and, in the event that s/he would be comfortable to work with the newly appointed members and Chairman, only then his/her name may be proposed by the parties as the member of the reconstituted tribunal. In reply thereto, after consulting with the present arbitrators and also receiving the instructions from their respective clients, Mr. Tanvir Parvez, the learned Advocate for the petitioner proposes the name of Mr. Debborata Laha, son of Promod Chandra Laha & Maya Rani Banik, RM Center (4th Floor), 101 Gulshan Avenue, Dhaka-1212 (Contact: 01978-004545) as the arbitrator of the petitioner, Mr. Ghulam Mohiuddin (Bhutto) and Ms. Faria Huq, the learned Advocate for the respondent no. 4, proposes the name of Mr. Afzal Husain Choudhury, son of late Abdul Hossain Chowdhury, House No. 55, Road No. 4A, Dhanmondi, Dhaka-1209 (Contact: 01714-004587) as the arbitrator of the respondent no. 4, Mr. Zahed Obaidul Huq Chowdhury.
